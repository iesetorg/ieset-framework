# Steelman: oecd_product_market_deregulation_tfp_panel

## Counter-argument

OECD PMR reductions in the 1990s and 2000s coincided with the ICT revolution,
EU Single Market deepening, and global value-chain integration. The observed
TFP gains may reflect these technology and trade shocks rather than domestic
deregulation. Moreover, some highly regulated economies (Germany, Japan)
maintained strong TFP growth through incremental innovation within regulated
frameworks, suggesting that regulation type matters more than regulation level.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **ICT confound**: The 1998-2019 period covers the ICT adoption wave.
   Countries with flexible labour markets (not just product markets) may have
   adapted faster to ICT, conflating product-market deregulation with labour-
   market flexibility.

2. **EU Single Market channel**: Many PMR reductions were driven by EU
   directives (telecoms, energy, postal services). The TFP gains may reflect
   market-size expansion rather than domestic deregulation.

3. **Enforcement gaps**: The PMR index measures de jure regulation, not de
   facto enforcement. Countries with low PMR scores but weak competition
   enforcement (e.g. Italy) may not have realised TFP gains.

4. **Sectoral heterogeneity**: PMR is an aggregate index. Deregulation in
   network industries (telecoms, electricity) likely has larger TFP effects
   than deregulation in professional services or retail.

## What would flip the verdict

- A null or negative coefficient after controlling for ICT capital deepening
  and EU membership.
- Decomposition showing that only telecoms/electricity deregulation drives
  the result, with no effect from professional-services or retail deregulation.
- Evidence that countries with large PMR reductions but weak competition
  agency budgets showed no TFP gains.
