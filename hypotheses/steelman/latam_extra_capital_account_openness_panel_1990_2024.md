# Steelman — Latin America capital-account openness panel 1990-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis claims openness pays off conditional on institutions. The opposing view (Rodrik, Stiglitz, Ostry-Loungani) is that capital-account openness in emerging markets imports volatility independent of institutional quality, and that the institutional-conditional framing is a confidence trick that cherry-picks successful cases.

Specific objections:

1. **Sudden-stops are institutionally-blind.** Mexico 1994, Brazil 1999, Argentina 2001/2018, Chile 1982 — all show that capital-flow reversals can hit institutionally-strong economies. The volatility cost is structural, not institutional.

2. **WGI is endogenous to economic outcomes.** Treating WGI as exogenous control while testing openness loads the dice; institutional quality and openness move together because both reflect elite-policy commitments.

3. **Chile post-1990 carries the regression.** Removing Chile from the sample collapses much of the openness-growth correlation in LATAM, suggesting one-country dominance.

4. **Reverse causation.** Faster-growing economies attract more FDI and accumulate more external assets. The coefficient on openness may identify reverse causation more than causal openness effects.

5. **2008 GFC and 2013 taper-tantrum impose conditional costs.** Open economies experienced sharper financial-condition tightening; the institutional-conditional framing must account for the fact that even high-WGI economies suffered.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report sample-without-Chile robustness check.
(b) Show 2008-2009 and 2013 sub-sample event responses.
(c) Acknowledge WGI endogeneity caveat.
(d) Treat openness-growth coefficient cautiously.
