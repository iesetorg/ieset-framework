# Steelman — Generative-AI sector productivity divergence 2023-2026

## The strongest opposing argument

Brynjolfsson's "great divergence" prediction is testable but premature on a 2026 endpoint, and the test as specified will produce a false positive or false negative for sector-aggregate measurement reasons unrelated to the underlying mechanism.

**Key opposing objections:**

1. **The J-curve is in the wrong phase for measurement.** Brynjolfsson's own model predicts a productivity *decline* in early adoption years as firms invest in complementary intangible capital (training, process redesign, organisational restructuring) — measured productivity should be flat or negative 2023-2026 even if long-run gains are real. Picking 2026 as the endpoint biases against the boost case the hypothesis is trying to test. A more honest test would have a 2030 endpoint and a flat-or-negative 2024-2026 prediction as supporting evidence for the J-curve.

2. **Sectoral exposure scores measure task exposure, not adoption.** Eloundou-Manning-Mishkin's GPT-exposure scores are theoretical — the share of tasks where LLM use *could* halve completion time. Actual adoption (Census BTOS) is far lower than exposure, very heterogeneous, and concentrated in a few firms within sectors. Sector-mean adoption masks within-sector heterogeneity that matters for the productivity coefficient.

3. **High-exposure sectors had high pre-period productivity growth anyway.** Information and professional services were already 1-2pp/yr above the economy mean 2015-2019. Sector-level FE absorbs the level but not the trend; the divergence threshold could be met from pure pre-existing trend extrapolation. The 2pp pre-trend control is necessary but may be insufficient.

4. **The Acemoglu objection.** Acemoglu (2024) argues macro-level productivity gains from generative AI are likely <1% over a decade — most use cases are "so-so automation" that displaces labour without raising aggregate output. On his calibration, the SUPPORTED threshold of 3pp+ over four years is essentially impossible regardless of mechanism strength.

## What the framework still captures

A 2026 sector-productivity test isn't dispositive on the long-run AI-productivity question, but it is a meaningful prior update. If high-exposure sectors run materially below low-exposure sectors 2022-2026, that's evidence the J-curve is real and the early-diffusion costs are dominating. If they run materially above, that's evidence the diffusion is faster than Brynjolfsson predicted. The wide PARTIAL band (1-3pp) reflects honest uncertainty about phase position, and the pre-trend baseline blocks the most obvious confound. Most usefully, this hypothesis pre-registers a measurable claim against a cohort of pundits making confident assertions in either direction with no falsification rule attached.
