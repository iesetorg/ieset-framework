# Steelman — US WWII fiscal expansion did not produce sustained inflation

**Position:** mmt (claim_index 2). **School prediction:** supported.

The WWII case is the cleanest large-deficit, high-debt natural
experiment in the 20th-century US record. Federal deficits reached
20-27% of GDP during 1942-1945 (peak 26.9% in 1943) and the
debt-to-GDP ratio approached 106% by 1946. MMT reads this as
confirming the real-constraint view: inflation only persists when
nominal demand chronically exceeds real productive capacity, not
mechanically because debt is high or deficits are large.

The mechanism the MMT framing emphasises is real-resource crowding
out, not a money-supply identity. WWII produced a genuine
real-resource bottleneck — war production crowded out civilian
output, and price controls (the Emergency Price Control Act of
January 1942 through OPA termination in November 1946) suppressed the
price-level adjustment that would otherwise have happened in real
time. Once controls came off and real productive capacity returned to
civilian use through demobilisation, the price-level reset happened
in a single post-control surge (1946-1948, peaking at 14.4% YoY in
1947) and then inflation fell back to roughly the pre-war norm by
1949-1953, averaging well under 3%/yr across that window.

The Korean War 1951 spike (7.9% YoY) is a separate fiscal-impulse
event, not a delayed WWII overhang. The fact that the federal balance
immediately reversed to surplus in 1947-1948 (as demobilisation cut
military spending from wartime peaks) is part of the same
real-constraint story: nominal demand fell back into line with real
capacity, and inflation followed.

The author's framing-dependence caveat (that on a generous 1946-1953
8-year window the mean is ~5%/yr, dragged up by the 1946-1947-1948
surge) is correct as a window-arithmetic point, but it does not
defeat the steelman. The MMT framing distinguishes the one-time
post-control price-level reset from sustained inflation; the
PRIMARY 1949-1953 window is exactly the window in which the
sustained-inflation question can be tested cleanly, with price
controls fully washed out and real productive capacity restored.
On that window inflation is at the pre-war norm despite a federal
debt of ~100% of GDP — the strongest available historical evidence
for the real-constraint view.
