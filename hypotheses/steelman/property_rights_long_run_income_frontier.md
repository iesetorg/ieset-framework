# Steelman — Property-rights protection vs state investment for long-run income

## Strongest version of the claim

Property-rights protection predicts 40-year real income per capita growth more strongly than state investment share of GDP across a broad panel of economies. The mechanism is Smithian: secure property rights lower the risk premium on irreversible investment, encourage capital accumulation, and enable the price-discovery process that allocates resources efficiently. State investment, without secure property rights, risks politicised allocation, white-elephant projects, and crowding out of private returns. The claim is a horse-race between institutional and fiscal channels.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Horse-race dominance:** Panel-FE or cross-sectional regression shows property-rights composite index with larger absolute t-statistic than state-investment share on 40-year income growth.
2. **Significance:** Property-rights coefficient positive and significant at p<0.05; state-investment coefficient insignificant or smaller in magnitude.
3. **Tercile magnitude:** Top-tercile property-rights countries show at least 0.5 pp higher annual growth than bottom-tercile countries over 40-year windows.
4. **Ex-Africa robustness:** Result holds when sub-Saharan Africa is excluded, confirming it is not driven by the continent's weak institutions alone.

## Best counterarguments

1. **East Asian developmental state:** Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and China combined rapid growth with high state-investment shares and, in some periods, weak formal property rights (especially China). State investment appears to have been the binding constraint during catch-up, not property rights.
2. **Measurement endogeneity:** Property-rights indices (WGI, Fraser, Heritage) are highly correlated with general income and institutional quality. Disentangling property rights from state capacity, education, and geography is difficult.
3. **Complementarity not substitution:** Secure property rights and state investment may be complements, not substitutes. The horse-race is misleading if the optimal policy mix requires both.
4. **China outlier:** China is the largest growth episode in history and occurred with weak property-rights protection (no private land ownership, insecure IPR). Excluding China substantially weakens the institutions-growth correlation.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for long-horizon cross-country comparisons where institutional differences compound. It weakens for: (i) catch-up economies where technology transfer via state investment may dominate institutional quality in early stages; (ii) resource-rich economies where property rights over natural rents, not productive capital, are the binding constraint; (iii) post-conflict reconstruction where state investment in infrastructure is urgently needed regardless of property-rights formalisation.
