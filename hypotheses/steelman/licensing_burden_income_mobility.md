# Steelman — Licensing burden, entrepreneurship, and intergenerational mobility

## Strongest version of the claim

Higher occupational and business licensing burdens causally reduce entrepreneurship rates and lower intergenerational income mobility over long horizons. The mechanism is dual: (i) licensing raises time and money costs of entry, filtering out capital-constrained potential entrepreneurs from lower-income backgrounds; (ii) restricted entry reduces competition in licensed occupations, raising prices and reducing service accessibility for low-income consumers. The claim is strongest for the United States, where state-level variation in licensing prevalence is large and well-documented.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Negative panel-FE coefficient:** State-year panel shows occupational-licensing prevalence negatively associated with firm-formation rate and absolute upward mobility at p<0.05.
2. **Magnitude:** A one-standard-deviation increase in licensing burden reduces absolute upward mobility by at least 1.5 percentage points and firm formation by at least 5-10%.
3. **Robustness to state trends:** Coefficient remains negative when state-specific linear time trends are included, addressing reverse-causation concerns.
4. **Bartik-style instrument:** Using national licensing trends interacted with state baseline occupational composition as an instrument, the IV estimate is also negative and significant.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Quality assurance:** Licensing may improve service quality, consumer safety, and health outcomes, which benefit low-income populations disproportionately. A null or positive coefficient on mobility could reflect quality gains offsetting entry costs.
2. **Endogenous regulation:** States with more professional-class voters (higher education, higher income) both regulate more and have different mobility patterns. The correlation may reflect political economy rather than causal licensing effects.
3. **Alternative mobility drivers:** Declining mobility is driven by residential segregation, school finance inequality, and family structure — all of which swamp licensing effects in magnitude.
4. **Cross-country weakens:** The US result may not generalise to OECD countries with lower baseline licensing prevalence (Denmark, Sweden) or to developing countries where informal sector entry is unaffected by formal licensing.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for US state-level analysis where data is richest. It weakens for: (i) occupations where licensing genuinely addresses information asymmetry and health risks (medicine, engineering); (ii) countries with strong apprenticeship or vocational pathways that provide alternative upward-mobility routes; (iii) periods of rapid technological change where new occupations emerge faster than licensing boards can regulate them.
