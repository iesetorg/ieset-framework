# Steelman: Government Consumption Share and TFP Growth

## Strongest version of the claim
Government consumption expenditure—spending on public administration, defence, and current operations—may crowd out more productive uses of resources or signal a state that prioritises redistribution and bureaucracy over productive investment. The strongest claim is that, holding public investment, education, and health constant, a larger government-consumption share predicts slower TFP growth because it reflects less efficient resource allocation, higher tax distortions, or reduced room for private-sector experimentation.

## Key evidence the claim would need
- A robust negative coefficient on government-consumption share in a panel of countries with country and year fixed effects, controlling for public investment, human capital, and initial income.
- The result should survive when using alternative TFP measures (PWT, OECD, growth-accounting residuals).
- Sub-sample stability: the negative association should hold among OECD countries and not be driven entirely by emerging-market outliers.

## Best counterarguments
- Government consumption includes productive services (law and order, contract enforcement, public administration) that are complements to private TFP; a negative coefficient may capture reverse causation (low-growth countries spend more on transfers disguised as consumption).
- Measurement error in decomposing government spending into consumption vs investment is large and varies across countries.
- The association may disappear when controlling for overall state capacity or regulatory quality.

## Boundary conditions
- The claim applies to long-run TFP growth, not short-run demand management or crisis periods.
- It is most testable among middle- and high-income countries where government consumption is a deliberate policy choice rather than a donor-funded residual.
- Separating consumption from investment in developing-country budgets is often unreliable.

## Relation to existing hypotheses
- Complements `public_spending_composition_growth` by isolating the consumption component.
- Tensions with `infrastructure_gap_state_investment_high_return`, which finds high returns to state investment in low-income settings where consumption and investment are harder to separate.
