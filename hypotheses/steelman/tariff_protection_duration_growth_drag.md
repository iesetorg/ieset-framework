# Steelman — Longer protection duration predicts weaker subsequent export competitiveness and lower consumer welfare

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that sustained tariff protection creates dynamic inefficiencies that compound over time: protected domestic firms face weaker competitive pressure to innovate, reduce costs, and upgrade quality; resources are locked into import-substituting sectors with below-frontier productivity; and the anti-export bias of protection reduces the economy's exposure to learning-by-exporting and scale economies in global markets. The duration dimension matters because temporary protection may allow infant-industry learning, while prolonged protection becomes permanent shelter for inefficiency. The consumption drag arises because protected domestic prices are higher than world prices, compressing real household purchasing power.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Negative duration coefficient**: A robustly negative coefficient on protection duration in export-growth and consumption-growth regressions, significant at conventional levels, with the level of current tariff also controlled.
2. **Dynamic pattern**: Local projections should show that the negative effect strengthens with the length of the protection spell, not just its level.
3. **Frontier vs catch-up heterogeneity**: The coefficient should be more negative for high-income countries (where protection has no infant-industry rationale) than for low-income countries (where temporary protection may have learning benefits).
4. **Channel evidence**: Import penetration should be lower and export diversification slower in long-protection economies, confirming the mechanism.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Infant-industry success cases**: South Korea, Taiwan, and to some extent Brazil achieved rapid industrialisation under sustained protection. The duration measure would code these as long-protection episodes, yet export competitiveness and growth were strong. The hypothesis must engage these cases rather than dismiss them as outliers.
2. **Endogeneity of protection duration**: Countries maintain protection because they are not growing, not the other way around. Slow export growth may cause political pressure for protection, creating reverse causality that the panel FE design cannot fully resolve.
3. **Commodity-price confounding**: Protection duration may be correlated with primary-commodity dependence (resource-rich countries often protect manufacturing while exporting commodities). The terms-of-trade control may not fully capture this.
4. **Consumer welfare ambiguity**: Protection raises prices for importables but may also raise wages in protected sectors. The net effect on real household consumption depends on labour-market structure, which the specification does not model.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for middle-income and high-income economies where the infant-industry rationale is weak and where protection is primarily political-economy driven. It is weakest for: (a) least-developed countries where nascent manufacturing genuinely needs temporary shelter; (b) economies facing predatory dumping where temporary protection may preserve viable industries; (c) strategic sectors (defence, food security) where non-economic objectives justify protection regardless of growth effects.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a dynamic extension of `unilateral_tariff_liberalisation_growth_20yr` and a negative mirror of `infant_industry_protection_capability_effect`. It tests the temporal dimension: not whether protection helps or hurts on average, but whether sustained protection creates compounding costs. A null result would support the "protection is neutral in the long run" view; a positive result (longer protection predicts faster growth) would strongly support the developmentalist position.
