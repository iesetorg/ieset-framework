# Steelman — Household debt Minsky cycle 2008

**The strongest argument against the Minsky-framed reading:**

The empirical pattern — household credit-to-GDP rose pre-crisis in countries that subsequently experienced banking distress — is not in dispute. The contestable steps are: (a) calling the pattern "endogenous fragility" rather than the joint outcome of housing-supply restriction, monetary policy, and securitisation regulatory choices; (b) the predictive power surviving conditioning on real-house-price change; and (c) the cross-country generalisability of a hypothesis whose archetypal cases (US, UK, Spain, Ireland) all share a specific housing-and-finance institutional mix.

Specific objections:

1. **Conditioning collinearity.** Household credit growth and house-price growth are mechanically related — banks lend against rising collateral, and rising collateral supports more lending. In panel-FE specifications, conditioning on house-price change typically attenuates the household-credit coefficient sharply, sometimes to insignificance. If the credit-change coefficient is significant only when house prices are NOT controlled for, the Minsky-framed reading is on weak ground; the housing-and-finance-bundle reading is at least as good.

2. **Cross-country selection.** USA, UK, Ireland, Spain, Netherlands all had simultaneously: restrictive residential land-use planning, low interest rates 2002-2006 (Greenspan-Bernanke disinflation environment + ECB convergence), securitisation-and-rehypothecation banking infrastructure. Germany, Switzerland, Japan, Belgium had less of these and did not have a household-credit boom or banking crisis. The cross-sectional variation in household-credit growth is largely cross-sectional variation in the housing-and-finance-policy bundle, not in autonomous Minsky dynamics.

3. **Public-policy-encouraged credit (US).** US household-credit growth pre-2008 was materially policy-encouraged: GSE expansion targets, Community Reinvestment Act enforcement intensification 1995-2007, FHA-insured loans, and the homeownership target of 67.5% under the 2002 Bush administration. Treating the resulting credit growth as the outcome of an autonomous private-sector fragility cycle elides the policy-direction component.

4. **Laeven-Valencia coding controversy.** The "banking crisis" indicator is a discretionary classification — episodes are coded by analyst judgment about whether public intervention occurred. Some borderline cases (Switzerland 2008 UBS rescue, Belgium 2008 Fortis nationalisation) produce different crisis-onset rosters under alternative codings, and the predictive results are sensitive to which roster is used.

5. **Out-of-sample 2010-2024 record.** Several OECD countries (Canada, Australia, Sweden, Norway, South Korea) have run household credit-to-GDP higher than any 2007 pre-crisis level for over a decade without a banking crisis. The Minsky-framed reading needs to explain this. The mainstream answer is that macroprudential regulation, capital-and-liquidity ratios, and stress-test infrastructure since Basel III have rendered the same credit levels less crisis-prone. That answer is consistent with "credit growth predicts crisis CONDITIONAL on weak macroprudential", which is a substantially weakened version of the original Minsky claim.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report household-credit-change coefficient both unconditional and conditional on real-house-price change; treat the conditional coefficient as the binding test.
- Report the pre-2008 sample's predictive AUC vs the 2010-2024 sample's predictive AUC for the same model; if AUC has degraded, note that macroprudential reform has weakened the signal.
- Treat the housing-supply / monetary-policy / GSE-policy bundle as a confound the Minsky framing cannot fully separate from autonomous fragility; an honest result card states that "supported" here is consistent with both the Minsky reading and a policy-bundle reading.
- ROC-curve report against alternative crisis predictors (current-account deficit, real-house-price change alone) so readers see incremental predictive content.
