# Steelman — Nuclear phaseout is not the dominant driver of industrial exit

**The strongest arguments for nuclear phaseout and against the industry-exit chain:**

1. **The 2022-2023 gas-price shock dwarfs any phaseout-specific effect.** TTF gas prices rose from ~20 EUR/MWh in 2020 to peak ~340 EUR/MWh in August 2022. Industrial electricity prices across Europe — INCLUDING in countries without any nuclear phaseout (Italy, Spain, UK) — rose in the same window. Attributing BASF's Ludwigshafen adjustment, Dow's Europe restructuring, and aluminium-smelter closures to the nuclear phaseout specifically, rather than to the gas shock, is an identification reach. The synthetic-control approach is fragile in this window.

2. **Germany's nuclear share was already falling before Atomausstieg.** Nuclear provided ~30% of German electricity in 2000, ~23% in 2010; the 2011 Fukushima decision accelerated a trajectory rather than reversing a baseline. Counterfactual "no Atomausstieg" scenarios that assume continued nuclear share would have required LARGE new-build programmes which were not politically viable. The true counterfactual is a slower retirement schedule, not continued share.

3. **Wind and solar build-out has largely filled the generation gap.** German renewables share rose from ~17% in 2010 to ~50%+ in 2023. The intermittency and dispatchability concerns are real but overstated; advances in battery storage, demand-response, and grid-interconnection capacity have materially softened the operational challenges. SAIDI metrics in Germany remain among the best in Europe (10-12 minutes/year in 2023 vs UK ~30 minutes).

4. **Industry relocation is multi-causal.** US IRA subsidies (2022+), Chinese industrial policy, labour-cost differentials, and supply-chain localisation concerns all drive energy-intensive industry relocation. Single-factor attribution to nuclear phaseout misses a much larger policy competition among jurisdictions.

5. **Belgium's 2022 reversal is a political, not empirical, event.** The Belgian government's decision to extend Doel 4 and Tihange 3 was political, driven by geopolitical gas-supply concerns, not by measured failures of the phaseout schedule. It does not count as an empirical refutation of phaseout policy.

6. **The carbon-intensity transition-window rise is temporary.** Power-sector carbon intensity in Germany peaked in the 2012-2014 transition window as lignite filled the gap, then declined sharply as renewables expanded. A hypothesis that takes a snapshot of transition-window carbon intensity without windowing to 2030 misses the longer trajectory.

7. **Nuclear safety and waste are real externalities.** Fukushima, Three Mile Island, Chernobyl, and persistent high-level-waste-storage uncertainties are tail risks with low probabilities and large consequences. The precautionary case for phaseout rests on these tail risks, not on day-to-day cost-benefit. A hypothesis that omits safety externalities from the outcome set gives an incomplete welfare accounting.

8. **France's dependency on nuclear has its own vulnerabilities.** In 2022, French nuclear availability collapsed due to stress-corrosion cracking across multiple reactors; France was a net electricity importer for much of the year. High nuclear share is not robust; over-reliance on any single technology has costs.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report all results with and without the 2022-2023 gas-shock window; the contrast is the key identification check.
- Control explicitly for ETS prices and TTF gas prices; report the residual phaseout-specific electricity price gap.
- Report SAIDI in levels not just differences; Germany remains reliability-leader in absolute terms even if the trend is slightly adverse.
- Distinguish transition-window carbon intensity from steady-state; report 2030 trajectory projections separately.
- Report energy-intensive industry adjustment with and without US IRA effects; IRA may be doing substantial work post-2022.
- Flag safety-tail-risk omissions in the outcome set explicitly; do not headline the result as full welfare accounting.
