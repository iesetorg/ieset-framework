# Steelman — Real exchange-rate appreciation mean-reverts

Real exchange-rate mean reversion is a strong prior, but the empirical trap is timing. A 15 percent three-year appreciation may be an equilibrium Balassa-Samuelson shift, a terms-of-trade improvement, or a persistent safe-haven repricing rather than misalignment. In those cases a two-year reversal window is too short and a negative coefficient would understate true equilibrium persistence.

The BIS real broad EER index is high quality, but it is still an index. It does not identify whether adjustment occurs through nominal exchange rates, relative inflation, wage compression, or productivity growth. The test only asks whether large appreciations are followed by weaker REER growth, not why.

Country and year fixed effects leave major time-varying confounders: commodity cycles, euro adoption, currency pegs, capital controls, crisis programs, and monetary-policy regime changes. Those forces can dominate short horizon REER dynamics.

If supported, the prudent verdict is that a simple large-appreciation trigger has predictive mean-reversion content. If refuted, the likely reason is not that purchasing-power parity is false in the long run, but that this two-year panel horizon is too short or too contaminated by equilibrium shocks.
