# Steelman — NZ Rogernomics: institutional complements vs deregulation alone

The strongest version of this claim is the Hall-and-Soskice / North
institutional-complementarities argument: market-oriented reforms
raise growth only when supporting institutions — an independent
central bank disciplining inflation expectations, fiscal-rule
infrastructure binding political discretion — credibly anchor the
regime change.

New Zealand 1984-1988 is widely read as a textbook deregulation that
did NOT immediately deliver growth (the late-1980s "transformation
recession," with NZ unemployment doubling and per-capita GDP
underperforming most of the OECD). Only after the 1989 Reserve Bank
Act stabilised inflation expectations through the world's first
explicit inflation-targeting regime, and the 1994 Fiscal
Responsibility Act bound fiscal policy to transparency and balanced-
budget rules, did productivity catch-up resume. The institutional
reading: deregulation stripped away controls but left the price-
formation environment chaotic; the 1989/1994 institutional layer
gave private agents the credible policy commitments needed to act
on the new price signals.

A market-liberal counter-steelman is that the J-curve from any deep
liberalisation is mechanical (asset-reallocation costs and bankruptcy
losses hit before efficiency gains accrue), and the post-1994
acceleration is the delayed return on the original 1984 reforms —
not new institutional causality. On that reading, the RBA Act and
FRA are downstream consequences (or at most parallel implementations)
of the same liberalising government's reform agenda, not independent
causal additions. The 5-10 year lag between deregulation and
productivity payoff is a feature of every credibility-establishing
reform episode, not a sign that intervening institutional layers
were doing the lifting.

This hypothesis privileges the institutionalist reading. The
empirical test is whether the deregulation-only window (1984-1988)
already shows a growth premium over the donor pool — which would
refute the complements story, since the institutions had not yet
been built — or whether the premium emerges only in the full-stack
window (1994-2000), consistent with the institutionalist reading.
The test cannot, by construction, distinguish the institutionalist
story from the J-curve story if both windows show the predicted
pattern; falsification is asymmetric.
