# Steelman — Lula Bolsa Família poverty-reduction decomposition (Brazil, 2003-2010)

**The strongest arguments that the 2003-2010 distributional improvement should not be predominantly attributed to Bolsa Família and minimum-wage policy:**

1. **The 2003-2008 commodity boom was substantial and highly favourable to Brazil.** Iron ore, soybean, oil, and meat export prices all surged. Export volumes rose. The resulting fiscal revenue bump, trade-balance strength, and real-exchange-rate appreciation produced a tightening of labour markets and formal-employment expansion that would have generated distributional gains in any CCT-absent counterfactual. The Colombian, Peruvian, and Chilean peers that shared the boom also saw distributional improvements (Gini declines 2003-2010), suggesting a common regional-cycle channel.

2. **The PT industrial-policy legacy includes BNDES expansion and directed-credit channels that may have had distributional spillovers separately from BF.** Crediamigo, PRONAF rural credit, and BNDES social-development lending all delivered subsidised credit to bottom-tercile populations. These are PT-era interventions distinct from BF cash transfers; decomposition that attributes all policy-channel effects to "BF + minimum wage" risks under-counting other PT-era instruments.

3. **Formal-employment growth is partly an endogenous response to both policy and boom conditions.** The 2003-2010 period saw ~13 million formal-sector jobs created. The minimum-wage valorisation pulled up the bottom of the formal-wage distribution; but the creation of the formal jobs in the first place was responsive to growth (boom-driven) and to payroll-tax-credit policies (Simples Nacional expansion 2006). Decomposing the combined effect across policy and non-policy drivers is genuinely difficult.

4. **The decomposition is sensitive to the donor-pool specification.** MEX, COL, PER, CHL, ARG are each different: Argentina 2001-2003 crisis pulled poverty sharply up then down, biasing the comparator; Mexico's CCT (Progresa/Oportunidades) pre-dates 2003; Chile's Solidario launched 2002. A clean "no-BF counterfactual" is hard to construct because CCTs are widespread in the region.

5. **Panel FE with time trends can over-attribute to year-specific factors.** The year FE captures global commodity prices, US monetary policy, and regional cycle effects; the country FE captures time-invariant differences. The within-country-within-year residual variation is where BF scale and minimum-wage changes enter — but this residual variation may be small, making statistical power for Shapley attribution limited.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report the Shapley variance shares with bootstrap confidence intervals. If the CIs on the BF + min-wage share span 30-60%, the pre-registered 40% threshold is a sharp test.
- Show the Brazilian poverty-headcount trajectory against each donor pool member individually AND the pool-weighted synthetic. Transparency over which donors drive the comparison is important.
- Present the counterfactual explicitly: "if commodity prices had followed the pre-2003 trend path and BF had not scaled, what poverty trajectory would the data imply?" Include counterfactual uncertainty bands.
- Acknowledge that Progresa/Oportunidades in Mexico, Juntos in Peru, and Solidario in Chile are contemporaneous CCTs that partially confound the comparison. The BF scale effect is what differentiates; not the existence of CCT.
- Present the decomposition alongside the Soares et al. (2010) and Barros et al. (2010) IPEA-academic prior estimates. A decomposition that confirms them within 10 percentage points is a replication; a larger divergence is a publishable disagreement.
- Report both extreme-poverty headcount (sensitive to bottom-tail policy) and Gini (sensitive to middle-distribution dynamics) because BF and minimum-wage channels affect these differently.
