# Steelman — Spain Sánchez economic trajectory 2018-2023

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "mixed but aggregate-fine, housing-weak" framing:**

The framework's pre-registered claim is deliberately nuanced: Spain's headline output and employment trajectory under Sánchez is at or above the euro-periphery donor pool once common shocks are absorbed, but housing affordability and real wages net of housing are worse. Both sides of this claim have steelman objections.

**Objections from the "Spain's aggregate economy is doing well" direction:**

1. **The 2023 headline growth finding may be partly a post-pandemic catch-up artefact.** Spain's 2023 GDP growth (~2.5%) was the highest in the big-four EU economies, but Spain's pandemic trough was also the deepest (-11% in 2020 vs -4 to -5% for Germany, France, Italy). The strong 2023 reads partly as deferred recovery from a larger 2020 contraction. A 2019-2023 compound growth comparison tells a less flattering story than a 2022-2023 comparison; the framework's year-FE-absorbing-common-shocks specification is correct, but headline commentary that "Spain led EU growth in 2023" is selective and a stricter cumulative metric (e.g., 2019-2023 total real-GDP change per capita) should be the primary headline.

2. **NextGenerationEU disbursements materially boost the 2021-2023 GDP number.** Spain received among the largest NGEU Recovery and Resilience Facility allocations (€163B ceiling split grants + loans). Measured GDP during disbursement years mechanically benefits from the public investment component. The Sánchez-era policy content should not be credited with a growth effect that is mostly EU-financed; the causal attribution to Spanish-government policy is weaker than the raw headline suggests. Absent the NGEU money, the headline trajectory might not outperform peers.

3. **Employment-rate improvements partly reflect 2021 labour reform's reclassification of existing temporary workers into permanent contracts, not net new hiring.** Spain's 2021 labour reform (RDL 32/2021) was successful at reducing temporary-contract share, but part of the observed improvement is reclassification of existing workers rather than new job creation. Employment-rate gains also benefited from unusually strong tourism recovery and from a tight European labour market post-COVID. Attributing employment improvement specifically to Sánchez-era policy (as opposed to tourism tailwind, European labour-market tightness, and mechanical reclassification) overstates the causal role of the reform.

**Objections from the "Spain's trajectory is actually worse than the framework admits" direction:**

4. **Unemployment-level comparison hides the persistent structural gap.** Spanish headline unemployment 2023 is ~12% vs euro-area average ~6%. The framework's hypothesis focuses on the CHANGE in unemployment (comparable to peers) but the LEVEL remains the worst in the peer pool and has remained so through multiple decades under multiple governments. Reporting "Spain improved" against a peer pool where everyone improved obscures that Spain is still far from convergence.

5. **Structural fiscal deficits and public-debt-to-GDP are elevated relative to peers.** Spain entered COVID with structurally weaker public finances than Germany, Netherlands, Belgium; exited COVID with substantially higher debt/GDP. The framework's hypothesis does not test fiscal-sustainability outcomes, but a fuller assessment of "economic trajectory" should include them. Sánchez-era policy has sustained primary deficits beyond what cyclical conditions required; the debt trajectory is a legitimate adverse finding the framework sidesteps.

6. **The "housing is the weak spot" framing somewhat lets Sánchez-era policy off the hook.** Spain's 2023 housing law (Ley 12/2023) has been criticised for not materially expanding supply and for creating regional-level rent-control options that empirical rent-control literature suggests will further constrain supply. If the data shows Spain's housing-affordability trajectory is the worst in the pool, it is not purely a structural-tourism story; there are specific Sánchez-era housing-policy choices (rent-cap enablement, vivienda-vacacional tolerance in key markets, construction-permit friction) that the framework's movement-level coding captures but that the "housing is exogenous structural" narrative obscures.

7. **Real-wage-net-of-housing stagnation is a meaningful welfare finding even if aggregate output is fine.** The framework's pre-registered differentiated finding (aggregate output OK, housing weak) risks being interpreted politically as "so Sánchez is fine". But a median voter who works for wages and pays rent or a mortgage cares far more about real purchasing power net of housing than about headline GDP per capita. If the housing-netted real-wage trajectory is negative, that IS economic decline for the representative household, and labelling the aggregate fine can read as technocratic obfuscation.

**Objections on methodology:**

8. **Synthetic-control for Spain against a euro-periphery donor pool is fragile.** The pre-treatment match quality (2005-2017) will be sensitive to Greece (debt-crisis scarring), Portugal (austerity programme), and Italy (sovereign-debt-crisis repricing) all having idiosyncratic histories that make the donor-pool mean a poor counterfactual. Weights will concentrate on FRA, IRL (which is excluded baseline), DEU, NLD — giving Spain a counterfactual of core-Europe, which is not a fair match.

9. **The 2018 cutoff is a political date, not an economic one.** The June 2018 no-confidence vote was politically discontinuous but Spanish macroeconomic policy did not immediately discontinuity — PSOE in minority without budget-passage authority had limited capacity to enact its own programme until 2020. Dating the "Sánchez-era policy effect" from 2018 overweights the pre-implementation years; an alternative coding with a 2020-cutoff (PSOE-Podemos coalition formed) would isolate the actual policy-content period.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report 2019-2023 cumulative per-capita growth as the headline metric, not 2023 annual growth.
- Report NGEU-absorbing vs NGEU-removing specifications of the GDP outcome.
- Report level-gap unemployment alongside change-in-unemployment; honour that Spain still has the worst unemployment level in the pool.
- Report the housing-netted real-wage finding as a headline outcome alongside GDP per capita; it is the metric that captures household welfare.
- Report 2018-cutoff and 2020-cutoff specifications side by side.
- Explicitly attribute the housing-trajectory channel to both structural-supply factors (tourism, short-let demand) AND Sánchez-era policy content (Ley 12/2023) without picking one; the data does not separate them.
- Avoid framing the headline as "Sánchez-era Spain outperformed"; the honest framing is "aggregate output recovered strongly, but household purchasing power net of housing did not; both findings are real and neither invalidates the other."
