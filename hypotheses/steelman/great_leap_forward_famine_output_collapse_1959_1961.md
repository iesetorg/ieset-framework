# Steelman: Great Leap Forward Famine and Output Collapse 1959–1961

The most defensible challenge to the central-planning-as-cause argument focuses
on the specificity of the failure mechanism. The GLF famine was not a generic
result of socialist agriculture — the Soviet Union ran collective farms for
decades without comparable famine after 1933, and China's own collectives functioned
at subsistence level for years before the GLF. The specific catastrophe required
Maoist voluntarism: the uniquely irrational campaign to smelt steel in backyards,
the explicit persecution of accurate reporting, and Mao's personal suppression of
early famine warnings in 1959 (Lushan Conference, Peng Dehuai dismissal). This
is a dictator-specific failure, not a demonstration that central planning per se
causes famine.

The natural disaster argument, while disputed by Peng Xizhe's provincial
reconstruction, has not been definitively refuted. Walker and Manning (2014)
argue that 1959-1960 did feature below-average precipitation in key agricultural
regions and that the Peng methodology overstates weather normalcy. Soviet debt
repayment obligations and reduced Soviet technical assistance (Sino-Soviet split,
1960) materially constrained China's capacity to import food, a geopolitical
shock external to domestic institutions.

The export-during-famine fact — while damning — reflects grain export contracts
and diplomatic commitments, not necessarily the absence of any recognition of
crop failure. Excess mortality estimates also range threefold (15M to 55M), and
the upper estimates rest on demographic back-calculations with significant
modelling assumptions.
