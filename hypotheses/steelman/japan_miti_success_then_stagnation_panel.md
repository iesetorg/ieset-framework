# Steelman — Japan MITI Success Then Stagnation Panel

## Strongest version of the claim
Japan MITI-led industrial policy (1955-1989) successfully facilitated catch-up growth through coordinated investment, technology licensing, and export promotion. However, the post-1990 stagnation was not merely a monetary or demographic accident but was substantially caused by the persistence of protected sectors, weak domestic competition, and zombie lending that prevented resource reallocation from declining to growing sectors.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. A structural break in 1990 showing TFP and GDP growth slowdown, with pre-1990 growth positively associated with MITI-coordinated investment and post-1990 stagnation associated with rising protection and zombie lending.
2. Sectoral evidence that low-productivity protected sectors (construction, retail, agriculture) expanded while high-productivity tradable sectors stagnated after 1990.
3. Cross-country comparison showing that economies with similar demographics but stronger competition policy (e.g., Germany, Nordic countries) did not experience comparable stagnation.

## Best counterarguments
- **Demographics dominate:** Japan working-age population peaked in 1995 and fell sharply thereafter; the stagnation may be almost entirely a demographic story with policy playing a minor role.
- **Monetary policy failure:** The Bank of Japan was too slow to respond to deflation after 1995; a more aggressive monetary stance might have offset structural problems.
- **Asset bubble overhang:** The 1980s bubble created massive balance-sheet destruction that independently caused a lost decade; zombie lending was a symptom, not a cause.
- **MITI success is overstated:** Japan growth 1955-1989 may have been driven by catch-up convergence, high savings, and US security guarantees rather than by MITI industrial policy specifically.

## Boundary conditions
- Claim is about the *combination* of MITI success and post-1990 stagnation causes; either half can be true without the other.
- Zombie-lending measurement is contested; different methodologies give different sectoral distributions.
- The claim does not require that MITI policy caused the stagnation, only that the stagnation was amplified by protected sectors and weak competition.
