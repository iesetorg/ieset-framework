# Steelman — Olson rise-and-decline: distributional coalitions as growth drag

## The strongest opposing argument

Olson's "rise and decline" thesis is empirically over-claimed. The cross-section of long-stable democracies includes many of the world's highest-productivity economies (Switzerland, Sweden, Netherlands, Denmark, Germany), and the version of "distributional coalition density" that Olson predicted should chill growth — high union density, comprehensive collective bargaining, strong professional licensing — is in fact correlated with high TFP, low inequality, and high innovation rates in the Nordic-coordinated-market-economy bloc.

**Key social-democratic / coordinated-market-economy objections:**

1. **Nordic counter-example.** Sweden, Norway, Denmark, and Finland combine 70%+ union density, comprehensive sectoral bargaining, strong professional certification, and high tax-and-transfer welfare states. By Olson's prediction these should be productivity laggards. They are productivity leaders. The Hall-Soskice "varieties of capitalism" framework explains why: coordinated wage-bargaining produces wage compression that drives productivity-improving investment in low-skill labour, exactly the opposite of the rent-seeking-drag story.

2. **Union density measures the wrong thing.** Olson's "distributional coalitions" include lobbying associations, regulatory cartels, and professional licensing — not just unions. Modern professional licensing (US occupational licensing, German handwork certification) and corporate lobbying (US K Street, EU Brussels) deliver more rent-extraction than industrial unions, but the panel measures union density rather than these. The hypothesis tests one weak proxy for what Olson actually meant.

3. **TFP measurement bias.** Long-stable democracies have sophisticated service economies where TFP measurement (PWT rtfpna) is most subject to measurement error. The Bouis-Christensen-Klenow critique of cross-country TFP differentials applies most strongly to exactly the comparison the hypothesis runs.

4. **Reverse causality.** Countries that hit the productivity frontier and are at the convergence asymptote will mechanically show lower TFP growth than catch-up economies. The "long-stable democracies grow slower" finding is partly the convergence-saturation effect, not Olsonian institutional sclerosis. The control for initial GDP per capita absorbs some of this but not all.

## What the framework still captures

The question of whether long-stable polities accumulate rent-seeking coalitions that drag growth is among the most-discussed in comparative political economy. The pre-registered test is deliberately weak — looking only at quartile differences in TFP growth controlling for convergence — and the test will support Olson's hypothesis only if the gap is meaningful and the within-country panel coefficients also point that way. A "refuted" verdict here would constitute meaningful evidence against an influential market-liberal claim. A "supported" verdict still leaves the welfare interpretation contested between Olsonian sclerosis and Nordic-style welfare-productivity virtuous circle.
