# Steelman — Wage-led vs profit-led growth (OECD)

**The strongest argument against the Bhaduri-Marglin reading:**

The Bhaduri-Marglin demand-regime framework is theoretically elegant but empirically fragile. Country-level coefficient estimates of consumption, investment, and net-export responses to labour-share changes typically have very wide confidence intervals; the headline classifications in the Stockhammer-Onaran literature flip across specifications, lag structures, and sample windows. A market-liberal reader would argue the "demand regime" is more of an analytical taxonomy than an empirical structural feature.

Specific objections:

1. **Closure-rule sensitivity.** The Bhaduri-Marglin model's wage-led-vs-profit-led classification depends on the closure rule that pins down which variables adjust to clear which markets. Different closure rules — Kaleckian, Sraffian, Keynesian — produce different classifications for the same data. The empirical procedure of summing single-equation component coefficients implicitly assumes a Kaleckian closure that is not the only defensible choice.

2. **Identification weakness.** Labour share is endogenous to all three demand components: consumption rises -> investment responds -> labour share adjusts. Single-equation OLS coefficients on labour-share are not causal estimates of the demand response; they are partial correlations contaminated by simultaneity. Stockhammer-Onaran acknowledge this; the empirical literature has not converged on a credible identification strategy.

3. **Open-economy collapse.** In an open economy with substantial capital mobility, a wage-share rise that raises consumption simultaneously deteriorates the trade balance and may attract capital outflows, all of which act as drag on output. Bhaduri-Marglin formalise this as net exports being a profit-led component, but the empirical magnitude of the trade-balance-and-capital-flow channel typically dominates the consumption channel for small open economies, leaving most of them profit-led almost by construction. The interesting variation is largely cross-sectional in country size and openness, not in some deep structural demand regime.

4. **Coefficient magnitudes are tiny.** Even where wage-led classifications survive, the magnitude of the estimated demand response to a 1pp labour-share rise is typically 0.1-0.3 pp of GDP — economically modest. Treating this as a meaningful structural feature of national economies overstates the empirical signal.

5. **Stability across sub-periods.** Country classifications routinely flip between 1980-1999 and 2000-2020 sub-samples in published Stockhammer-Onaran results. If the "demand regime" is unstable across two-decade windows, calling it a structural feature is an over-claim. The current spec's stability falsification rule is the binding test, and the literature's track record on it is poor.

6. **Policy-irrelevance.** Even if a country is wage-led, the policy implication — that raising the wage share would raise demand — assumes the wage-share rise is exogenous to the demand response. In practice, wage-share rises are mostly endogenous to demand (tight labour markets raise wage shares), so a "wage-led demand regime" finding does not translate cleanly into a policy lever.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report country-specific coefficients with explicit confidence intervals; do not over-state classification certainty.
- Report sub-sample stability explicitly; flag countries whose classification flips.
- Report the magnitude of the average response, in pp of GDP per pp of labour-share change, so readers can see the economic-significance question.
- State explicitly that the test is descriptive ("does Bhaduri-Marglin classify OECD economies into a meaningful mix?") and not a policy-test ("does raising the wage share raise demand?"). The latter requires identification the spec does not have.
