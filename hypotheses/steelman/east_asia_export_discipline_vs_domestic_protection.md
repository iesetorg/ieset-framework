# Steelman — East Asian Export Discipline vs Domestic Protection

## Strongest version of the claim
East Asian economies that achieved sustained convergence (Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong) did so because export discipline subjected domestic firms to hard market tests. In contrast, economies that relied on protected domestic industrial policy without comparable export discipline (Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines) experienced weaker long-run productivity and plateaued at middle income. The critical difference was not state involvement per se but whether state support was conditional on export performance.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. A cross-country panel showing that export-discipline indices positively predict TFP growth while domestic-protection indices negatively predict TFP growth across East Asia.
2. Case evidence that Korea and Taiwan export-push periods (1960s-1980s) involved explicit performance standards tied to export targets, while Malaysia and Thailand protection lacked such conditionality.
3. Sectoral evidence that exporting sectors in East Asia achieved higher productivity growth than import-substituting sectors within the same countries.

## Best counterarguments
- **Bundle inseparability:** Export discipline and domestic protection were often part of the same policy package (e.g., Korea allowed tariffs on imports while subsidising exports); separating them may be analytically impossible with available data.
- **State capacity confound:** The countries with export discipline also had stronger bureaucracies and less corruption; the relevant dimension may be state capacity rather than export orientation.
- **Initial conditions:** Korea and Taiwan started with Japanese colonial industrial infrastructure and US aid; Malaysia and Thailand did not. The export-discipline result may reflect these inherited advantages.
- **Small-N problem:** There are very few East Asian success cases; the correlation may be spurious or driven by a single outlier.
- **China exception:** China achieved massive convergence with a mixed model that included both export discipline and heavy domestic protection; the hypothesis may not fit the largest East Asian case.

## Boundary conditions
- Claim applies to manufacturing-led catch-up; service-led or resource-led growth may follow different dynamics.
- The hypothesis is about the *average* effect across East Asia; individual countries may deviate.
- Post-2000 global value chains changed the nature of export discipline; the claim may be era-specific.
