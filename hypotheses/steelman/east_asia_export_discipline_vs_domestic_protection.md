# Steelman — East Asia Export Discipline vs. Domestic Protection

## Strongest version of the claim

East Asian high performers (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong) achieved superior long-run productivity growth because export-market discipline forced firms to meet global quality, cost, and innovation standards. Industrial policy worked where and only where it was conditional on export performance, sunset clauses, and competitive benchmarking. Conversely, economies or sectors that relied on protected domestic markets experienced weaker TFP growth, slower technology upgrading, and entrenched inefficiency. The active ingredient in East Asian success was not state targeting per se but the export discipline that subjected targeted firms to relentless market tests.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. Cross-country panel showing positive and significant export-orientation coefficients on TFP growth, and negative or insignificant domestic-protection coefficients, across East Asia 1960-2020.
2. Sectoral studies comparing export-exposed sectors with high productivity growth versus protected sectors with stagnant productivity.
3. Firm-level microdata showing higher productivity and low misallocation in exporters versus sheltered domestic-market firms.
4. Historical episode analysis linking tariff reductions and export-promotion phases to TFP accelerations, and protectionist reversals to slowdowns.

## Best counterarguments

1. Successful cases are cherry-picked. East Asian exporters had unique geopolitical alignments, cultural traits, and demographic windows; export discipline is one of many confounding factors.
2. Industrial policy created export capability. State-targeted infrastructure, skills, and technology diffusion enabled firms to export before they could have done so privately.
3. Export discipline is endogenous. Firms that already had high productivity self-selected into exports; the causal arrow may run from productivity to exports, not the reverse.
4. Protected sectors are second-best. Some domestic protection is necessary for learning-by-doing before exporting; abstract comparisons ignore optimal sequencing.
5. General equilibrium effects. Export discipline may raise productivity in exporters while draining resources from non-exported sectors.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for manufacturing and tradable services with clear export markets.
- Less defensible for resource-rich or geopolitically strategic sectors (oil, defence) where export discipline is not applicable.
- The claim is conditional: industrial policy plus export discipline can work; it does not deny industrial policy unqualified.
