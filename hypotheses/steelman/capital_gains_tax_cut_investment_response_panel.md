# Steelman: capital_gains_tax_cut_investment_response_panel

## Counter-argument

Capital-gains tax cuts primarily increase asset realisations (lock-in relief)
rather than real investment. Investors respond by selling appreciated assets,
producing a short-term revenue loss and windfall gains for existing asset
holders, without increasing capital formation. Real investment decisions are
driven by expected returns, demand conditions, and technological opportunity
rather than by capital-gains tax rates, which affect only a small share of
investment financing (most investment is debt-financed or retained-earnings
financed).

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Realisation vs real response**: The dominant empirical finding is that
   capital-gains tax cuts increase realisations (the lock-in effect) but have
   small or zero effects on real investment or entrepreneurship.

2. **Windfall effect**: Most capital-gains tax revenue accrues to the top
   1% of wealth holders. A rate cut is a regressive transfer that does not
   necessarily flow to productive investment.

3. **Retained earnings dominance**: Most corporate investment is financed by
   retained earnings or debt, not by equity issuance. Capital-gains taxes
   affect secondary-market transactions, not primary capital formation.

4. **Temporal shifting**: Investors may bunch realisations into low-rate
   windows, creating transitory revenue losses without any permanent change
   in investment behaviour.

## What would flip the verdict

- Evidence that investment responses are concentrated in years immediately
  following rate cuts and disappear thereafter (temporal shifting).
- A positive coefficient on capital-gains rate (i.e. higher taxes associated
  with more investment), consistent with public-investment complementarity.
- Instrumental-variable estimates showing no significant effect of rate
  changes on capital formation.
