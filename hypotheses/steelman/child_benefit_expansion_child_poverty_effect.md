# Steelman: Child benefit expansions and child poverty

The strongest skeptical reading is that the short-run poverty reduction is partly mechanical. The US Supplemental Poverty Measure counts refundable tax-credit resources directly, so the 2021 expanded Child Tax Credit should lower measured SPM poverty even if it has little durable effect on family earnings, housing security, or long-run child outcomes. The 2021 window also overlaps with pandemic recovery, stimulus payments, SNAP emergency allotments, and unusually fast labour-market tightening. A cleaner causal test would use eligibility thresholds, monthly administrative data, or household microdata to isolate the child-credit change from simultaneous policy and recovery effects.

The UK comparison is also only a sign check. The post-2013 child-benefit means-test tightening was not randomly assigned, and OECD child-poverty data are annual, sparse, and affected by labour-market and housing-cost changes. The result is still informative because official poverty series should visibly move if large child-transfer changes mattered in real time, but it should not be read as a full structural estimate of permanent child-benefit policy.
