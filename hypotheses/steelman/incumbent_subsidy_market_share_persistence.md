# Steelman — Incumbent Subsidies and Market-Share Persistence

## Strongest version of the claim

Subsidies directed at incumbent firms—state aid, bailouts, directed credit guarantees, and procurement preferences—distort market selection by protecting inefficient producers from competitive pressure. Over time, this protection raises market concentration as subsidised incumbents absorb market share without necessarily improving productivity, while innovative entrants are denied the revenue needed to scale. The result is persistent concentration and weaker total-factor-productivity growth. The claim is that a one-percentage-point increase in incumbent-subsidy share of GDP is associated with a measurable increase in top-firm concentration and a measurable reduction in TFP growth over the subsequent decade.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Sign consistency:** The panel-FE coefficient on incumbent subsidy is positive and significant for top-4 concentration change, and negative and significant for TFP growth.
2. **Magnitude:** A one-pp increase in subsidy/GDP is associated with at least a 0.25–0.5 percentage-point increase in top-4 concentration over 10 years and at least a 0.15pp reduction in annual TFP growth.
3. **Within-country identification:** The result is driven by within-country changes in subsidy intensity (e.g., EU state-aid waves, Korean chaebol support episodes, Chinese SOE subsidy expansions) rather than cross-country level differences.
4. **China exclusion robustness:** The result survives dropping China, the largest subsidy outlier, confirming it is not driven by one country's idiosyncratic political economy.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Developmentalist counter:** Amsden and Wade argue that subsidies to incumbents enable learning-by-doing, scale economies, and technology transfer that would not occur under pure market selection; the short-term concentration may be a necessary cost of long-term capability building.
2. **Strategic-industry argument:** Some incumbents operate in sectors with high fixed costs, network externalities, or national-security value where market-driven exit would be socially costly; subsidies may prevent under-provision.
3. **Measurement limitations:** State-aid data is best for EU members; outside the EU, incumbent subsidies are proxied by SOE subsidies, bailouts, and directed credit with substantial coverage gaps and classification error.
4. **Concentration may reflect efficiency:** If subsidies flow to the most productive firms, rising concentration may coincide with rising productivity; the sign pattern depends on subsidy allocation rules.
5. **Crisis exclusion bias:** Excluding systemic banking-crisis years removes episodes where subsidies are most defensible as emergency liquidity provision, potentially biasing the sample toward rent-seeking uses.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to **OECD and emerging-market economies** over 1995–2020. It is most relevant where subsidy data is disaggregated between incumbents and new entrants. It does not apply to **general tax incentives** available to all firms or to **start-up subsidies** that explicitly favour entry. The exclusion of crisis-response subsidies is important because crisis bailouts have different welfare implications than routine industrial policy.
