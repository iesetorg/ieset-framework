# Steelman — Immigration net fiscal contribution by origin, skill, duration

**The strongest arguments against the framework's channel-decomposition framing:**

The hypothesis operationalises migration fiscal effects as explainable by five measurable channels (origin institutional quality, skill, age, duration, legal status). This is the mainstream-economics framing, and it is more defensible than competing framings — but several serious objections remain.

1. **Dynamic congestion / externality effects on natives are excluded.** The per-migrant fiscal accounting framework counts direct taxes paid and services received; it typically does NOT count indirect congestion effects on housing costs, wait times for GP access, school capacity, or wage pressure on specific native sub-groups. A rigorous fiscal + welfare accounting would include these; most published studies don't, and therefore understate the total cost to natives in high-migration periods. Dustmann-Frattini acknowledge this limitation; the hypothesis as specified inherits it.

2. **Integration policy is a large moderator the hypothesis does not control.** The same migrant demographic can be net contributor in Canada (points-based, language-tested, integration-programmed) and net recipient in Sweden (high-acceptance asylum + limited labour-market integration in early years). Attributing outcomes to origin-country-institutional-quality and skill-at-arrival misses that destination-country integration policy is a first-order moderator. The hypothesis should be revised in v2 to interact origin × destination-integration-policy.

3. **Second-and-third-generation effects are not in the first-generation accounting.** Children of immigrants contribute education costs (negative near term) but tax contributions (positive long term). Long-run NPV accounting requires intergenerational projections with assumptions that are contested. Reasonable assumptions produce large ranges in aggregate fiscal estimates — the 50% reconciliation threshold in the falsification is set recognising this.

4. **Data access is highly uneven by country.** UK has HMRC-DWP-ONS linked access for research purposes. USA has no equivalent — NASEM 2017 is a one-off committee-assembled estimate, not replicable-on-demand. Nordic countries have the best administrative linkage; Italy, Spain, Greece have minimal linkage. The sample of 9 countries will effectively be more like 4-5 with usable data, and whether that subset is representative of OECD is itself a research question.

5. **The ethnicity-is-confound claim requires more than residual-magnitude testing.** Even if origin-country institutional quality dummies absorb most of the variance, there can still be genuine destination-country-specific reception effects (labour market discrimination, neighbourhood segregation, school quality gaps) that produce group-level outcome differences. These are not "ethnicity causing outcomes" in any biological sense; they are destination-country-policy-causing-outcomes. Framework needs to distinguish the two framings and admit that residuals can reflect destination-country policy failures rather than migrant-side causes.

6. **The skilled-migrant-uniformly-positive claim is less robust than the average suggests.** Skilled migrants from specific low-productivity labour markets (depending on recognition of credentials, language ability, sector specificity) can still be initial net recipients if credential recognition is slow. "Skilled" as a category masks meaningful heterogeneity in early-arrival productivity.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report the channel coefficients + origin-country residuals separately.
- Run a destination-country-integration-policy interaction as a pre-specified sensitivity.
- Disaggregate "skilled" into sub-categories with credential-recognition controls.
- Include housing congestion + service pressure as a secondary outcome panel (not just fiscal) to complete the welfare picture the steelman objection 1 raises.
- Explicit language in the interpretation: origin-country residual magnitude tells us how much is NOT explained by measured channels — and should be interpreted as potential destination-policy-reception effects, NOT as ethnicity-as-cause.
