# Steelman: Korea EITC Labour Supply Effect

The strongest counter is that Korea's EITC is too small relative to take-home income (peak credit roughly 2-3% of average wage versus 8-10% for US EITC at expansion peak) to generate detectable extensive-margin responses, and the apparent labour-supply effect is confounded by the 16% minimum-wage hike in 2018 and 11% in 2019 — both of which raise low-end labour income mechanically and shift the EITC eligibility region. KOSTAT panel data also have weak coverage of part-time-irregular employment where secondary-earner responses are largest, biasing measured ATT toward zero. Korean female-LFP is shaped primarily by childcare-availability and chaebol career-track persistence, neither of which the EITC expansion addresses. A defensible reading: EITC contributed marginally to documenting low-income employment relationships (administrative effect on registration) but the labour-supply-elasticity claim of 2-4pp ATT is at the upper bound of plausibility and likely confounds with concurrent minimum-wage shocks.
