# Steelman — Cost-push / distributional-conflict reading of Eurozone 2021-2024 inflation

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

Profit-margin "contribution" to deflator growth is an accounting decomposition, not a causal mechanism. Margins rise nominally when nominal demand outpaces marginal cost; in a high-demand environment with binding capacity, that is exactly what monetarist theory predicts. The PK framing reverses cause and effect: high margins are a symptom of demand-pull inflation, not its cause.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Decomposition is identity, not mechanism.** Y_deflator = (W·L + π·K + T) / Y, by construction. Whatever combination of W, π, and T moves with deflator growth, you can decompose into shares. The decomposition does not establish that profits *drove* inflation any more than it establishes that wages drove inflation when wage shares are high. The IMF and ECB papers cited in the spec are careful about this; the strong PK reading sometimes is not.

2. **The Eurozone is a price-taker on tradable energy and food.** The 2022 cost-push origin is uncontested across schools. The contested step is whether the propagation through to non-tradable services and core CPI is "distributional conflict" or "second-round effects" or "wage-price spiral pre-empted by anchored expectations." All four schools point at the same data; the labels differ.

3. **Real wages fell because the terms-of-trade shock was a real income loss.** When import prices rise more than productivity, somebody has to absorb the real loss — workers, firms, or government. Real wages fell because workers absorbed the largest share. This is a distributional outcome, but it is not by itself evidence that margins-with-market-power caused the inflation; it is consistent with any framework in which wages are stickier than prices in the short run.

4. **The 2024 disinflation pathway is contested.** The ECB story (rate-hike-induced demand cooling) and the PK story (terms-of-trade reversal) are partly observationally equivalent because both happened simultaneously. The ECB's own decomposition assigns roughly half the disinflation to terms-of-trade reversal and half to demand cooling; reading 50% as "terms-of-trade reversal driven" is at the edge of the ECB's own attribution.

5. **Cross-country variation.** Country-level inflation outcomes 2021-2024 correlate with energy-import intensity and with labour-market tightness independently. Germany (high energy exposure) and Spain (looser labour market) experienced different inflation paths. A clean test would isolate the margin channel from labour-market and energy-exposure channels — the spec attempts this through panel FE but the identification is partial.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The result card must:

(a) Report the IMF and ECB decompositions as primary; both produce profit-margin contribution estimates in the 35-45% range, which is meaningful but not dominant.

(b) Distinguish the descriptive decomposition (which the data supports) from the causal claim (which the data does not adjudicate).

(c) Report the real-wage path country-by-country; uniform real-wage declines 2021-2023 are a robust pattern.

(d) Note that the 2024 path is mixed: ECB tightening and terms-of-trade reversal both contributed, in roughly equal share per the ECB's own September 2024 staff projections.

(e) Frame as: "consistent with the PK distributional-conflict reading, not dispositive against the demand-pull reading." The schools' difference is partly framing, not just data.
