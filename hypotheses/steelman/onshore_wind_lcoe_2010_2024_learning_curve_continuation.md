# Steelman - onshore_wind_lcoe_2010_2024_learning_curve_continuation

1. The global onshore-wind LCOE series mixes very different sites. Cost declines can reflect better wind resources being commissioned in some years and worse sites in others, not just technology learning.

2. LCOE is sensitive to financing costs. The 2022-2024 interest-rate shock may raise project costs independently of turbine technology, while country-specific subsidy or auction rules can hide the true cost of capital.

3. Installed capacity is an imperfect learning driver. Turbine manufacturing experience, cumulative turbine output, supply-chain scale, blade size, hub height, and grid integration may be more direct measures than installed megawatts.

4. Global aggregation can mask policy failures. A falling world LCOE does not mean every country's wind programme got cheaper; permitting bottlenecks, grid constraints, and local-content rules can still raise delivered costs.

5. The test is associational. It shows whether the cost trajectory continued through shocks, not whether public subsidies, markets, or industrial policy caused the continuation.
