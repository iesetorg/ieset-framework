# Steelman — Post-Soviet market reform and life expectancy recovery

**The strongest arguments against the framework's market-reform-causes-health-recovery framing:**

1. **The Stuckler-King-McKee argument: mass privatisation CAUSED the mortality crisis, not slow-reform retention.** A widely-cited 2009 Lancet paper found that rapid mass privatisation in post-Soviet countries correlated with higher male working-age mortality. Their mechanism was alcohol consumption + job insecurity + collapse of social supports during shock. If this is right, the framework's "fast reformers = better health outcomes" story is backwards for some countries and time windows. The fast-reformer outcome advantage emerged LATER, after the initial shock absorbed; the immediate 1991-1995 period was worse in some fast-reformer cases (e.g. Russian mortality 1992-1994).

2. **EU accession conditionality is a massive confound.** The 8 "fast reformer" countries in the sample (POL, EST, CZE, HUN, SVN, SVK, LVA, LTU) all pursued EU membership and received substantial pre-accession technical assistance on health-sector institutions + budgetary support + cohesion funds. Their post-2004 life expectancy improvements coincide with EU accession. A "capitalism caused life expectancy improvements" framing obscures that EU-specific institutional capacity-building may be doing the work. The right counterfactual would separate countries that did market reform WITHOUT EU accession path — there are few such cases.

3. **Alcohol policy is a separate and large lever.** Russian male life expectancy crisis 1992-2003 is heavily driven by alcohol mortality. Russia's partial recovery post-2003 coincides with alcohol excise increases + restrictions on production, NOT with deepening market reform. A country-level analysis of "reform speed" can miss that alcohol-specific policy interventions are an independent variable explaining most of the within-Russia-over-time variance.

4. **Slow-reformer selection is contested.** Belarus is classified as slow-reformer, and has maintained better healthcare infrastructure + lower Gini coefficient + lower alcohol-related mortality than Russia, producing life expectancy comparable to some fast reformers in specific cohorts. "Slow reform = bad health outcomes" is not cleanly borne out by the Belarusian experience.

5. **Pre-1989 baselines were already diverging.** Czech Republic, Slovenia, and Hungary had better health metrics than Russia in the 1980s for reasons pre-dating the transition (income levels, health-sector institutional quality, diet patterns). Attributing post-transition divergence to transition policy may over-credit the reform period for effects that would have happened anyway given the pre-existing institutional quality gap.

6. **Aggregating fast/slow ignores within-group heterogeneity.** Poland's recovery trajectory differs substantially from Latvia's; Estonian recovery from Bulgarian. Russia differs from Ukraine. The "fast/slow" binary hides distributional features that matter for policy-actionable conclusions.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report the aggregate DiD coefficient AND the event-study per-year coefficients so the 1991-1995 dip for slow reformers is visible + so the timing of fast-reformer recovery is shown honestly.
- Include a 1989-1992 "pre-transition" window in the event study — if fast-reformer countries already had HIGHER life expectancy in 1989, the reform-speed attribution is weakened (Slovenia, Czech already had ~72-73 vs Russia ~69).
- Disaggregate by country within each group; report per-country deviation from counterfactual.
- Acknowledge alcohol-policy channel explicitly; a v2 hypothesis should include Russian excise-tax timing as a separate treatment variable.
- Avoid overclaiming the direction on the immediate 1991-1995 window; the data supports the longer-run divergence but is more contested on the short-run crisis.
