# Steelman — Policy uncertainty and private investment

## Strongest version of the claim

The most robust formulation is that policy uncertainty operates through an option-value-of-waiting channel: when the regulatory, fiscal, or institutional environment is unstable, irreversible investments become riskier because future policy may alter payoffs. This is particularly damaging after state-led reform shifts because reforms often bundle multiple policy changes with ambiguous implementation timelines. The pre-registered magnitudes — a 1.5 pp GDP drop in private investment and 0.2 pp drop in TFP growth per one-SD uncertainty increase — are derived from Baker-Bloom-Davis (2016) and Gulen-Ion (2016) estimates for developed economies.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Reform-episode event studies.** Argentina's 2001 corralito, India's 1991 liberalisation, Brazil's 1990 Collor plan, and Indonesia's post-1998 reforms all generated large uncertainty spikes. Private investment should show a identifiable dip in the 2-3 years surrounding these episodes, with recovery only after uncertainty resolves.

2. **Cross-country EPU correlation.** The Baker-Bloom-Davis EPU indices for ~30 countries should correlate negatively with business-investment shares in a panel FE framework, even after controlling for stock-market volatility (to separate policy uncertainty from general macro uncertainty).

3. **Heterogeneity by reform type.** The effect should be larger for fiscal/tax reforms than for trade reforms, because tax uncertainty directly affects after-tax returns while trade liberalisation is more predictable.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Endogenous uncertainty.** High uncertainty may reflect underlying economic distress rather than cause low investment. Reverse causality is a serious threat: crises drive both uncertainty and investment collapse.

2. **Reform success reduces long-run uncertainty.** State-led reforms that are well-implemented (e.g. Poland 1990s, Estonia 1990s) may raise short-run uncertainty but lower long-run uncertainty, producing a J-curve in investment. The hypothesis's 5-year forward window may miss this.

3. **Emerging-market data quality.** EPU indices are available for only ~30 countries. The composite index for remaining countries relies on lower-quality electoral-calendar and regulatory-turnover proxies, introducing measurement error that biases coefficients toward zero.

4. **Alternative uncertainty proxies.** Stock-market volatility and exchange-rate volatility often predict investment better than policy uncertainty in emerging markets. If the EPU coefficient is insignificant but VIX/volatility coefficients are significant, the policy-uncertainty channel is weaker than claimed.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest in democracies with regular elections and institutionalised policy review, where EPU indices are most valid. In autocracies (China, Gulf states), policy uncertainty may be lower but investment may be state-directed rather than private, changing the mechanism. The 1990-2020 window captures the post-Cold-Wave reform era; earlier periods had different uncertainty regimes.
