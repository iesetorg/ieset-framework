# Steelman — Czech and Slovak market transition comparison

## 1. Strongest version of the claim

The Czech Republic maintained stronger rule of law, lower corruption, and a more open regime to foreign direct investment after the 1993 dissolution. These institutional differences compounded over three decades into a persistent income and productivity advantage. Slovakia’s state-led automotive strategy (VW, Kia, Jaguar) was itself FDI-driven, confirming that market openness, not domestic industrial planning, was the operative channel.

## 2. Key evidence the claim would need

- A decomposition showing that the Czech–Slovak GDP-pc gap is statistically attributable to differences in market-institution quality and FDI inflows rather than government consumption or state-aid intensity.
- Robustness to controlling for pre-dissolution industrial structure (e.g., heavy-industry share in 1992).
- Evidence that Slovakia’s post-2000 reform catch-up narrowed the gap precisely where institutions and FDI converged.

## 3. Best counterarguments

1. **Pre-dissolution industrial structure.** The Czech lands inherited a more advanced and diversified manufacturing base from the Austro-Hungarian and Soviet eras. Slovakia’s economy was more agrarian and less industrialised in 1993.
2. **Geography.** The Czech Republic borders Germany and Austria; Slovakia is landlocked and borders only Austria, Czechia, Poland, Hungary, and Ukraine. Geographic market access is a powerful confound.
3. **Slovakia caught up.** After 2000, Slovakia implemented aggressive reforms (flat tax, labour-market liberalisation) and saw rapid FDI-driven growth. The Czech–Slovak gap narrowed in the 2000s, suggesting the initial divergence was temporary, not structural.
4. **Euro adoption timing.** Slovakia adopted the euro in 2009; the Czech Republic did not. The monetary regime difference affects growth dynamics and is not captured by the institutional indices used.
5. **Decomposition assumptions are strong.** Panel FE decomposition assumes additive, linear effects and treats government consumption as a proxy for state industrial strategy, which it is not. Industrial policy can operate through tax expenditures, regulatory exemptions, and off-budget vehicles that do not appear in consumption data.

## 4. Boundary conditions

The claim applies most cleanly to the immediate post-dissolution decade (1993–2003). After 2004, EU accession homogenised many policies, making the Czech–Slovak comparison less informative about institutional causes.

## 5. Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis bridges the transition-economy cluster (`poland_market_transition_30yr_growth`, `estonia_market_reform_30yr_income_convergence`) and the broader market-institutions literature. It is distinct because it explicitly pits market openness against state industrial strategy within a near-identical initial-conditions pair.
