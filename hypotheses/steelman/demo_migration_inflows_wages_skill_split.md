# Steelman: Migration inflows × wages (skill-split)

## Strongest case for the claim

The empirical migration literature (Card, Peri, Ottaviano on the positive side; Borjas on the
negative side) converges on a skill-split conclusion: high-skill inflows complement native
labour and raise productivity, while low-skill inflows produce small negative effects
concentrated in the bottom of the native earnings distribution. Cross-country panel evidence
should reproduce the within-country findings if external validity holds.

## Strongest case against

(1) Endogeneity: migrants concentrate in booming destinations, biasing OLS toward zero or
positive. (2) Bottom-decile wage data quality is weak in many OECD countries pre-2000.
(3) Skill classification (tertiary-educated as "high-skill") misses cross-country variation
in credential portability. (4) Natives may exit affected occupations rather than take wage
hits, displacing the impact channel from wages to employment.

## Key uncertainties

- Bartik-style instrument needed for clean identification (deferred to v2).
- Whether the skill-split effect generalises to non-Anglo destinations.

## What would change my mind

If the bottom-decile coefficient on low-skill migrant share is large negative (>2pp/decade)
in countries with strong wage-setting institutions, the "small negative effects" reading
fails.
