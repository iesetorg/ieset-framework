# Steelman — Fed QT 2022-2025 market response asymmetry

## The strongest opposing argument

The "QT is half-size of QE" prediction is plausible theoretically but probably untestable on the 2022-2025 sample, and the event-study design will produce a null or noise-dominated result that the falsification rule cannot distinguish from genuine asymmetry.

**Identification problems:**

1. **QT announcements were highly anticipated.** Unlike QE1 in 2008 (genuinely surprising) or QE3 in 2012 (open-ended novelty), QT 2022 was telegraphed for months — Powell discussed it in early 2022 testimony, the May FOMC released a detailed plan, June implementation was already priced in. Event-study windows on heavily-pre-announced policy capture residual surprise, not the full effect. The cumulative term-premium change being modest (or near-zero) on event days could be entirely consistent with large total-effect QT priced in over months.

2. **Concurrent rate-hike contamination.** Every QT event coincided with active 75bp+ FOMC rate-hike cycle. Decomposing term-premium movements into rate-expectation vs term-premium components on these dates requires structural assumptions; the ACM model itself is a moving target with model-revision biases.

3. **Regional-bank stress 2023-Q1 and BTFP.** SVB/Signature collapse forced Fed to inject reserves *while QT continued nominally*; net liquidity oscillated. The 2023-Q1 exclusion window addresses this but the contamination continued through BTFP wind-down in 2024.

4. **Treasury issuance shock 2023.** TGA rebuild after debt-ceiling resolution flooded the market with $1T+ bills in 2023-Q3-Q4, swamping any QT signal in the term-premium series. The control for issuance-pace is necessary but insufficient — the 2023-2024 issuance regime change was structurally different.

**Theoretical objections to the asymmetry prediction:**

1. **Symmetry is the more natural prior.** Vayanos-Vila preferred-habitat models predict roughly symmetric portfolio-rebalancing effects in QE and QT — the asymmetry literature (D'Amico et al, Smith-Valcarcel) leans on stress-period vs normal-period differences rather than QE-vs-QT direction. A finding that QT effects are ~equal to QE per dollar would falsify the hypothesis but might be the actual answer.

2. **Stock-not-flow channels imply small QT effects from any cause.** If long-run-stock effects dominate and the unwind is gradual relative to peak, small per-dollar QT effects are over-determined — they don't specifically support the asymmetry mechanism over the gradualism mechanism.

## What the framework still captures

Even granting these problems, the hypothesis pre-registers a falsifiable test: per-dollar QT effects materially smaller than QE-era estimates of ~25bp/$T. A clean refutation in the symmetric direction (per-dollar effects equal to QE) would be a major prior update. The expected result — a noisy estimate in the SUPPORTED band consistent with multiple readings — is itself informative because it implies QT is not a major macro lever, which has policy-relevance for future cycles. The mortgage-spread leg adds a second outcome dimension where measurement is cleaner (mortgage rates respond to liquidity-channel components QT directly affects). The contamination concerns are real but fall within the falsification rule's PARTIAL band rather than producing false signals.
