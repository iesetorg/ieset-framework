# Steelman — South Africa load-shedding manufacturing decline 2007-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Premature deindustrialisation hits all middle-income economies.** Brazil's manufacturing share fell from ~16% to ~11% over 2000-2024 without an electricity crisis. Mexico, Turkey, Indonesia all show similar trajectories. The China-trade-shock and global-value-chain effects are powerful enough to explain much of South Africa's manufacturing decline. Attributing the South African case primarily to load-shedding overcredits an electricity-supply story when the larger force is global trade restructuring.

2. **Mining is the binding constraint on electricity demand, not manufacturing.** Eskom's pricing structure historically subsidised energy-intensive mining (gold, ferrochrome, aluminium smelting) at the expense of household and SME users. As mining contracted (gold sector decline 2000-2020), electricity demand structure shifted. The "manufacturing hurt by power cuts" story understates the mining-sector dynamics.

3. **Capital flight and policy uncertainty have larger effects than electricity.** South African private fixed-investment-to-GDP fell sharply post-2008 due to political uncertainty (Zuma era state capture), labour-relations volatility (Marikana 2012), and rand depreciation regimes. Investment collapse drives manufacturing decline more directly than electricity availability does.

4. **The post-2021 IPP liberalisation is a counter-treatment that the spec partially ignores.** REIPPPP rounds 5/6, embedded-generation regulatory relaxation, and corporate self-generation built rapidly 2021-2024. Loadshedding hours peaked in 2023 then fell sharply in 2024-2025 as private supply came online. The treatment intensity is non-monotonic; the spec's continuous-intensity coding handles this but result interpretation must.

5. **The "manufacturing share" outcome may be mismeasured.** Service-sector reclassification (e.g., back-office operations, business services) shifted activity from "manufacturing" categorisation in some periods. Cross-country comparison is fraught when sectoral categorisation differs.

6. **The COVID confound is severe and South-Africa-specific.** South Africa's lockdown was among the strictest globally; manufacturing suffered disproportionately in 2020. The post-COVID recovery was asymmetric across donor-pool countries. The 2020 outlier needs careful exclusion not just from fitting but from cumulative-loss measurement.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report manufacturing-share decomposition by sub-sector (energy-intensive vs other).
- Report the trade-shock-from-China channel explicitly.
- Acknowledge the post-2021 IPP supply-side recovery in result interpretation.
- Frame load-shedding as one driver among several, not the sole driver.
