# Steelman — US long-run Phillips curve vertical (Friedman natural rate)

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is the Blanchard-Summers (1986) hysteresis tradition: long-run unemployment in the US is not exogenous and natural-rate-determined; it is partly the cumulative consequence of demand history. When the Fed allows unemployment to remain elevated for several years (1981-1986, 2009-2014), labour-force attachment, skills, and matching efficiency degrade, and the natural rate itself drifts upward; conversely, the high-pressure labour market of 2017-2019 and 2022-2024 brought participation rates back faster than any natural-rate model predicted. If hysteresis is real even partially, the long-run Phillips curve has a non-zero slope at usable horizons (5-10 years) and the decade-mean unemployment coefficient should be significantly negative. The hypothesis is also vulnerable to a Stiglitz-Akerlof low-inflation tradeoff argument: at very low rates of inflation, downward nominal wage rigidity means a permanently low inflation target produces permanently higher unemployment, generating a measurable long-run tradeoff in the US 1990-2019 sample. The decade-mean spec has a separate problem: with only 7-8 non-overlapping observations, statistical power on the slope is so weak that "p > 0.10" is consistent with substantial true tradeoffs being present and undetected — the spec requires effect-size cutoffs to handle this, but the cutoffs (|coef| < 0.3) are themselves chosen rather than identified. From the post-Keynesian side the entire framing assumes that money growth is exogenous, when in fact M2 growth is a residual of nominal demand, so the spec's headline finding (decade-mean money growth predicts decade-mean inflation, decade-mean unemployment does not) is mechanical: M2 growth equals nominal-GDP growth divided by velocity, so regressing inflation on M2 growth is regressing inflation on nominal-GDP growth, which is regressing inflation on itself plus real growth. The hypothesis would be cleaner with an instrument for M2 growth (Romer-Romer monetary shocks, narrative IV) so the decade-level money-growth coefficient is identified off something other than nominal-demand accounting. As specified, even a clean "supported" verdict on Spec B leaves the causal interpretation contested.
