# Steelman — Latin America inflation-targeting staggered DiD 1999-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis claims IT adoption reduced inflation without a growth cost. The opposing view is that the global disinflation environment 1999-2020 was the dominant driver, and IT adoption is a passenger on a global trend.

Specific objections:

1. **Global disinflation environment.** Advanced-economy inflation fell substantially 1999-2020 from a combination of China supply-shock disinflation, monetary-policy convergence, and globalisation. LATAM inflation declines may reflect this background rather than IT adoption.

2. **Adoption timing is endogenous to inflation success.** Countries adopt IT only after their central banks are credible enough to commit to a numerical target; the "treatment effect" is partly the credibility that preceded adoption.

3. **Argentina is a counter-example absent from the panel.** Argentina has never adopted formal IT and has had multiple inflation crises; conversely Venezuela has had hyperinflation under various nominal regimes. Sample selection materially affects the IT-effect estimate.

4. **2021-2024 inflation surge tests the regime.** Most LATAM IT central banks responded to the 2021-2022 inflation surge with sharp tightening, but inflation rose substantially before falling. The IT regime did not prevent the inflation episode; it managed the response. The strong-form claim ("IT prevents inflation") is unsupported.

5. **Output cost is hard to measure.** Counterfactual estimation of the output cost of disinflation is sensitive to method; the Callaway-Sant'Anna ATT may not capture distributional or sectoral output costs.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report adoption-timing endogeneity bound.
(b) Show 2021-2024 inflation surge evidence to qualify the strong-form claim.
(c) Acknowledge ARG/VEN exclusion as sample-selection issue.
(d) Treat output-cost falsification cautiously.
