# Steelman — Unilateral tariff liberalisation and 20-year growth

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest defensible version is that sustained, autonomous tariff reductions improve allocative efficiency, expand input variety, and raise competitive pressure, producing measurable cumulative gains in labour productivity and household welfare over a 20-year horizon. The claim is not that every tariff cut helps every country, but that the average treated liberaliser outperforms its credible counterfactual, and that the effect is separable from FTA-driven liberalisation (which bundles institutional and market-access changes) and from crisis-driven IMF conditionality.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Synthetic-control trajectories**: Clear positive gaps for treated liberalisers versus synthetic counterfactuals, with pre-trend RMSPEs showing good fit and post-treatment gaps persisting beyond 10 years.
2. **Cross-estimator agreement**: Callaway-Sant'Anna staggered DiD and local projections should produce the same sign and comparable magnitude.
3. **Welfare translation**: The consumption-per-capita result should track the GDP-per-worker result; if growth is driven by capital deepening without welfare gains, the policy case is weaker.
4. **Non-commodity robustness**: The result should survive dropping major commodity exporters, confirming that the mechanism is trade-policy rather than terms-of-trade windfall.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Endogeneity of reform**: Countries liberalise after crises or during IMF programs. The pre-reform dip and post-reform recovery may be mean reversion, not policy effect. Synthetic control can match pre-trends but cannot match unobserved reform motivation.
2. **Missing complementary reforms**: Successful liberalisers often paired tariff cuts with macro stabilisation, privatisation, or labour-market reforms. The synthetic control may miss this bundle, attributing bundled effects to trade policy alone.
3. **Enclave growth without structural change**: Liberalisation may raise GDP per worker in export enclaves (mining, cash crops, maquiladoras) without broad productivity spillovers. The consumption-per-capita check is designed to catch this.
4. **Argentina 1990s and Mexico post-NAFTA**: Both undertook major unilateral or quasi-unilateral liberalisation and saw disappointing 20-year productivity growth relative to East Asian peers that maintained more interventionist trade regimes.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for middle-income economies with basic state capacity and diversified production. It is weakest for: (a) least-developed countries where tariff revenue is a large share of government financing and liberalisation creates fiscal crisis; (b) economies with pre-existing severe balance-of-payments constraints where rapid import growth triggers currency crises; (c) countries where protection was sheltering genuinely learning-by-doing infant industries.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis directly engages `infant_industry_protection_capability_effect` and `trade_lib_india_1991_tariff_cut_export_response`. It is a broader test of the Washington-Consensus trade pillar. A split result — unilateral liberalisation raises GDP but not consumption, or works only when bundled with institutional reform — would be the most informative outcome and would shift the framework's priors toward "complementarity" rather than "trade policy alone."
