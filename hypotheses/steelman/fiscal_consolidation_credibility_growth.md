# Steelman — Credible fiscal consolidation predicts stronger long-run investment and income growth than repeated discretionary stimulus in mature economies

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that credible fiscal consolidation — consolidation that markets and households believe will be sustained — reduces sovereign risk premia, raises business confidence, and frees up fiscal space for future counter-cyclical policy. In mature economies with high initial debt, repeated stimulus without consolidation erodes credibility, raises risk premia, and creates expectations of future taxation that depress private investment. The claim is not that all consolidation is expansionary, but that credible consolidation outperforms stimulus-reliance in the long run for economies where debt sustainability is a binding constraint.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Synthetic-control gaps**: Clear positive gaps for GDP growth and private investment in credible consolidation episodes versus synthetic counterfactuals, with good pre-trend fit.
2. **Credibility mechanism**: Sovereign bond yield spreads should decline and business confidence should rise in credible consolidators but not in non-credible or stimulus-reliant economies.
3. **Dynamic local projections**: A positive impulse response of investment to consolidation initiation, with the effect building over 3-5 years as credibility is established.
4. **Ex-Eurozone-austerity robustness**: The result should survive excluding the 2010-2015 European episodes, confirming a general pattern rather than a pre-GFC or non-Eurozone story.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Eurozone austerity falsification**: The 2010-2015 consolidation in Greece, Italy, Spain, and Portugal was followed by deep recessions, not expansion. Proponents argue this consolidation was not credible (markets feared default), but opponents argue that consolidation itself is contractionary when monetary policy cannot offset it. The hypothesis must engage this episode directly.
2. **Endogenous timing**: Governments consolidate when growth is already recovering or when markets force them to. The post-consolidation growth may reflect mean reversion or pre-existing momentum rather than policy effect.
3. **Stimulus definition**: "Repeated discretionary stimulus" is hard to define and code. Automatic stabilisers during recessions are not discretionary, but the cyclical-adjustment method may misclassify them. The comparison group may be contaminated by crisis episodes.
4. **Japan's stimulus persistence**: Japan has run persistent fiscal stimulus for three decades without a debt crisis or collapse in investment. If stimulus-reliance is supposed to produce worse outcomes, Japan is a prominent counter-example (though its growth has been mediocre).

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to mature OECD economies with high initial debt (>60% of GDP) and independent monetary policy (or at least a monetary union with credible central banking). It does not apply to: (a) economies in deep recession where monetary policy is at the zero lower bound and fiscal multipliers are elevated; (b) emerging markets with low initial debt where fiscal space is not constrained; (c) currency-union members without fiscal transfers where consolidation may trigger balance-of-payments crises.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a medium-run counterpart to `debt_overhang_private_investment_30yr` and a direct engagement with `eurozone_austerity_distributional_incidence` and `fiscal_multipliers_zlb_higher_than_normal_regime`. It tests the "expansionary austerity" claim in its most defensible form (credibility channel, not Keynesian contraction). A null result would support the Keynesian view that consolidation is always contractionary; a positive result would support the credibility view under specific conditions.
