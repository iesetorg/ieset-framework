# Steelman — Australia Hawke-Keating reform long run

## 1. Strongest version of the claim

Hawke-Keating reforms ended Australia’s protectionist “fortress” model, modernised the financial system, and created a more flexible labour market. These changes allowed Australia to ride the China commodity boom without succumbing to Dutch disease, because resources were reallocated efficiently across sectors rather than being locked into declining industries by tariff walls and state direction.

## 2. Key evidence the claim would need

- A positive and significant panel-FE or synthetic-control gap for Australia post-1983 on GDP-pc growth, labour productivity, and trade openness.
- Evidence that tariff reduction schedules correlate with sectoral productivity gains.
- Robustness to controlling for China demand (commodity prices, terms of trade) and to excluding the post-2003 China boom period.

## 3. Best counterarguments

1. **The China boom is the dominant driver.** Chinese industrialisation and infrastructure investment created unprecedented demand for Australian iron ore, coal, and LNG between 2000 and 2012. Without this exogenous demand shock, the reform-era growth path would look far less impressive.
2. **The state never left.** Australia maintained large public research institutions (CSIRO), state-owned enterprises (initially Qantas, CBA, Telstra), and sectoral support programs (mining R&D tax concessions, automotive subsidies until 2017). The “market vs state” framing oversimplifies a mixed economy.
3. **Housing and migration drove aggregate demand.** Post-reform financial deregulation fuelled a sustained housing boom and high immigration, both of which boosted aggregate GDP. These demand-side drivers are distinct from the supply-side efficiency gains the hypothesis claims.
4. **New Zealand enacted similar reforms and grew more slowly.** If the same reform package produced weaker results in a nearby, culturally similar economy, Australia-specific factors (minerals, scale, geography) likely dominate the policy content.
5. **Manufacturing employment collapsed.** Tariff cuts and competition policy contributed to a sharp decline in manufacturing share and employment, with persistent regional costs in Victoria and South Australia that aggregate GDP masks.

## 4. Boundary conditions

The claim is most relevant for commodity exporters to a rapidly industrialising neighbour. External validity is limited because the China boom was a historically unique terms-of-trade shock.

## 5. Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis mirrors `new_zealand_reform_long_run_productivity_recheck` but with an opposite prior: it expects market liberalisation to explain Australia’s strong performance, whereas the New Zealand hypothesis is skeptical of strong aggregate effects. The two form a natural antipode pair within the framework.
