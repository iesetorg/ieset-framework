# Steelman: Australia Hawke-Keating Reform Long Run

## Strongest version of the claim
The Hawke-Keating reforms transformed Australia from a protected, financially repressed, commodity-dependent economy into an open, diversified, service-exporting economy with world-leading pension assets. Tariff cuts forced manufacturing to modernise; financial deregulation improved capital allocation; the floating exchange rate absorbed terms-of-trade shocks; and compulsory superannuation built a domestic savings and investment pool. The resulting 30-year expansion (1991-2023 without recession) is better explained by these market-liberalising reforms than by state direction or resource rents alone.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. GDP and productivity data showing Australia outperforming comparable commodity exporters (South Africa, Brazil, Chile) and converging toward Canadian/Norwegian levels.
2. Export-composition data demonstrating diversification into services, education, and high-value agriculture.
3. Microeconomic evidence of improved firm dynamism, entry rates, and allocative efficiency post-reform.
4. Superannuation-fund growth data linking pension reform to domestic capital-market depth.

## Best counterarguments
- Australia's 30-year recession-free record was primarily driven by Chinese commodity demand and terms-of-trade boom (2003-2011), not domestic reform.
- The reforms coincided with a global trend toward financialisation that inflated asset prices (housing) while hollowing out manufacturing; measured GDP growth masked distributional harm.
- New Zealand enacted similar reforms (Rogernomics) but performed worse, suggesting Australian success was driven by geology and geography, not policy.
- Compulsory superannuation imposed a regressive labour-tax burden and channelled savings into fee-heavy retail funds with mediocre returns.
- Post-2010 productivity growth stagnated, suggesting reform dividends were exhausted by 2000.

## Boundary conditions
Applies to commodity-rich, English-speaking democracies with Westminster systems and strong initial institutions. Generalises poorly to manufacturing-specialised or institutionally fragile economies.
