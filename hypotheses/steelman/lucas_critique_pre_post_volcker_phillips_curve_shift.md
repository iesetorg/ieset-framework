# Steelman — Lucas critique on pre/post-Volcker Phillips curve

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is that the Phillips-curve flattening between the pre-Volcker and post-Volcker regimes is a slow-moving institutional phenomenon rather than the discrete expectations-reanchoring discontinuity that the Lucas critique predicts. Three pieces of evidence undermine the discrete-break reading. First, the slope flattening continues into the 2010s and is most pronounced in the 2010-2019 sub-sample, which is 30+ years after the Volcker regime change — if expectations re-anchoring did the work, the flattening should have been complete within a decade and stable thereafter, not still progressing into the Bernanke-Yellen-Powell era. Second, the cross-country pattern is similar in regimes that did not have a discrete Volcker-style credibility event (Eurozone post-1999, Japan post-1990s); a Lucas-critique reading needs each country's flattening to map to its own monetary-regime change, but the data look more like a global secular pattern tied to globalisation, declining wage-bargaining institutions, and demographic shifts in labour-supply elasticity. Third, the wage-Phillips curve (which is closer to the structural object Lucas had in mind) shows a different time pattern from the price-Phillips curve, with wage-Phillips actually steepening in the 2017-2019 high-pressure labour market period and 2022-2024 post-pandemic period, which is incompatible with a stable post-1985 reduced-form regime. From the institutional / post-Keynesian side, the relevant variation is in wage-setting institutions: the share of US workers under multi-year COLA-indexed contracts collapsed from 60% in 1979 to under 10% by 1990, and the union density decline plus globalisation-induced reduction in wage bargaining power explains the lagged-inflation coefficient decline directly without reference to expectations or central-bank credibility. The hypothesis's pre-registered Bai-Perron test in 1979-1984 is a natural test of the Lucas reading, but if the break detected is gradual rather than discrete, or if the post-1985 sample fails its own no-break test (i.e. parameters drift further within the post-Volcker regime), the data are more consistent with the institutional reading than the Lucas reading. The hypothesis would be sharper if it required the structural break to be discrete (single break, not multiple breaks within the post-1985 sub-sample), and if it included a wage-Phillips robustness check that the institutional reading would predict to look different from the price-Phillips finding. As specified, the test admits a "supported" verdict that is consistent with both the Lucas reading and the institutional reading, which is the kind of underdetermination the falsification rule should close off.
