# Steelman — Israel high-tech economy 2000-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Soviet/Russian-Jewish immigration is the load-bearing pre-condition.** The 1989-1995 absorption added ~1m highly-educated migrants (engineers, mathematicians, physicists) to a base population of ~5m. This is the largest single human-capital injection per capita in OECD history. Israeli high-tech growth 2000-2024 builds on this base; without it, the 2000-treatment-date framing collapses. The immigration-attribution test is essential, and the residual policy-content effect may be small.

2. **Defence-sector subsidy and US military aid distort the "private innovation" story.** US military aid (~$3bn/yr) underwrites the IDF's R&D spending; Unit 8200, Talpiot, and Mamram personnel pipeline produces commercially-deployable expertise without normal labour-market cost. The "venture-capital plus innovation policy" framing under-credits the publicly-funded defence-talent subsidy.

3. **GDP-per-hour outperformance is partly compositional.** Israel has a high-tech sector with very high productivity AND large low-productivity sectors (construction, retail, ultra-Orthodox employment, Arab-Israeli employment). The compositional effect — small high-productivity sector pulling up the average — masks substantial within-sector disparities.

4. **Israel's regional security environment is a confound.** Persistent military tensions, periodic wars (2006 Lebanon, 2008-9 Gaza, 2014 Gaza, 2023+ Gaza/Lebanon/Iran), and conscription costs reduce Israeli economic potential. The model has performed well DESPITE this, but a clean OECD-peer comparison should adjust for security-environment differences (which the spec does not).

5. **Productivity stagnation in non-tech sectors is a known problem.** Israeli construction, retail, and services productivity has lagged OECD; CBS/MoF reports have repeatedly documented this. The aggregate productivity outperformance comes from one sector.

6. **Post-October-7 (2023) reservist mobilisation and tech-worker disruption is recent and material.** ~360k reservists were mobilised 2023-2024; high-tech workforce diversion; foreign-investor and credit-rating responses. The 2024 data may reflect these disruptions; cumulative-2024 measurements should disclose this confound.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Run the immigration-attribution check prominently.
- Report sectoral productivity disaggregation (high-tech vs other).
- Acknowledge defence-sector publicly-subsidised talent pipeline.
- Note 2024 data sensitivity to October-7 war effects; flag for v1.1 revisit.
- Frame as "Start-Up Nation built on extraordinary 1989-95 immigration shock + sustained policy + defence-tech-talent pipeline" rather than as policy-alone success.
