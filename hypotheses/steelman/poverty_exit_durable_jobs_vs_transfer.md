# Steelman — Durable Poverty Exit via Jobs vs Transfers

## Strongest version of the claim

Cash transfers alleviate poverty in the short run but do not produce durable exit because they do not build productive capacity, human capital, or social networks. Private formal-sector job creation, by contrast, generates sustained income streams, on-the-job training, and integration into market institutions that keep households above the poverty line even after external support ends. Over 15-25 year windows, countries and regions with faster formal-job growth show higher rates of sustained poverty exit than those relying primarily on transfer expansion.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Panel data** on poverty dynamics showing that formal-job creation predicts 5-year sustained exit rates better than transfer coverage or transfer generosity.
2. **Micro-panel data** (LSMS, household surveys with panel structure) tracking individual households and showing that job-acquisition events predict sustained exit while transfer receipt alone does not.
3. **Natural experiments**: conditional cash-transfer programmes with and without job-training components, or regions with differential manufacturing-job growth.
4. **Robustness**: the result should hold when controlling for initial poverty depth, human capital, and agricultural productivity.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Transfers are complements, not substitutes**. Bolsa Familia, Progresa, and similar programmes improve child health and education, raising future employability; separating transfer from job effects is artificial.
2. **Job quality matters**. Many formal jobs in developing countries are precarious, low-wage, and insufficient to lift households durably out of poverty.
3. **Selection bias**. Households that find jobs may be more capable or motivated than those that rely on transfers; the correlation may reflect heterogeneity rather than causation.
4. **Macro context**. Job creation requires aggregate demand and investment; in stagnant economies, transfers may be the only available poverty tool.
5. **Measurement of durability**. Five years is an arbitrary threshold; households may cycle in and out of poverty regardless of income source.

## Boundary conditions

- Most relevant for **low- and lower-middle-income countries** where extreme poverty is prevalent and labour markets are expanding.
- Less relevant in **high-income welfare states** where transfers are generous and long-term, and poverty is less about income than about multidimensional deprivation.
- Formal-job definition varies across countries; comparability is a concern.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to labour-market, welfare-architecture, and growth hypotheses. Tensions with claims that social-protection floors and universal basic income are sufficient for poverty reduction.
