# Steelman — Ramsey-Mirrlees deadweight loss and high top marginal rates

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is that the Diamond-Saez-Piketty (2011-2014) optimal-top-rate framework used by progressive readers reaches a 70-83% revenue-maximising top rate by combining three robust empirical findings: ETI estimates concentrated near 0.25 (Saez-Slemrod-Giertz's central estimates after stripping out tax-base-shifting and evasion responses, which are policy-malleable), Pareto inequality parameters around 1.5 in modern OECD top-income distributions, and welfare weights on top earners near zero under standard utilitarianism. The hypothesis's threshold (c) — that the revenue-maximising rate must lie below 70% in every major OECD country — is therefore a direct bet against the Diamond-Saez-Piketty published conclusion, and the empirical evidence on which they rely is, broadly, the same evidence the hypothesis cites. The disagreement is less about ETI magnitude and more about which ETI to use: Saez-Slemrod-Giertz argue that real labour-supply responses (the welfare-relevant elasticity for Ramsey-Mirrlees) are small, on the order of 0.12-0.20, and that the larger total ETI estimates are inflated by tax-avoidance responses (income shifting between corporate and personal forms, deferral, charitable deductions) that can be policed by base-broadening rather than rate-cutting. If the welfare-relevant ETI is 0.15 rather than 0.40, the optimal top rate is in the 75-85% range, and the hypothesis fails its (c) threshold. The hypothesis's framing also obscures the Goolsbee finding that the 1993 US top-rate increase produced large short-run ETI estimates that decayed to near zero over 5+ years — implying short-run responses are dominated by timing and reporting effects, not by the long-run real-margin elasticities Ramsey-Mirrlees needs. The second-strongest objection is that the convex deadweight-loss assertion is not actually well-identified in the OECD panel: there are very few country-years above 60% top marginal rates after 1990 (mostly Sweden 1980-1989, Denmark 1980-1989, and high-income France post-2012), and the convex shape is identified largely off these few episodes rather than off a continuous distribution. From the heterodox / Piketty side, the entire Ramsey-Mirrlees framework misses that very high top rates have a distinct social-norm enforcement function (suppressing rent-extraction, compressing top managerial pay, reducing capital concentration over time) that is not captured by ETI at all and that may dominate the welfare calculation even if the static deadweight-loss term is non-trivial. The hypothesis would be sharper if it required the convex shape to hold in a continuous specification (not just in a discrete bin comparison) and if it included a Goolsbee-style decomposition of short-run vs long-run ETI; as specified, the test is vulnerable to a "supported" verdict that is consistent with the Diamond-Saez-Piketty framework if the welfare-relevant ETI is well below 0.4.
