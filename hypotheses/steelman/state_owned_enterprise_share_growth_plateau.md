# Steelman: State-Owned Enterprise Share Growth Plateau

## Strongest version of the claim
Once a country has achieved basic infrastructure and literacy thresholds, further increases in SOE share of output or employment tend to reduce productivity growth because state-owned firms face softer budget constraints, weaker competitive pressure, and politically constrained management incentives. The negative association is not about ownership per se but about the absence of market discipline; well-governed SOEs (Singapore, Norway) can perform adequately, but these are outliers. In the broad cross-country panel, higher SOE shares predict slower convergence to frontier productivity levels.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Cross-country panel showing negative SOE-productivity growth association after controlling for infrastructure, literacy, and initial income.
2. Firm-level data showing lower TFP, higher subsidies, and slower restructuring in SOEs versus private firms within the same country and sector.
3. Privatisation event studies showing productivity gains post-privatisation (Megginson-Netter meta-analysis).
4. Sectoral comparisons of SOE-dominated versus competitive sectors (telecom, airlines, banking).

## Best counterarguments
- Singapore (Temasek), Norway (Equinor), and France (EDF) demonstrate that well-governed SOEs can match or exceed private-sector productivity; the issue is governance, not ownership.
- SOEs may pursue social objectives (universal service, employment stability, strategic autonomy) that reduce measured productivity but increase welfare.
- In developing economies, SOEs fill coordination gaps that private markets cannot; the negative association may reverse at low income levels.
- Privatisation studies suffer from selection bias (governments privatise promising firms, liquidate hopeless ones) and post-privatisation regulatory capture.
- Natural-resource SOEs are mechanically large but not necessarily inefficient; excluding them biases the sample.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest in middle-income and advanced economies with functioning private capital markets. Weak in low-income or fragile states where SOEs are the only providers of essential infrastructure.
