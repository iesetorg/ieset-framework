# Steelman — Ecuador Correa default 2008

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis assumes the default carried a measurable reputational cost. The opposing view is that the default was rational, the haircut substantial, and Ecuador's continued market access from 2014 onward demonstrates the long-run cost is overstated.

Specific objections:

1. **The haircut was substantial.** Ecuador bought back the defaulted bonds at ~35 cents on the dollar in mid-2009, achieving an effective $2 billion fiscal saving in nominal terms. On a present-value basis the gain is meaningful for a small economy.

2. **Market access restored quickly.** Ecuador re-entered international debt markets in 2014 with a 10-year bond at 7.95% — only modestly above LATAM peers given oil-price weakness in late 2014. The "permanent reputational cost" frame is empirically weak.

3. **Refinancing in Chinese commercial loans.** The post-2008 funding gap was filled by Chinese-policy-bank loans; whether these were costlier or cheaper than Eurobonds depends on accounting (non-financial conditionality vs interest rate). The standard borrowing-cost metric understates the substitution.

4. **Counterfactual debt servicing was unsustainable.** Ecuador's external-debt-service ratio in 2008 was historically high; a non-default counterfactual likely involves IMF programme conditionality the political coalition would have rejected.

5. **2008 default was politically necessary for the post-Correa fiscal frame.** Without the default, the 2007-2014 oil-funded social-investment programme might not have been politically possible; the policy regime is endogenous to the default decision.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report buyback haircut net of subsequent interest-cost penalty.
(b) Show Chinese policy-loan substitution explicitly.
(c) Note the political economy of the alternative (IMF conditionality) in commentary.
(d) Treat the borrowing-cost penalty as bounded, not infinite.
