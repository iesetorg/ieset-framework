# Steelman: Industrial Policy Without Exit Discipline Failure

## Strongest version of the claim
Industrial policy succeeds only when accompanied by hard performance conditions—sunset clauses, export targets, productivity benchmarks, and credible threat of subsidy withdrawal. Without these "exit discipline" mechanisms, industrial policy degenerates into political patronage, protecting inefficient incumbents, creating zombie firms, and depressing economy-wide productivity. The comparison is not "industrial policy versus free markets" but "industrial policy with discipline versus industrial policy without discipline." East Asian success stories (Korea, Taiwan) featured aggressive performance monitoring, while Latin American and African failures featured open-ended protection without benchmarks.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Comparative case studies or panel data showing better long-run outcomes for disciplined versus undisciplined industrial-policy episodes.
2. Firm-level data showing productivity convergence toward targets in disciplined programmes and stagnation in undisciplined ones.
3. Political-economy evidence linking absence of sunset clauses to lobbying intensity and incumbent capture.
4. Event studies of subsidy withdrawal showing either firm improvement (disciplined cases) or collapse (undisciplined cases).

## Best counterarguments
- Exit discipline is easy to announce but hard to enforce; even East Asian states often extended support to failing champions (Daewoo, Hynix).
- Performance conditions may be gamed through accounting manipulation, political interference in benchmarking, or regulatory forbearance.
- Some sectors (semiconductors, vaccines, green energy) require decades of support before reaching competitiveness; premature exit destroys socially valuable capacity.
- The correlation between exit discipline and outcomes may reflect underlying state capacity, not policy design; weak states cannot enforce conditions even when they exist on paper.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest in middle-income economies with moderate state capacity. Weak in low-capacity states where conditionality is unenforceable and in frontier sectors where development timelines exceed political horizons.
