# Steelman — Post-COVID inflation episode supply vs demand decomposition

## The strongest opposing argument

The "60/40 supply/demand" Solomonic split papers over deep methodological disagreement about how to identify supply shocks at all in a post-COVID economy where supply and demand were jointly determined by the pandemic and policy response.

**Pure-monetarist case (Summers, Blanchard early-2021, Hanke):**
- The 2021 American Rescue Plan delivered ~$1.9T fiscal stimulus on top of an economy already recovering, with output gap closing in 2021-Q3. M2 grew 25%+ 2020-2021, the largest peacetime monetary expansion ever. The inflation was *predicted* by Summers in 2021 from demand alone; calling it 60% supply-driven retroactively absorbs forecast error into the supply category.
- "Supply chain disruption" is itself a function of demand — durables consumption rose 30% above trend in 2021, overwhelming production capacity. The supply-chain "shock" was demand-pull on inelastic short-run supply.
- Energy-price spikes 2022 happened *globally*, but US inflation peaked higher and earlier than peers — implying US-specific demand factors.

**Pure-supply-shock case (Bivens, Weber, MMT-adjacent):**
- Bernanke-Blanchard's own decomposition assigns 80%+ of 2021-2022 inflation to product-market shocks (energy + supply chain + reopening); demand shocks contribute <15% by 2022-Q2.
- The fact that US, EU, UK, Japan, Canada all had simultaneous inflation peaks despite very different fiscal stances refutes the demand-driven framing: only common shocks (energy + supply chain) operated everywhere.
- The post-2022 disinflation tracked supply-chain normalisation (GSCPI) almost perfectly, with negligible monetary credit needed — confirms the supply-driven attribution symmetric.

**Methodology objections:**

1. **Bernanke-Blanchard NK identification is structural-prior-laden.** Any decomposition into supply/demand shocks requires a model; the model bakes in views about Phillips-curve slope, expectations formation, and price-stickiness that are themselves contested.
2. **The 60/40 SUPPORTED band is wide enough to be a soft target.** Both an "80/20 supply" and a "55/45 supply" finding fall in support — the test isn't sharp on the central debate of how much demand mattered.
3. **Heterogeneity within OECD is the real story.** The US-EU divergence in inflation peaks (US 9%, EU 10%, but US peaked earlier and core ran higher) is itself diagnostic — testing the cross-country median averages over the most informative variation.

## What the framework still captures

The hypothesis correctly pre-registers *both* extreme readings as refutation cases — pure-monetarist (supply-share <30%) and pure-supply-shock (>85%) — which means the falsification rule is sharp at the tails even if the SUPPORTED band is wide. The cross-country panel design is the right framework for this question: anyone defending one extreme has to explain why the panel-median tells a moderate story. The US-specific demand-share lower-bound (>=40%) tightens the test on the most contested case (was ARP inflationary). A clean refutation in either direction would be a substantial prior update across the macroeconomics profession.
