# Steelman — US household debt sustains demand, 1990-2008

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The post-Keynesian framing treats the pre-2008 US pattern of rising household
debt against stagnant real median wages as a near-axiomatic refutation of
representative-agent rational-expectations models — wages stagnated, debt
substituted, demand held up, then debt collapsed. The strongest counter-argument
is twofold: (a) the descriptive co-movement is real but its interpretation as
evidence against rational-expectations models is model-dependent — modern
heterogeneous-agent New Keynesian (HANK) models, which are still
rational-expectations frameworks, can absorb the pattern by combining
intertemporal substitution, collateralised borrowing against rising house
prices, and idiosyncratic income risk; and (b) "real median household income"
is the wrong wage measure for a consumption-decision frame because it (i) omits
benefits and employer-paid healthcare which were the fastest-growing component
of compensation 1990-2007, (ii) uses CPI rather than the PCE deflator (CPI
overstates inflation by ~0.4pp/yr per the Boskin-Lebergott literature), and
(iii) measures households not workers, so demographic shifts (smaller
households, more single-person households) mechanically depress the median
even if individual real compensation rose.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **HANK models accommodate the pattern.** Kaplan-Moll-Violante (2018) and
   the broader HANK literature explicitly model wealthy-hand-to-mouth
   households whose consumption tracks current income while their balance
   sheets carry significant illiquid wealth (housing). The debt expansion of
   1990-2008 maps cleanly to a collateral channel in HANK without requiring
   any departure from rational expectations. Calling the pattern "inconsistent
   with rational-expectations models" requires specifying WHICH model — the
   Lucas-Sargent representative-agent benchmark cannot match the cross-section,
   but no serious macro modeller has used that benchmark for consumption since
   the late 1990s.

2. **Real-median-household-income is the wrong measure.** The CPI-vs-PCE-deflator
   wedge (~0.4pp/yr) compounds to about 7pp over 1990-2007. Total compensation
   (BLS ECI) including employer-paid healthcare grew faster than wages alone
   for production and nonsupervisory workers. Real per-capita compensation —
   the measure that maps to a consumption decision — grew materially faster
   than real median household income. The wage-stagnation premise is partly an
   artefact of the chosen series.

3. **Wealth, not just debt, sustained consumption.** Real US household net
   worth rose from about $25T in 1990 to about $65T at the 2007 peak, driven
   by both housing and equity gains. A standard life-cycle / permanent-income
   model with wealth effects predicts the consumption-debt comovement without
   any departure from rational expectations or any need for a "debt-substitutes-
   for-wages" mechanism. The Mian-Sufi cross-section identifies the marginal
   propensity to consume out of housing wealth (large, ~5-7 cents on the
   dollar) but does not establish that wage stagnation was the *cause* of
   the debt expansion — falling real interest rates, mortgage product
   innovation, and the GSE-driven expansion of credit access can all
   explain the debt boom independent of wage trends.

4. **Causality runs the other way: debt expansion enabled house-price growth,
   which enabled consumption.** The Mian-Sufi mechanism is a wealth-effect
   channel through housing collateral, not a "debt-substitutes-for-wages"
   channel. The consumption response is to perceived wealth gains, not to
   wage-growth shortfalls. A worker whose real wages were rising at +2%/yr
   in 1990-2007 (as production-nonsupervisory series suggest after correcting
   for the CPI bias) would still have borrowed against rising home equity.

5. **The 2008 crisis does not retrospectively validate the wage-stagnation
   reading.** That household debt was unsustainable does not establish that
   wage stagnation was its proximate cause. Loose monetary policy (the
   Greenspan put), Basel-2 capital arbitrage, originate-to-distribute
   securitisation, and GSE leverage all operated independent of the
   wage-distribution debate.

The descriptive co-movement (wages flat, debt up, consumption up) is robust
and the test in this hypothesis correctly identifies it. The deeper claim
that this is "inconsistent with representative-agent rational-expectations
models" is true of the 1980s benchmark but not of any modern incarnation,
and the deeper claim that wage stagnation *caused* the debt expansion is
not identified by the descriptive test.
