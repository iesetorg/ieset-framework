# Steelman — wid_top_wealth_concentration_rise_post_1980

**The strongest case against grading this SUPPORTED**, written before the run.

## 1. The capitalisation method may manufacture the trend
The headline WID/Saez-Zucman top-wealth shares are not directly observed —
they are *imputed* by capitalising income-tax flows (dividends, interest,
capital gains) into wealth stocks. Smith, Zidar & Zwick (2021) argue this
over-weights fixed-income assets held by the old-rich and inflates the
measured top share precisely in the period when interest rates fell. If the
apex "rise" is partly an artifact of the imputation assumptions, a positive
year-trend coefficient is measuring the method, not the world. The avoidance-
robustness the spec advertises cuts both ways: imputation fills the offshore
gap with *assumptions*, not data.

## 2. "Advanced economies" hides enormous heterogeneity
The cross-country panel pools the US (a steep, well-documented climb) with
countries where the top share was flat or *fell* (the spec's own data shows
the UK roughly flat). A pooled positive trend with country FE can be driven
by a handful of large risers — chiefly the US and China-adjacent cases — while
the modal advanced economy shows no significant apex drift. A significant
pooled coefficient is therefore consistent with the claim being false for
most countries in the sample. A within-country breakdown could refute the
"across advanced economies" framing even if the pooled coefficient is positive.

## 3. A linear trend is the wrong functional form
Apex wealth shares are U-shaped over the long run and, post-1980, look more
like a level-shift around the 2000s (tech/finance booms, asset-price era) than
a steady linear climb. Forcing a linear trend through a series that actually
steps up at a break point will report a significant slope that misdescribes
the dynamics — and would also report a significant slope on noise if the true
process is a near-random-walk in shares (spurious-trend / unit-root risk that
country-clustered SEs do not fully tame).

## 4. End-point and coverage sensitivity
WID interpolates sparsely-observed country-years; the 1980 base and the
2024 terminal values lean on imputation at both ends. Trimming the
interpolated tails, or ending the panel in 2019 to drop the COVID asset-price
spike, could move the coefficient materially. The verdict should be read as
conditional on WID's interpolation choices, not as a model-free fact.

## Why the spec author still expects SUPPORTED
None of the above disputes the *direction* for the apex US case, which is
large and replicated across independent methods (estate-multiplier, SCF,
Forbes-based). The steelman bites on **scope** ("across advanced economies")
and **mechanism/functional form**, not on the existence of a US apex rise.
A robustness follow-on (per-country trends; break-point vs linear; 2019
end-date) is the right response to a SUPPORTED verdict here, not celebration.
