# Steelman: privatisation_transition_tfp_panel

## Counter-argument

Mass privatisation in transition economies often produced insider-controlled
firms with weak corporate governance, asset stripping, and minimal restructuring.
The rapid transfer of assets frequently bypassed the institutional preconditions
for effective markets (rule of law, competition policy, financial supervision),
producing output collapses rather than productivity gains. Gradual reformers
(China, Belarus, Uzbekistan) often experienced smaller initial output declines
and faster recovery, suggesting that speed of ownership change matters less than
institutional quality and state capacity.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Insider control**: Voucher privatisation in Russia and Czech Republic
   concentrated ownership in insiders (managers, workers) who lacked capital
   and restructuring incentives, producing asset stripping rather than
   productivity gains.

2. **Output collapse**: Rapid privatisation was associated with large output
   falls in the early 1990s (the "transformational recession"). TFP growth
   may recover from a low base without representing genuine efficiency gains.

3. **Institutional void**: Mass privatisation preceded the development of
   courts, securities regulators, and competition authorities needed to make
   markets work. Without these institutions, private ownership did not
   translate into market discipline.

4. **China counterexample**: China retained state ownership while introducing
   market incentives (TVEs, SOE contract responsibility) and achieved the
   fastest sustained growth in history, suggesting that ownership form is
   less important than competitive pressure and hard budget constraints.

## What would flip the verdict

- A null or negative coefficient on mass privatisation after controlling for
  EU candidacy and rule-of-law quality.
- Evidence that outsider-privatised firms show TFP gains while insider-
  privatised firms show TFP losses, with the aggregate effect near zero.
- Synthetic-control estimates showing that gradual reformers (China, Belarus)
   outperformed mass privatisers on TFP growth over 1990-2015.
