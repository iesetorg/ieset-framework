# Steelman — Latin America commodity-cycle dependence 1990-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis treats commodity-cycle as the dominant driver. The opposing view is that domestic policy choices (fiscal rules, central-bank credibility, exchange-rate regime) are the primary differentiator and that commodity-cycle is a secondary modifier.

Specific objections:

1. **Chile's fiscal rule decoupled it from the cycle.** The structural fiscal rule (2001) sterilised much of the supercycle revenue and produced counter-cyclical buffers. The "commodity dependence" frame overstates exposure for institutionally credible economies.

2. **Mexico's manufacturing integration with US dominates its cycle.** Mexico's export base is dominated by manufactured exports to the US; commodity-cycle is a small modifier. Including Mexico in commodity-cycle panel obscures this.

3. **Brazil's domestic political-economy cycles are larger than commodity cycles.** The 2014-2016 contraction in Brazil reflects Lava Jato + Dilma impeachment political crisis, not just commodity reversal.

4. **Sub-panel construction is fragile.** Classifying countries as "commodity-heavy" vs "manufacturing-heavy" is a judgement call; different classifications produce different results.

5. **2014-2019 post-supercycle pain was uneven.** Argentina's 2018 crisis was domestic-policy-driven; Venezuela's collapse is non-commodity Chavismo; the cross-section in this window is dominated by idiosyncratic political-economy events.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report Chile as a fiscal-rule outlier in the panel.
(b) Show Mexico-out-of-sample robustness.
(c) Note 2014-2019 idiosyncratic-event composition.
(d) Acknowledge sub-panel-construction sensitivity.
