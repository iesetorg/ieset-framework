# Steelman: Mortgage-Rate Shocks and Housing Starts

The strongest version of the claim is that residential construction is one of the cleanest high-frequency channels of monetary transmission. A large mortgage-rate increase raises user cost, weakens affordability, and should reduce new starts before broader labour-market effects show up.

The strongest counterargument is that mortgage rates often rise when expected income growth is strong, so starts can initially remain firm. Supply constraints, demographics, and zoning can also dominate rates in particular cycles. The test therefore looks for large, discrete rate increases and requires a material starts decline within a fixed forward window.
