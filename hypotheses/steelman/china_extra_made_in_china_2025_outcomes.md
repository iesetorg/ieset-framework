# Steelman — Made in China 2025 outcomes

## The strongest opposing argument

1. **Frontier wins are masked by aggregate share.** EV penetration (BYD overtaking Tesla globally), battery manufacturing (CATL, ~37% world market), solar PV (>80% world supply chain), high-speed rail, and drones (DJI) all dominate global frontiers — but they are small slices of total manufacturing value-added. The aggregate "manufacturing share of GDP" misses the qualitative shift to frontier sectors.

2. **External constraints distort the test.** US export controls (CHIPS Act, semiconductor equipment restrictions 2022+) impose a binding constraint that no industrial policy can break in the test window. MIC2025 may be vindicated by 2030+ even if 2024 results look mixed.

3. **R&D intensity is partly mechanical.** China's R&D / GDP rose from 2.05% (2014) to 2.41% (2021) — large absolute, but partly driven by GDP denominator slowing rather than R&D acceleration. The peer-panel-differential test partly absorbs this but is not perfect.

## What the framework still captures

The two-test structure is the steelman: capability INPUT (R&D) AND share-break OUTPUT. If both move, the policy succeeds beyond expectations. If only input moves, the policy is at most "buying capability." If neither moves, the policy is a misallocation. The pre-registration locks the framing so post-hoc cherrypicking of frontier-sector wins doesn't drive the verdict. Frontier-sector hypotheses (semiconductor, EV, battery) are handled separately.
