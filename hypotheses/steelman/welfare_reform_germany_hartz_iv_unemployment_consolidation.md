# Steelman: Germany Hartz IV Long-Term Unemployment Effect

The strongest counter is that Germany's post-2005 long-term-unemployment decline is primarily a product of three concurrent forces that Hartz IV did not cause: (1) the 2003-2008 export boom riding the China-driven commodity supercycle, which lifted German manufacturing capacity utilisation and pulled long-term unemployed back into work via vacancy growth rather than activation pressure; (2) Kurzarbeit short-time-work expansion through GFC, which preserved employment relationships and kept long-term unemployment counts below counterfactual during 2009-2010; (3) demographic ageing — the cohort flowing through age 55-64 in 2005-2012 was smaller than the cohort that exited, mechanically reducing measured long-term unemployment. The synthetic-control donor pool (FRA, BEL, NLD) did not benefit equally from the Chinese export channel, biasing the gap in DEU's favour for non-Hartz-related reasons. Krebs-Scheffel's structural model assumes utility losses to displaced workers are first-order, which Bruckmeier-Wiemers (2018) contests. A defensible reading: Hartz IV plausibly raised job-search intensity at the margin, but attributing 30% of the long-term-unemployment fall to activation rules requires shutting down the export-cycle and demographic channels, which the synthetic-control design does only partially.
