# Steelman — Petro reform package economic trajectory (Colombia, 2022-present)

**The strongest arguments that Petro-era economic outcomes should not be coded as a policy failure:**

1. **Only ~2 years of observable data at time of drafting and ~4 years at the v1 run endpoint.** Major reforms (pension, labour, health) were legislatively or judicially blocked or substantially amended. The treatment intensity measured at the run date is far smaller than the treatment intensity implied by the administration's programmatic agenda. Assessment of Petro-era economic outcomes is assessment of "Petro policies that passed and were implemented," which is a much thinner treatment than "Petro's stated programme."

2. **Global macro context 2022-2024 is unusual and non-Petro-specific.** Post-COVID inflation peaks and real-rate tightening hit every LatAm peer; Colombia's 2023 growth slowdown (0.7%) and 2024 recovery (~1.7%) are within the LatAm peer range. The donor pool's own 2023-2024 path is not a clean "what-would-have-been-without-Petro" baseline — it reflects shared global conditions.

3. **FDI did not collapse as critics predicted.** Oil-and-gas exploration moratorium posture was widely predicted to produce investment flight; measured FDI remained broadly in line with LatAm peers through 2024. A clean falsification of the "policy-content costly" reading requires the FDI gap to actually materialise, not to be predicted ex-ante.

4. **Tax reform 2022 delivered ~1.3% of GDP in revenue without the growth collapse predicted by its critics.** The ex-ante case against Ley 2277/2022 was that it would suppress investment and activity; the ex-post record shows revenue materialised and activity softened modestly but broadly in line with the regional cycle. Attribution of the softening to the tax reform rather than to monetary tightening requires the synthetic-control identification to isolate the policy channel.

5. **Peace policy ("paz total") is not an economic-policy variable in the narrow sense but shapes the medium-term rural-productivity trajectory.** Rising homicide rates in some regions are a cost; but the programme's medium-term effects on rural demobilisation and territorial state presence cannot be assessed on a 4-year horizon. Assessing Petro's institutional-quality effects too early risks type-I errors on the costs and type-II errors on the benefits.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Distinguish enacted reforms from proposed-but-blocked reforms in the treatment-exposure construction. The Petro run's policy content is substantially smaller than the programmatic agenda.
- Report the donor-pool weights transparently. If PER, CHL, or MEX dominate the synthetic, the finding is narrower than "Petro vs LatAm peers" in aggregate.
- Present inflation, unemployment, and poverty trajectories alongside GDP and FDI. The aggregate growth slowdown might coexist with distributional improvements (minimum-wage rises, expanded transfer coverage) that the GDP metric does not capture.
- Control explicitly for the regional monetary-tightening cycle. A coefficient estimated without this control over-attributes regional effects to Petro.
- Commit to the symmetric-assessment classification: supported-negative, supported-null, supported-positive, or not-informative. Publishable in all four cases.
- Acknowledge the judicial-constraint dimension: Constitutional Court intervention on pension reform is an institutional-quality signal (functioning checks) that cuts against a narrative of executive-branch overreach, even if the governance tension is real.
