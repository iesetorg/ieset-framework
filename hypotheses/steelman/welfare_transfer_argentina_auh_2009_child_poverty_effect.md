# Steelman: Argentina AUH 2009 Child Poverty Effect

The strongest counter is that Argentina's 2009-2015 child-poverty trajectory is dominated by inflationary distortion and statistical-agency manipulation. INDEC under the Kirchner administration systematically understated CPI from 2007 onwards, biasing real-income and poverty-headcount measurement; even the CEPAL/IDB harmonised series rely on inputs that are partially INDEC-derived. The 2003-2008 commodity boom was already pulling Argentine child poverty down through formal-employment growth and minimum-wage indexation; the 2009 AUH coincided with the global crisis and post-crisis rebound, making the synthetic-control identification window exceptionally noisy. AUH was also accompanied by macroeconomic deterioration — fiscal deficit, capital controls, exchange-rate-distortions — that eroded real benefit value rapidly, so the headline 8pp reduction is partly mechanical from the launch-year level effect rather than a sustained structural improvement. Lustig-Pessino (2014) attributes most of the 2009-2012 distributional improvement to the minimum-wage and pension-indexation channels rather than to AUH per se, with AUH contributing 1-3pp at most. A defensible reading: AUH was a meaningful programmatic addition but the 8pp causal-attribution claim is hard to sustain once data-quality and channel-decomposition issues are addressed.
