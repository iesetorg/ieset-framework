# Steelman — US–EU divergence decomposition

1. **The US-EU gap is partly currency + nominal effects, not real divergence.** Measured in PPP (which the spec uses) the gap is smaller than in market-exchange-rate dollars. The "EU is 50% poorer" claim you hear publicly is mostly a dollar-appreciation story. The PPP gap is meaningful but smaller (~30%), and part of it reflects US-specific cost-of-living differences the PPP deflator may not fully capture.
2. **The decomposition confuses correlation with causation.** US shale, EU regulation, EU energy crisis all happened over the period; all correlate with the gap widening. Attributing causal shares to each requires identifying variation that the simple time-interaction design doesn't provide. The v1 hypothesis explicitly flags this as descriptive, not causal.
3. **EU average obscures large internal variation.** POL grew faster than USA; SWE, IRL, NLD kept pace with USA; DEU, ITA, FRA fell behind. "EU vs US" aggregates a widening internal distribution into a single gap. The widening may be specific to particular EU members (ITA, ESP) rather than an EU-wide phenomenon.
4. **Demographic convergence matters.** US population grew ~15% over 2000-2023; EU grew ~5%. GDP level gap widened partly because US had more workers. Per-capita metrics partly address this but aging-adjusted productivity per hour might show smaller divergence.
