# Steelman: mortgage_market_liberalisation_homeownership_panel

## Counter-argument

Mortgage-market liberalisation often produces housing-price bubbles that raise
affordability barriers for first-time buyers, offsetting any homeownership gains
for existing wealth holders. The observed correlation between liberalisation and
homeownership may reflect debt-fuelled demand that collapses during financial
crises (e.g. USA 2008, Ireland 2008, Spain 2008), producing long-run
homeownership declines. Moreover, state mortgage banks in some countries
(Germany, Singapore) achieve high homeownership with conservative lending
standards, suggesting that liberalisation is not necessary for broad access.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Bubble-and-bust cycle**: Mortgage liberalisation in the USA, Ireland,
   Spain, and UK preceded housing bubbles and subsequent busts that wiped out
   homeownership gains for marginal buyers.

2. **Affordability trade-off**: Liberalisation raises house prices by expanding
   demand without expanding supply. First-time buyers face higher entry costs,
   and homeownership gains may accrue only to wealthier households.

3. **State-bank alternative**: Germany's Bausparkassen and Singapore's HDB
   achieve high housing access without securitisation or high loan-to-value
   ratios, showing that conservative finance can also expand ownership.

4. **Crisis endogeneity**: Countries that liberalised mortgage markets were
   more likely to experience banking crises (2008 GFC), which reduced
   homeownership through foreclosures.

## What would flip the verdict

- Evidence that mortgage-liberalisation-induced homeownership gains are
  reversed within 10 years due to foreclosures or house-price corrections.
- A negative coefficient after controlling for house-price growth, suggesting
  that the ownership effect is purely a price-bubble artefact.
- Synthetic-control estimates showing no persistent homeownership gap for
  liberalising countries vs matched peers.
