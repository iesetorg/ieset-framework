# Steelman — GFC balance-sheet recession (Koo diagnostic)

**The strongest argument against the Koo balance-sheet-recession reading:**

The Koo framework is intellectually coherent and the post-2008 advanced-economy data exhibit some patterns consistent with it (rising household saving rates, household deleveraging, ZLB-bound monetary policy). But the headline cross-country claim — that countries running larger government deficits experienced shallower output collapses — is heavily contaminated by reverse causality and selection, and the Koo diagnostic does not fit several important post-2008 cases without substantial modification.

Specific objections:

1. **Reverse causality on the deficit-output correlation.** Countries with deeper output collapses had larger automatic-stabiliser-driven deficits (revenue collapse + transfer expansion) without any discretionary fiscal-policy choice. The cross-country correlation between average 2008-2012 deficit and 2007-2014 cumulative output change is therefore at least partly the recession driving the deficit, not the deficit shallowing the recession. Identifying the discretionary component requires a Blanchard-Leigh-style forecast-error instrument, which the spec gestures at but does not fully implement.

2. **Eurozone periphery flips the sign on a naive specification.** Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain ran constrained fiscal paths (Troika programmes) and had widely varying output paths: Greece collapsed, Ireland recovered, Spain recovered after delay, Portugal stagnated. A naive deficit-vs-output regression that includes them produces a misleading coefficient because their deficit choice was constrained by sovereign-financing constraints, not chosen optimally. The hypothesis treats this as a sub-sample issue; honest reporting requires showing that the supported result depends on the sub-sample selection, which is a fragility.

3. **Corporate-saving leg generalises poorly outside Japan-1990s.** Koo's signature finding — corporate sector turning net saver — was striking in Japan 1995-2005 because Japanese corporates actively paid down debt for a decade. Post-2008 advanced-economy corporate sectors did NOT pay down debt en masse; corporate debt-to-GDP ratios continued rising in most countries (US, UK, France, Australia). The Koo diagnostic in its strict form fits the household sector in some countries but does NOT fit the corporate sector in the same countries.

4. **Ireland-Spain recovery is awkward for the Koo reading.** Both countries ran consolidated fiscal paths (Spain 2010-2014 cyclically-adjusted balance improved by 5pp; Ireland by 7pp) and BOTH recovered to pre-crisis output levels by 2018-2019, faster than the Koo prediction. The Alesina-Favero-Giavazzi reading — composition of fiscal adjustment matters, expenditure cuts can be consistent with recovery — fits these cases better than the Koo "fiscal-expansion-required" reading.

5. **The household deleveraging story is geographically narrow.** Household debt-to-disposable-income peaked and fell post-2008 in USA, UK, IRL, ESP, NLD. It continued rising in Australia, Canada, Sweden, Norway, Korea. The "household deleveraging" diagnostic of a post-2008 balance-sheet recession does not apply globally; it applies to the subset of countries that had a 2003-2007 housing boom. Generalising the Koo diagnostic to "post-2008 advanced economies" overstates its reach.

6. **Monetary policy at ZLB was not impotent.** Krishnamurthy-Vissing-Jorgensen, Hamilton-Wu, Gagnon and others document that QE materially compressed term premia and supported asset prices, easing balance-sheet repair. The Koo "monetary policy is impotent" claim is a strong characterisation that the empirical literature on QE effectiveness disputes.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report the deficit-output regression coefficient with and without the eurozone-periphery sub-sample; flag fragility.
- Report the household saving / corporate saving / deleveraging diagnostic separately country-by-country; do NOT collapse to an aggregate "supported / refuted" without showing the geographic heterogeneity.
- Acknowledge Ireland and Spain as cases where post-2008 recovery occurred WITH consolidated fiscal paths.
- Discuss QE effectiveness explicitly; the "monetary policy impotent at ZLB" claim is an empirical sub-question the spec must engage with.
- A "supported" finding should be qualified as "supported in the housing-boom sub-sample with sovereign-financing flexibility", not as "supported as a general post-2008 advanced-economy diagnostic".
