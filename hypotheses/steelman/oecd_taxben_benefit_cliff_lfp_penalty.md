# Steelman: OECD TaxBEN Benefit Cliff LFP Penalty

The strongest counterargument is that modelled benefit cliffs overstate real behavioural constraints. Households may value health coverage, childcare, housing stability, and predictable income enough that the relevant decision is not a narrow one-year net-income calculation. High-cliff countries may also provide childcare, training, or job guarantees that offset benefit withdrawal.

TaxBEN household types are stylised and may miss local rules, take-up rates, informal work, and non-financial barriers to employment. A measured cliff can be high because a country provides generous support to non-workers, not because it punishes work in practice. The hypothesis should be overturned if high participation-tax-rate systems maintain equal or higher single-parent employment once childcare, job placement, and local labour demand are included.
