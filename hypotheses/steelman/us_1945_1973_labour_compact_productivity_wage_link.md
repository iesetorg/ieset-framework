# Steelman — US 1945-1973 labour compact productivity-wage link

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The cleanest causal reading of the 1947-1973 productivity-compensation co-movement is not "the labour compact made it happen" but "two-thirds of US adult labour was excluded from price-setting power (the South was a low-wage non-union shop, women were not yet at full LFP, immigration was restricted by the 1924 quota system) AND the rest of the industrialised world's capital stock was sitting at the bottom of war damage." Real compensation per hour in the unionised core could grow with productivity because (a) the marginal supply curve of skilled male labour was steep and (b) US manufacturers had no foreign competitors to discipline pricing power. Both conditions reversed in 1973-1980 (Bretton Woods collapse, German + Japanese reconstruction complete, women's LFP doubling, 1965 immigration liberalisation maturing, southern industrialisation undercutting Detroit), and the wedge opened. Read this way, the 1947-1973 tracking is the statistical shadow of an unrepeatable demographic and geopolitical configuration; the labour-compact institutions (Wagner Act, Treaty of Detroit, 91% top marginal tax) were causally downstream of the rents available to bargain over, not upstream of the compensation share.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **OPHNFB and COMPRNFB are both nonfarm business aggregates and both deflate the wage with a CPI-W-derived index.** The "tracking" between productivity and compensation in this dataset is partly a definitional construction: real compensation per hour = nominal compensation per hour ÷ CPI; productivity = output per hour, where output is measured in constant prices via a similar deflator. Drift in the deflator (1965-1973 oil-price increases, the Nixon wage-price controls of 1971-1974) shows up coherently in both numerator and denominator and inflates the correlation. A v2 should report the wedge using the GDP deflator rather than CPI, and the BLS major-sector productivity series rather than only nonfarm business.

2. **Median compensation diverged from mean compensation per hour even in 1947-1973.** EPI-Mishel reports median-worker compensation tracking productivity tightly through 1973; aggregate compensation per hour also tracks but with a higher growth rate because the top of the wage distribution rose faster. The distinction matters because the social-democratic claim is about "broad-based" wage growth tracking productivity. A v2 should report median-worker hourly earnings (e.g., FRED LES1252881600Q is post-1979 only, so use the BLS Current Employment Statistics historical hours-and-earnings series for production and nonsupervisory workers, available 1964+) alongside the aggregate. If the median-vs-mean gap is large in 1965-1973 the "broad-based" qualifier weakens before the canonical 1973 break.

3. **Labour share of nonfarm business income, not the productivity-compensation wedge, is the cleaner test of the labour-compact claim.** Compensation per hour can track productivity per hour while the labour share falls if hours fall faster than employment, or if non-compensation costs (employer health-insurance contributions, pension accruals, payroll taxes) absorb compensation growth. The labour-share series (FRED PRS85006173 if available; BLS 'Nonfarm Business Sector: Labor Share' otherwise) is the dispositive aggregate. A v2 should report the change in labour share 1947-1973 alongside the wedge, and refute the strong-form labour-compact claim if the labour share fell appreciably even while the wedge stayed small.

4. **The 1947 starting index conceals a 1947-1953 catch-up that does most of the work.** Real compensation per hour in 1947 was still depressed by wartime price controls (final controls lifted June 1946 caused 1946-1947 CPI to spike +14% and +8%) so indexing to 1947Q1 mechanically loads the early period with high compensation growth. The labour-compact claim is about the institutional regime in steady state, not about the ending of price controls. A robustness check should re-index to 1953 (post-Korean-War price normalisation) and report whether the wedge stays small over 1953-1973. If the bulk of the small wedge comes from the 1947-1953 reflation episode, the institutional-channel claim weakens.

5. **The Bretton Woods external-balance constraint, not the labour compact, plausibly held the labour share up.** The 1947-1973 fixed-exchange-rate regime made unilateral domestic wage growth that exceeded productivity costly via balance-of-payments pressure (the UK's repeated devaluations are the cautionary case). US firms could pass productivity gains to wages without balance-of-payments stress because the dollar was the reserve asset and US current-account surpluses were structural through 1971. Once Bretton Woods broke and the dollar floated, the same labour-compact institutions remained on the books for another decade but the wedge opened anyway. A causal-identification version of the hypothesis would need to separate the contribution of (i) collective-bargaining coverage rates, (ii) the top marginal income-tax rate, and (iii) the external-balance regime. The descriptive correlation does not, and over-attributes the regularity to the labour-compact channel.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report the cumulative log wedge and Pearson correlation as the primary statistics, but flag prominently that descriptive tracking of two BLS-deflated series is consistent with multiple causal stories — the labour compact is one of several. The verdict line should not over-claim institutional causation from descriptive co-movement.

(b) Report the labour share trajectory over the same window as a secondary diagnostic if the data is available. If the labour share fell appreciably while the wedge stayed small, the strong-form claim is weakened regardless of the primary verdict.

(c) Note the 1947 starting-point sensitivity in the methodology section and recommend a v2 robustness check re-indexing to 1953Q1.

(d) Treat a SUPPORTED verdict here as evidence that the productivity-compensation tracking pattern existed, not as evidence that the labour-compact institutions caused it. The causal-identification version of the hypothesis (e.g., comparing US vs UK vs France 1947-1973 wedges conditional on collective-bargaining coverage) is a separate, harder pre-registration the framework should hold open.

The strongest version of the steelman argues the hypothesis conflates "the postwar US productivity-compensation tracking happened" (descriptively true and worth measuring) with "the labour-compact institutions caused it" (a causal claim the descriptive evidence underdetermines). A SUPPORTED verdict on the descriptive primary should be paired with explicit caution that the geopolitical-demographic conjuncture of 1947-1973 was an unrepeatable confounder, and the institutional-channel reading is one of at least four (compact, Bretton Woods, demographic exclusion, capital-stock asymmetry) that would all predict the same observed wedge.
