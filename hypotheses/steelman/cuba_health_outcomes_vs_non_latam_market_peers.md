# Steelman — Cuba health outcomes vs non-LATAM market peers, 1960-2000

The strongest argument *against* this harder companion spec is that widening the
pool outside Latin America fixes one problem and creates another. It corrects the
"friendly regional pool" issue, but it may still fail to identify the causal
question people actually care about: whether Cuban socialist institutions produced
health outcomes that were exceptional *because of the political-economic system*,
rather than because Cuba prioritised public health, received Soviet transfers, or
started with a relatively strong pre-revolution baseline.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **A broader pool is harder, but still not a clean counterfactual.** Comparing
   Cuba to Southern Europe, East Asia, South Asia, and MENA is a useful stress
   test, but these countries differ enormously in state capacity, demography,
   epidemiological transition, war exposure, education baseline, and income path.
   A strong Cuban rank in this pool is interesting; it is not a clean socialism-
   versus-market identification.

2. **Raw ranks can flatter Cuba by mixing rich and poor comparators.** If Cuba
   beats Bangladesh, Pakistan, Egypt, or Morocco on life expectancy and infant
   mortality, that is informative but not dispositive. The fairer objection is:
   does Cuba beat the *best* non-socialist market comparators, or does it merely
   clear a broad pool by sitting between advanced markets and poorer developing
   ones? Result cards must show the advanced-market subgroup separately.

3. **Income-rank outperformance is more meaningful than raw rank, but still not
   uniquely socialist.** If Cuba's health rank beats its GDP-per-capita rank by
   several places, that supports "health outcomes outran material-income rank."
   But that can be generated by many mechanisms besides socialism: public-health
   prioritisation, vaccination campaigns, primary-care diffusion, female literacy,
   or selective state capacity. The spec should not collapse those into "Marxism
   wins" without argument.

4. **Soviet subsidies remain a central confound.** Through 1991 Cuba benefited
   from large external transfers through favourable oil and sugar terms. A broad
   outside-LATAM pool does not solve that. If Cuba ranks well in 2000, the fair
   question is whether the rank persists *after* subsidy removal or reflects
   long-lived capital and training accumulated during the subsidy era.

5. **The data-quality objection survives unchanged.** WDI back-fills the Cuban
   1960-2000 health series from Cuban official reporting. The same concerns apply
   here as in the LATAM-peer spec: neonatal classification conventions, abortion-
   denominator effects for IMR, and limited independent endpoint cross-checks. A
   strong rank in the pool is not meaningless, but it inherits a measurement-risk
   band that the result card should state plainly.

6. **Excluding other socialist comparators narrows the inference.** The spec
   deliberately excludes China, Vietnam, and North Korea because the question is
   Cuba against market economies. That is defensible. But it also means the result
   cannot be read as "socialism versus all alternatives" in a general sense; it is
   a narrower test of whether Cuba's health achievements travel outside a friendlier
   Latin American comparison frame.

How this steelman should shape the result card:

- Report Cuba's exact 2000 rank on life expectancy, IMR, and GDP per capita PPP.
- Show the advanced-market subgroup separately: {ESP, PRT, GRC, ISR, JPN, KOR}.
- Report the 1960 and 2000 ranks side by side so readers can see whether Cuba
  climbed, held, or slipped.
- Report the 1991-2000 sub-period explicitly; the subsidy-era caveat matters.
- Distinguish three conclusions carefully:
  1. "Cuba ranks well in a broad non-LATAM market pool."
  2. "Cuba's health rank beats its income rank."
  3. "Marxist-Leninist system superiority is demonstrated."

The data may support (1) and sometimes (2) without justifying (3).
