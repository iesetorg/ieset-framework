# Steelman — Bukele FDI GDP investment climate 2019-2024

**The strongest argument against the causal claim:**

The hypothesis is that Bukele-era policy content (security reform + tax reform + tech incentives + Bitcoin-brand publicity) produced statistically distinguishable FDI, GDP, tourism, and business-formation acceleration relative to Central American peers, with the homicide-rate channel as the dominant mechanism. The opposing view is that the growth acceleration is modest, largely cyclical, and substantially offset by the rule-of-law cost, and that the headline narrative overstates the Bukele-specific policy effect.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage:

1. **SLV absolute growth remains modest.** Real GDP growth averaged ~3.0% 2019-2024 — below the Central American peer average (~3.4%) for the same window once you exclude the 2020 COVID contraction and 2021 base-effect rebound. FDI inflow as a share of GDP rose from ~1.3% (2019) to ~1.8% (2023) — a modest increase, not the dramatic acceleration the hub-narrative claims. On a per-capita basis FDI stock remains below Honduran and Guatemalan levels. The "growth acceleration" thesis may be more a communications victory than a measurement victory.

2. **Rule-of-law regression is well documented to deter FDI.** WGI Rule of Law score for SLV declined from -0.41 (2019) to -0.80 (2023), a material deterioration placing SLV in the bottom 20th percentile of emerging markets. The standard FDI literature (Daude-Stein 2007, Globerman-Shapiro 2003, Kaufmann-Kraay growth regressions) finds a strong positive association between rule-of-law scores and FDI inflows. The Salvadoran observation — rule-of-law declined substantially and FDI rose modestly — is unusual and requires either a very large compensating channel (the security-homicide channel) or a rejection of the institutions-drive-FDI thesis. Most researchers would interpret the modest FDI rise despite rule-of-law decline as the absence of a genuine climate improvement, not evidence of one.

3. **Tourism growth is dominated by post-COVID base effects plus Bitcoin-conference tourism.** SLV tourism arrivals grew from 2.5m (2019) to 3.5m (2023) — a 40% increase — but Central American peers (Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Panama, Guatemala) also recorded 20-30% arrivals growth over the same base-effect-dominated window. The Bitcoin-conference / CECOT-tour component of the residual is real but is not generalisable tourism growth; once the novelty effect subsides, the sustainable tourism-demand channel may normalise. The post-2024 trajectory is the critical test.

4. **The homicide-to-FDI channel is temporally mis-aligned.** The homicide decline largely preceded the most recent FDI inflow window (2022-2023). If the security-channel mechanism were operative, FDI should have accelerated sharply after 2022 synchronised with the homicide collapse. The data instead show FDI inflows rising modestly through 2023 with no sharp post-régimen inflection. A channel-decomposition test should find the homicide-rate mediator explains a meaningful share of the FDI gap; if it does not, the security-channel story is not supported.

5. **Remittance-driven consumption is the dominant growth engine, not investment.** The SLV GDP expenditure breakdown shows consumption growing faster than investment throughout 2022-2024. Remittance inflows (~24% of GDP) are the primary engine of consumption-driven growth; investment as a share of GDP remains below 20% and is not dramatically higher than the peer average. A "growth acceleration" driven primarily by remittance-funded consumption is not equivalent to the "investment-climate improvement" story the hypothesis tests. A policy-content-causal claim requires a rise in investment share, not just consumption.

6. **Central American peer selection matters enormously.** Costa Rica, Panama, and Dominican Republic are much larger and more diversified FDI destinations and may not be the right comparators for a tiny, remittance-dependent SLV. Honduras and Guatemala are structurally more comparable but have their own Bukele-adjacent political dynamics (Honduras Castro administration 2022-, Guatemala Arévalo 2024-) that may contaminate the donor pool. The synthetic-control weighting will need to be reported transparently.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report FDI inflow-share-of-GDP at peer-gap level, not in absolute terms — an SLV share of 1.8% is below the peer average despite the acceleration.

(b) Decompose real-GDP growth into consumption (remittance-driven), investment, and government — and report the treatment effect on each component separately. A treatment effect concentrated in consumption is not an investment-climate improvement.

(c) Report the channel-decomposition test attributing the GDP gap to the homicide-rate mediator vs other channels. If the homicide channel explains less than 30%, the security-to-growth story is weakly supported.

(d) Present the WGI Rule of Law score trajectory alongside the FDI series so readers can see the paradox directly. If the rule-of-law-regression coefficient remains negative in the peer-panel, the Salvadoran deviation is the signal — and its fragility bears noting.

(e) Accept that the result is likely to be "modest acceleration, possibly statistically marginal, with the homicide channel as partial but not dominant mediator." The policy takeaway is that Bukele-era reforms produced a mixed investment-climate signal rather than a dramatic transformation.
