# Steelman — Mitterrand nationalisations and French productivity

**The strongest argument FOR the hypothesis (and against the country-level refutation):**

The 1981-1983 Mitterrand nationalisation programme touched 16 industrial groups (Saint-Gobain, Thomson, Pechiney, Rhône-Poulenc, Bull, CGE, Sacilor, Usinor, Matra, Dassault, the surviving private banks, etc.) plus 36 banks. These firms together represented roughly 24% of French industrial employment and 13% of value-added at the time of nationalisation. A country-level TFP series for FRA pools the nationalised perimeter with the 87% of French value-added that remained private, mechanically attenuating any nationalisation-specific productivity effect by a factor of ~7. The country-level estimator used in this replication therefore cannot directly falsify the firm-level proposition; a +2% above-pretrend country-level TFP signal is consistent with private-sector outperformance offsetting a nationalised-perimeter underperformance of any plausible magnitude.

Specific objections the country-level refutation must engage with:

1. **Scale dilution.** A 5pp underperformance concentrated in 24% of industrial employment dilutes to ~1.2pp at the aggregate. The 1.5% threshold pinned at the country-level is therefore demanding even if the firm-level claim is correct, and the +2.19% above-pretrend reading does not rule out a meaningful firm-level dip — it only rules out a perimeter dip large enough to dominate the rest of the economy.

2. **Pre-trend selection.** The 1975-1980 pre-period spans the second oil shock and the Barre stabilisation, both of which depressed measured TFP. A pre-trend fitted on those years projects forward an artificially low counterfactual and inflates any post-1980 measurement above trend. A longer 1965-1980 pre-trend (when PWT coverage extends) would project a higher counterfactual and shrink the apparent FRA outperformance.

3. **Composition shift, not productivity gain.** Mitterrand's industrial policy bundled nationalisation with sectoral restructuring, accelerated depreciation of obsolete capacity, and selective firm closures. If low-productivity capacity exited faster under state ownership, measured aggregate TFP can rise even if surviving units are less productive than they would have been under continued private ownership. A levels test cannot distinguish "nationalisation raised TFP" from "nationalisation accelerated low-productivity-firm exit."

4. **Investment absorption.** The Mitterrand-era state injected ~150 bn FF of equity into nationalised firms 1982-1986. That capital was eventually expensed against the state budget but recorded as private-sector investment in NIPA. PWT's TFP construction may absorb some of that subsidy as productivity rather than as capital input, biasing measured TFP upward in the treated economy.

5. **Tournant timing.** The 1983 tournant de la rigueur was a macro-policy reversal (disinflation, end of devaluation cycle, fiscal consolidation, EMS-stay decision) NOT a privatisation reversal. Privatisation did not begin until the 1986 Chirac cohabitation and accelerated only under the second Mitterrand cohabitation 1993. The country-level 1984-1990 "recovery" window therefore measures the macro turn, not the ownership-reversal logic the hypothesis posits.

**How this steelman should shape v2:**

The v2 result card must:

(a) Source a Compustat-EU or Banque-de-France firm-level panel covering the 16 nationalised groups plus an industry-matched private comparator set, and re-run the spec's original firm-level event study with firm + year FE.

(b) Add a 1965-1980 pre-trend specification as a robustness check (PWT coverage permitting) to test whether the pre-period selection is driving the FRA above-trend reading.

(c) Decompose the FRA TFP movement 1981-1983 into within-firm productivity change vs between-firm reallocation using the available manufacturing-survey data (BNS / DARES extracts, if fetched), to address the composition-shift confound.

(d) Move the recovery window to 1986-1993 (actual privatisation phase) instead of 1984-1990 (macro-tournant phase), since the original claim is about ownership reversal not macro stabilisation.

The strongest version of the steelman argues the country-level test in v1 is the wrong instrument for the firm-level claim. A REFUTED verdict at the country level should be read as "the nationalised perimeter was too small relative to the rest of the economy for the productivity hit, if real, to dominate the aggregate" rather than as "the firm-level claim is wrong." The verdict warrants a v2 firm-level rerun before the classical-liberal proposition can be considered properly tested.
