# Steelman — Fiat expansion erodes currency purchasing power (long run)

**The strongest argument against drawing hard-money conclusions from this hypothesis:**

The descriptive pattern — fiat currencies lose purchasing power against hard assets over 50-year horizons — is true but trivial, and the hard-money political reading commonly attached to it does not follow from the data.

Specific objections the framework must engage with:

1. **The comparison is rigged by base-year choice.** 1971 is the trough of the gold price after decades of suppression under Bretton Woods pegging. Starting the index at 1971 frontloads gold's re-rating and exaggerates the purchasing-power loss. Rolling base years produce materially different indices.

2. **Hard assets are not the relevant consumption basket.** What ordinary people buy — food, housing services, healthcare, transportation — has a different inflation trajectory than gold or broad commodity baskets. Real wages and real consumption have risen substantially in fiat terms across the 1971-2025 period. "Purchasing power vs gold" is not "purchasing power vs the goods people actually consume."

3. **Real economic variables have risen.** Real GDP per capita, real median household consumption, real hourly wages (in most developed economies, with caveats) have all increased across this period. The fiat regime presided over substantial real economic improvement, not the impoverishment that hard-money readings often imply.

4. **Historical gold-standard periods had severe deflations and frequent banking panics** (1873-1896 Long Depression; panics of 1873, 1884, 1890, 1893, 1907). The counterfactual to fiat erosion is not "stable money" but "stable money with deflationary episodes and banking crises." A full welfare comparison requires both.

5. **Volcker 1979-1982 demonstrated that fiat under an independent central bank can restore price stability.** The framework's own scoping decision (per D.1.5) acknowledges this. The long-run erosion pattern does not imply that fiat is structurally ungovernable.

**How this steelman should be handled in the result card:**

The hypothesis is descriptive and almost certain to be supported. The result card must explicitly note that the pattern does not license the strongest hard-money political readings: (a) base-year sensitivity must be reported; (b) real wages / real median consumption trajectories over the same period must be shown alongside; (c) the framework's D.1.5 scope decision must be linked, making clear that this hypothesis does not endorse a gold standard or strict ABCT orthodoxy.
