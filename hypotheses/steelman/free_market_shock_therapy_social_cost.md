# Steelman — Shock Therapy Social Costs

## Strongest version of the claim

Rapid market liberalisation in post-communist and post-authoritarian transitions—price decontrol, mass privatisation, and trade opening within 2-3 years—produced catastrophic short-run welfare losses that could have been avoided by gradual, sequenced reform. Male life expectancy fell by 3-7 years in the most aggressive reformers (Russia, Ukraine, the Baltic states); GDP contracted by 30-50%; and inequality spiked as state assets were captured by insiders. These losses persisted for a decade or more, challenging the Chicago-school argument that speed minimises transition costs by pre-empting political reversal.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Synthetic-control or DiD evidence** showing that rapid reformers experienced larger mortality, GDP, and inequality deteriorations than gradual reformers (China, Vietnam) or synthetic counterfactuals.
2. **Microdata** on household consumption, health-seeking behaviour, and labour-market transitions documenting the mechanisms (withdrawal of subsidies, collapse of public health, unemployment).
3. **Cross-reformer panel** correlating reform speed (EFW change in first 5 years) with peak-to-trough output loss and life-expectancy decline.
4. **Robustness** to alternative speed measures, donor pools, and pre-trend tests.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Initial conditions dominate**. Former Soviet republics inherited over-industrialised, militarised economies with distorted prices; any transition path would have been painful.
2. **Gradual reform preserved inefficiency and corruption**. China's gradual path worked because it started from agriculture; industrial restructuring without shock therapy may have prolonged stagnation.
3. **Post-communist mortality was partly alcohol-driven**. The mortality spike is better explained by alcohol consumption and stress during social dislocation than by policy speed per se.
4. **Political economy of reversal**. Slow reform risked communist restoration or populist backsliding; speed may have been necessary to lock in the transition.
5. **Heterogeneity within rapid reformers**. Poland and Estonia recovered quickly despite rapid reform; Russia's failure reflected weak institutions and insider capture, not speed itself.

## Boundary conditions

- Best tested in **post-communist transitions** where starting conditions were similar and reform speed varied.
- Less applicable to **agricultural economies** (China, Vietnam) where gradual de-collectivisation was feasible.
- Mortality effects are most pronounced in **middle-aged males**; aggregate life expectancy may mask this.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to state-capacity, institutional-quality, and inequality literatures. Directly challenges laissez-faire shock-therapy claims and aligns with developmentalist and social-democratic critiques of rapid liberalisation.
