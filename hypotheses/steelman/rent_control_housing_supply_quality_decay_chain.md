# Steelman — Rent control's 2nd/3rd-order chain is overstated

**The strongest arguments for rent control and against the full causal chain:**

1. **The first-order distributional gain is enormous and usually ignored.** Diamond-McQuade-Qian 2019 find that San Francisco tenants in rent-controlled apartments gained roughly $2.9B in present-value terms from the 1994 expansion. This is a real welfare transfer to mostly low- and middle-income renters, many of whom are displaced if forced to move at market rates. A hypothesis that weights the 2nd/3rd-order supply decay against this gain is incomplete without acknowledging that the supply elasticity needed to produce equal harm is implausibly large.

2. **Housing supply is inelastic for reasons other than rent control.** Zoning restrictions, historic-preservation, environmental review, car-parking requirements, and capital costs dominate the determinants of new-build supply in most high-cost cities. Attributing low supply growth in SF, NYC, Stockholm, or Berlin to rent control — when SF approves ~2000 units/year regardless of rent regime, and NYC approves more new construction in controlled than uncontrolled boroughs — risks attribution error.

3. **Berlin Mietendeckel 2020-2021 is a very short treated window.** The Mietendeckel lasted only ~14 months before being struck down by the Constitutional Court. Attributing a quality-decay or stock-erosion effect to a sub-two-year policy is implausible; the supply-chain response time for residential real estate is 3-7 years. Short-window studies capture at most an announcement/intent effect, not a structural chain.

4. **Stockholm queues are a housing-policy-design artefact, not a rent-control artefact per se.** Sweden's rental model couples the "utility rent" regime with an administrative allocation queue by explicit policy design. The queue exists BECAUSE the state chose a non-price allocation mechanism, not as an unintended consequence of price control. Calling it a "third-order emergent distortion" misreads the institutional history.

5. **Modern "second-generation" rent regulations are empirically different.** Oregon's 2019 statewide cap (CPI+7%), California AB1482 (CPI+5%), and St Paul's 3% cap-then-reformed regime are explicitly designed to bind less often and to exempt new construction. The "rent control" label covers a huge regime heterogeneity; lumping second-generation regimes with first-generation NYC-1947-style regulation is a category error that inflates the apparent effect size.

6. **Mobility costs are overstated because many stay-in-place decisions are optimal.** A tenant staying in a below-market unit is not trapped in the negative-welfare sense; they are consuming the subsidy. Labour-mobility frictions matter, but the welfare loss from any individual tenant choosing not to move for a marginal wage improvement is small relative to the rent transfer they retain.

7. **Quality decay is controllable via code enforcement.** Many rent-control regimes couple price caps with obligatory maintenance standards and city inspection; NYC's Housing Maintenance Code is an example. Quality decay observed in controlled units partly reflects weak enforcement, not an inherent flaw in price control. A policy design that combines caps with enforcement can keep the first-order transfer without all the second-order decay.

8. **The Airbnb/corporate-let "exemption distortion" is driven by platform economics more than by rent control.** Airbnb growth is well-documented in cities without rent control (Nashville, Austin, Miami); attributing Airbnb-share rises in controlled cities to the rent-control regime rather than to the platform itself is an identification reach.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Always weight the first-order transfer alongside the second-order supply loss; report a welfare-ratio number.
- Report the mobility-loss estimate separately from the rent-transfer estimate; the signs are not directly comparable.
- Flag the Mietendeckel short-window limitation prominently.
- Distinguish first-generation NYC-1947-style rent control from second-generation cap-and-exempt regimes; do not pool.
- Disaggregate new-build effects by exemption status; if new construction is explicitly exempt, the supply channel should be null and the test underpowered.
- Report synthetic-control placebo inference so the reader can evaluate whether claimed effects would plausibly appear by chance in donor cities.
