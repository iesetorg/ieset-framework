# Steelman — Net Migration Revealed Preference for Market Institutions

## Strongest version of the claim

Net migration flows are a revealed-preference measure of where people choose to live and work when they can vote with their feet. After accounting for income differentials, language networks, and conflict proximity, migrants systematically favor countries with stronger rule of law, lower regulatory barriers to entry, and more secure property rights. This is not merely an income story: at comparable GDP per capita, market-liberal polities attract more net inflows than state-directed peers, suggesting that the institutional environment itself is a consumption amenity and a productivity complement.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Panel data** showing that EFW, PMR, and WGI rule-of-law indices predict net migration rates with positive, significant coefficients after country and year fixed effects and income controls.
2. **Heterogeneity**: the effect should be stronger for working-age migrants and for high-skill migrants who have more option value.
3. **Natural experiments**: post-communist transitions, EU enlargement episodes, or post-apartheid South Africa migration shifts where institutional quality changed discontinuously.
4. **Robustness**: the result should survive alternative institutional measures (Heritage, V-Dem) and alternative migration sources (UN DESA, OECD, bilateral stock data).

## Best counterarguments

1. **Income dominates everything**. Raw migration is overwhelmingly driven by wage gaps; once income is controlled, institutional quality may add little explanatory power.
2. **Diaspora networks create path dependence**. Migration flows to former colonial metropoles or existing ethnic hubs regardless of current institutional quality.
3. **Selection on unobservables**. Countries with good institutions also have better climate, geography, or historical luck that confounds the coefficient.
4. **Reverse causation**. High migration may strain institutions (welfare state, housing markets) rather than institutions attracting migration.
5. **Refugee flows are not preference**. Forced migration from conflict dominates net migration in many country-years, washing out preference signals.

## Boundary conditions

- Applies primarily to **voluntary economic migration**; forced migration should be excluded or analysed separately.
- Most informative in **middle-income and high-income comparisons** where income differentials are smaller and institutional quality variation is larger.
- Less informative for **low-income to low-income flows** where income gaps and geography dominate.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with hypotheses on brain drain, diaspora return, and institutional quality as a growth determinant. Tensions with claims that welfare-state generosity or social-democratic institutions are the primary migration pull (Nordic exception may cut both ways).
