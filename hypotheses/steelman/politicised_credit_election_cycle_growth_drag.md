# Steelman: Politicised Credit Election Cycle Growth Drag

## Strongest version of the claim
Development banks in emerging economies systematically increase lending in election years to finance politically targeted projects, subsidise favoured constituencies, and boost short-run demand. This politicised credit allocation weakens subsequent productivity growth because projects are selected for political rather than economic returns, and because evergreening of bad loans to politically connected borrowers crowds out credit to efficient firms. The result is higher NPL ratios and lower TFP growth in the 1-3 years following election-year lending surges.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Time-series of development-bank lending showing cyclical spikes around elections.
2. Cross-country panel showing negative TFP and positive NPL correlations with election-year credit surges.
3. Project-level data showing lower returns and higher default rates for election-year loans.
4. Firm-level evidence of credit misallocation (connected firms receiving loans at below-market rates).

## Best counterarguments
- Development banks may front-load socially valuable infrastructure projects to align with electoral cycles; the productivity drag is temporary and offset by long-run capital-stock accumulation.
- Election-year lending may be a response to anticipated downturns (counter-cyclical policy), not political manipulation.
- NPLs are accounting constructs subject to regulatory forbearance; measured NPLs may not reflect true credit quality.
- Some development banks (Korea Development Bank, China Development Bank) achieved high project returns despite political oversight.
- The critique conflates political interference with developmental mandate; distinguishing the two requires detailed project-level coding.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest in democracies with weak rule of law and partisan development banks. Weak in autocracies (no electoral cycle) or in countries with independent development-bank governance (e.g., KfW in Germany).
