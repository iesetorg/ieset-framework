# Steelman — Guyana / Suriname oil discovery effects 2015-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis treats the discovery as a clean growth windfall. The opposing view is that the headline numbers are statistical artefacts of small-base accounting, that the contracts (especially Stabroek) under-capture rents for the host state, and that the medium-term Dutch-disease and institutional risks dominate the welfare assessment.

Specific objections:

1. **Statistical artefact of GDP rebasing.** Guyana's GDP base is small (~$5bn pre-2019); a few hundred thousand barrels of new daily production produces dramatic per-capita-GDP gains in the national accounts that overstate household welfare improvement.

2. **Contract rents accrue substantially to ExxonMobil consortium.** The 2016 PSC has been criticised as under-favourable to Guyana (cost recovery up to 75%, royalty 2%, profit-share with ringfencing limitations). Headline-GDP gains overstate fiscal-revenue gains to the state.

3. **Dutch-disease pressure on non-oil sectors.** Guyanese-dollar appreciation and labour reallocation from rice, sugar, and bauxite into oil-services threaten non-oil tradables. The pre-discovery export base may be hollowed out.

4. **Institutional fragility.** Guyana's pre-2015 institutional rankings (Government Effectiveness, Control of Corruption) were weak; the rapid resource inflow risks the standard Mexico/Venezuela/Equatorial-Guinea pattern of rent-seeking institutional erosion.

5. **Suriname's debt overhang complicates its windfall.** Suriname entered the 2020 discovery period with a sovereign default and IMF programme; the discovery's net effect must be netted against debt-service drag.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report fiscal-revenue gains separately from headline GDP.
(b) Show non-oil exports trajectory (Dutch disease channel).
(c) Note WGI institutional series in commentary.
(d) Frame Suriname effect as conditional on debt-restructuring success.
