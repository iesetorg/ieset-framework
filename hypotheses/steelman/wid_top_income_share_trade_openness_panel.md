# Steelman - WID Top Income Share And Trade Openness

The strongest objection is that broad trade openness is a poor treatment for
top-income concentration. Trade/GDP is a composite of country size, geography,
value-chain position, commodity dependence, and macro cycles. A small open
economy can have high trade/GDP because its domestic market is small, not
because it recently liberalized in a way that changed bargaining power or top
income formation.

The WID top 0.1 percent pre-tax income share also captures tax reporting,
capital-income realization, and legal income shifting. Those channels can move
with top marginal tax rates, incorporation rules, and capital gains timing even
when real inequality changes less. If trade openness is correlated with tax
systems or reporting incentives, the panel coefficient may be picking up fiscal
institutions rather than an openness mechanism.

Country and year fixed effects reduce some confounding, but they do not solve
reverse causality. High-income sectors that raise top shares can also increase
exports and imports through global value chains. A positive coefficient would
therefore be consistent with internationally scalable firms expanding trade,
not necessarily trade policy raising top shares.

A serious update against the claim would be a small or statistically weak
coefficient after GDP and governance controls, especially if the raw high-trade
contrast points in the opposite direction. That pattern would imply the simple
openness-inequality story is too broad and that future tests should use sharper
trade-shock or policy-event measures.
