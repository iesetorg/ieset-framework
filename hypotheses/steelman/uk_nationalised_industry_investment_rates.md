# Steelman — UK nationalised-industry investment rates 1950-1979

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis (i.e. for "public ownership did not categorically starve investment"):**

The market-socialist claim that UK post-war nationalised industries achieved investment rates comparable to West German and French sectoral peers is defensible — but the strongest version of the steelman has to engage with the country-level investment-share gap that PWT documents (UK 23.0% vs DEU+FRA 31.9% mean of csh_i, 1950-1979), and reframe it as a function of structural macro constraints rather than ownership form.

Specific objections the v2 country-level test must engage with:

1. **Ownership-comparability problem.** France and West Germany were not "private-sector" comparators. France nationalised Renault, the coal mines (Charbonnages de France), gas (Gaz de France), electricity (EDF), and most of the banking sector in 1945-46. West Germany retained Volkswagen, Lufthansa, Bundesbahn, Bundespost and the Länder banks under public ownership. The DEU+FRA peer mean therefore *includes* large state-owned-enterprise investment. If those nationalised industries in the comparator countries were investing at high rates, that is itself evidence that public ownership did not categorically suppress investment — and the UK's lower country-level number must then have a non-ownership explanation.

2. **Stop-go macro policy and balance-of-payments crises.** UK gross capital formation was repeatedly constrained by sterling crises (1947, 1949 devaluation, 1956 Suez, 1961 Selwyn Lloyd squeeze, 1964 sterling crisis, 1967 devaluation, 1976 IMF programme). Each cycle imposed investment-restraining fiscal and monetary tightening that bound public and private investment alike. The 8.9pp UK shortfall is at least partly a stop-go effect that would have shown up under any ownership regime.

3. **Sectoral capital intensity differences.** UK manufacturing was concentrated in lower-capital-intensity sectors (textiles, light engineering) than German manufacturing (chemicals, machine tools, autos with heavy capital deepening). A country-level investment-share gap partly reflects sector mix, not ownership. Pryke (1981) "The Nationalised Industries: Policies and Performance Since 1968" reports that UK nationalised-sector capital intensity was broadly in line with private-sector capital intensity given factor mix.

4. **The Wirtschaftswunder is the wrong baseline.** German 1950s-60s investment rates were driven by post-war reconstruction, Marshall Plan capital flows, and a large reserve army of refugee labour — none of which the UK had. Comparing UK investment to a country in extraordinary recovery overstates the "underinvestment" gap. France's 31.4% mean csh_i reflects similar reconstruction dynamics. A within-country pre/post-nationalisation comparison would be a cleaner test, but the data do not span pre-1945.

5. **Sector-level Pryke evidence.** The original spec ambition was a sector-level test (coal, rail, steel, gas) precisely because country-level comparisons conflate too many things. Pryke's sector-level data, and the BoE Three Centuries dataset, suggest UK nationalised-industry investment rates were broadly comparable to private-sector capital intensity for the same kind of capital. The v2 country-level test cannot resolve this and is acknowledged in `methodology_note` as a downgrade.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v2 result card already runs as `weakened` rather than `refuted` because the 8.9pp gap falls between the SUPPORTED (5pp) and REFUTED (10pp) bands. The steelman supports treating this as the right verdict — the country-level test is too noisy to dispositively refute the ownership-not-the-cause claim, but the magnitude is too large to dismiss. A v3 with sector-level data (OECD STAN, Pryke 1981 hand-coded) would supersede this and is the right next step. Until then the verdict reads honestly: directionally consistent with the market-liberal prior of public-ownership investment crowd-out, but not dispositive given the stop-go and sector-mix confounds.

**The strongest version of the steelman:** ownership form is not the dominant explanatory variable for the UK 1950-1979 investment shortfall — stop-go macro policy, BoP-driven fiscal contractions, and sectoral mix dominate. A test that isolates publicly-owned vs privately-owned sectors *within* each country (the original spec ambition) would be the discriminating test, and the v2 country-level test cannot adjudicate it.
