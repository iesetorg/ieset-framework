# Steelman — Price controls and shortage effect

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The shortage signature the hypothesis looks for is built partly into the canonical-case selection. Cases where price controls produced no shortage — because they were non-binding, because they were accompanied by adequate supply management, or because they were withdrawn before shortages developed — are not in the sample. The hypothesis is effectively asking: "In the cases where price controls clearly failed, did they clearly fail?" The answer is yes, but the framework has not tested the more interesting question: under what conditions do price controls bind without producing the failure signature?

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Survivor / case-selection bias.** World War II price controls (US OPA 1942-1946, UK wartime rationing) are canonical examples of price-controls-that-worked-at-scale-for-a-defined-duration. They featured rationing coordination, production-allocation complementarity, and broad social legitimacy. They produced queueing and some black-market activity but did not produce supply collapse or regime-destabilising failure. These cases are excluded by the "24-month minimum binding duration" rule, which rules out most wartime cases and implicitly privileges peacetime-controls-under-monetary-expansion as the modal case. A spec that included wartime controls would need to explain why the mechanism differs — which is a real explanation (rationing complementarity, accepted legitimacy) but also points to a more nuanced hypothesis than "price controls produce shortages."

2. **Monetary-expansion confounding.** Venezuela, Argentina, and late-Soviet all combined price controls with substantial monetary expansion. The shortage signature may be fundamentally driven by the monetary excess, with price controls merely determining the form the shortage takes (queues rather than hyperinflation). The US 1973-1981 case had different dynamics — a supply-shock origin (OAPEC embargo) with controls attempting to manage the adjustment. Treating these as instances of a single mechanism is a strong-claim simplification. The control variable for monetary expansion is present in the spec but, with N=4 countries, cannot identify the partial effect cleanly.

3. **Rent-control and housing controls are different.** The spec focuses on goods-price controls (petroleum, consumer goods). Rent control is a large and important class of binding peacetime price ceilings with much more mixed outcomes — New York and San Francisco rent control has persisted for decades without the kind of "collapse" signature the hypothesis looks for, though it produces different distortions (supply reduction, quality under-maintenance, allocation inefficiency). A framework that claims "price controls produce shortages" should either engage the rent-control case or narrow its claim to goods-market controls. The parallel hypothesis rent_control_reduces_housing_supply_and_quality does the latter, which is the honest move.

4. **The Venezuela parallel-market divergence is partly currency, not price-of-goods.** Hanke's Troubled Currencies Project measures the black-market exchange rate for US dollars, not the black-market price of shortage goods. The 2-4 orders of magnitude divergence is a currency-control signature as much as a price-control signature, and conflating them leads to overstatement. A clean test would use goods-specific black-market prices (where documented), which exist in scattered form for Venezuela but are not as cleanly quantifiable as the exchange rate.

5. **Quality degradation is not measured.** The hypothesis lists quality degradation as a pre-registered outcome but provides no quantitative measurement apparatus. Quality degradation is real — Soviet consumer goods, Venezuelan packaged goods, Argentine supermarket stockouts — but without a measurement approach it is a qualitative impression that cannot be falsified. Including it in the outcome list without a measurement strategy weakens the hypothesis's falsifiability.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report the shortage-signature findings alongside the wartime counter-examples (US 1942-1946, UK WWII) as a comparator set, even though they are excluded from the primary sample. This establishes the boundary of the claim.

(b) Present the Venezuela and Argentina findings with currency-controls-vs-goods-controls separation where possible. If the goods-specific signature is weaker than the exchange-rate signature suggests, the hypothesis is partly measuring a different mechanism.

(c) Explicitly acknowledge that the quality-degradation outcome is qualitatively coded and not subject to the same falsification discipline as the quantitative outcomes.

(d) Note that the 92% prior confidence is calibrated on a literature that itself has survivorship bias (the failed cases are the ones that got written up). A honest prior would reflect the literature's sample characteristics rather than the extrapolated universal claim.

The strongest version of the steelman argues the hypothesis should be re-written as: "Peacetime price controls under monetary expansion and without complementary rationing produce shortages." That claim is more defensible and less universal. The v1 falsification rule still binds it — if any of the 4 canonical cases fails to show the signature, the narrower claim is also disturbed — but the headline framing would better match what the evidence supports.
