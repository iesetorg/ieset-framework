# Steelman: Portugal RSI Rollback Effect

The strongest counter is that Portugal's 2010-2014 deprivation rise is fully explained by the macro recession (output contracted ~6% peak-to-trough, unemployment hit 17%) rather than by RSI eligibility tightening. Severe material deprivation is highly cycle-sensitive; identifying an RSI-specific component requires separating it from the unemployment effect, which the synthetic-control donor pool with ESP cannot fully do given Spain's parallel double-dip. RSI caseload halving partly reflects mechanical denominator changes (population emigration, particularly young workers to Brazil/Angola/UK) rather than benefit-design cuts per se. The 2015-2019 socialist-government RSI restoration also rapidly returned eligibility to pre-2010 levels, complicating the medium-horizon identification window. Bank of Portugal 2017 monitoring noted that RSI rules pre-2010 were already among the strictest in the EU, and the rollback was modest in international comparison. A defensible reading: RSI rollback contributed at the margin to the deprivation rise, but the dominant cause was the macro shock and emigration; attributing 3pp+ to RSI-specific design changes overshoots the available identification.
