# Steelman — Initial state-share predicts drift reversal

## The strongest case AGAINST this hypothesis

### 1. Mean reversion is not the same as conditional convergence

Cross-sectional regression of slope on initial level *will* produce a
negative coefficient even under pure measurement noise (Galton's regression
fallacy). A country with an unusually high baseline at the corpus start —
maybe inflated by transient war-financing, banking-crisis stimulus, or
data-vintage idiosyncrasy — will revert toward its long-run mean even if
nothing structural drives convergence. The framework's drift index is a
sum of legislated direction moves, not a level, but the regression of
slope on initial level is still vulnerable to this artefact if the
1976–1980 spending-share averages are noisy estimates of a stable
country-level mean. Using the 1995–1999 window for post-transition
countries (Poland, Czechia, Hungary) is itself a bias source: those
countries' market-pivot reforms were *already happening* by 1995, so coding
them as "high initial baseline" with subsequent market drift is partly
mechanical.

### 2. The mechanism may be EU-conditionality, not domestic convergence

Six of the strongest cases for the hypothesis (GRC, ITA, ESP, PRT, IRL,
HUN) are EU member states whose post-2010 market-pivot moves were
externally imposed by Eurozone fiscal-surveillance procedures, EFSF
memoranda, or IMF-Troika programmes. If you control for "subject to EU
fiscal-rule discipline 2010–2018" the residual relationship between
initial spending share and drift slope may collapse. The headline
finding could be picking up the EU's institutional homogenisation
project, not a universal political-economy convergence force.

### 3. The starting baseline is country-specific in non-spending ways

Countries with high spending shares in 1976 also tended to have:
strong corporatist coordination (Sweden, Denmark, Belgium, Netherlands),
indicative-planning legacies (France, Italy), or outsized military
budgets (Israel post-1973, US during Cold War). Some of those features
are themselves the cause of subsequent reform moves (corporatism eased
adaptation to globalisation; indicative planning eroded as state firms
were privatised; military spending fell with the Cold War end). The
spending-share number summarises all of these in one variable, so the
regression cannot tell you which one is driving the apparent reversion
effect.

### 4. The hypothesis predicts the obvious cases and ignores the inconvenient ones

Greece's market drift is dominated by the 2010–2018 memoranda episode;
Italy's by the 2011 Monti government; Sweden's by the 1990–1993 banking
crisis. These are *forced* market pivots in response to specific
country-level crises, not gentle reversion from a high baseline.
Conversely, countries that look like clean cases of low-baseline
expansion (Korea, Japan, Switzerland) had domestic political reasons for
expansion (post-IMF-1997 chaebol accountability + welfare-state catch-up
in Korea; Abenomics second arrow in Japan; SVP-driven expansion of
Bilaterals in Switzerland). The "convergence" pattern fits the data
because the data already includes both ends; there's no separate
prediction made by the convergence story that's not made by the
country-specific narratives.

### 5. The slope-vs-final-level signal is mathematically constrained

Per construction, a country with a high cumulative final value (ITA, GRC
look high in the early 1980s) and a long subsequent reform run *must*
show a negative slope; conversely a country with a low cumulative final
value and a long subsequent expansion run *must* show a positive slope.
Without a level-anchor at corpus start, the slope just records "where
did the country end up" and the regression of slope on initial level is
half-tautological. Using initial general-government spending share as
the predictor partially fixes this (it's exogenous to the cumulative
move sequence) but not fully — high-state-share countries are also more
likely to have been coded with strong-magnitude statist movements pre-
1976 in the corpus, which set the initial cumulative position.

## What would change my mind

A version that controls for: (a) EU-conditionality treatment indicator
post-2010, (b) banking-crisis incidence (Reinhart-Rogoff dating), and
(c) corporatism indicator (CMP/varieties-of-capitalism scores). If the
β on initial spending share survives those controls at p<0.05 with
R² > 0.20, the convergence story has bite. If it collapses, the result
is just describing crisis-induced reform timing, not a structural
political-economy convergence force.
