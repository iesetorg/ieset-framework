# Steelman — Italy Jobs Act employment effect

The strongest heterodox reading: Italy's 2015-2018 employment recovery is dominated by ECB QE and the cyclical rebound from the 2011-2013 sovereign-debt crisis, both of which moved the labour-demand schedule independently of the Jobs Act, and the synthetic-control gap is statistically indistinguishable from the donor-pool noise once placebo permutations are run. Boeri-Garibaldi 2019 and the Bank of Italy's own evaluations show the Jobs Act's tutele crescenti contract was front-loaded by hiring subsidies (decontribuzione) that expired in 2017, and once those subsidies ended the differential hiring rate collapsed — consistent with a tax-incentive story rather than a dismissal-cost-reform story. The contract-composition test is the discipline: if the increase concentrates on fixed-term and apprenticeship rather than tutele crescenti permanent contracts, the headline employment gain is masking a precarisation channel rather than the Article-18-flexibility story. ISTAT data on contract type 2015-2018 actually show the permanent-contract share of new hires fell after the subsidy expiry in mid-2017, which a fair reader could weight as evidence that the structural reform component was small. A clean refutation pathway exists.
