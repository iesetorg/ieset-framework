# Steelman — Costa Rica social-democratic model 1980-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis credits the CRI policy bundle with a development premium. The opposing view is that pre-1980 path-dependent advantages (early literacy, ethnic homogeneity, low political violence in the late 20th century relative to Central American neighbours) drive the outcomes; the policy bundle is a marker, not a cause.

Specific objections:

1. **High pre-1980 baseline.** Costa Rica's literacy rate, life expectancy, and per-capita income were already substantially above Central American peers by 1980. Treating 1980-2024 outcomes as a programme effect overstates the policy contribution.

2. **Avoidance of civil war.** Guatemala (1960-1996), El Salvador (1979-1992), and Nicaragua (1978-1990) suffered civil wars that destroyed human and physical capital. Costa Rica's military abolition was a result of the 1948 settlement; the absence of civil war 1980-2000 produces enormous mechanical convergence advantages independent of social-democratic policy.

3. **Fiscal trajectory deteriorated 2010-2020.** Costa Rica's central-government deficit widened materially through the 2010s, and the 2018 fiscal-rule legislation was reactive rather than preventive. The "successful model" frame ignores the fiscal stress that culminated in the 2021 IMF programme.

4. **Free-zone FDI gains accrue narrowly.** The Intel cluster and medical-devices boom produced concentrated formal-sector gains but did not transmit broadly to small-firm productivity. Informal-sector employment remains substantial.

5. **Inequality has risen.** CRI's gini index increased modestly through the 2000s and 2010s, contradicting the standard "redistributive social-democratic success" story.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Show pre-1980 baseline gap separately from post-1980 trajectory.
(b) Note civil-war confound for peer countries explicitly.
(c) Report fiscal-deficit trajectory 2010-2020 in commentary.
(d) Acknowledge the inequality-trend caveat.
