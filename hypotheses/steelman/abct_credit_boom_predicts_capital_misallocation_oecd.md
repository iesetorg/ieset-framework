# Steelman — ABCT credit boom predicts capital misallocation (OECD panel)

## The strongest opposing argument

The Austrian story conflates two mechanisms — financial-frictions credit cycles (Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist, Mian-Sufi) and inter-temporal capital-structure distortion (Hayekian triangle) — and claims credit for the former while testing only correlations consistent with both.

**Key heterodox/post-Keynesian/New-Keynesian objections:**

1. **Endogenous-money rebuttal.** In post-Keynesian theory (Moore, Lavoie, Werner), private-credit growth is an *accommodation* of profitable investment demand, not an exogenous distortion injected by central banks. The same correlation between credit growth and post-bust volatility can be explained by Minskyian financial fragility — leverage rising during expansions and unwinding in busts — without any reference to a Hayekian production-structure distortion.

2. **TFP-shortfall channel is over-claimed.** Cross-country TFP measurement (PWT rtfpna) is famously noisy, especially during financial crises when capital utilisation rates collapse and measured TFP drops mechanically. Attributing post-bust TFP shortfalls to "malinvestment" rather than to demand-driven utilisation collapse requires structural identification the panel-FE design cannot deliver.

3. **Credit-deepening confound.** The strongest credit booms occurred in financially-deepening economies (Spain, Ireland, Korea, Greece). These economies were converging in financial development to the OECD frontier; some of the credit growth was structural catch-up, not malinvestment. A panel-FE design with country fixed effects partly absorbs this but cannot fully control for the convergence channel.

4. **2008 dominates the result.** Most of the cross-country variation in post-bust investment volatility comes from a single global event (2008-2009). The hypothesis as written claims a generalised ABCT pattern, but the panel may be substantially identifying off one big shock.

## What the framework still captures

The BIS credit-gap → financial-crisis link is one of the most-replicated findings in macro-prudential research (Borio-Drehmann, Schularick-Taylor, Jordà-Schularick-Taylor). The hypothesis is testing a weaker version of the Austrian claim — that credit booms predict subsequent capital-stock-reallocation pain — and that weaker version is well-supported in the historical record. The honest reading is that the ABCT mechanism is one consistent interpretation among several; the panel test cannot adjudicate between Austrian and Minskyian readings of the same correlation, but a clean refutation (no positive credit→volatility coefficient) would falsify all of them simultaneously.
