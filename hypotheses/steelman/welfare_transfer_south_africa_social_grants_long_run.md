# Steelman: South Africa Social Grants Long-Run Effect

The strongest counter is that ZAF's poverty reduction is hostage to its catastrophic unemployment trajectory — official rate above 30% since 2009 and broad-rate above 40% — which the social-grants system mitigates but does not address structurally. Without grants, ZAF poverty would be substantially higher, but the proper question is whether the same fiscal cost (around 3.5% of GDP for grants) deployed differently — wage-subsidies, public-employment, infrastructure — could have generated both poverty reduction and labour-market integration. The synthetic-control donor pool (BWA, NAM with very different demographic profiles; BRA, MEX, COL with different commodity-cycle exposures) gives weak counterfactual fit and the apartheid-legacy starting point makes ZAF's pre-treatment poverty path uniquely shaped by unwinding institutional inheritance rather than by grant-system design. The 2020 SRD grant introduced Universal Basic Income-adjacent design that has failed to integrate with formal grants and produces operational fragility. A defensible reading: social grants prevented ZAF poverty from being catastrophically worse, but the long-run claim of 6-10pp gap relative to peer counterfactual requires assumptions about the synthetic-control fit that the donor pool cannot fully support.
