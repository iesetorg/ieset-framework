# Steelman — Eurozone austerity distributional incidence

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The democratic-socialist framing of post-2008 eurozone austerity treats the
distributional incidence ("hit the bottom quintile harder") as both empirically
established and as evidence that the consolidation was inherently regressive.
The strongest counter-argument is that the empirical correlation between
austerity and bottom-quintile harm is partly mechanical (any large recession
hits employment and the bottom quintile harder, regardless of whether the
fiscal stance is tight or loose), and the counterfactual that "tax-side
consolidation would have been distributionally neutral" rests on assumed
elasticities that the data cannot identify cleanly.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Recession-mechanical incidence vs austerity-causal incidence.**
   Bottom-quintile income tracks employment closely, and employment fell hard
   in periphery countries 2008–2014 for reasons that include the sovereign-debt
   crisis, banking-sector deleveraging, the loss of monetary-policy space at
   the ZLB, and the collapse of property and construction sectors in Spain,
   Greece, and Ireland. Disentangling the share of bottom-quintile harm
   attributable to fiscal consolidation from the share attributable to the
   broader macro shock requires a counterfactual the spec cannot construct.
   The Gini-rises-more-in-periphery-than-core finding is consistent with
   austerity-causal incidence but ALSO consistent with periphery countries
   having simply suffered larger recessions for non-fiscal reasons.

2. **Tax-side consolidation might have been worse for the bottom quintile.**
   The democratic-socialist counterfactual presumes tax-side consolidation
   could have been progressive (top-rate income-tax increases, wealth taxes,
   capital-income taxes). In practice, periphery countries that did lean on
   tax measures (Greece, Portugal) raised VAT — a regressive instrument —
   because (a) progressive direct-tax bases are thin in southern Europe and
   (b) capital-mobility within the EU caps the wealth/capital-tax rate that
   any single country can sustain. A higher VAT or higher pension-contribution
   rate would mechanically have hit bottom-quintile disposable income MORE
   than spending cuts to programs that have means-tested or tapered designs.
   The "tax-side alternative would have been kinder" claim ignores the
   feasible-tax-base constraint and may have the sign wrong.

3. **Greece is sui generis and may anchor the result.** Greece's 2008–2014
   GDP collapse was ~25% — far larger than any other periphery country and
   driven by a debt crisis with restructuring, capital flight, bank runs, and
   eventually capital controls (which is why the spec drops 2015). Including
   Greece in the periphery aggregate inflates the periphery-vs-core gap on
   any distributional measure regardless of the fiscal-stance contribution.
   A periphery-vs-core comparison that excludes Greece would be a useful
   robustness check; if the gap collapses without Greece, the headline finding
   is a Greece-specific case study, not a generalised austerity-incidence
   claim.

4. **Ireland's recovery distorts both ends of the comparison.** Ireland did
   consolidate aggressively but recovered growth and employment by 2014–2015,
   with the fastest distributional rebound in the periphery. Including Ireland
   in the periphery dampens the periphery-vs-core gap (good — that is honest)
   but also conflates "country that consolidated and recovered" with "country
   that consolidated and stagnated." A heterogeneity check would clarify
   whether the bottom-quintile harm is austerity-causal or a function of which
   countries had tradeable-sector dynamism that absorbed the demand shock.

5. **Within-country level differences vs change differences.** The
   periphery-vs-core endpoint comparison on Δ Gini and Δ AROP is a within-
   country first-difference test. It is informative about CHANGES but does
   not engage with the level fact that periphery countries already had
   higher Gini and AROP in 2008 — meaning the bottom quintile in periphery
   countries was further down the cumulative income distribution to start
   with. A purely within-country first-difference test cannot distinguish
   "austerity hit the bottom quintile" from "the bottom quintile was already
   the marginal worker most exposed to the recession the austerity was
   responding to."

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The replication's result card should:

(a) Report the periphery-vs-core gap with and without Greece as a sensitivity
    check, even if the formal v1 estimator includes Greece. If the gap shrinks
    materially without Greece, the headline finding needs Greece-specific
    qualifier language.

(b) Report the periphery-vs-core gap on the primary-balance change as the
    treatment-confirmation step. If the periphery did NOT consolidate harder
    than the core, the periphery-vs-core distributional gap is not a
    treatment effect — it is an unconditional macro-shock difference and
    the verdict should downgrade to PARTIAL or INCONCLUSIVE.

(c) Acknowledge explicitly that the test is consistent with both the
    democratic-socialist and the recession-mechanical readings, and that
    the SUPPORTED verdict says "the distributional footprint of the
    austerity period IS larger in periphery than core" — not "the
    distributional harm was caused by the austerity rather than the
    underlying shock."

(d) Flag as a v2 question the AFG narrative-shock LP-IRF that would identify
    austerity-causal incidence directly. Until that lands, the v1 verdict
    should be read as evidence on the FOOTPRINT (correlation of austerity
    period with rising inequality and poverty) rather than on the CAUSAL
    INCIDENCE (austerity-shock-driven inequality and poverty rising).

The strongest version of this steelman argues that the bottom-quintile
incidence claim is empirically true on the level facts but the
"counterfactual: tax-side consolidation would have been kinder" rests on
assumptions about feasible tax bases in southern Europe that the data
cannot validate. A run that finds rising periphery inequality and poverty
2008–2014 is consistent with the claim but does not prove it; the claim
that consolidation was DISTRIBUTIONALLY UNNECESSARY is a counterfactual
that no observational test in this period can settle.
