# Steelman: State Credit Allocation and Zombie Firm Persistence

## Strongest version of the claim
When state-owned or state-directed banks allocate credit based on political priorities, social stability, or national-strategy goals rather than risk-return criteria, they systematically prop up insolvent firms. These "zombie" firms crowd out credit and demand from healthy competitors, depress market prices, and reduce economy-wide TFP growth. The effect is cumulative over 15-30 years because each subsidised survival period extends the misallocation. Japan (1990s), China (post-2008), and several European banking sectors provide canonical examples.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Cross-country panel showing positive correlation between state-credit share and zombie-firm incidence.
2. Firm-level data showing state-bank borrowers have lower productivity growth and higher survival rates conditional on insolvency.
3. TFP growth regressions demonstrating negative association with state-credit share after controlling for financial depth and institutions.
4. Natural experiments (banking crises, privatisation events) showing zombie reduction and TFP improvement after credit-allocation reform.

## Best counterarguments
- State credit may target sectors with positive externalities (infrastructure, green energy, R&D) where private markets underinvest; zombie-firm measures misclassify socially valuable long-term investment.
- In developing economies with shallow financial markets, state banks perform a coordination function that private banks cannot; short-run zombie costs may be outweighed by long-run development gains.
- Zombie-firm definitions (interest coverage < 1 for 3 years) capture cyclical downturns, not structural insolvency; measurement error is substantial.
- Japan's zombie problem was caused by forbearance regulation, not state ownership per se; privately owned banks also evergreened loans.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest in middle-income and advanced economies with existing private credit markets. Weaker in low-income economies where state banks are the only financial intermediaries.
