# Steelman — Korea HCI drive capability effect (vs LATAM ISI)

Live concerns about the developmentalist (Amsden/Wade) reading of the
1973-1979 HCI drive that this run is built around.

## 1. The 1964 export-promotion turn pre-dates HCI

Park's regime devalued the won, unified the exchange rate, and pivoted
hard to export promotion in 1964 — nine years before the 1973 HCI plan.
Korea's real exports rose from a negligible base in 1961 to ~10% of GDP
by 1973 (cum log change ≈ +3.35 over 1961-1973), already exceeding the
LATAM mean by ~+2.6 log points BEFORE HCI launched. The DiD-around-1973
test on real exports is therefore *negative* (KOR's post-HCI export
acceleration is slower than its pre-HCI rate). This is consistent with
revisionist reads (e.g. Yoo, Stern-Kim) that HCI was an *oversteer* — a
diversion of credit and capital toward heavy industry that *slowed*
export-channel productivity growth in the late 1970s.

If the developmentalist channel is "export-discipline conditionality"
specifically, the data favour Park's 1964 pivot, not the 1973 HCI plan,
as the policy that built capability. The run treats 1973 as the cut
because the spec frames HCI as the proximate treatment, but a v2
should test against a 1964 cut as well.

## 2. US security umbrella + Cold War demand

US military procurement during the Vietnam War (1965-1973) was a major
demand shock for Korean manufacturers — Hyundai's first overseas
construction contracts came from the US Department of Defense. This
period overlaps almost exactly with KOR's pre-1973 export takeoff.
LATAM economies received no comparable demand shock. The KOR-LATAM
divergence may reflect demand-side geopolitics, not supply-side
industrial policy.

## 3. Land reform + literacy + Japanese colonial infrastructure

Korea entered 1961 with universal land reform (1950), >70% literacy,
and a colonial-era industrial base in the north. None of these are in
the v1 controls. LATAM's donor pool had highly unequal land tenure,
lower literacy, and weak industrial-scientific infrastructure. The
KOR vs LATAM gap may be substantially pre-determined by initial
conditions.

## 4. The LATAM "ISI counterfactual" is itself disputed

The "LATAM ISI failed" narrative is sympathetic to the developmentalist
claim. Brazil's 1968-1973 "miracle" (~10% real GDP growth) and Mexico's
1960s "stabilising development" are not obvious failures. The 1980s
debt crisis hit LATAM harder than Korea, but that's a 1982+ shock not
clearly attributable to ISI policy choice — it's largely Volcker-rate
spillovers.

## 5. Capital allocation distortions

HCI is widely credited as a financial-system distortion (Stallings,
Cumings): the Korean government directed >50% of bank lending to HCI
sectors, which contributed to the 1979-1980 financial near-collapse
and to the chaebol-overleverage that detonated in 1997. Counting only
1961-1985 capability divergence selects on the period before those
costs materialise. A v2 should extend through 1997 to net out the
HCI-induced 1997 crisis cost against the 1985 capability gain.

## 6. Outcome-variable substitution

The original spec asks for heavy-industry export share (HCI sector
exports / total exports) and Hidalgo-Hausmann economic complexity
index (ECI). Neither is on disk in the current vintages. v1 substitutes
WDI real exports + WDI industry value added, which conflate
HCI-specific and non-HCI capability. A v2 against ECI would be a
sharper test of the "complexity build-up" claim specifically — a high
ECI gap by 1985 would be much harder to explain via demand shocks
alone.
