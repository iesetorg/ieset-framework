# Steelman — Freiburg "two pillars": rule-of-law × CBI synergy panel

## The strongest opposing argument

The synergy claim — that rule-of-law and central-bank independence multiply rather than add — is the kind of compound institutional assertion that often fails to survive panel-FE testing once mechanical correlations are stripped out. Heterodox and revisionist readings will press several lines of attack:

1. **WGI–EFW collinearity.** Countries that score high on WGI Rule of Law typically also score high on EFW Sound Money. The interaction term is multiplicative on two near-collinear regressors and may identify off limited high-high outliers (Switzerland, Germany, Singapore, Nordics) rather than off generalisable variation.

2. **Outcome-contaminated indices.** Both WGI and EFW are partly built from outcome-adjacent components. WGI RL is constructed from expert assessments that incorporate observed economic stability; EFW area-3 includes inflation-volatility itself as an input. Regressing inflation on EFW area-3 risks circularity. The hypothesis tries to mitigate this by using lagged levels and within-country variation, but the bite of the critique is real.

3. **Compositional heterogeneity.** "Strong state plus independent CB" countries are mostly OECD; "weak state plus dependent CB" countries are mostly LATAM and post-Soviet. The panel-FE coefficient may identify off the OECD–LATAM gap, not on a cleanly identified institutional-design margin.

4. **Heterodox alternative: capital-mobility regime drives both.** Open capital accounts force both rule-of-law and monetary discipline through bond-market price signalling; the institutions are downstream of the capital regime. The synergy claim then mistakes a single capital-regime channel for two separable institutional pillars.

5. **R² gain may be trivial.** Interaction terms can be statistically significant in large panels while explaining negligible additional variation. The R²-gain INFORMATIVE check is the spec's own concession to this; if the gain is below 2pp the synergy claim is empirically negligible.

## What the framework still captures

The dual-outcome design (inflation AND volatility) is harder to satisfy by chance than a single-outcome test, and the R²-gain gate gives the heterodox reader a clean way to win on substantive negligibility even when the t-statistic is fine. The spec is testing the strong form of the Freiburg "two pillars" claim — that the institutional design is interactive — rather than the weak form (each pillar matters separately). The strong form may not survive; the spec is designed so the field can see clearly when it doesn't.
