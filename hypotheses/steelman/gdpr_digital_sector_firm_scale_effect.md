# Steelman — GDPR + DMA + AI Act cumulative effect on EU digital firm scale

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "EU digital regulation suppresses firm scale" framing:**

1. **EU VC-market thinness and capital-market fragmentation pre-date GDPR by decades.** The EU's comparative disadvantage in scaling digital firms has well-understood structural origins: no unified capital market, bank-dominated financing, pension-fund asset allocation constraints, 27 insolvency regimes, and no EU-wide equity vehicle for cross-border listings. These are the dominant mechanism; GDPR is a late addition. A hypothesis that attributes the 2018-2024 scale gap to GDPR/DMA/AI Act risks overreading what is mostly a continuation of pre-existing capital-market fragmentation. The placebo test at pre-2018 fake treatment dates is not a gotcha — it is the actual risk.

2. **US Big Tech concentration is an antitrust and tax story, not a regulation-avoidance story.** The US "scale" advantage in the digital sector is substantially an artefact of the Chicago-school antitrust consensus (1980s-2010s), a favourable tax code, favourable immigration policy for technical talent, and network-effect lock-in from the 1990s web era. The EU "did not produce a Big Tech" partly because the US got there first during a different regulatory regime, not because recent EU regulation prevented it. Running "EU-post-2018 vs US" rigs the comparison against the EU.

3. **GDPR has a real and substantial consumer-welfare benefit the outcome set excludes.** Privacy is a genuine public good; GDPR's consent-based processing regime, subject-access rights, and enforcement architecture are being copied globally (Brazil's LGPD, California's CCPA, India's DPDP). A hypothesis measuring only firm-scale outcomes loads the welfare calculus against the policy — which is a legitimate narrow exercise, but any headline that reads "GDPR hurt EU digital sector" is materially incomplete without the privacy-externality side. Framework honesty requires flagging this in all reporting.

4. **Scale is not the right unit of welfare.** A fragmented digital ecosystem with many mid-sized firms may be competitively healthier than a concentrated one with a few giants; consumer-surplus comparisons between EU and US digital services are not obviously favourable to the US side. Median-quality-adjusted consumer surplus is hard to measure but probably not worse in the EU; some metrics (broadband prices, mobile data costs, cross-border consumer protection) favour EU consumers. "Lower firm scale" is a neutral-to-positive outcome under some welfare weightings.

5. **Intra-EU heterogeneity undermines a pooled "EU" effect.** Ireland hosts outsized digital-sector activity (admittedly partly tax-motivated); Estonia is startup-friendly; the Nordics have produced Spotify, Supercell, Skype, Klarna. France's La French Tech produced real scale. The "EU as monolith failed to produce scale" framing ignores this; if regulation were the dominant driver, the heterogeneity should be smaller. The pattern looks more like "capital-market depth + talent-pool + language market" mattering more than regulation.

6. **Measurement is severely limited.** Crunchbase and PitchBook under-cover non-US firms; VC funding measured in dollars biases toward US deal flow. "Revenue per firm" requires firm-level accounting data that is available for public firms only; privately held EU digital firms are under-measured. Treatment-effect estimates using these data sources should be reported with the caveat that a measurement artefact could produce a false "EU firms are smaller" result.

7. **Some firms may be MOVING from US to EU, not away.** Privacy-conscious firms (ProtonMail, Tutanota, Qwant, some neobanks) explicitly cite GDPR as a competitive advantage. Treating GDPR as a uniform tax on digital firms ignores that it creates a differentiated market segment EU firms can specialise in. This is a marginal effect but not zero.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report the capital-market-depth control's coefficient explicitly. If most of the "EU-post-2018" coefficient is soaked up by capital-market-depth, the regulation story is weakened.
- Disaggregate by EU country. If Ireland, Sweden, Estonia perform comparably to US despite identical regulatory exposure, the regulation mechanism is incomplete.
- Distinguish median from mean outcomes. US means are inflated by FAANG-scale outliers; EU medians may be similar. The relevant magnitude question is "is the median EU digital firm smaller, or just the right-tail?"
- Acknowledge privacy-externality benefits as out-of-frame. Do not headline the result as a full welfare accounting; frame it as a narrow scale-outcome measurement.
- Report the AI Act 2024 effect as provisional given the compliance deadlines have not bitten yet.
- Be conservative about attribution. Even in a finding consistent with the claim, the share attributable to regulation specifically (vs capital-market thinness + US-first-mover + tax arbitrage) is plausibly 20-40%, not dominant.
