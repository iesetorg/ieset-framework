# Steelman — Decentralized property rights and agricultural productivity

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest formulation is that secure private or household land-use rights create incentive structures that align farmer effort and investment with productivity outcomes. When farmers have transferable, long-horizon tenure, they invest in soil conservation, irrigation, and productivity-enhancing inputs; when tenure is collective or state-allocated, the incentive gap between individual effort and individual reward produces shirking, under-investment, and misallocation of land to less productive uses. Over long windows (20+ years), these incentive differences compound into large productivity gaps.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Within-country land-reform episodes.** China's Household Responsibility System (1978-1984), Vietnam's Doi Moi (1986), Ethiopia's 1995 land certification, and Rwanda's 2000s land titling all provide natural experiments. Agricultural productivity should show a break in trend following reform, with the break larger in regions with stronger implementation.

2. **Cross-country panel with tenure-security indices.** The FAO land-tenure indicators, PRIndex property-rights module, and V-Dem land-rights equality should jointly predict agricultural productivity growth in a panel FE, with the coefficient stable across different tenure measures.

3. **Input-response evidence.** Secure tenure should predict higher fertiliser use, irrigation investment, and tree-cropping (long-horizon investments) even after controlling for output prices and credit access.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Scale-economy and coordination benefits.** Collective systems can overcome coordination failures in irrigation, mechanisation, and input procurement that fragmented household plots cannot. Israel's kibbutzim and some Chinese village collectives achieved high productivity through collective action, not individual incentives.

2. **Credit-market confound.** Land titling improves access to formal credit (De Soto channel), but the productivity gain may come from credit rather than tenure security per se. Disentangling the two requires instrumenting for titling with legal-origin or political-factors variables.

3. **Measurement error in tenure security.** FAO indicators are often ordinal and patchy; PRIndex has limited country coverage; V-Dem measures de jure rights that may diverge from de facto security. Measurement error biases coefficients toward zero, so a null result may reflect data quality rather than absence of effect.

4. **General-equilibrium effects.** Titling one region may raise local productivity while displacing landless labourers to less productive areas, leaving national productivity unchanged. The hypothesis tests country-level aggregates that may miss these distributional offsets.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for smallholder agriculture in rain-fed staple-crop systems where individual effort is the binding constraint. It is weaker for plantation crops, highly mechanised systems, and economies of scale (large grain belts in Argentina, Ukraine). The 1980-2020 window captures the global wave of land titling and decollectivisation; earlier periods had different dominant tenure regimes.
