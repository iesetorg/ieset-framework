# Steelman — Consumer Choice Variety After Trade and Market Reform

## Strongest version of the claim

Trade liberalisation and competition-policy reforms expand consumer variety and price-adjusted welfare more effectively than state industrial policy because they reduce entry barriers, lower tariff walls, and force domestic incumbents to compete on quality and assortment. State industrial policy, by targeting specific sectors and often protecting national champions, may raise output in those sectors but at the cost of reduced variety and higher consumer prices elsewhere. Over long horizons, the variety gains from market reform dominate the targeted output gains from industrial policy.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Event-study or DiD evidence** from trade-liberalisation episodes showing post-reform increases in import variety, domestic SKU counts, and quality-adjusted real consumption.
2. **Head-to-head comparisons** of industrial-policy episodes (e.g., Korea's Heavy and Chemical Industry drive, Brazil's ISI period) versus trade-liberalisation episodes in the same country or peer group.
3. **Microdata** on household consumption baskets showing faster new-product adoption and lower relative prices of durables after reform.
4. **Cross-country panel** with PMR, tariff levels, and industrial-policy intensity as competing predictors of variety indices.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Industrial policy also expands variety in targeted sectors**. Korea's export targeting created new product lines (ships, semiconductors, autos) that consumers eventually enjoyed.
2. **Trade liberalisation can reduce domestic variety** if imports drive local producers out of business, even as total variety rises.
3. **Variety is not welfare**. More product lines may reflect marketing differentiation, not true quality improvement.
4. **State provision of public goods complements variety**. Industrial policy may fund infrastructure or R&D that indirectly expands variety beyond what trade alone achieves.
5. **Measurement bias**. Import-variety counts overstate consumer welfare if many new lines are intermediate goods or luxury imports unavailable to median households.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **consumer durables and tradeable services** where import competition is strongest.
- Less defensible for **strategic sectors with long gestation periods** (semiconductors, aerospace) where infant-industry protection may be necessary to achieve variety at all.
- Catch-up economies may see larger variety gains from state-directed technology adoption than from passive trade opening.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with trade-liberalisation welfare-gains literature and competition-policy consumer-benefit claims. Tensions with industrial-policy success hypotheses, especially in East Asian catch-up contexts.
