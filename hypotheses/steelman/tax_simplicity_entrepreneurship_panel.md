# Steelman: Tax Simplicity and Entrepreneurship

## Strongest version of the claim
Complex tax systems impose compliance costs that fall disproportionately on small firms and potential entrants, creating a barrier to entrepreneurship. The strongest version predicts that a one-standard-deviation reduction in compliance time or payment frequency is associated with a meaningfully higher rate of new-business formation and SME employment growth, after controlling for income, regulatory quality, and credit access.

## Key evidence the claim would need
- A negative, statistically significant coefficient on tax-compliance hours or payment count in a country-year panel with country and year FE.
- Robustness to alternative entrepreneurship measures (World Bank new business density, GEM early-stage entrepreneurship, OECD SME employment).
- The effect should be larger in countries with weaker legal institutions, where compliance costs are less predictable.

## Best counterarguments
- Tax simplicity is highly correlated with overall regulatory quality and state capacity; the coefficient may pick up a broader institutional package rather than taxes per se.
- Some complex tax systems (e.g., VAT with many rates) finance public goods that support entrepreneurship.
- Doing Business tax indicators have been criticised for measurement inconsistency across countries.

## Boundary conditions
- The claim is most relevant for formal-sector entrepreneurship; informality may insulate some economies from tax-compliance costs.
- Very low tax burdens may undermine state capacity, so the claim is about complexity conditional on a minimum revenue level.
- Effects may be nonlinear: reducing compliance from 400 to 200 hours matters more than from 50 to 25.

## Relation to existing hypotheses
- Aligns with `licensing_burden_income_mobility` and `informality_market_entry_barriers` as part of a broader regulatory-barriers-to-entry family.
- Distinct from tax-level hypotheses (e.g., `tax_burden_frontier_growth_non_linear`) because it focuses on compliance costs, not marginal rates.
