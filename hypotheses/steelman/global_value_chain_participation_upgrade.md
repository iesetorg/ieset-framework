# Steelman — GVC participation predicts income upgrading with free entry, but not with protected incumbents

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that global value chains create opportunities for technology transfer, quality upgrading, and scale economies, but these benefits are realised only when domestic firms are free to enter supplier relationships, compete for contracts, and reallocate resources toward high-productivity activities. When incumbents are protected by entry barriers, procurement preferences, or regulatory capture, GVC participation produces enclave growth: foreign lead firms source from a narrow set of privileged local partners, linkages remain shallow, and spillovers to the broader economy are minimal.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive interaction**: A robustly positive coefficient on (GVC participation × entry freedom) in an income-growth panel, with the main effect of GVC participation insignificant or negative.
2. **Tercile evidence**: Top-tercile entry-freedom countries should show a positive GVC-income link; bottom-tercile countries should show no link or a negative link.
3. **Channel evidence**: Backward and forward linkage intensities should mediate the interaction effect, with backward linkages (imported input quality) stronger in high-entry-freedom settings.
4. **Bartik-IV robustness**: An instrument for GVC participation using exogenous global demand shifts should produce the same interaction sign, reducing reverse-causality concern.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Endogeneity**: Countries with higher income and better institutions both participate more in GVCs and maintain lower entry barriers. The interaction may capture a common institutional driver rather than a true complementarity.
2. **China dominates**: China's massive GVC participation coincided with rapid income growth but also with significant entry barriers (SOE privileges, local content requirements). The cross-country panel may code China as "moderate entry freedom," but the within-China heterogeneity is enormous.
3. **GVC measurement**: TiVA and Eora GVC measures are model-dependent and sensitive to input-output table assumptions. Measurement error in GVC participation may attenuate the main effect and bias the interaction.
4. **Enclaves in free-entry economies**: Even with free entry, GVC participation in small economies may be concentrated in a few export-processing zones with limited domestic spillovers. Entry freedom is necessary but not sufficient for broad upgrading.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for middle-income economies with existing manufacturing capacity and access to major GVC hubs (East Asia, Eastern Europe, Mexico). It is weakest for: (a) low-income economies without basic infrastructure or skills to enter GVCs; (b) resource-dominated economies where GVC participation is structurally limited; (c) very small economies where scale constraints prevent deep supplier networks regardless of entry policy.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis bridges `trade_lib_china_wto_2001_manufacturing_export_surge` and `export_complexity_market_access_vs_subsidy` by focusing on production fragmentation rather than final-goods trade. It engages the competition-policy literature by testing whether domestic market structure determines the payoff from global integration. A null interaction would support the "GVCs help regardless of domestic policy" view; a negative interaction would support the "GVCs without reform create dependent enclaves" view.
