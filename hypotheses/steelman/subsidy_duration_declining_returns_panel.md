# Steelman: Subsidy Duration Declining Returns Panel

## Strongest version of the claim
Industrial subsidies can accelerate output and employment in targeted sectors during early phases by overcoming coordination failures, financing fixed costs, and signalling government commitment. However, when maintained beyond 10 years, they become captured by incumbent firms and political constituencies, distorting resource allocation, depressing competitive entry, and reducing productivity growth. The declining-returns pattern is systematic across OECD and emerging economies because political economy dynamics overwhelm initial policy rationales.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Cross-country panel showing positive output coefficients for young subsidies (<5 years) and negative or zero TFP coefficients for old subsidies (>10 years).
2. Firm-level data showing rising markups, falling innovation, and increasing political donations in long-subsidised sectors.
3. Event studies of subsidy removal showing productivity gains in formerly protected sectors (e.g., EU sugar, US cotton).
4. Comparison of time-limited versus open-ended subsidy programmes.

## Best counterarguments
- East Asian developmental states (Korea, Taiwan) maintained subsidies for decades while achieving rapid productivity growth; export discipline and performance criteria prevented capture.
- 10 years is too short for industries with long development cycles (semiconductors, aerospace, pharmaceuticals); premature removal destroys sunk investments.
- Subsidy duration is endogenous: governments extend subsidies precisely because sectors are struggling due to exogenous shocks, not because subsidies caused the struggle.
- Output effects are socially valuable even if TFP effects are neutral; employment and regional development goals may justify long-duration support.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest in mature, non-tradable, or politically sensitive sectors (agriculture, steel, coal, shipbuilding). Weakest in high-technology sectors with strong export-discipline and performance monitoring.
