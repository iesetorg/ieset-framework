# Steelman — Financial Liberalisation Without Prudential Regulation Raises Crisis Risk

## Strongest version of the claim

Opening the capital account and deregulating domestic financial markets without simultaneously strengthening prudential supervision, capital adequacy, and macro-prudential buffers increases the probability and severity of banking and currency crises. The mechanism is familiar: liberalised markets allow rapid credit expansion, maturity mismatch, and currency mismatch that outstrip supervisory capacity. Crises erase long-run growth gains from financial deepening and can leave countries worse off than if they had maintained controls. The claim challenges the Washington Consensus assumption that financial opening is unconditionally beneficial.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing positive interaction between financial liberalisation and weak prudential regulation for crisis probability (Laeven-Valencia database).
2. **Event studies** of liberalisation episodes with divergent outcomes based on regulatory framework (e.g., Chile 1980 vs Chile 1990s; Malaysia pre- vs post-1997).
3. **Credit-boom evidence**: financial liberalisation predicts credit booms, and credit booms predict crises, but only where supervision is weak.
4. **Growth-cost accounting**: estimating the present value of crisis losses versus the present value of liberalisation gains.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Crises are cleansing**. Financial crises eliminate inefficient firms and reallocate capital; the long-run growth path may be higher despite short-run pain.
2. **Prudential regulation is endogenous**. Countries that liberalise also tend to strengthen supervision; disentangling the two is difficult.
3. **Controls also have costs**. Capital controls reduce investment, raise borrowing costs, and encourage evasion; the counterfactual is not zero cost.
4. **Creditor-country responsibility**. Crises in emerging markets often reflect shocks from advanced-economy monetary policy, not domestic liberalisation.
5. **Survivorship bias**. Countries that liberalised successfully (e.g., Korea post-1997) are studied less than crisis cases, skewing perceptions.

## Boundary conditions

- Most relevant for **middle-income emerging markets** with shallow financial systems and limited supervisory experience.
- Less relevant for **advanced economies with deep markets and strong institutions**, though 2008 shows they are not immune.
- Applies to **wholesale liberalisation**, not targeted deregulation of specific segments.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with financial-stability and macro-prudential literatures. Tensions with claims that capital-account openness and financial deregulation are always welfare-improving.
