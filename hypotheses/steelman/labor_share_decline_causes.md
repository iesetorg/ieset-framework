# Steelman — Labor share decline causes

The strongest opposing argument: the framework's decomposition absorbs the most politically convenient channels first, leaving the most politically inconvenient channel (market concentration / markup expansion) as a small residual. The De Loecker-Eeckhout-Unger reading should be the default, with the other channels as corrections.

## De Loecker-Eeckhout-Unger's aggregate markup claim

De Loecker-Eeckhout-Unger (QJE 2020) estimate aggregate US markups rose from roughly 1.2 over marginal cost in 1980 to roughly 1.6 in 2016. That magnitude is quantitatively sufficient to explain the entire US labour-share decline on its own. The cross-country generalisation (De Loecker-Eeckhout 2018) shows similar markup expansion in Europe, Japan, and Korea. If the aggregate-markup estimate is taken at face value, the framework's "concentration channel is material but smaller" framing is wrong: concentration is the dominant cause, and the other channels are either correlates of concentration or noise.

## The Karabarbounis-Neiman capital-intensity story has a problem

The KN 2014 story requires an elasticity of substitution between capital and labour above unity, and the decline in the relative price of capital driving firms to substitute capital for labour. Subsequent literature (Oberfield-Raval 2021, Grossman-Oberfield 2022) has documented two difficulties: (i) estimated elasticity of substitution using more recent methods is closer to 0.6-0.8, well below unity, which predicts capital deepening should raise labour share, not lower it; (ii) the relative-price-of-capital decline is partly an index-number artefact from hedonic adjustment of ICT equipment. If KN's mechanism is empirically weak, attributing labour-share decline to capital intensity is attributing it to a channel the recent literature does not support.

## Rognlie's housing correction is fragile

Rognlie's 2015 paper is genuinely important but narrower than the framework implies. The housing-stripped US labour-share decline over 1948-2010 is roughly 3 percentage points (vs. 5 in the headline series), meaning the housing-measurement channel absorbs ~40% of the decline, not all of it. Moreover, Rognlie's critique applies most forcefully to the US post-1980; in Germany, France, and Korea the housing-sector share of value added did not rise comparably, yet labour share still declined, suggesting the housing-measurement story is US-and-UK specific. The framework's use of housing VA share as a cross-country channel may work for US/UK/AUS but not for the full panel.

## Union decline is not just a control

The hypothesis includes union density as a *control* rather than a channel. This is a methodological choice that presupposes labour-bargaining-power decline is not the primary cause. But cross-country evidence (Blanchflower-Bryson, Farber-Herbst-Kuziemko-Naidu) links the timing of US/UK union decline tightly to the timing of labour-share decline, with Nordic countries preserving both union density and labour share. Moving union density from control to channel would likely absorb a substantial fraction of the variation that the decomposition currently attributes to capital intensity or import penetration.

## Self-employment is bidirectional

The Gollin measurement story (self-employment income split) is real, but the direction runs both ways: in countries where self-employment declined over 1980-2020 (most OECD), the Gollin correction *reduces* the measured labour-share decline, so using self-employment share as a channel may be correcting in the wrong direction for part of the sample. The self-employment channel should be signed explicitly by country.

## The import-penetration proxy is weak

Manufactures-imports-share is a noisy proxy for the Elsby-Hobijn-Sahin channel. The cleaner identification uses country-pair bilateral import exposure weighted by source-country wages (China shock style). The v1 spec using aggregate manufactures-import-share will under-identify the globalisation channel, likely causing it to appear weaker than it is. This biases the decomposition toward finding large roles for capital intensity and housing by default.

## What would update the framework's prior

If the concentration channel absorbs more than 40% of explained variation despite a weak PMR-based measurement, the De Loecker-Eeckhout markup reading earns standing and the framework should update toward antitrust-and-concentration-policy prescriptions rather than housing-supply prescriptions. Symmetrically, if the housing-stripped labour share shows less than 1 percentage point of decline across the OECD panel, the framework should acknowledge that the entire phenomenon is a measurement artefact and the policy agenda is narrower than the markup and globalisation literatures imply.

## Policy implications

Capital-intensity + measurement channels prescribe asset-price-driven redistribution (housing supply, wealth taxation) and better statistics. Markup + globalisation channels prescribe antitrust and trade-adjustment policy. Union-bargaining channel (not in the v1 spec) prescribes labour-market institutional reform. The framework's decision to omit union density as a channel is worth stress-testing in v2.
