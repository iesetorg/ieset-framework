# Steelman — German Energiewende industrial cost trajectory

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "Energiewende raised German industrial electricity prices and suppressed manufacturing VA" framing:**

1. **The 2022 Russia-Ukraine gas shock is an exogenous confound, not an Energiewende outcome.** A large share of post-2020 German industrial-price divergence from US peers is mechanically driven by the Russian gas-supply collapse, which was imposed on Germany from outside rather than chosen. Even a counterfactual Germany that kept all its nuclear plants would have experienced a gas-induced spike in gas-indexed wholesale prices during 2022-2023. The hypothesis's pre-registered sensitivity (drop 2021-2024) must be taken seriously: if the Energiewende-attributable gap shrinks sharply once the war window is excluded, the honest reading is that most of the headline divergence is geopolitics, not policy-content.

2. **Manufacturing VA share decline in Germany pre-dates Energiewende.** Germany's manufacturing share of GDP has been drifting down since the mid-1990s, driven by services-sector expansion, rising real wages relative to Eastern European peers, and (from the 2000s) Chinese import penetration in intermediate goods. Attributing post-2011 VA decline to Energiewende requires separating it from these secular trends; the synthetic control's pre-trend fit window (2005-2010) is short enough that a spurious mid-2000s trough in German manufacturing could make the fit artificially good. The German Kurzarbeit-driven post-2009 manufacturing recovery also makes that window atypical.

3. **Competitiveness is multi-dimensional — VA is not the only relevant outcome.** German manufacturing EMPLOYMENT has been remarkably stable through the 2011-2023 window even as VA softened; export volumes in mechanical engineering remained competitive. "Manufacturing VA share" is a convenient aggregated outcome but imports a value judgement (nominal VA is the welfare metric) that the steelman contests — a high-wage, lower-volume German manufacturing sector may be preferable to a high-volume low-margin one regardless of VA-share movement.

4. **Intra-EU heterogeneity undermines the pooling.** Ireland, Netherlands, Denmark — all exposed to EU climate regulation and to common European energy markets — maintained or grew manufacturing productivity in the same window. If Energiewende were the dominant driver of the German decline, we would expect less heterogeneity among similarly-exposed peers. The country-specific element of the German experience (Russian pipeline dependence, nuclear-phaseout speed, auto-sector China exposure) is doing most of the work, not the generic "green transition" story.

5. **Policy design choices matter, and aggressive climate transitions CAN be low-cost.** Sweden decarbonised industrial heat with nuclear + hydro + biomass and retained world-leading steel + forest-products exports; Norway runs aluminium smelters on hydro at world-competitive costs. The steelman reframes the result: it is not "green transition raises industrial cost" but "gas-indexed wholesale market design + premature firm-capacity retirement + retail EEG surcharge design raises industrial cost." These are correctable policy-design choices, not inherent features of decarbonisation. The finding, taken honestly, points toward grid-design reform, not policy-retreat.

6. **Climate-welfare benefits are entirely outside the measurement frame.** The hypothesis measures industrial electricity prices and manufacturing VA. It does not measure avoided-warming damages, air-quality improvements from coal-retirement, or the option value of a decarbonised grid at the margin where climate-tipping becomes a real risk. A hypothesis that finds "Energiewende raised industrial costs" says nothing about the full welfare calculus — and advocates of the policy would point to outcomes the hypothesis is designed not to measure.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report the 2005-2020 window separately from the 2021-2024 war window; report both clearly, do not headline the pooled estimate.
- Interact the price divergence with grid-design complements (baseload share, interconnection, wholesale market structure) — the "good grid + green" reference cases (SWE, NOR, FIN) should damp the price effect.
- Report manufacturing EMPLOYMENT and EXPORTS alongside VA; if the VA effect is real but employment is preserved, that is a different story than industrial collapse.
- Disaggregate by energy-intensity: chemicals, basic metals, cement. If the effect concentrates there, the policy-cost channel is credible; if it is diffuse across manufacturing, other causes are doing the work.
- Acknowledge the climate-welfare omission explicitly in the writeup. The hypothesis tests a narrow economic question, not the full policy merit.
- Be cautious about headline magnitude. Even under the favourable specification, the attributable share of the total industrial-price gap that lives with "Energiewende policy-content choices" (as opposed to the gas shock + secular trends + Germany-specific Russia exposure) is plausibly 30-50%, not 100%.
