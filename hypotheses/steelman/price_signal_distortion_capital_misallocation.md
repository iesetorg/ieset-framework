# Steelman — Price-Signal Distortion and Capital Misallocation

## Strongest version of the claim

When governments impose price controls, directed input subsidies, and credit allocation mandates, they sever the link between relative prices and marginal products. Capital then flows not to the highest-return uses but to politically favoured sectors, state-connected firms, or regions with subsidised inputs. The result is a widening dispersion of the marginal product of capital (MPK) across firms and sectors—capital misallocation—which drags down aggregate total-factor-productivity growth. The claim is that a one-standard-deviation increase in a composite price-distortion index is associated with a significant increase in MPK dispersion and a significant reduction in annual TFP growth, even after controlling for financial development and state capacity.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Sign consistency:** The panel-FE coefficient on price-distortion index is positive and significant for MPK dispersion, and negative and significant for TFP growth.
2. **Magnitude:** A one-SD increase in price distortion is associated with at least a 2.5–5.0% increase in the standard deviation of log-MPK and at least a 0.2pp reduction in annual TFP growth.
3. **Natural-experiment validation:** Transition-economy episodes (China 1978–1992, Vietnam Doi Moi, ex-USSR 1991–1995) show that price liberalisation reduces MPK dispersion and raises TFP growth.
4. **Alternative misallocation measure:** The result holds using sectoral labour-productivity dispersion (coefficient of variation of value added per worker) when firm-level MPK is unavailable.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Successful directed credit:** Korean directed credit in the 1970s–80s and some Green Revolution input subsidies achieved rapid productivity growth despite price distortions, suggesting that context and implementation quality matter.
2. **Infant-industry argument:** Temporary price controls and subsidies may correct coordination failures or externalities in early-stage industries; the observed misallocation may be a short-term investment in long-term capability.
3. **Measurement scarcity:** Firm-level MPK dispersion data is scarce and patchy; primary sources are cross-sectional WB Enterprise Surveys, making panel identification weak.
4. **State-capacity confound:** Price controls are more common in low state-capacity countries; the misallocation may reflect weak public administration rather than price distortion per se.
5. **Second-best logic:** In economies with multiple distortions, removing one price control may worsen allocation if others remain; the partial-correlation approach may misstate the welfare effect of comprehensive reform.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to **developing and emerging-market economies** over 1980–2020 where price controls and directed subsidies are prevalent. The immediate post-socialist transition window (1990–1994) is excluded because ongoing liberalisation confounds the treatment variable. The claim is weakest for **frontier economies** where price controls are rare and for **small island states** where market power justifies some regulation.
