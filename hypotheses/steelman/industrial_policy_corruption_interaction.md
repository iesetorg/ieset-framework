# Steelman: Industrial Policy Corruption Interaction

## Strongest version of the claim
Industrial policy is not inherently good or bad; its effectiveness is critically dependent on governance quality. In high-rule-of-law environments (Northern Europe, East Asia), state allocation can correct market failures, coordinate investment, and generate positive externalities. In low-rule-of-law environments (much of Africa, Latin America, post-Soviet states), the same tools become vehicles for corruption, patronage, and rent-seeking that reduce long-run growth. The interaction is therefore negative: industrial policy intensity multiplied by weak institutions predicts worse outcomes than either factor alone.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Cross-country panel showing positive industrial-policy coefficients in high-WGI subsamples and negative coefficients in low-WGI subsamples.
2. Corruption indicators (V-Dem, WGI, Transparency International) rising with industrial-policy spending in low-rule-of-law countries.
3. Case-study evidence of successful industrial policy in high-governance contexts (Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) versus corrupt failures (Zaire, Venezuela, pre-reform India).
4. Firm-level data showing political connections determining subsidy receipt in low-governance countries versus performance criteria in high-governance countries.

## Best counterarguments
- Governance quality is endogenous to industrial policy: successful industrial policy builds state capacity ("state-in-society" feedback), making the interaction recursive rather than conditional.
- Low-rule-of-law countries may need industrial policy precisely because markets fail most severely; abandoning it leaves them with neither state nor market coordination.
- Measurement error in governance indices may create spurious interaction effects; objective performance metrics are needed.
- Some low-governance states (Ethiopia, Rwanda) have achieved rapid growth with state-led models, challenging the governance-interaction claim.
- The hypothesis may be tautological: "industrial policy works where institutions are good" is true by definition if institutions are defined broadly.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest when comparing middle-income countries across the governance distribution. Weak at extremes (failed states where no policy works, and Scandinavian states where most policies work).
