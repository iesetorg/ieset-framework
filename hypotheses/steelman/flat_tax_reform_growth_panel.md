# Steelman: flat_tax_reform_growth_panel

## Counter-argument

Flat-tax reforms in Eastern Europe and Central Asia coincided with post-Soviet
convergence, EU accession preparation, and large-scale institutional overhaul.
The observed growth accelerations may reflect these confounding factors rather
than the tax structure itself. Many flat-tax adopters also experienced rising
inequality and flat or declining tax revenues as shares of GDP, suggesting a
redistribution-to-efficiency trade-off rather than a pure efficiency gain.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Confounding by convergence**: Most flat-tax adopters were low-income
   transition economies with large catch-up potential. Their faster growth
   may reflect conditional convergence independent of tax policy.

2. **EU accession channel**: Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Slovakia, Czech
   Republic, Hungary, Bulgaria, and Romania all adopted flat taxes during
   EU accession preparation. The accession process itself drove regulatory
   and institutional improvements that boosted growth.

3. **Revenue loss**: Flat taxes often reduced revenue-to-GDP ratios, forcing
   spending cuts or deficit increases. If growth occurred, it may have been
   via austerity rather than supply-side incentive effects.

4. **Reverse causality**: Governments with strong growth momentum may have
   had the fiscal space to cut taxes, meaning growth caused the reform rather
   than vice versa.

## What would flip the verdict

- A null or negative coefficient on flat-tax adoption after controlling for
  EU accession candidacy status and transition-economy convergence.
- Synthetic-control estimates showing no meaningful growth gap for the
  majority of adopters.
- Evidence that flat-tax adoption was followed by revenue collapses and
  subsequent spending cuts that explain any growth differential.
