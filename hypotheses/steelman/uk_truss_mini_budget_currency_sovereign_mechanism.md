# Steelman — Truss 2022 mini-budget as institutional-rupture, not hard fiscal limit (MMT mechanism reading)

**The strongest argument against the MMT institutional-rupture reading:**

The September 2022 gilt crisis is not best read as "institutional
rupture that the BoE resolved by intervening as the issuer's bank."
The cleaner reading is that markets repriced UK fiscal credibility
when an unfunded GBP 45bn package landed without an Office for Budget
Responsibility costing, and the BoE intervention was a financial-
stability action narrowly targeted at LDI margin cascades, not a
demonstration that the sovereign faces no hard resource constraint.
The institutional-vs-resource-constraint distinction the MMT framing
draws is in this case partially semantic: GBP fell sharply, gilt
yields spiked sharply, and both moves persisted (in level terms)
even after the BoE intervention reversed the panic component. That
persistence is exactly what a fiscal-credibility repricing model
predicts, and is hard to attribute to an "institutional framework
rupture" that BoE action then closed.

Specific objections this hypothesis must engage with:

1. **The persistent post-intervention level matters more than the
   panic-spike retrace.** Even if the BoE retrace is sharp (which it
   was), the UK 10y gilt closing yield through October 2022 settled
   well above the 22-Sep pre-anchor; GBP/USD did not durably regain
   pre-announcement levels until the Truss-Hunt-Sunak political
   reversal made the fiscal package effectively dead. This is what a
   hard-budget-constraint repricing looks like.

2. **The BoE was explicit that the 28-Sep facility was a financial-
   stability operation, not a sovereign-debt-management one.** The
   Bank insisted the facility was time-limited and aimed at LDI
   collateral cascades, not at compressing the sovereign risk
   premium. Reading the success of the facility as proof of "BoE-as-
   issuer-of-sterling resolves any hard limit" risks confusing
   liquidity-of-last-resort with monetisation.

3. **A hard-fiscal-limit framing is consistent with the political
   resolution.** The crisis ended when Truss reversed policy and
   resigned — not when the BoE stopped buying gilts. If the
   institutional-rupture-not-resource-constraint mechanism were
   dominant, the political reversal would not have been necessary;
   the BoE intervention alone should have been sufficient.

4. **The cross-currency check.** GBP fell against EUR as well as USD
   in the same window — not just against USD on global-rate moves.
   That is a UK-specific repricing, consistent with a fiscal-
   credibility (not pure global-rate) story.

**What would shift weight back to the MMT framing:**

A finding that the BoE intervention causes a sharp, durable retrace
of the UK-US 10y excess gilt-yield spike to within ~25% of its
pre-announcement level inside one trading week — i.e. the
intervention substantively normalises the sovereign-yield channel
and does not just calm the panic spike. The dispositive primary in
this hypothesis is set at the ≥50% retrace level on exactly that
logic; if the retrace clears 50%, the institutional-channel evidence
is real. If it doesn't clear 50% — or if the residual spike persists
through the political reversal — the resource-constraint reading is
the better fit.
