# Steelman — Solar LCOE 2010-2024 learning-curve continuation

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "learning curve held through inflation shock" framing:**

1. **Most of the post-2020 cost recovery is Chinese overcapacity dumping, not learning.** The 2022-2024 module-price decline reflects substantial Chinese polysilicon + wafer + cell + module overcapacity (China's polysilicon capacity quintupled 2020-2024). Selling below cost during a capacity glut is not "learning"; it is industrial-policy-driven oversupply that may not be sustained. If Chinese capacity rationalises 2025-2027, module prices will rise toward production cost regardless of cumulative-capacity learning. The headline learning-rate continuation conflates true cost-of-production decline with margin compression that is not durable.

2. **Module $/W is increasingly the wrong outcome — system $/W matters.** Modules are now ~25% of utility-scale solar system cost (down from 50%+ in 2010); BOS, inverters, structural mounting, electrical, labour, permitting + interconnection have grown to ~75% of system cost in OECD markets. If module learning continues but BOS flatlines or rises, system-level LCOE will plateau even as the headline module learning rate persists. The hypothesis's primary outcome (module $/W) measures a shrinking share of total cost.

3. **Permitting + interconnection delays are the binding LCOE constraint in 2024+.** Utility-scale solar interconnection queues in US PJM, MISO, ERCOT have multi-year wait times; permitting timelines lengthened post-2020. The "soft cost" share of LCOE includes time-cost-of-money during these delays. A learning-curve test on $/W cost ignores the time-cost channel; even if cost-per-watt-installed continues falling, time-to-energise has risen, and revenue NPV is lower.

4. **Trade defences distort the global learning-curve fit.** US Section 201 tariffs (2018-2026), Section 301 tariffs on Chinese modules, and post-IRA domestic-content requirements created a US-domestic module market priced 30-60% above world spot through 2022-2024. The "global" learning curve fit on world spot understates US-actual costs; a US-specific learning curve would show much weaker 2020-2024 progress. The hypothesis's "GLOBAL" scope obscures regional heterogeneity that matters for policy-relevant LCOE.

5. **Capacity-factor improvements (bifacial, tracker) are doing some of the LCOE work that gets attributed to module learning.** A 2024 bifacial-tracker system delivers ~25% higher AC energy yield per kW-DC than a 2010 fixed-tilt mono system; this raises capacity factor + lowers $/MWh independent of module $/W. The LCOE learning rate compounds module learning + bifacial premium + tracker uptake; decomposing them would show module-only learning is a smaller share of the total LCOE decline than the headline "learning curve held" suggests.

6. **The 2022 polysilicon spike WAS a structural break, even if it reverted.** From 2020-2022 polysilicon spot rose 5x ($7/kg to $35/kg); module $/W rose ~30%; reverted to baseline by 2024. Coding 2010-2024 as a single learning regime smooths over a real disruption that, in another world (no Chinese polysilicon overinvestment 2020-2023), might have produced a sustained higher cost level. The "no structural break" finding is partly fortunate-counterfactual: a different world would have given a different test result.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Decompose module $/W from BOS/system $/W. The headline learning-rate-held conclusion lives in module; system is more mixed.
- Report per-region learning curves (CHN, GLOBAL ex-US, US, EU). The US trajectory is materially different from global spot.
- Net out capacity-factor improvements when interpreting LCOE learning. The decomposition matters for understanding what is doing the work.
- Treat the 2022 polysilicon spike as a sensitivity case — re-run the learning-rate test with 2020-2022 excluded.
- Discuss Chinese overcapacity / dumping as a confounder. A "learning curve continued" conclusion that depends on persistent dumping is fragile.
- Permitting + interconnection time costs are real and rising; the cost-per-watt outcome misses this. Acknowledge as out-of-frame.
- Climate-policy relevance (cheaper solar means cheaper decarbonisation) is downstream and out-of-frame for this empirical test.
