# Steelman — M2 velocity stability and the 2008 break

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is that pre-2008 M2 velocity was never as stable as Friedman and Schwartz claimed; the apparent stability is a consequence of choosing M2 (rather than M1, MZM, or Divisia) and a particular sample window (post-WWII US) that excludes the 1930s collapse and the 1970s-1980s velocity drift. Goodfriend and McCallum and others documented velocity instability long before 2008. Treating 2008Q3 as a clean structural break elevates a continuation of pre-existing drift to the status of a regime change. A more honest reading of the data is that velocity has trended downward whenever interest rates have fallen and inflation has stabilised, and that the post-2008 decline is the cumulative effect of two decades of falling nominal rates plus the introduction of interest on reserves, not a discontinuity. If that is right, the descriptive claim that "velocity was stable then broke" is the wrong frame; the right frame is that velocity is a money-demand function and the function shifted continuously. From the heterodox / endogenous-money side, the entire concept of velocity is an accounting artefact — M2 demand is determined by transactions and portfolio motives that have nothing to do with the central bank, so observing that "velocity broke" tells you nothing about monetary policy and everything about depositor behaviour during the GFC. The hypothesis's pre-registered Bai-Perron test on the pre-2008 sub-series is therefore the load-bearing element: if the pre-2008 series fails the no-break null, the post-2008 break is not a regime change but a continuation, and the entire monetarist self-rescue narrative collapses. The hypothesis would also be sharper if it required the break to coincide with the IOR introduction (2008Q4) at quarterly precision rather than within a +/- 4 quarter window, since the wider window admits a Lehman-shock interpretation that is consistent with endogenous-money rather than money-demand accounts.
