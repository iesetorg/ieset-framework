# Steelman — Diaspora Return After Market and Property-Rights Reform

## Strongest version of the claim

Diaspora communities monitor institutional quality in their origin countries and return when credible reforms improve market opportunity and property-rights security. Return migration is not merely a response to income convergence; it is a signal that the institutional environment has become predictable enough to justify sinking costs into businesses, homes, and careers. Sustained improvement in EFW and WGI rule-of-law indices over 5+ years predicts measurable increases in return-migration rates, controlling for GDP growth and political stability.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Event-study or staggered-DiD** around reform episodes showing post-reform increases in return-migration rates relative to synthetic controls or non-reform peers.
2. **Return-migrant survey data** documenting that institutional improvement (not just wages) was a primary return motive.
3. **Heterogeneity**: returns should be stronger among entrepreneurs and investors than among pensioners or family-reunion migrants.
4. **Robustness** to alternative reform measures (Heritage, V-Dem, Doing Business) and migration data sources.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Income drives return**. Return migrants primarily respond to wage convergence or booming sectors, not abstract institutional quality.
2. **Network effects**. Return is facilitated by existing diaspora networks that lower job-search and settlement costs; institutional quality is secondary.
3. **Selection bias**. Return migrants are often those who failed in the destination country, not a positive selection on institutional optimism.
4. **EU accession confounds**. Most post-communist reform episodes coincided with EU accession, which granted free mobility and cohesion funds; disentangling market reform from EU effects is difficult.
5. **Reversibility risk**. Diaspora members may wait for 10-15 years of sustained reform before returning; a 5-year window may be too short to detect the response.

## Boundary conditions

- Best tested in **post-communist and post-authoritarian transitions** where reform was discrete and observable.
- Less informative for **gradual reformers** where institutional change is slow and continuous.
- Excludes **forced-return migration** (deportation, refugee repatriation) which is institutionally exogenous.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with brain-drain, net-migration, and institutional-reform hypotheses. Tensions with claims that income differentials or network effects alone explain return flows.
