# Steelman — China WTO accession 2001 export surge

## The strongest opposing argument

A naive reading credits WTO accession alone with the post-2001
Chinese export takeoff. The genuinely hard objections are:

1. **WTO accession is the proximate marker, not the deep cause.**
   By 2001, China had two decades of capability accumulation under
   Deng-era reforms, special economic zones, FDI joint-venture
   technology absorption, and an explicit export-promotion
   industrial policy. The WTO accession date is when the formal
   tariff-and-quota regime was bound, but the manufacturing
   capability that produced the surge was already there. Wade
   (2003) and Chang (2002) document this prior capability
   accumulation extensively.

2. **The managed exchange rate did much of the work.** From 1994
   through 2005 the renminbi was pegged to the dollar at a level
   widely regarded as undervalued. Bergsten and others estimated
   20-40% undervaluation through the mid-2000s. A WTO accession
   under a freely-floating currency would have produced a much
   smaller export surge.

3. **MFN was already de facto granted year-by-year.** The US had
   extended annual MFN renewals to China through the 1990s; the
   WTO accession permanently removed the political risk of
   non-renewal but did not introduce dramatically lower tariffs
   on day one. The "tariff shock" interpretation overstates the
   discontinuity.

## What the framework still captures

The synthetic-control test is agnostic about deep causes — it asks
whether China's post-2001 trajectory diverged from a counterfactual
constructed from comparable emerging economies. Even granting that
prior capability and the managed exchange rate did much of the
work, the post-2001 acceleration is a real and large pattern that
a working framework must detect. The PARTIAL verdict path
naturally accommodates the interpretation that WTO accession is
the marker rather than the deep cause; the magnitude is what the
framework records.
