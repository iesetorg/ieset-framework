# Steelman — Lula 3 NIB / industrial policy 2023-2026

**The strongest argument FOR the causal claim (steelmanned against the author's skeptical prior):**

The author's prior is on the industrial-policy-skeptical side. The strongest case for NIB's
effectiveness — which the v1 spec must take seriously — is that the 2024 mission-orientation
design corrects the specific flaws of older Brazilian industrial policy (PND-II 1974,
Plano Brasil Maior 2011) that produced poor measured outcomes.

Specific points the hypothesis must engage from the pro-NIB side:

1. **Mission-orientation is structurally different from sectoral picking-winners.** The Mazzucato-
   advised 2024 design picks problems (decarbonisation, health-complex import substitution,
   digital transformation) rather than firms or sectors. This addresses the classical
   Hayek-Stigler critique of industrial policy (information problems in picking winners) by
   defining outcomes rather than recipients. A skeptical hypothesis that fails to find a
   sectoral divergence may be testing the wrong unit of analysis: the right unit is mission-
   completion outcomes, not 4-digit CNAE sector value-added.

2. **BNDES credit cost-of-capital effect is real and large.** TJLP/TLP-rate BNDES on-lending
   to NIB-targeted firms typically runs 300-500 bp below market rates. In a high-Selic
   environment (12-15%) this is a 25-40% reduction in financing cost for affected firms. The
   hypothesis should detect this in sectoral investment shares even if value-added effects
   take longer to materialise. A null on investment share would be more decisive than a null
   on value-added in 2026.

3. **Procurement-preference channel is not modelled.** NIB includes 8% domestic-preference
   margin in federal procurement for NIB-targeted product categories. This is a demand-side
   intervention with relatively quick pass-through. Hypothesis spec uses sectoral value-added
   which would capture both supply and demand effects, but a separate procurement-flow test
   would be more direct.

4. **Health-complex import-substitution has explicit COVID-era political wind.** Brazil's
   pharmaceutical and medical-device imports were exposed during COVID; political support
   for the NIB health mission is high and bipartisan. The political-durability of the
   policy may be sufficient to actually drive the multi-year capex commitments that older
   Brazilian industrial policy lacked. The hypothesis's pre-2024 placebo window may
   underestimate post-2024 sustainability.

5. **2-pp annualised threshold is high.** The author's threshold (2 pp annualised log-VA
   divergence) is higher than typical industrial-policy effect sizes in the empirical
   literature (Kalouptsidi 2018, Juhasz 2018 find effects in 0.5-1.5 pp range). A "refuted"
   verdict on the 2-pp threshold may simply mean the policy has effects in the literature-
   typical range but not at the author's high threshold. The mixed-verdict band (1-2 pp)
   should be the most likely outcome.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report sectoral investment-share divergence as a separate, possibly-leading metric. A
    "supported on investment-share, mixed on value-added" verdict is consistent with normal
    industrial-policy lag structure.

(b) Add a procurement-flow channel test (federal procurement to NIB-targeted vs control
    sectors) as a more direct demand-side test. Source: tesouro_nacional procurement data
    if accessible.

(c) Calibrate the threshold against the empirical literature: the 2-pp threshold is
    aggressive. Report the 0.5-pp, 1-pp, 1.5-pp, 2-pp variants of the test so readers can
    locate the result in the literature distribution.

(d) Track BNDES on-lending volume to NIB-targeted vs control sectors as a treatment-intensity
    measure, not just the binary post-2024 indicator. Heterogeneous-treatment effects across
    BNDES-credit-receiving subsets within the NIB-targeted group would strengthen any positive
    finding.

(e) Accept that the most likely outcome is a "mixed" or "marginal-support" verdict and that
    the policy takeaway is "mission-oriented industrial policy in middle-income contexts
    produces effects in the 0.5-1.5 pp range — neither the catastrophic-failure nor the
    Korean-miracle outcome."
