# Steelman: Great Depression over-accumulation vs monetary cause

The strongest skeptical reading is that the Great Depression was primarily a monetary and financial-collapse event. The sharp fall in money, nominal GDP, banking stability, and debt-service capacity from 1929 to 1933 can explain the scale and speed of the output collapse without requiring a prior over-accumulation crisis. A Shiller earnings-yield proxy may also mix valuation, expected earnings, and risk-premium changes rather than measuring the economy-wide profit rate. If direct profit-rate and capital-output series do not show a clear deterioration before 1929, then the over-accumulation story risks becoming a post-hoc narrative attached to a monetary shock.

The strongest pro-over-accumulation version is that financial collapse exposed a deeper 1920s imbalance rather than causing it from nothing. Equity-market earnings yields, investment composition, and capacity indicators may show profitability and accumulation stress before the Fed/banking channel became catastrophic. The current local wrapper is useful because it forces that narrative to make contact with measurable pre-1929 indicators, but direct historical profit-rate and capital-output data are needed before the hypothesis can be treated as more than partial.
