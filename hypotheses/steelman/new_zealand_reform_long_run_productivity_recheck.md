# Steelman — New Zealand reform long-run productivity recheck

## 1. Strongest version of the claim

New Zealand’s reforms successfully ended a sclerotic protectionist era, stabilised macroeconomic imbalances, and reoriented the economy toward tradables. Aggregate productivity was held back by structural headwinds — extreme distance from markets, small domestic scale, and commodity dependence — rather than by reform failure. The correct counterfactual is not Australia or Canada, but a continued protectionist New Zealand that would have performed even worse.

## 2. Key evidence the claim would need

- A synthetic-control gap showing that post-1984 New Zealand experienced lower inflation volatility and higher trade openness relative to peer small open economies.
- Aggregate labour-productivity and TFP gaps that are either insignificant or modestly positive, but not large enough to vindicate strong market-optimist claims.
- Robustness to donor-pool composition (e.g., excluding Australia, which also liberalised).

## 3. Best counterarguments

1. **Australia liberalised too.** Australia enacted comparable reforms (Hawke-Keating) yet achieved stronger aggregate productivity growth, suggesting that New Zealand’s reform design or implementation was flawed, not that distance nullified gains.
2. **The distance penalty is constant.** If geography is the binding constraint, reforms should still raise productivity relative to a counterfactual New Zealand with the same geography. The absence of a large aggregate gap implies reforms did less than claimed.
3. **Measurement issues.** New Zealand’s productivity data may understate quality improvements in services and agriculture. If so, the skeptical limb rests on a measurement artifact.
4. **Migration-driven dilution.** Post-2000 population growth was heavily migration-driven, which can dilute per-capita GDP and productivity measures even if total output grew.
5. **Financial deregulation contributed to a housing boom.** The post-1984 financial liberalisation fuelled a sustained increase in household debt and house prices, redirecting investment from tradables to non-tradables and undermining the tradables-productivity channel.

## 4. Boundary conditions

The claim is most relevant for small, remote, commodity-dependent economies. It is not a generic test of market reform; the skeptical limb may be driven by idiosyncratic structural constraints rather than by the failure of liberalisation per se.

## 5. Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is an explicitly skeptical counterpart to strong market-reform claims. It mirrors `australia_hawke_keating_reform_long_run` but with an adversarial framing that accepts tradables gains while withholding judgment on aggregate productivity. It is also related to `market_reform_duration_growth_persistence` by testing whether reform effects decay in small open economies.
