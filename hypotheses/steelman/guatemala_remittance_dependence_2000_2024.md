# Steelman — Guatemala remittance dependence 2000-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis treats remittance dependence as a partial substitute that does not produce productive-investment convergence. The opposing view is that remittances do finance human-capital investment (education, health, small business) at the household level, and that the "incomplete development" frame understates the developmental contribution.

Specific objections:

1. **Household-level investment from remittances.** Survey evidence (BANGUAT and microfinance studies) shows a non-trivial share of remittance income funds education and small-business formation; treating remittances as pure consumption smoothing understates the development channel.

2. **Macro stability is real.** Guatemala has avoided the inflation, currency, and debt crises that hit several Central American peers. Remittance-buffered macro is genuinely stable, even if growth is moderate.

3. **Migration is a labour-market option, not a development failure.** Households exercise migration as an income-diversification strategy; framing this as a "failure to develop domestically" embeds a normative judgement.

4. **Indigenous and rural exclusion is the binding constraint.** Guatemala's productivity gap is concentrated in the rural Mayan economy; remittances may be the only realistic income channel for this population without large-scale state investment that has not been forthcoming.

5. **Capital-formation share understates infrastructure investment.** Remittance-funded housing improvements appear in residential investment but not always in standardised capital-formation metrics.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Decompose remittance use into consumption / investment / human-capital channels where data permit.
(b) Note macro-stability as a positive even if growth is modest.
(c) Reframe finding as "stable but slow convergence" rather than failure.
(d) Acknowledge the rural-indigenous exclusion as a separate but related issue.
