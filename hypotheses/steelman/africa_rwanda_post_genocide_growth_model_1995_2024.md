# Steelman — Rwanda post-genocide growth 1995-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Post-conflict catch-up dynamics explain a large share of the growth premium.** Rwanda's 1994 GDP collapsed by ~50%; mechanical recovery toward the pre-genocide trend would deliver large growth numbers regardless of policy quality. The hypothesis's "premium vs synthetic" must isolate genuine regime-specific gains from base-effect catch-up. Mozambique's post-civil-war recovery (1992+) shows similar early gains; Ethiopia's post-Derg recovery shows similar gains. Rwanda is on the high end but the band is broad.

2. **Rwanda's GDP statistics are state-managed and have been challenged.** External academic work (Andrew Mwenda; Filip Reyntjens; Susan Thomson) has questioned the official Rwandan growth statistics, especially poverty headcount data. NISR is widely viewed as politically constrained. The hypothesis treats the WDI series as ground truth; a steelman must acknowledge that Rwandan GDP-pc may be measured with politically-induced upward bias.

3. **Aid-financing share is exceptionally high.** Rwanda's ODA-to-GNI ratio averaged 15-20% over much of the post-1995 window; donor support partly underwrote the reconstruction. The "RPF developmental success" framing under-credits the external financing channel. Counterfactual peers without comparable aid intensity may simply have been resource-constrained.

4. **The civil-liberties regression should be priced as a welfare cost.** V-Dem and Freedom House show Rwanda's polity moving more authoritarian over 2010-2024 (RPP electoral dominance, opposition repression, exiled journalists, suspected extraterritorial assassinations). The framework should not treat the GDP-vs-civil-liberties tradeoff as a wash; it requires explicit utility-aggregation that the hypothesis sidesteps.

5. **Geopolitical adventurism and DRC mineral extraction is an under-reported income source.** UN panel-of-experts reports have repeatedly documented Rwandan involvement in eastern DRC mineral economies (coltan, gold). To the extent measured Rwandan GDP includes proceeds from extraterritorial mineral rents, the developmental-state framing is partially mis-classified.

6. **Vision 2020 / Vision 2050 targets have been missed.** Middle-income status by 2020 was missed; export targets missed; the structural-transformation-from-agriculture goal partially achieved. The "developmental success" framing should account for missed self-imposed benchmarks, not just SSA-peer comparisons.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Run a robustness with measured-statistics-uncertainty bands (NISR ± 20%).
- Report ODA share decomposition to estimate aid-channel contribution.
- Report the civil-liberties regression alongside the economic gain.
- Acknowledge the DRC mineral-economy externality even if not directly tested.
- Frame Rwanda as "high-performing autocratic developmentalist case with material trade-offs", not unalloyed success.
