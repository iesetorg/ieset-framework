# Steelman — Iceland 2008 canonical case

## The strongest opposing argument

Iceland 2008 is so extreme it is almost unfalsifiable: any reasonable threshold the framework writes down will be cleared by a banking system that grew to ~700% of GDP and depreciated 50%+ inside twelve months. Treating it as a canonical case risks overweighting one tail event in the cross-country comparison; Iceland's smallness, foreign-currency wholesale-funding model, and absence of an effective lender-of-last-resort are sui generis.

The capital-controls and creditor-bail-in path Iceland took (Emergency Act October 2008, ring-fencing domestic deposits) was non-standard and arguably softened domestic outcomes relative to what a Cyprus-style or Greek-style alternative would have produced; counting "real GDP fell 8%" as a metric does not isolate the part of the response attributable to the crisis vs the policy response to it.

## What the framework still captures

Pre-committing to specific thresholds (assets/GDP >= 700%, krona depreciation >= 50%) on a near-certain case calibrates whether the framework's threshold-setting is too loose or too tight elsewhere. Iceland is a deliberate "easy case"; if the spec requires walking back a metric to register support, that is a flag the threshold was too aggressive.
