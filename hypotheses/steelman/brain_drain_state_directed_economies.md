# Steelman — Brain Drain from State-Directed Economies

## Strongest version of the claim

Skilled workers vote with their feet. In state-directed economies with weak market opportunity—high barriers to entry, insecure property rights, and limited private-sector career ladders—tertiary-educated emigration rates are systematically higher than in market-liberal peers at similar income levels, even when the state invests heavily in education and research. Public investment in human capital becomes a subsidy to recipient countries when the institutional environment fails to retain the talent it produces.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing negative association between market-opportunity indices (EFW business freedom, entry barriers, property rights) and skilled emigration rates, after income and public R&D controls.
2. **Microdata** from emigrant surveys (Gallup, OECD talent mobility data) documenting motive: career limitation, lack of meritocracy, and business-startup barriers as push factors.
3. **Event studies** of reform episodes where skilled emigration decelerates after market-liberalisation or property-rights upgrades.
4. **Sectoral heterogeneity**: the effect should be strongest in STEM and business fields where private opportunity is most important.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Income dominates**. Skilled emigration is primarily wage-driven; controlling for income may absorb most of the institutional effect.
2. **Non-monetary motives**. Family ties, language, culture, and patriotism retain skilled workers even in poor institutional environments.
3. **State-directed economies create domestic opportunities**. Public R&D, state-owned enterprises, and government careers may satisfy skilled workers without private markets.
4. **Reverse causation**. High emigration may weaken domestic institutions by depleting the reformist middle class, rather than weak institutions causing emigration.
5. **Data quality**. Skilled-emigration rates are estimated from census stock data with imputed education levels; measurement error is substantial for developing countries.

## Boundary conditions

- Most informative for **middle-income countries** where exit options are financially feasible but institutional variation is large.
- Less informative for **low-income countries** where even weak institutions may be better than alternatives, or for **high-income autocracies** (e.g., Gulf states) that use restrictive exit policies.
- Applies to **voluntary emigration**; politically motivated exile is a different phenomenon.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to diaspora-return, net-migration revealed-preference, and institutional-quality hypotheses. Tensions with claims that public R&D and education spending alone can retain talent.
