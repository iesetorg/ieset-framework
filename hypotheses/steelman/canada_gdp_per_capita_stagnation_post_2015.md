# Steelman — Canada GDP per capita stagnation post-2015

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "Canadian per-capita stagnation under Trudeau" framing:**

1. **The "decline under Trudeau" framing is partly an optical illusion driven by nominal-USD accounting and CAD depreciation.** Much of the popular commentary that Canada "fell behind" uses GDP per capita measured in nominal US dollars, which is mechanically dragged down by CAD depreciation from ~0.96 USD/CAD in 2011 to ~0.72 in 2023. The framework hypothesis uses PPP-constant-international-dollars precisely to remove this confound, but commentators who don't make that adjustment overstate the decline. On PPP real per capita, the gap with the US widened but Canada did not contract — it stagnated.

2. **Population denominator growth is the biggest single driver and it's a policy-deliberate choice, not a policy failure.** Canadian population grew ~15% 2015-2023, driven by a permanent-residents admission target that almost doubled and a large temporary-resident buildup. Faster denominator growth mechanically compresses per-capita figures even when total output continues to expand. A policy that deliberately doubles immigration is TRADING real output growth for per-capita level; whether that trade was worth it depends on downstream outcomes (fiscal solvency of aging demographics, labour-force participation, housing adequacy) that are separate from GDP-per-capita arithmetic. Calling the per-capita trajectory "stagnation" without acknowledging it is the mechanical consequence of a deliberate and defensible population-expansion choice is intellectually incomplete.

3. **The productivity gap with the US is mostly a US-specific phenomenon, not a Canada-specific failure.** The same Bank of Canada Senior Deputy Governor Rogers speech that generated the "it's an emergency" soundbite explicitly said US productivity growth since 2019 has been exceptional and "not normal" by historical standards. Most advanced economies — France, Germany, UK, Japan — have also fallen behind US productivity 2015-2023. If everyone is behind the US, calling this a Canada-specific policy failure requires a fairer donor pool and a counterfactual that isolates Canada-specific structural problems (low business investment, interprovincial trade barriers, internal labour mobility) from the general advanced-economy vs US divergence.

4. **The Trudeau-era time window coincides with a commodity-super-cycle reversal that hit Canada mechanically.** Oil prices peaked 2014 and collapsed 2014-2016 (Brent from ~$110 to ~$40), coinciding with the 2015 election. Canada's terms of trade deteriorated sharply for mechanical commodity-market reasons, not policy reasons. Any Canadian government in 2015 would have inherited that shock. Attributing the early-Trudeau-period per-capita weakness to Trudeau-era policy content is a post hoc that the framework's TWFE with year fixed effects partly handles but does not fully eliminate (year FE capture common shocks, not Canada-specific commodity exposure). The framework includes a terms-of-trade control precisely for this reason; honest coefficient interpretation must condition on it.

5. **The comparator donor pool is partly anglophone-coincidence rather than economic similarity.** USA, AUS, NZL, GBR are anglophone but their growth models are quite different: US is tech-and-finance-deep, AUS is hard-commodity-specialised, NZL is agriculture-specialised, GBR is services-and-finance-centric. NOR and CHE are small-population high-SWF / high-financial-sector economies. Canada is uniquely a resource-plus-services federation with high provincial heterogeneity. A tighter comparator pool (AUS alone, perhaps) might tell a different story than a broad pool that mixes AUS, NZL, USA, NOR, CHE.

6. **The causal attribution to specific policy content is weak.** Even if Canadian per-capita trajectory is demonstrably worse than the donor pool, the framework is claiming it is "the 2015-present policy mix" that did it. But: (a) the carbon-pricing backstop's drag on output is mechanically small in cross-country comparisons; (b) the 2024 capital-gains inclusion rate increase is too recent to appear in the 2015-2023 sample; (c) the CCB and CPP enhancement are transfer policies with uncertain growth effects (plausibly positive via demand, plausibly negative via disincentive, empirically near-zero in most studies); (d) the immigration expansion mechanically dilutes per-capita but contributes to aggregate growth. Attributing a specific magnitude of per-capita stagnation to Trudeau-era policy content as opposed to provincial-level housing and planning restrictions, interprovincial-trade friction, or long-run declining business investment (problems that predate 2015) is a larger claim than a movement-level TWFE can support.

7. **The counterfactual is not "same population with different policies"; it's "same country with different possibilities".** If the claim is that a different Canadian government 2015-2023 would have delivered materially better per-capita trajectory, that counterfactual has to be articulated. The plausible alternative-policy scenarios (e.g., slower immigration, no carbon tax, different transfer mix) would have produced different aggregate-growth / population-denominator tradeoffs, but the counterfactual per-capita level is not obviously higher. The framework tests "Canada deviated from donor pool"; it does not test "Canada would have done better under different policy", which is the harder and more interesting counterfactual.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report both PPP-real and LCU-real specifications; never use nominal-USD as a headline number.
- Decompose the per-capita gap into (population-denominator-growth × aggregate-real-output-growth) shares; state explicitly how much of the per-capita stagnation is absorption-of-output-by-population-growth.
- Report the terms-of-trade-controlled coefficient separately from the uncontrolled coefficient; commodity-cycle exposure is a first-order confound 2014-2016 and 2022.
- Report donor-pool-sensitivity: AUS-only, USA-only, and all-six-donors, to show how much of the finding depends on comparator choice.
- State explicitly that the test is "Canada deviated from donor pool", not "Trudeau caused Canadian underperformance"; the causal attribution to specific policy content is a v2 decomposition, not a v1 finding.
- Treat the Bank of Canada Rogers "emergency" framing as ONE data point among many; it is a central banker flagging a long-run problem, not a verdict on Trudeau-era policy content.
