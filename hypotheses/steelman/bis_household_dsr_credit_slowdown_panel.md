# Steelman — Household DSR stress predicts credit slowdown

The main challenge is simultaneity. Household DSR rises when interest rates rise, debt stocks rise, or income falls. A later credit slowdown may reflect the same tightening cycle or recessionary shock rather than household cash-flow stress itself. Country and year fixed effects help only with persistent cross-country differences and global shocks, not domestic policy timing.

The stress threshold combines an absolute 12 percent cutoff with a country-specific 75th percentile. That makes the test more comparable across countries, but it also means "stress" is partly relative. A country with structurally low DSR can still enter the high group if it is high for itself, while a country with structurally high household leverage might need a very high reading to qualify.

BIS WS_CREDIT_GAP CG_DTYPE=B is broad private credit/GDP, not household-only credit. If corporate borrowing drives the credit aggregate, a household DSR signal may look weak even when household credit slows. A cleaner test would use household credit stocks directly where available.

If supported, the result is best read as evidence that household cash-flow pressure contains incremental warning information for aggregate credit growth. If refuted, it may reflect aggregate-credit measurement mismatch rather than absence of household stress effects.
