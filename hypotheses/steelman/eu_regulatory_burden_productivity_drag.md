# Steelman — EU regulatory burden and productivity drag

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "EU regulation causes productivity drag" framing:**

1. **EU-US productivity divergence pre-dates the GDPR-era regulatory stack.** US-EU TFP gap has been widening since ~2005, well before GDPR 2018. Attributing the 2018+ divergence specifically to post-2018 regulation requires separating pre-existing trends from post-regulation effects, which the simple two-way FE design does imperfectly. The steelman: most of the "EU productivity drag" visible in the data is explained by factors that predate the regulatory stack — demographic aging, R&D spending ratios, capital-market depth, university research commercialisation — NOT by compliance costs.

2. **US productivity acceleration post-2010 is unique, and the comparison rigs the outcome.** The US has shale revolution + Big Tech concentration + deep capital markets + higher R&D intensity + tax-incentive-heavy + looser antitrust. Attributing US outperformance to "EU over-regulated" reverses the causation — it may be that US-specific features produced extraordinary productivity growth, not that EU features impeded. "EU is slower than US" and "EU regulation slows EU" are different claims.

3. **Regulation has identifiable economic benefits the productivity framing excludes.** GDPR compliance cost is real; GDPR consumer-welfare benefit is also real but not measured in productivity aggregates. AI Act compliance cost is real; avoidance of AI-driven labour market disruption is a policy goal with welfare value not captured by measured TFP. The framework's narrow productivity outcome imports a value judgement (productivity is the right welfare metric) that the steelman disputes — especially on regulations whose stated goal is consumer protection or harm avoidance rather than productivity.

4. **Intra-EU variation is large.** Ireland, Netherlands, Sweden have maintained productivity growth comparable to US on some metrics; Italy, Spain, Portugal have diverged much more. Pooling "EU" into one panel coefficient obscures that the regulatory stack applies uniformly but productivity effects are country-specific — suggesting regulation is not the primary driver. The framework should disaggregate.

5. **Measurement problems in TFP are severe, especially for digital-economy services that much of the regulation targets.** Sectors like digital platforms produce measurement-resistant output (free services, data-as-input, ad-supported). Mismeasurement biases cross-country TFP comparisons in unknown directions. The steelman: "productivity drag" could be mismeasurement artefact.

6. **The Draghi Report's framing is itself contested.** Draghi's claim is mostly about fragmented single market + R&D underinvestment + capital market depth + defence underinvestment + energy costs, with regulation as one of several factors. The framework's "regulatory stack drag" framing over-privileges one of Draghi's list — regulation — relative to the others. A fair reading of Draghi would attribute maybe 20-30% of the productivity gap to regulation, not the dominant share.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report pre-2018 trend on US-EU productivity gap explicitly; the "post-2018" coefficient must be understood relative to an existing trajectory.
- Interact with country-specific characteristics; the pooled EU coefficient is likely less informative than country-level decompositions.
- Report alternative treatment dates (placebo pre-2015) as sensitivity.
- Acknowledge measurement limitations of TFP, especially in digital sectors.
- Frame the magnitude conservatively: regulatory-compliance-cost is one of several factors; the Draghi list's other items (R&D, capital markets, single-market fragmentation, defence spending, energy) deserve parallel hypothesis coverage.
- Avoid the slogan "EU bureaucracy causes degrowth"; the empirical claim is "measurable compliance-cost productivity effect in scale-sensitive sectors" — narrower, more defensible, less politically weaponisable.
