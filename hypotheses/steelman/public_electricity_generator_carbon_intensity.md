# Steelman — Public electricity generators (EDF, Vattenfall) lower-carbon mix vs privatised, 1970-1999

**The strongest arguments FOR the eco-socialist claim that public ownership of electricity generation produced lower-carbon generation mixes than privatised counterparts, taken seriously:**

1. **Public generators internalised long-horizon planning that markets wouldn't.** EDF's nuclear build-out from the Messmer plan (1973) onward required ~30-year payback horizons that private utilities, exposed to fluctuating fuel-price expectations and quarterly equity markets, would not have committed to. The 1970s oil shocks made the economics ambiguous, but state ownership absorbed the discount-rate risk. Vattenfall similarly underwrote large hydro and later nuclear capex on planning horizons that private actors would have priced higher. The lower-carbon mix is partly a consequence of who-bears-the-risk under each ownership form.

2. **Privatisation introduced a fossil-fuel bias via the dash-for-gas in 1990s UK.** Privatised UK generators (after 1990) shifted aggressively into combined-cycle gas turbines because gas plants were faster to permit, cheaper to finance under shorter equity-payback hurdles, and avoided the political risk of nuclear new-build under the new market regime. The post-privatisation generation mix moved fossil-heavy in ways the pre-privatisation CEGB had not. This is at least suggestive that ownership form influenced fuel-mix decisions on the margin.

3. **Public utilities had political mandate to pursue energy security as decarbonisation by-product.** The Messmer plan was explicitly motivated by oil-import dependency, not climate policy, but the consequence was a near-fully-decarbonised French grid by ~1985. Public ownership and the willingness of the French state to commit to energy-security goals via nuclear is a key part of the story. Sweden's hydro+nuclear mix similarly reflects state-led energy security choices that translated into low carbon intensity downstream.

4. **The privatised counterfactual would likely have been more coal/gas.** What did privately owned generation actually look like in this window? US IOUs were heavily coal-dependent through the 1980s-1990s; UK post-privatisation was a dash-for-gas; West German RWE/E.ON-precursors were lignite-heavy. Across the privatised comparator pool, the empirical generation mix is in fact materially more carbon-intensive than FRA/SWE. This is what the data show, and the eco-socialist framing offers a simple causal story: ownership form mediated the choice of fuel.

**The strongest arguments AGAINST (the framework's prior):**

1. **Resource endowment is doing most of the causal work.** Sweden has more hydropower potential per capita than almost any OECD country; France pursued nuclear because it had no domestic oil/gas/coal of consequence. The "public ownership caused the low-carbon mix" claim conflates ownership form with national resource constraints. Norway is another extreme hydro country with public ownership AND low carbon intensity, but a private Norwegian electricity sector (counterfactual) would also be hydro-dominated. Endowment, not ownership.

2. **Postwar industrial planning operated regardless of ownership form.** Italy's ENEL, Spain's Endesa-precursors, Belgium's Tractebel/Electrabel, Netherlands' SEP — all operated under state-led planning regimes in the 1970s-1980s, and most were state-owned through much of the window. The "public vs private" cohort distinction in the spec is really "France/Sweden vs everyone else with different fuel endowments," because most of the comparator-cohort utilities were also publicly owned for substantial portions of 1970-1999.

3. **The matched-private comparator pool is selection-biased.** GBR was only privatised in 1990 (last decade of the window). USA's IOU model is heavily regulated and cost-pass-through, not market-disciplined. Privately owned generators with operational autonomy at scale, in the OECD, are vanishingly rare in this window. The N is too small to support any causal claim.

4. **Confounding with timing of nationalisation/privatisation.** EDF was nationalised in 1946; the carbon-intensity gap (favourable) emerged 30 years later, as nuclear plants finished construction. The temporal lag between treatment (ownership) and outcome (carbon intensity) is enormous, and many other policy variables intervened.

5. **The 1970-1984 sub-window is unmeasured here.** OWID-Ember coverage starts 1985, so half of the spec's 30-year window is invisible. Pre-1985 fuel mix in FRA was still partly oil/coal (the nuclear ramp-up was 1977-1985); the cohort-vs-cohort gap before 1985 is plausibly smaller than the 1985-1999 gap reported. The spec's claim is overstated for the early portion of its window.

6. **Causal identification requires matched-pair within-country variation.** A clean test would compare the same country before and after privatisation. The UK 1990 privatisation is the cleanest natural experiment in the cohort, and the dash-for-gas argument (#2 in the FOR list above) suggests ownership form did shift fuel choice — but the dash-for-gas is also explained by gas-price collapse and CCGT technology maturation in the 1990s, both of which are technology-clock and price-clock variables, not ownership variables.

**How this should shape the result-card framing:**

- Report the descriptive cross-cohort gap as a fact — it is large (ratio ~0.12 over 1985-1999), and the eco-socialist school is correct that the empirical pattern looks favourable.
- Distinguish "the gap is real" from "the gap is caused by ownership form." The first claim is supported by the data; the second is not testable here and is contested by competing explanations (resource endowment, postwar planning, technology timing).
- Flag explicitly that the 1970-1984 sub-window is unmeasured. The framework should not let the spec's 30-year claim ride on a 15-year sub-window result.
- The right "actionable implication" framing is: ownership form may matter at the margin (UK dash-for-gas suggests as much), but the 25-percentage-point gap reported here is mostly endowment + planning, not ownership per se. A privatised French utility in 1985 would still have inherited ~70% nuclear from the 1973-1985 build-out.
- Be humble about confounding. The strongest case for the school's prediction is the post-1990 UK dash-for-gas, NOT the 1970s-1980s French nuclear story.
