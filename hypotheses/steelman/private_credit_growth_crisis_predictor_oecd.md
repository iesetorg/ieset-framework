# Steelman — Private credit growth as crisis predictor (OECD)

**The strongest argument against the autonomous-credit-cycle reading:**

The Schularick-Taylor empirical pattern is robust and replicates across vintages of the JST sample. The contestable steps are interpretive: (a) reading the coefficient as evidence of an autonomous Minsky cycle rather than a joint outcome of capital-flow dynamics + macroprudential policy choices; (b) treating AUC ~0.70 as conclusive evidence rather than a moderate signal; and (c) generalising from a small number of post-1980 episodes to a structural feature of capitalist credit systems.

Specific objections:

1. **Capital-flow / sudden-stop reading subsumes credit-cycle reading.** Calvo-Reinhart and Mendoza-Terrones argue that what looks like a "private-credit cycle" is, in open-economy specifications, a capital-inflow cycle: foreign capital floods in during boom, banks intermediate it into the private sector, then capital reverses. Conditioning on current-account deficit attenuates the credit-change coefficient substantially. A market-liberal version of the same reading attributes the cycle to specific exchange-rate / capital-control policy choices, not autonomous fragility.

2. **Sample-size sparseness.** OECD post-1980 contains roughly a dozen banking-crisis episodes (1991 Nordic, 1992 EMS, 1997 Asian via JPN/KOR, 2007-10 GFC sweep, 2010-2012 Euro periphery). Many of these cluster in 2007-2010, so the Schularick-Taylor / JST result is driven heavily by one cross-sectional event with shared global causes. Out-of-sample predictive performance for non-2008 events is materially weaker.

3. **AUC of 0.70 is moderate, not strong.** A predictor with AUC 0.70 has substantial false-positive and false-negative rates. Many high-credit-growth episodes did NOT result in crises (e.g., Korea 2010s, Australia ongoing, Canada ongoing) and several crises occurred without preceding credit booms (Iceland 2008 was very recent; Greece 2010 was driven by sovereign solvency, not private-credit boom). Treating "supported" on AUC >= 0.65 as confirmation of a Minsky-style autonomous cycle is over-claiming.

4. **The post-2010 macroprudential breakpoint.** Basel III, countercyclical capital buffers, and stress-testing infrastructure since 2010 plausibly weaken the private-credit-as-predictor signal. The hypothesis sample to 2020 captures only a decade of the new regime. If predictive AUC has degraded since 2010, the autonomous-cycle reading is contradicted by policy responsiveness — banking-system fragility responds to macroprudential rules, not to autonomous Minsky dynamics.

5. **Definition of "distress".** Combining bank-NPL-ratio, Laeven-Valencia onset, and credit contraction into one indicator increases statistical power but blurs the causal mechanism. NPL ratios reflect realised losses on existing loans (a stock measure), credit contractions reflect bank balance-sheet adjustment (a flow measure), and Laeven-Valencia onsets reflect public intervention decisions (a policy measure). The hypothesis blends three distinct phenomena that the original Minsky reading would treat differently.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report the unconditional and CA-conditional credit-change coefficient with equal weight; the difference is the binding test.
- Report sub-period AUC (1980-2007 vs 2010-2020) so readers see whether macroprudential reform has weakened the signal.
- Decompose the "distress" outcome into NPL-rise, credit-contraction, and L-V-onset; report which sub-outcome the credit-change variable best predicts.
- State that "supported" here is consistent with the Minsky reading but does not by itself rule out the capital-flow / sudden-stop reading; complementary tests are needed.
- Acknowledge the small-number-of-episodes constraint on the post-1980 OECD sample and refer to JST long-run for breadth, but with the caveat that the long-run sample is dominated by interwar gold-standard dynamics that may not generalise to fiat-floating regimes.
