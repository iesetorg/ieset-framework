# Steelman — Middle-Class Asset Building and Market Depth

## Strongest version of the claim

Secure property rights and deep capital markets are complements to middle-class wealth accumulation. Where stock markets, mortgage systems, and legal title systems function well, median households can convert savings into appreciating financial and real assets. Where these institutions are shallow or insecure, wealth concentrates among elites with access to offshore markets or political connections, and middle-class asset accumulation stagnates despite comparable income growth.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing positive associations between stock-market capitalisation, private credit, property-rights scores, and median household wealth growth.
2. **Microdata** (HFCS, SCF cross-walks, Credit Suisse Global Wealth Databook) documenting stronger median wealth growth in high market-depth countries.
3. **Natural experiments**: financial-sector reforms, land-titling programmes (e.g., Peru), or stock-market liberalisations that increased retail participation.
4. **Decomposition**: separating housing wealth from financial wealth and testing whether both respond to market-depth proxies.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Wealth inequality rises with market depth**. Deeper markets may benefit the top decile more than the median, leaving middle-class wealth share unchanged or falling.
2. **Informal wealth is under-measured**. In countries with weak formal markets, households hold wealth in gold, livestock, family businesses, and social networks that surveys miss.
3. **Public housing and pension systems substitute**. Countries with generous social housing and defined-benefit pensions may show lower private asset accumulation but equivalent lifetime security.
4. **Financial crises erase gains**. Deep markets are volatile; the long-run association may be positive but punctuated by devastating crises that disproportionately hit the middle class (2008, Asian crisis).
5. **Endogeneity**. Richer middle classes demand deeper markets, so the causal arrow may run from wealth to market depth rather than vice versa.

## Boundary conditions

- Most relevant for **countries with median income above subsistence** where asset accumulation is feasible.
- Less relevant where **informal property systems** are functionally secure (e.g., customary tenure in stable communities).
- Financial-crisis episodes may produce **short-run refutations** even if the long-run association holds.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to financial-development, institutional-quality, and inequality literatures. Tensions with claims that public provision of housing and pensions outperforms market-based asset building for median households.
