# Steelman — Monetary expansion shows up in assets, not CPI (1987-2007 panel)

## The strongest opposing argument

The "money goes into assets, not CPI" framing recasts a well-understood demand-composition story (rising savings flowing into financial-asset accumulation as populations age and wealth concentrates) as a monetary-policy failure. It mistakes a regularity in advanced-economy savings behaviour for a central-bank pathology.

**Key mainstream/post-Keynesian objections:**

1. **Endogenous money invalidates the regression direction.** In post-Keynesian theory and most modern central-banking practice, broad money is endogenous — it expands because banks extend credit in response to credit demand, not because the central bank "injects" reserves. The right-hand-side variable in the panel regression is therefore not a policy instrument but an outcome co-determined with asset-price growth. The coefficient is a correlation, not a causal effect of monetary stance.

2. **Disinflation is the right baseline 1987-2007.** The Great Moderation period combined disinflationary supply shocks (China entering the WTO, Eastern Europe joining global value chains, oil-cycle moderation pre-2002) with rising goods-trade openness. The flat CPI in that window is not evidence that money "skipped over" the goods market — it is evidence that the supply curve was flatter than the demand curve, which is a microeconomic story, not a monetary failure.

3. **Asset-price targeting has serious downsides.** Even granting the empirical dissociation, the prescriptive Austrian implication — central banks should target money or asset prices — runs into the well-known problems Bernanke-Gertler (2001) identified: targeting asset prices forces the central bank to pick which asset class is "fundamental" vs "bubble", which is not a question central banks can answer in real time. The CPI-targeting consensus emerged precisely because asset-price indicators are too noisy to anchor monetary policy.

4. **3x ratio threshold is arbitrary.** The pre-registered 3x asset-to-CPI coefficient ratio sounds disciplined but is unmotivated by theory. Why 3x and not 2x or 5x? The threshold could be re-spec-shopped to whatever number makes the data "support" the hypothesis. A more principled test would be specifying ex ante what the heterogeneous lag structure looks like (asset prices respond fast, CPI slow) and whether the long-run elasticities equalise.

## What the framework still captures

The empirical regularity — broad money correlated with asset-price growth more strongly than with CPI in the 1987-2007 window — is robust and uncontroversial. What is contested is the policy implication. The framework's pre-registered test gives the Austrian framing a clean, falsifiable target: if the ratio is not at least 3x, the strong version of the dissociation claim fails. A "supported" verdict still leaves open whether the dissociation is best explained by Austrian malinvestment, Borio-Lowe macroprudential gaps, or post-Keynesian endogenous-money dynamics — but it documents the pattern that all three frameworks try to explain.
