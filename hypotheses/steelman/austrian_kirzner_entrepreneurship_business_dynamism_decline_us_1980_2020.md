# Steelman — Kirzner entrepreneurship / business dynamism decline US 1980-2020

## The strongest opposing argument

The US business-dynamism decline is real but the Austrian causal story (regulation chills entry, concentration follows) is one of several mutually-consistent explanations. Plausible non-Austrian alternatives — population aging, urbanisation slowing, Schumpeterian winner-takes-most dynamics in IT-intensive sectors, healthcare-and-finance secular share growth (both naturally low-dynamism sectors) — together account for a meaningful share of the trend without invoking regulatory chilling.

**Key heterodox / mainstream-IO objections:**

1. **Demographic-aging mechanically lowers firm formation.** Firm formation rates depend heavily on the 25-44 age cohort that does most entrepreneurship. The US working-age age structure shifted older over 1980-2020. Karahan-Pugsley-Şahin show the demographic channel alone accounts for roughly half the dynamism decline. Attributing the full decline to regulation or concentration over-claims.

2. **Sectoral-mix shift does substantial work.** The US economy's share of healthcare, finance, and education rose substantially from 1980 to 2020. These sectors are structurally low-dynamism (long firm lifecycles, high regulatory frictions for non-Austrian reasons such as licensure for patient safety and consumer protection). Composition shift drives the headline number; within-sector dynamism declined less.

3. **Concentration rise is partly Schumpeterian.** Autor-Dorn-Katz-Patterson-Van Reenen ("The Fall of the Labour Share and the Rise of Superstar Firms") argue the rise in industry concentration reflects winner-takes-most dynamics in IT-intensive sectors where the most productive firms scale across markets. This is the opposite of the Olsonian rent-seeking story — the leading firms got bigger because they were genuinely more productive, not because regulation suppressed entrants.

4. **Occupational-licensing growth is partly real consumer protection.** The Kleiner-Krueger licensed-share growth is overwhelmingly in sectors with genuine information asymmetries (healthcare, finance, legal services). Some licensing is regulatory capture (hairdressing, interior design), but the bulk is a defensible response to market failure that classical-liberal critiques systematically downplay.

5. **The multi-metric design is over-determined.** Five metrics where four are mechanically related (firm formation, young-firm share, reallocation rate are all measuring the same underlying phenomenon) overstates independent confirmation.

## What the framework still captures

The descriptive pattern — declining firm formation, declining young-firm employment share, declining reallocation, rising occupational licensing, rising industry concentration — is genuinely robust. The Decker-Haltiwanger papers established it; the multi-metric checklist tests whether the canonical pattern holds in the data, not the causal Austrian story. A "supported" verdict means the dynamism decline is real and real-sized; whether the right policy response is occupational-licensing reform and antitrust enforcement (Austrian-classical-liberal) or industrial policy and labour-market floors (heterodox) is a separate, contested question the framework does not adjudicate here.
