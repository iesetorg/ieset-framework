# Steelman — GFC endogenous Minsky leverage mechanism

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The Minsky framing — that the 2007-2009 crisis emerged endogenously from a hedge→speculative→Ponzi transition in private balance sheets — competes with at least three alternative explanations that fit the same pre-crisis credit-boom data: (1) the Bernanke-Gertler "global savings glut" + Fed-too-low-for-too-long story, in which low policy rates 2003-2004 imported from East Asian / oil-exporter saving caused the leverage build-up exogenously; (2) the Calomiris-Haber "fragile by design" deregulation story, in which specific US legal changes (Gramm-Leach-Bliley 1999, the SEC's 2004 net-capital-rule revision, OFHEO's expansion of Fannie/Freddie subprime mandates) created the conditions for rapid leverage growth; and (3) the Acharya-Schnabl-Suarez "shadow-bank regulatory-arbitrage" story, in which Basel-II risk weights drove banks to move credit risk off-balance-sheet through ABCP conduits and SIVs. All three are consistent with rising private credit-to-GDP, rising household DSR, and a real-estate boom. A pre-crisis trajectory test that finds these patterns therefore supports Minsky no more than it supports the alternatives.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Minsky-pattern build-up is not unique to crisis episodes.** Private credit-to-GDP also rose materially over 1985-2000 in many of the same countries without a 2008-style crisis. Without an out-of-sample falsification test (a country with rising DSR and credit-to-GDP that did NOT have a crisis — Australia is the canonical case in the literature), the trajectory test is at best a necessary-condition check, not a sufficient one. The spec's >=5/9 country threshold treats the pattern as a sufficient signal when the literature treats it only as one of several "macroprudential warning" indicators.

2. **The credit-to-GDP gap (I2) is the BIS's own crisis-prediction tool.** Drehmann-Borio-Tsatsaronis 2011 specifically built the credit-gap measure as an early-warning indicator. Finding that it crossed BIS's own warning threshold pre-2008 is mechanically expected and does not independently validate the Minsky theory; it validates that the indicator captures what its designers built it to capture.

3. **The German counter-example is informative, not noise.** Germany shows up as a non-passing country across all four indicators — credit-to-GDP did not rise, DSR did not rise, real house prices did not rise, and the credit gap did not breach 10pp. Yet Germany was hit by the GFC (Hypo Real Estate, IKB, Landesbanken). If the Minsky mechanism is the dominant cause, Germany's lack of a domestic Minsky build-up should have insulated its banking system, but it did not. The transmission channel was cross-border exposure to US/Spanish/Irish assets, which suggests the binding constraint was financial integration, not domestic leverage cycles.

4. **Quarterly OLS slopes over an 8-year window will return p<0.10 for almost any monotonic trending series.** Setting p<0.10 on 32 quarterly observations is a weak filter; the magnitude thresholds (20pp credit-to-GDP rise, 10pp credit gap, 1pp DSR, 20% real house-price rise) carry essentially all the dispositive weight. Three of those four thresholds are calibrated against ex-post crisis data, so the test is partly tautological — it asks whether the pre-2008 data look like the data BIS used to define what a pre-crisis build-up looks like.

5. **Replacing the spec's broker-dealer leverage and MBS-issuance indicators with BIS sector aggregates moves the test away from the financial-sector-balance-sheet channel that is most distinctively Minskyan.** Minsky's mechanism centres on financial-sector funding fragility (short-term wholesale funding of long-dated assets), not on household-sector debt service or aggregate credit-to-GDP. The 4-indicator scorecard tests household / aggregate-credit dynamics, which would also rise under a pure consumer-credit-boom story (e.g. the UK's secured-mortgage expansion, which had little wholesale-funding fragility). A clean Minsky test would need broker-dealer leverage from Fed Z.1, ABCP outstanding, and triparty-repo haircuts — none of which are on disk.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Lead the verdict with the indicator-by-indicator pass count and the German counter-example, not with a headline "Minsky vindicated" framing. SUPPORTED here means "the trajectory data are consistent with Minsky" — not "alternative theories are refuted."

(b) Explicitly note that the credit-to-GDP gap (I2) is BIS's own warning indicator, so its threshold-crossing in IRL/ESP/USA/ITA is partly a measurement artefact of how the gap is constructed.

(c) Flag the missing financial-sector indicators (broker-dealer leverage, MBS issuance, ABCP, repo haircuts) as the right test of the Minsky mechanism, and treat the 4-indicator scorecard as a partial proxy that rules out a pure exogenous-shock story but does not discriminate between Minsky and "saving glut + deregulation" alternatives.

(d) Include Australia (or another monotonic-credit-build-up country with no GFC crisis) as a falsifying out-of-sample case in any v2 promotion, since the current sample is selected on the dependent variable (these are the GFC-affected economies).
