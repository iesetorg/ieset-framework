# Steelman — Ghana 2022 IMF programme + DDEP

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Disinflation is the easy outcome; the hard question is sustainability.** Headline CPI fell from above 50% to single digits by mid-2025, but core inflation and inflation expectations remained sticky. The 25-ppt-decline threshold is met mechanically by base effects. A more stringent test would require inflation to return to the BoG 6-10% target band and stay there for 8 consecutive quarters.

2. **The DDEP destroyed the domestic financial system's balance sheet.** Ghanaian pension funds and banks held large GHC bond stocks at par; the DDEP haircut wiped out a chunk of pension wealth and required government recapitalisation of state-owned banks. Counting the DDEP as a "fiscal stabilisation" without accounting for the financial-sector recapitalisation cost is a partial-equilibrium error.

3. **The "fiscal repair" is heavily front-loaded with one-time measures.** VAT increases, the E-Levy, and electricity tariff adjustments delivered the bulk of the 2023 revenue gain. Expenditure-side reform (wage bill, SOE losses, statutory transfers) progressed less. A 4-ppt primary-balance improvement that reverses by 2027 should not be classified as a successful programme.

4. **External-debt restructuring under the G20 Common Framework was slow and incomplete.** The 2023 OCC negotiations stretched into 2024; bondholder restructuring concluded only in late 2024. The "stabilisation" period overlaps the negotiation period during which arrears accumulated. The cedi-USD rate test should distinguish the official-rate from grey-market rate during this window.

5. **Cocoa-sector collapse is an idiosyncratic shock the framework should isolate.** Ghanaian cocoa output fell 35-40% in 2023-2024 due to swollen-shoot disease and weather. This depressed export earnings and FX inflows independent of macro policy, hurting the BoP and complicating the synthetic-control fit. The cocoa-price control variable is necessary but not sufficient — output volume effects matter too.

6. **The political-economy fragility undermines durability.** The 2024 election cycle and NDC return to power (December 2024) creates uncertainty about post-2025 programme adherence. A "successful programme" framing that ignores subsequent political reversal is incomplete.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report the cocoa-output volume effect as a sub-decomposition.
- Disclose the financial-sector recapitalisation cost as a memo item.
- Report sovereign Eurobond yields as a credibility-channel secondary outcome.
- Defer durability assessment to a v1.1 run after 2027 (one full electoral cycle post-treatment).
