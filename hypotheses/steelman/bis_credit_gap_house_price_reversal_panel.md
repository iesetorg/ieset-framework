# Steelman — BIS credit gap predicts real house-price reversal

The strongest objection is that a credit gap is not a housing-specific signal. It can rise because of corporate leverage, financial deepening, measurement changes, or trend-filter end-point problems rather than speculative residential credit. A negative property-price response after high-gap quarters may therefore mix true housing vulnerability with unrelated deleveraging episodes.

Several country panels also have binding omitted-variable concerns. Zoning constraints, migration flows, construction cycles, tax treatment of mortgages, macroprudential limits, and local banking supervision all vary through time. Country and year fixed effects do not remove those forces. The raw BIS screen is useful as a verdict on predictive content, but it should not be read as proof that the credit gap caused the reversal.

The 10 percentage-point threshold is intentionally demanding, but it may select crisis-prone outliers such as Spain, Ireland, or the Nordics and leave normal booms underweighted. Conversely, countries with chronically high private-credit systems may display elevated levels without imminent reversal.

If supported, the cautious interpretation is "high BIS credit gaps are a useful warning flag for weak forward real house-price growth." If refuted, the result would not prove credit cycles irrelevant; it would say this simple high-gap panel trigger fails the predeclared predictive gate.
