# Steelman — Japan high debt, no fiscal-dominance crisis

**The strongest argument against the post-Keynesian "Japan refutes Sargent-Wallace" reading:**

The Japan case is real and the Sargent-Wallace prediction has not played out on a 35-year horizon at debt-to-GDP > 200%. But Japan is a complex special case, and reading it as a clean refutation of fiscal-dominance theory ignores the contingent institutional, demographic, and savings-balance conditions that have made the path possible. A more measured reading is "high public debt under monetary independence is sustainable under specific conditions, none of which are infinite-horizon guarantees".

Specific objections:

1. **Japan-specific savings absorption.** Roughly 90% of JGBs are held domestically, primarily by the BoJ (post-QE), Japan Post Bank, life insurers, and pension funds. Deep domestic savings absorption at near-zero rates is a precondition that other high-debt countries (Italy, Greece, Spain) have not had. Post-Keynesian readings that generalise from Japan to "all currency issuers can run unlimited debt" ignore this savings-balance precondition.

2. **Demographic ZIRP stability.** Japan's persistently below-target inflation reflects a specific demographic and labour-market context (aging population, lifetime-employment norms, cautious household consumption). The fact that monetisation has not produced inflation in this context does not generalise to economies without these features. The 2022-2024 Japan inflation rise (~3-4%) is the first material test of accommodation under tighter labour markets, and the policy response (rate hikes, YCC abandonment) suggests the BoJ believes the regime needs to change.

3. **Sargent-Wallace is a long-run prediction.** The original 1981 paper does not specify a year-by-year crisis path; it predicts that under continuous primary deficits and stable monetary policy, fiscal authority eventually dominates. Japan has run sustained primary deficits for 35 years and the BoJ now owns ~50% of JGBs outstanding, which IS the monetisation Sargent-Wallace predicts. The absence of a crisis SO FAR does not refute the prediction; it is consistent with the prediction operating over a horizon longer than 35 years, especially in an economy with demographic disinflation.

4. **Real costs that aren't crisis symptoms.** The hypothesis tests for crisis symptoms (inflation, FX collapse, yield spike) but ignores accumulating real costs: erosion of fiscal space to respond to future shocks, crowding-out of public investment by debt-service contingencies under any future rate normalisation, and constraints on policy independence as the BoJ becomes structurally important to JGB-market functioning. A "no crisis yet" reading misses these.

5. **The 2022-2024 inflation rise is a stress test the hypothesis truncates.** As of 2024-2025, Japan CPI inflation has been ~2-3% for two years, JGB 10y yields have risen from ~0.2% to ~1.7%, and BoJ has begun unwinding QQE/YCC. A 2024 cutoff captures the very early phase of this regime test; the next 5 years will be much more informative about whether the Sargent-Wallace prediction was simply slow.

6. **Dependent on BoJ-MoF coordination, not independence.** YCC was de facto fiscal-monetary coordination — the BoJ pegged the long end of the JGB yield curve, which is exactly the kind of monetary-policy subordination Sargent-Wallace describes. Coding this as "monetary independence preserved" requires a definition of independence that is permissive about coordination as long as it is not formally a deficit-monetisation mandate. The qualitative-coding leg of the multi-metric design is the weakest one.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Acknowledge the savings-absorption, demographic, and JGB-domestic-holding preconditions explicitly; do not generalise from "Japan can run high debt" to "all currency issuers can".
- Treat the BoJ's ~50% JGB ownership as a form of monetisation; the multi-metric design must not implicitly count this as "non-monetisation".
- Report 2022-2024 inflation and yield dynamics in a separate column; the early-2020s regime test is informative.
- State explicitly that "supported" on this hypothesis does NOT refute Sargent-Wallace as a long-run prediction; it confirms that the predicted crisis has not occurred on a 35-year horizon under specific Japanese conditions.
- Discuss the real-costs-without-crisis category honestly; "non-crisis" is a low bar that misses important sub-crisis costs.
