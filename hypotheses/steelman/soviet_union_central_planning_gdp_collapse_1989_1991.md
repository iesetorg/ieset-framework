# Steelman: Soviet Central Planning GDP Collapse 1989-1991

The strongest counter-argument is that the Soviet stagnation narrative overstates planning failure and understates Western measurement bias. Maddison-series estimates for the USSR rely on Western intelligence reconstructions (CIA, PlanEcon) that systematically undervalued Soviet non-market output including military hardware, public health provision, housing, and education services — goods that are hard to price in the absence of markets but which constituted genuinely high shares of Soviet production. Adjusted for purchasing-power parity on the actual consumption basket of Soviet citizens, some revisionist estimates (Harrison 1993, Rosefielde 2003) place USSR living standards at 40-50% of US levels in 1970 and at similar levels in 1989, implying no deterioration — consistent with the regime's internal stability throughout the period.

On convergence specifically: the Japan comparison may be misleading because Japan benefited from technology transfer, export-led growth into open US markets, and Marshall Plan-era capital inflows unavailable to the USSR under Cold War trade restrictions. The USSR's near-autarky was a security constraint imposed by US export controls (COCOM), not a freely chosen policy failure. Under this reading, the productivity stagnation was as much a product of forced isolation as of planning per se.

The 1989-1991 collapse is then better explained by the abrupt withdrawal of plan enforcement — itself a political choice by Gorbachev — rather than any endogenous exhaustion of the planning model. Evidence: East Asian socialist economies (China, Vietnam) that maintained plan enforcement while selectively opening markets did not collapse, suggesting enforcement rather than planning type was the binding variable.
