# Steelman — QE asset inflation vs CPI divergence post-2008

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The observed 2009-2020 divergence between asset prices and consumer prices is not QE's signature but the signature of a demand-depressed economy with a safe-asset shortage and aging demographics. Attributing the gap to central bank balance-sheet expansion overstates the instrument's causal role and understates the structural forces that would have produced a similar divergence under different monetary regimes.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **The "pushing on a string" objection cuts both ways.** If QE had limited CPI pass-through because demand was depressed and credit channels were impaired, then the asset-price response must have operated through a different channel (portfolio rebalancing, duration compression, signalling). But those channels would also weaken in a genuinely demand-depressed environment. The observed asset-price rise may reflect recovery from post-GFC undervaluation plus the safe-asset premium rather than QE-specific mechanics.

2. **Counterfactual contamination.** The pre-registered comparison compares post-2008 QE-active country-years against their own earlier history, but the earlier history was not monetary-policy-neutral. Pre-2008 policy rates were themselves held low for extended periods (2002-2005 "Greenspan put" era), and the asset-price run-up of 2003-2007 is excluded from the base. Choosing 2008 as the regime break privileges the QE interpretation over an alternative narrative in which the regime break occurred earlier.

3. **Aggregate CPI masks sectoral pass-through.** Headline and core CPI did not rise, but shelter costs, healthcare, and services CPI did rise meaningfully in the USA over the period. If QE's price pass-through was bottlenecked through interest-sensitive sectors (housing), the framework's "asset-only" divergence story is partly artefact of which price index is the comparator. A hypothesis that allows CPI components to decompose would likely find more pass-through than the headline comparator shows.

4. **The 2021-2023 reconvergence may not be the "sanity check" the hypothesis treats it as.** The post-pandemic CPI surge is widely attributed to supply shocks, fiscal transfers, and post-lockdown demand reopening — not to the 2020 QE wave specifically. Treating the reconvergence as confirmation that "QE eventually gets to CPI" conflates two different policy-shock sources. A clean test would need to isolate the QE contribution to the 2021-2023 shock, which requires identification the spec does not attempt.

5. **Japan is a pre-2008 counter-example the spec de-emphasises.** Japan ran QE from 2001 onwards and experienced neither asset-price inflation nor consumer-price inflation. The 2008-2023 panel includes Japan post-2008, but Japan's longer QE history suggests the asset-inflation channel requires conditions (capacity utilisation, household leverage, financial structure) that Japan did not share and that the panel spec does not capture. Generalising from a USA/EUR pattern to an all-country-QE-active claim may be unjustified.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card should:

(a) Report the impulse-response gap alongside a Japan-only subpanel, treating Japan as a falsification-leaning case and explicitly reporting whether the hypothesis-level finding is driven by USA + EUR alone.

(b) Report CPI-component decomposition (headline, core, shelter, healthcare, services-ex-shelter) so readers can see which part of the CPI "failed to rise" — this materially changes the story if shelter did rise and headline did not.

(c) Present the Fawley-Neely narrative-identification robustness specification alongside the macro panel, since it more cleanly isolates QE announcement effects from coincident demand-side factors.

(d) Note that the finding — even if supported — does not by itself establish that QE caused the divergence rather than coincided with it. A direct causal claim would require identification of a QE surprise uncorrelated with the demand-depression state variables, which is a stronger requirement than the pre-registered specification delivers.

The strongest version of the steelman points at a v2 agenda: identify QE surprises via high-frequency event windows (as Rigobon-Sack, Swanson, and others have done) and rebuild the impulse-response comparison from that narrower base. If the divergence still shows up on surprise-identified shocks, the QE-specific story survives; if it disappears, the hypothesis is largely measuring regime-wide demand depression.
