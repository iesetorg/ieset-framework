# Steelman — US median wage stagnation 1973-2000 decomposition

The strongest opposing argument: the compositional/measurement decomposition is a sophisticated way of explaining away a genuine distributional shift. The wage-productivity gap over 1973-2000 is not principally a measurement artefact; it is the single cleanest empirical fact of the postwar US political economy, and the framework's decomposition risks minimising it.

## Piketty-Saez-Zucman challenge to measurement-first readings

The Feldstein CPI-vs-GDP-deflator argument has been interrogated extensively and produces smaller adjustments than Feldstein's original 2008 essay claimed. Subsequent BLS methodological work (CPI-U-RS; Boskin Commission adjustments incorporated) narrowed the deflator wedge substantially. The Distributional National Accounts of Piketty-Saez-Zucman (2018) reconstruct the entire income distribution using national-accounts-consistent aggregates, removing the CPI-vs-GDP-deflator ambiguity entirely, and still find median real pre-tax national income growth of roughly 0.3% annualised over 1980-2014 against productivity growth of roughly 1.4%. A factor-of-four gap survives the measurement critique. The framework's "channels absorb 50-85%" band is wide enough to call the finding "supported" under readings that the distributional literature has largely abandoned.

## Benefit substitution is not a worker windfall

The benefits-substitution channel is mathematically real (employer healthcare grew from ~5% to ~12% of total compensation 1973-2000) but its interpretation is not neutral. If healthcare premium inflation substantially exceeded the value of care delivered (a point the framework concedes in its US-vs-EU healthcare hypotheses), then cash wages foregone for healthcare premiums represent a transfer to the US medical-industrial complex, not equivalent non-wage worker income. Attributing the wage-productivity gap to "benefits substitution" and stopping there treats healthcare inflation as a productivity-like benefit; that is a framing choice, not a measurement correction.

## Female LFP is bidirectional

The compositional channel (rising female LFP lowering median experience) is real in accounting terms but bidirectional in welfare terms: female LFP also raised *household* income and total hours worked per household. The median-worker statistic missed the household shift. A cleaner test uses median household income per hour worked, which controls for compositional LFP effects. The framework's decision to use median individual wage rather than median household income per hour is a methodological choice that maximises the compositional channel's apparent size.

## The top-strata residual is unusually robust

The top-1%-income-share doubling 1973-2000 is documented in three independent datasets (IRS tax returns via Piketty-Saez, Survey of Consumer Finances, and the DINA reconstruction), with minor disagreement on levels but strong agreement on the 1980-2000 inflection and trajectory. Any decomposition producing a top-strata residual below 15% is incompatible with those three independent measurements and indicates model mis-specification. The framework's falsification lower bound of 0.15 is well-calibrated; any claim of "below 0.15" should be regarded with high scepticism.

## Karabarbounis-Neiman and the labour-share overlay

Over 1973-2000 the US labour share of GDP declined from roughly 65% to roughly 60%, a 5-percentage-point shift that translates to approximately one-third of the raw wage-productivity gap at the aggregate level. If the decomposition channels absorb the labour-share shift into "benefits substitution" or "measurement", that is double-counting; labour share is a distinct phenomenon from median wage compression and has its own causal story (capital-intensity technological change, import competition). Cross-validation against the labour_share_decline_causes hypothesis is essential.

## What would update the framework's prior

If the decomposition returns compositional-and-measurement share above 85%, the framework should treat the result as suspect and specifically probe: (i) whether the benefits-substitution channel includes healthcare inflation windfall to providers; (ii) whether the CPI-vs-GDP deflator correction is using CPI-U (unadjusted) rather than CPI-U-RS (methodologically updated). Symmetrically, if the top-strata residual lands between 0.30 and 0.50, the framework should acknowledge that a substantial portion of the 1973-2000 "stagnation" is distributional in character and update its prior toward the Bivens-Mishel reading.

## Policy implications differ by channel weights

A finding dominated by compositional/measurement channels prescribes improved statistics and targeted healthcare-cost reform. A finding with a 0.30+ top-strata residual prescribes redistributive or pre-distributive (architecture-based) interventions. The decomposition's numerical output carries direct policy weight; the framework should resist the temptation to prefer the policy implication that matches its priors.
