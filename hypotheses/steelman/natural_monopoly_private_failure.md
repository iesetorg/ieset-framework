# Steelman — Natural Monopoly Privatisation Without Regulation Fails Consumers

## Strongest version of the claim

Privatising natural monopolies—electricity grids, water networks, rail track, fixed-line telecom—without creating strong independent regulation transfers monopoly rents from the state to private owners without improving consumer welfare. Prices rise, quality deteriorates, and access expansion stalls because the privatised firm has no competitive pressure and the regulator lacks the information, budget, or political autonomy to mimic competition. Well-governed public ownership or tightly regulated private provision outperforms unregulated privatisation on consumer welfare metrics. The claim challenges the assumption that private ownership is unconditionally superior to public ownership in non-contestable markets.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** comparing pre- and post-privatisation consumer prices, quality, and access in sectors with strong versus weak regulation.
2. **Case comparisons**: UK water (regulated, mixed results) versus Argentine water (unregulated, price spikes and service cuts); Chile telecom (regulated, success) versus Russian electricity (unregulated, asset stripping).
3. **Event-study evidence** showing price and quality trajectories diverging based on regulatory strength post-privatisation.
4. **Consumer surplus estimates** incorporating quality and access, not just prices.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Public ownership is also inefficient**. State-owned natural monopolies suffer from political interference, overstaffing, under-investment, and cross-subsidies that distort prices.
2. **Regulation can be captured**. Strong regulatory agencies may be captured by incumbent firms, producing outcomes no better than unregulated privatisation.
3. **Long-run efficiency gains**. Even unregulated privatisation may raise efficiency through better management, with consumer gains arriving later through infrastructure investment.
4. **Selection bias**. The worst public enterprises are privatised first, making post-privatisation comparisons unfavorable to the public benchmark.
5. **Access is not a consumer-welfare metric**. Unregulated privatisation may raise prices for existing customers while expanding access to new customers; the net welfare effect is ambiguous.

## Boundary conditions

- Most relevant for **network industries with sunk costs and non-contestable elements**.
- Less relevant for **contestable segments** (power generation, telecom services, rail operations) where competition is feasible.
- Applies to **middle- and high-income countries** with regulatory capacity; in low-capacity countries, even regulation may fail.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to regulation, privatisation, and institutional-quality literatures. Tensions with blanket pro-privatisation claims and with hypotheses that treat ownership rather than market structure as the primary determinant of performance.
