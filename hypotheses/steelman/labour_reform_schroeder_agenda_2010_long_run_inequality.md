# Steelman — Agenda 2010 long-run inequality

The strongest pro-reform reading: the German bottom-quintile-to-median wage compression is partly a sectoral-composition effect rather than a pure wage-rule effect — Hartz I-IV expanded employment in services and care sectors that have structurally lower wages, mechanically lowering the P10/P50 ratio without any individual worker losing wages. Dustmann et al. 2014 argue the German wage decline 1995-2008 was driven by trade-induced manufacturing-wage compression rather than Hartz reforms, and that the reforms are misattributed as the binding cause. The 2015 minimum-wage introduction provides a clean within-Germany test: if the P10/P50 ratio rebounds materially after 2015 (which DIW and Mindestlohn-Kommission evaluations suggest it did, by approximately 1.5pp), the durable-wage-compression claim is weakened. The spec's threshold (-3pp gap, not closing post-2015) is designed to give the pro-reform reader a clean win if either condition fails. A v2 should use individual longitudinal data (SOEP) to separate composition effects from price effects.
