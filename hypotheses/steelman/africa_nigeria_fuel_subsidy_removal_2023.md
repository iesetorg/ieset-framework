# Steelman — Nigeria fuel subsidy removal 2023

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **The "fiscal repair" claim is partly an accounting illusion.** Pre-2023, the petrol subsidy was reported as NNPC under-recovery rather than as a federal budget line; its elimination changes the recognition path of the same underlying flow. Headline primary-balance improvement may overstate the real fiscal gain. A clean test must reconcile NNPC remittances to the federation account before and after the reform, not just read the federal budget.

2. **The June 2023 naira float is the larger shock.** The official rate jumped from ~460/USD to ~750/USD within weeks, then to ~1500+/USD by 2024. Inflation acceleration is overwhelmingly an FX-passthrough phenomenon, not a subsidy-removal phenomenon. Attributing the inflation cost to "subsidy removal" overcredits the fiscal channel and undercredits the parallel monetary-regime change.

3. **The household-welfare cost is substantial and politically destabilising.** Real wages collapsed; food inflation peaked above 40% YoY in 2024; the protests of August 2024 ("End Bad Governance") signalled regime fragility. A reform whose welfare cost triggers political instability cannot be scored as a clean win even if the fiscal numbers improve.

4. **The donor pool is small and contaminated.** SSA oil exporters with quarterly fiscal data plus comparable scale are scarce; Angola has its own fiscal-adjustment programme; Algeria has a fundamentally different (state-led) fiscal regime; Gabon and Equatorial Guinea are tiny. Synthetic-control weights may be unstable and permutation inference will have low power with a 5-unit donor pool.

5. **The counterfactual matters: would Nigeria have repaired its fiscal position anyway?** The 2023 oil-price level (~$80) was favourable. A simulated "no-reform, oil-price-only" counterfactual might show federal revenue improving regardless of the subsidy decision. The spec's terms-of-trade control partly addresses this but cannot fully disentangle.

6. **The political-economy story matters more than the macroeconomic story.** The credibility signal to creditors (S&P upgrade path, Eurobond spread compression) was real and is itself a partial answer to the reform's value. But that's a sovereign-spread test, not a primary-balance test. The hypothesis's outcome variables may be the wrong frame.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report subsidy-removal effect AND naira-float effect separately where possible.
- Reconcile NNPC remittances to federation account as a sub-test of "real fiscal repair".
- Report sovereign spread and Eurobond yield as a secondary outcome.
- Disclose if the fiscal effect is dominated by FX-accounting rather than real revenue gain.
