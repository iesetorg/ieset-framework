# Steelman: post_soviet_transition_institutional_variation

The strongest objection is endogeneity. Rule-of-law scores after 1996 may partly
measure the same successful transition path that GDP per capita also records,
rather than an independent institutional cause. EU accession prospects, geography,
Soviet-era industrial structure, commodity exposure, and war risk all plausibly
drive both institutional reform and growth. Country and year fixed effects remove
some static differences and common shocks, but they do not make rule of law
exogenous.

The second objection is timing. WGI begins after the most violent early-transition
collapse had already occurred. A cleaner design would use pre-1991 institutional
or historical predictors and test subsequent growth, or an EU-accession instrument
with transparent exclusion restrictions. This panel FE result should therefore be
read as an associational discipline check, not as a causal proof that institutional
quality caused the entire transition-growth spread.
