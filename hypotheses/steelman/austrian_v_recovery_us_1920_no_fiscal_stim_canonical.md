# Steelman — 1920-1921 US recovery as canonical "no stimulus needed"

## The strongest opposing argument

The 1920-1921 episode is uniquely poorly suited to bear the weight Austrian retellings place on it. It is a postwar demobilisation shock, not a credit-boom bust; it occurred under price-flexible 1920s wage-setting institutions that no longer exist; and the Fed did materially loosen post-trough. Citing it as evidence that "Keynesian-style intervention is unnecessary" extrapolates from one structurally-anomalous case to a generalised policy lesson the case cannot support.

**Key Keynesian / mainstream objections:**

1. **Postwar reconversion is the wrong analogue.** The 1920-1921 contraction reflected the wartime production complex demobilising and price levels collapsing back from inflated 1918-1919 levels. There was no credit-boom misallocation to be liquidated, no insolvent banking sector, and no debt-deflation feedback loop. Comparing it to 1929 or 2008 is comparing a sprained ankle to a heart attack.

2. **Wage flexibility was structurally different.** In 1920-1921, US labour markets had no minimum wages, no Wagner Act, no collective-bargaining infrastructure, and price-level deflation flowed through to nominal wage cuts within months. The "rapid recovery" was substantially delivered by a 25%+ nominal wage decline that is institutionally unavailable to any modern economy. Generalising the recovery speed to today is a category error.

3. **The Fed actually did loosen substantially.** The discount rate fell from 7% in mid-1920 to 4% by mid-1922 — a 300 basis point cut over 18 months, larger than most modern recession-fighting easings. The "no monetary stimulus" claim depends on choosing the right window. Austrian retellings emphasise the 1920 tightening but understate the 1921-1922 easing that actually accompanied the recovery.

4. **Lebergott unemployment estimates are themselves contested.** The 10%+ unemployment peak rests on Lebergott's 1957 historical reconstructions, which Romer (1986) re-estimated using different assumptions and arrived at meaningfully smaller peaks. Multi-metric verdicts that depend on Lebergott's series are sensitive to which historical-statistics convention is used.

## What the framework still captures

The 1920-1921 case really did happen — output dropped, federal spending was cut, the Fed initially tightened, and recovery was achieved within approximately 24 months. As an existence proof that recovery without active fiscal stimulus is empirically possible, the case is uncontroversial. As a generalisable model for handling 1929-style debt-deflation or 2008-style banking-sector insolvency, it is severely limited. The framework's multi-metric test asks only the existence question; the generalisability question is left to follow-on hypotheses.
