# Steelman — Wage-price spiral OECD panel 2021-2024

## The strongest opposing argument

The "real wages fell, no spiral" framing is broadly correct for 2021-2022 but increasingly inadequate as a description of 2023-2024 dynamics in tight-labour-market economies, and the profit-led-inflation alternative has its own measurement weaknesses.

**Wage-price spiral defence (orthodox / late-cycle reading):**

1. **Spirals are second-order phenomena that emerge with lag.** Classical spiral theory (Tobin, Phelps) predicts wage-leads-price emerges only when inflation expectations de-anchor and contracts indexation kicks in. The 2021-2022 phase looked supply-driven precisely because expectations remained anchored; 2023-2024 wage growth in UK (private-sector regular pay 7%+), Australia (Fair Work Commission award decisions), and Eurozone (DEU IG Metall, FRA SMIC) is starting to show backward-looking-indexation patterns. A test ending 2023-Q4 may capture only the supply-shock phase and miss the emerging spiral leg.

2. **Aggregate Granger tests miss the relevant micro pattern.** Wage growth at the median worker can lag CPI while wage growth at the union-covered / minimum-wage / public-sector tail is strongly indexed and leads. The OECD-level VAR averages over heterogeneous wage-setting institutions; the spiral may be operating in a sub-population not captured.

3. **The Bivens/Weber profit-led framing has data-quality problems.** Markup estimates require imputing capital costs, and most "excess profits" 2021-2022 estimates in the published debate include energy-sector windfalls (oil majors capturing oil-price spike). Strip the energy sector and ex-energy markups rose modestly. The "profit-led" framing is partly an oil-price story relabelled.

**Spiral-skeptic case (supports the hypothesis as written):**
- Real wages indisputably fell across OECD 2021-2023 (ECB, BIS, ILO confirmed).
- Bernanke-Blanchard 2023 structural decomposition assigns the inflation episode to supply shocks + labour-market tightness, with wage-price feedback playing a minor role (<10% of cumulative inflation).
- Long-run inflation expectations stayed anchored — the textbook precondition for spiral propagation never activated.

## What the framework still captures

The hypothesis tests a narrow but important claim: that the *2021-2024 episode* did not exhibit wage-price-spiral dynamics in the technical Granger / cumulative-real-wage sense. That claim is plausibly true and falsifiable on the data. The endpoint dependence is a real limitation, and the hypothesis would be strengthened by including a 2023-Q1 to 2025-Q4 sub-period sensitivity to test whether late-cycle dynamics flip the result. The asymmetry of priors — easy to falsify if real wages rose, robust support if Granger direction confirms — makes this a clean test of one of the most-debated macro questions of the early 2020s.
