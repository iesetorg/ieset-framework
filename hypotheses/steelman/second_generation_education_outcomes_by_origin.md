# Steelman — Second-generation education outcomes by origin

**The strongest arguments against the channel-decomposition framing:**

1. **SES and "ethnicity" are empirically near-collinear in many destination countries, so regression-attenuation on controlling SES can be a statistical artefact of the collinearity rather than a causal story.** The claim that "ethnicity doesn't explain outcomes, SES does" is equivalent to "ethnicity correlates with outcomes via SES" — but this collinearity makes regression-attenuation interpretation ambiguous. If ethnicity-of-parent determines SES-of-parent (via discrimination, origin-country selection, credential non-recognition), then calling the residual "SES effect, not ethnicity effect" obscures the causal chain.

2. **Specific origin group cells show non-convergence of educational outcomes across generations.** Some US Black-African second-generation data shows convergence toward Asian-American outcomes; some European data on specific MENA origin groups shows persistent gaps through the second and third generation even after parental-SES controls. The framework's "2nd-gen converges" test has exceptions the honest report should flag.

3. **School-quality-equality is itself shaped by ethnicity-related residential segregation.** In the US especially, school quality varies by neighbourhood; neighbourhood composition correlates with ethnicity; the "school quality" variable is partly a proxy for the ethnicity composition of the school. Separating school-quality effects from peer-group-composition effects is methodologically hard on PISA data (which doesn't capture school demographic composition well).

4. **PISA tests are culturally specific.** The test instruments were developed in OECD-cultural contexts; students from different cultural/educational traditions may perform differently for reasons related to test-taking culture rather than underlying ability. The framework should acknowledge this limitation, though PISA attempts cross-cultural calibration.

5. **OECD aggregation masks within-country variation.** Large countries (US, DE) have more variation within than between origin groups. Averaging obscures this — and the politically-relevant question is often about specific subgroup patterns in specific regions, not national averages.

6. **Parental education alone is not parenting practice.** Two parents with the same years-of-education can differ materially in how they engage with children's schooling, how much language-of-instruction they use at home, whether they have time for homework supervision. The ESCS index captures part of this but not all; "parental education" as a channel partially absorbs practice differences that aren't captured in the instrument.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report country-specific results separately. Pan-OECD averages are informative for general pattern but policy-relevant findings are at country level.
- Disaggregate by specific origin regions (not just immigrant-vs-native) where PISA sample sizes permit.
- Separately report 1st-gen, 2nd-gen, and (where data permits) 3rd-gen — with generational-change statistics rather than just level comparisons.
- Interact parental-SES with destination-country school-quality-equality index and explicitly report whether SES-sensitivity varies by destination policy regime.
- Where specific origin × destination cells show non-convergence, REPORT IT — and examine destination-country policy moderators rather than re-aggregating the data.
- Include a "what the residual means" section: unexplained origin-group differences after SES + school + language controls are most plausibly destination-country-policy-reception effects or unmeasured parenting-practice variation, NOT ethnicity-as-independent-cause. The framework should flag the limits of its identification rather than overclaim.
