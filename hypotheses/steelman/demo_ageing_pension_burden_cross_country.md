# Steelman: Ageing × pension burden (OECD 1990-2023)

## Strongest case for the claim

PAYG pension architecture mechanically links pension expenditure to old-age dependency: as
the dependency ratio rises, transfer obligations rise proportionally unless replacement rates
or eligibility ages are cut. Cross-country evidence (OECD Pensions at a Glance) shows pension
shares of GDP have risen approximately in line with old-age dependency in Italy, France,
Japan, Greece. Crowding-out of public investment is a fiscal-arithmetic prediction once
deficits are constrained.

## Strongest case against

(1) Pension reforms (raising retirement age, indexation changes, capital-funded buffers)
break the mechanical link in many OECD countries (Sweden 1998, Germany 2004, Italy 1995/2011).
(2) Public investment is squeezed by many things; pension share is one of several covariates.
(3) Funded pension systems (NLD, CHE, AUS) decouple old-age dependency from public expenditure.

## Key uncertainties

- Reform-adjusted vs. raw elasticities.
- Whether crowding-out manifests as lower investment or higher debt.

## What would change my mind

A finding that the elasticity is below 0.4 or that countries with funded pension architecture
show no relationship while PAYG countries do — that would actually support the structural
claim more sharply by isolating the mechanism. A pure null finding across both architectures
would refute the headline claim.
