# Steelman — Labour share decline & capital mobility (OECD panel)

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

A market-liberal / institutionalist reader would argue that capital-account openness is the wrong "treatment" because it is endogenous to a country's institutional quality and trade exposure. Countries that liberalised the capital account in the 1980s and 1990s did so as part of a wider package — product-market deregulation, EU single-market accession, China-shock trade exposure, and IT-driven capital deepening — and the labour-share decline observed in those countries is the joint product of all of these forces, not a pure consequence of capital mobility raising capital's outside option.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Karabarbounis-Neiman dominance.** The strongest competing reading is that the price of investment goods fell secularly post-1980, capital and labour are gross substitutes (sigma > 1), and rising K/L mechanically reduced the labour share. Their cross-country decomposition explains roughly half the OECD labour-share decline with capital-deepening alone. The current spec partially conditions on this with PWT capital stock, but cannot fully disentangle openness from contemporaneous capital-deepening because they co-trend.

2. **Chinn-Ito is a de jure index.** KAOPEN measures legal restrictions on capital flows, not actual gross capital-flow volumes. De facto openness (gross flows / GDP) sometimes diverges sharply from de jure openness, especially in continental Europe pre-Maastricht. The hypothesis is testing the wrong measurement layer for the bargaining-power channel — what matters for worker bargaining is the credibility of the offshoring threat, which is closer to de facto openness. A de facto specification (Lane-Milesi-Ferretti external assets/GDP) might give a different result.

3. **Union density is endogenous to openness.** The Marxian reading treats union density as a moderator. But unions weakened over the same period as openness rose, partly because openness made unionised industries less viable. Treating union density as a control or interaction variable is closer to a bad-control problem than to a clean moderator design.

4. **Reverse causality / selection.** Countries with already-falling labour shares (because of declining manufacturing competitiveness) may have been more likely to liberalise the capital account to attract inward FDI. The IV with global liberalisation waves only partly addresses this; the exclusion restriction is contestable because global waves correlate with the China shock.

5. **The 2 percentage-point magnitude is heavy.** Empirical literature on capital-account openness and labour share (Furceri-Loungani, Jayadev) finds smaller effects in panel-FE specifications, typically 0.5-1 percentage point per standard-deviation move in openness, not 2pp over five years. The threshold may be set tighter than the literature would predict; the hypothesis must be honest if it falsifies on a "directionally correct but smaller magnitude" finding.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report the openness coefficient alongside the capital-deepening coefficient with comparable scaling, so readers can see which channel dominates.
- Run the de facto (Lane-Milesi-Ferretti) specification as a robustness column.
- Report the IV first-stage F-statistic; flag if instrument is weak.
- Treat the union-density interaction as a secondary test, not the headline.
- If the result supports a directionally-correct but smaller-than-2pp effect, qualify as "supported but smaller-than-claimed magnitude" rather than full support.
