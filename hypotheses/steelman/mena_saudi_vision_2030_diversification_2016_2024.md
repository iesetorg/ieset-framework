# Steelman — Saudi Vision 2030 diversification 2016-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Non-oil GDP share rises mechanically when oil prices fall.** The 2014-2016 oil-price collapse depressed Saudi oil GDP for years; the "non-oil share" rose without any non-oil sector growth. A clean test must measure non-oil GDP in level (real $) terms, not as a share. The framework's share-based test is vulnerable to oil-price-driven artifacts.

2. **PIF mega-projects are state-capital allocation, not market-driven.** NEOM, Red Sea Project, Qiddiya, Diriyah are massive sovereign-wealth-funded projects whose construction GDP is being booked now while their commercial viability is decades away. Counting these as "successful diversification" GDP is premature; if NEOM fails commercially, Saudi will have substituted oil-rent dependency for unproductive-construction dependency.

3. **Female labour-force participation gain is real but a regulatory unbundling, not Vision 2030.** Women driving was the 2017 royal decree; mahram requirements relaxed 2019; entertainment-sector and tourism sector opened simultaneously. These are correctly attributed to MBS-era reforms but the labour participation gain reflects opening latent supply, not Vision 2030 industrial-policy creating new jobs. Once the unbundling effect is exhausted, sustained LFP growth requires actual labour-demand creation.

4. **Saudisation policy has produced employment via quotas not productivity.** The Nitaqat programme requires firms to hire Saudi nationals, which raised Saudi employment numerically but reduced firm productivity and pushed expat employment abroad. Aggregate-employment metrics overstate the productive-employment gain.

5. **The 2017 Ritz-Carlton consolidation and subsequent expropriations damaged property-rights expectations.** Foreign investors and Saudi private-sector capital experienced reduced certainty about state-vs-private boundaries. WGI rule-of-law scores have not improved. The institutional-reform component of Vision 2030 is mixed at best.

6. **Tourism arrivals from a near-zero baseline are easy to grow.** Saudi Arabia opened tourism visas in 2019. Going from ~0 to a few million arrivals is mechanical; achieving the Vision 2030 target of 100m visitors/yr by 2030 is a different matter and current trajectory is well below path.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report non-oil GDP in real-level terms, not just share.
- Report PIF-project construction-GDP separately from non-oil organic activity.
- Acknowledge female-LFP gain as regulatory-unbundling, not industrial-policy success.
- Report Saudisation employment quality alongside quantity.
- Frame as "ambitious reform programme with mixed early signals" rather than completed transformation.
