# Steelman — Yugoslav self-management productivity, 1965-1980

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The market-socialist reading attributes Yugoslav 1965-1980 productivity growth to the worker-self-managed firm form. The strongest counter is that the headline GDP-per-capita series — which is the only available productivity proxy on disk for the period — is contaminated by three Yugoslavia-specific factors that have nothing to do with firm governance, and that an honest decomposition would attribute most of the measured growth to those factors rather than to self-management.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **The growth was leverage-financed, not productivity-financed.** Yugoslavia ran current-account deficits through the 1970s funded by syndicated Eurodollar borrowing and remittances from the gastarbeiter diaspora in West Germany and Austria. By 1980 external debt had risen to roughly 25-30% of GDP and the country was negotiating with the IMF; by 1983 the rescheduling was unavoidable. Growth funded by external borrowing that subsequently defaulted is not evidence of firm-level productivity — it is evidence of a soft budget constraint that delayed adjustment. A productivity claim that survives only on a window ending in 1980, immediately before the debt crisis hits, is suspiciously time-truncated.

2. **The proxy is GDP per capita, not productivity.** Strict productivity = output per worker-hour. Maddison gdppc divides output by population, conflating labour-force growth, female labour-force participation changes, and demographic structure. In a country with rural-to-urban migration and rising LFP through 1965-1980, gdppc growth systematically overstates per-worker productivity growth. The PWT series that would allow proper TFP decomposition for YUG simply do not exist on disk for this period — the absence is informative.

3. **The Maddison YUG series itself is a back-cast splice.** Maddison reports YUG and the six successor republics with surprisingly consistent growth rates (BIH 4.09%, MKD 4.64%, MNE 4.95%, HRV 4.96%, SRB 4.79%, SVN 5.27%). The republican series for 1965-1980 are constructed by allocating federal Yugoslav accounts back to republics using contemporary republican-account weights, with deflators that historians (Vinski, Mencinger) have flagged as plagued by the federal pricing-system distortions self-management itself created. The "comparable to southern Europe" finding is conditional on a deflator regime that the hypothesis is supposed to be testing.

4. **Selection of the comparison set flatters the result.** Southern Europe 1965-1980 is the canonical "convergence club" period — Spain, Portugal, Greece, and Italy were all catching up to the European core under autarky, autocracy, and post-autocracy regimes that themselves had unusual investment rates. Picking this comparison set anchors the test on a high-growth window. A more demanding comparison would include Belgium, Netherlands, Denmark, Austria — small open economies that were already at the productivity frontier — where Yugoslav growth would still look high but where the convergence-mechanics interpretation would be stronger than the firm-form interpretation.

5. **The institutional shock identified isn't really 1965.** The hypothesis attributes growth to self-management institutionalised "post-1953", but the 1965 market reform broadened firm autonomy substantially, and the 1976 Associated Labour Act recentralised it. A regime that changed character mid-window is not a clean treatment. The 4.67% YUG growth average obscures whether the early period (1965-1973) outperformed the late period (1974-1980) in ways consistent with the reform-and-counter-reform narrative.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Note that the productivity proxy is GDP per capita, not output per worker-hour, and that PWT TFP for YUG is unavailable. The result is conditional on this proxy.

(b) Present the successor-republic growth rates as informative, since they show that the YUG headline isn't anomalously high — it sits in the middle of the republican distribution, which weakens the "self-management caused this" attribution because the republics had heterogeneous institutional starting points.

(c) Acknowledge that ending the window at 1980 truncates immediately before the 1983 debt crisis, which would dramatically rebalance the assessment if the window were extended to 1985 or 1990.

(d) Be transparent that the result SUPPORTS the descriptive claim of comparability but does NOT support the causal claim that self-management produced the comparable growth. Establishing the causal claim requires conditioning on external borrowing, terms-of-trade windfalls, and a proper TFP decomposition — none of which are achievable with on-disk vintages for YUG in this window. A v2 should incorporate the 1981-1990 extension and a leverage-conditional comparison.

The strongest version of the steelman argues that the headline finding is correct in the descriptive sense — Yugoslav real GDP per capita did grow at roughly the southern-European pace 1965-1980 — but that this descriptive equivalence is a weaker form of evidence than the market-socialist school typically claims. The institutional-form mechanism is one of several plausible explanations and is not separately identified by the test the spec runs.
