# Steelman — Regulatory uncertainty chills investment (OECD 2000-2023)

## The strongest opposing argument

The "regulatory uncertainty chills investment" framing inverts the causal arrow on the central post-2008 policy story. Investment did not fall because regulation tightened; regulation tightened because the lightly-regulated 2002-2007 expansion produced the largest financial crisis since 1929 and the largest carbon emissions surge in human history. Treating that regulatory response as a cost — rather than as an internalisation of previously-unpriced social externalities — pre-loads the test against finding the welfare-relevant story.

**Key progressive / mainstream-Keynesian objections:**

1. **Reverse causality is dominant.** Heavy regulatory activity is concentrated in busy expansionary periods (lots to regulate) and slack periods produce regulatory pauses. The cross-country panel cannot separate "regulation chills investment" from "investment booms drive regulatory activity that lags by 2-4 years."

2. **The investment-share decline is over-determined.** Post-2008 OECD investment-share weakness reflects secular stagnation (Summers, Eichengreen), the demographic transition (working-age population peak), the China shock disrupting price-formation in tradable goods, sectoral shift from physical capital to intangible capital (which is mismeasured in NIPA NE.GDI.FPRV.ZS), and the energy-transition pause. Pinning the decline on regulatory uncertainty cherry-picks one driver among many.

3. **Mismeasured intangibles.** The 2010-2020s investment-share decline in physical capital coincides with an investment-share *rise* in intangibles (software, R&D, brand, organisational capital — the Corrado-Hulten-Sichel literature). NE.GDI.FPRV.ZS captures the former and misses much of the latter. The "regulation chills investment" finding may be measuring a real composition shift away from physical capital toward intangibles, which is consistent with high-productivity adjustment, not chilling.

4. **OECD PMR is not a regulatory-uncertainty measure.** PMR captures regulatory level (rules in force), not uncertainty (volatility of rules in force). Using PMR yoy change as an uncertainty proxy is a substantial measurement stretch — a country can have stable, predictable, and high regulation (Germany, Switzerland) and a country can have unstable, unpredictable, and low regulation (Argentina pre-Milei). The hypothesis is testing a level effect dressed up as an uncertainty effect.

## What the framework still captures

The investment-uncertainty link is among the best-documented empirical findings in modern macro (Bloom 2009, Baker-Bloom-Davis 2016). Whether the post-2008 OECD regulatory acceleration counts as the kind of uncertainty that chills investment is a real and contested question. The framework's pre-registered test asks whether the panel pattern shows up in the data; a "supported" verdict still leaves open whether the welfare verdict goes Hayek's way (regulation as unpriced cost) or progressive way (regulation as internalisation of externality). The test is informative either way.
