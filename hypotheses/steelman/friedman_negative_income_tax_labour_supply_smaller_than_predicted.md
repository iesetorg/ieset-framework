# Steelman — NIT and EITC labour-supply effect

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is that it conflates two empirically distinct programmes and uses the more favourable EITC results to vindicate the less favourable NIT predictions. The NIT experiments produced internally consistent estimates of male-head-of-household hours reductions in the 5-9% range and married-women hours reductions of 17-20%, which is hardly "smaller than predicted" — Killingsworth and Heckman's contemporaneous reviews concluded the experiments confirmed conservative critics' work-disincentive concerns more than they refuted them. The EITC expansion findings are positive on labour-force participation but do not support the bundled-mechanism interpretation: EITC raises the after-tax wage on the extensive margin (reduces marginal effective tax rate from 100%+ to roughly 60% for low-earners exiting traditional welfare) but raises the marginal effective tax rate on the intensive margin in the phase-out range, producing a Hoynes-Patel finding that hours are reduced for already-employed claimants. The single-mother LFPR gain is therefore a net result of a large extensive-margin gain offset by a smaller intensive-margin loss, and the framing that "labour-supply dis-employment is smaller than predicted" is misleading: the dis-employment is differently distributed, not smaller in aggregate. The 1986-1996 EITC effect is also confounded by the mid-1990s labour-market boom, the 1996 PRWORA reform, and the 1990 minimum-wage increase, all of which changed the binding constraint on single-mother employment. Eissa-Liebman's identification strategy isolates EITC, but the post-1996 cohort's LFPR gains are consistent with a welfare-reform-plus-strong-labour-market reading rather than an EITC-specific reading. From the progressive side, Rothstein (2010) and others show that a substantial fraction of the EITC's value is captured by employers through wage reductions (incidence is split roughly 70-30 employee-employer in low-skill labour markets), so the policy delivers less to workers than the headline transfer suggests, even when LFPR rises. From the conservative side, Mead and others continue to argue that the NIT experiments understated dis-employment because the experimental window was short and participants knew the treatment would end, so steady-state effects would be larger. The hypothesis would be sharper if it tested only the EITC extensive-margin effect (which is robustly positive) and dropped the NIT intensive-margin claim (which is empirically more contested), since the bundled framing currently allows a conservative critic to take the NIT results and a progressive critic to take the EITC incidence results, and both can read the evidence as falsifying.
