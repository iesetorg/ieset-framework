# Steelman — REACH and EU chemicals firm exit

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "REACH produced chemicals-sector SME exit" framing:**

1. **The EU chemicals sector is world-leading under REACH.** BASF, Bayer, Covestro, Solvay, Evonik, Clariant, DSM, Syngenta, and multiple Nordic / Benelux specialty-chemicals producers remain global top-tier players under the full REACH regime. The historical record directly rebuts the naive "EU regulation kills chemicals" narrative. Any finding of SME exit must be situated alongside the continued global competitiveness of EU chemicals majors — and the headline should NEVER read "REACH killed EU chemicals." If SME margin is affected while majors grow, the net welfare effect is ambiguous (consolidation under regulation is a known pattern and may be efficiency-enhancing in some dimensions).

2. **Consumer and worker health benefits from REACH are enormous and unmeasured.** REACH was designed to identify and restrict endocrine disruptors, persistent bioaccumulative toxins, CMR (carcinogenic, mutagenic, reprotoxic) substances, and to require safety data sheets for hazardous compounds. Health economists have estimated benefits in the hundreds of billions of euros over decades. A hypothesis measuring only firm-count and SME-share outcomes loads the ledger against the policy; the writeup must flag this asymmetry clearly. "Fewer substance registrations" may be a feature (riskier substances discontinued), not a bug.

3. **Substance-discontinuation is intended outcome, not failure.** Part of REACH's design is that high-hazard or low-volume substances should have their registration cost raised, forcing producers to either justify continued manufacture with safety data or discontinue. If data show reduced substance diversity on EU registries, that is partly the regulation working as intended. Framing this as "regulation caused exit" imports a pre-policy benchmark where hazardous-substance production with minimal data was the default — a benchmark the policy explicitly rejects.

4. **Global chemical market dynamics 2008-2023 are dominated by Chinese scale, not EU regulation.** The 2008-2023 window saw massive Chinese chemical capacity build-out (ethylene, PTA, urea, specialty intermediates) that reshaped global market shares independent of EU regulatory dynamics. Attributing EU chemical-sector SME exit or firm-count declines to REACH when Chinese competitive pressure is a likely dominant driver requires tight identification the DiD design may not provide. The steelman: most of the post-2008 EU chemicals SME-exit signal is China, not REACH.

5. **SME-share metric is noisy and small in absolute terms.** EU chemicals is structurally concentrated; SME share has been low for decades and is dominated by specialty, distribution, and formulation firms rather than bulk producers. Measurement fluctuations in Eurostat SBS are substantial. A 1-2 percentage-point SME-share shift may be statistical noise, merger-and-acquisition reclassification, or administrative redefinition rather than real firm-count effects. Be cautious about reading small SME-share movements as causal signal.

6. **REACH costs fall most heavily on specific producers, not the sector as a whole.** Producers of rare substances with small volumes face the highest registration-cost-per-revenue ratio and bear a disproportionate share of REACH cost. If these specific producers exit or reduce substance variety, sector aggregates may barely move. The narrow "specialty/rare substance producer" margin may show sharp effects that do not matter for sector VA. Reporting aggregate-sector outcomes alongside narrow margin outcomes clarifies where the cost actually bites.

7. **Non-EU substance-count comparison is structurally imperfect.** REACH's registration system is unique to the EU; comparing "registered substances in EU" to "TSCA active list in USA" or "CSCL / K-REACH elsewhere" requires harmonisation choices that bias in unknown directions. Cross-border comparability at the substance level is weak; the DiD design is partly constrained by data asymmetry.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Lead with the EU chemicals sector's continued global leadership among majors. Any negative finding on SME exit must sit alongside this.
- Disaggregate by firm size (SME vs large) and by subsector (bulk vs specialty vs formulation). Aggregate effects will be small; margin-specific effects may be larger.
- Distinguish "substance discontinuation" as intended outcome from "firm exit" as a welfare-relevant outcome. Not all substance-count decline is regulatory failure.
- Explicitly acknowledge the unmeasured consumer/worker-welfare benefits as a separate ledger the hypothesis does not evaluate.
- Control for Chinese chemical-sector capacity growth explicitly. Much of the 2008-2023 SME exit is plausibly Chinese competition, not REACH.
- Report 2010 vs 2018 deadline differential. If the mechanism is "fixed registration cost spread over small volumes hurts SMEs," the 2018 deadline should bite harder than 2010. If not, the mechanism story is wrong.
- Be careful about causal language. The identification relies on staggered DiD with a structurally thin EU-US comparison; do not overclaim.
