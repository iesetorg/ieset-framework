# Steelman: National Champions Long-Run Productivity Drag

## Strongest version of the claim
National-champion policies that shield dominant domestic firms from competitive pressure create long-run productivity stagnation by reducing incentives for cost reduction, innovation, and dynamic reallocation. After an initial scale-up phase (often driven by state subsidies, protected home markets, and preferential credit), champion-dominated sectors exhibit slower TFP growth than sectors with lower concentration and stronger competitive entry. This drag persists even in high-R&D sectors because protected incumbents prioritise rent defence over risky innovation.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Sector-level panel data showing negative correlation between top-firm concentration and subsequent TFP growth across OECD and emerging markets.
2. Firm-level data showing lower innovation rates (patents, R&D intensity) and higher markups in champion-protected sectors.
3. Event studies of deregulation or trade liberalisation in champion sectors showing productivity spurts after competitive entry.
4. Cross-country comparison of auto, steel, and telecom sectors with varying champion policies.

## Best counterarguments
- National champions generate scale economies and R&D spillovers that competitive fragmentation cannot replicate (Airbus, Samsung, TSMC).
- Global markets are winner-take-all; domestic concentration may be necessary to create globally competitive firms.
- Champion sectors often operate in industries with high fixed costs and long development cycles where short-run TFP measures are misleading.
- State aid may correct for capital-market failures in high-risk sectors (semiconductors, aerospace, green energy).
- Measurement error: TFP is residual and may misattribute technological catch-up to factor accumulation.

## Boundary conditions
Claim is strongest in mature, tradable sectors with low global scale thresholds. Weak in frontier technology sectors with extreme R&D fixed costs and strong export-discipline conditions.
