# Steelman — Occupational licensing and consumer-price burden

## The strongest opposing argument

Friedman 1962 and Kleiner-Krueger 2013 framed occupational licensing as a textbook barrier-to-entry that protects incumbent rents at consumer expense. The strongest opposing reading is more nuanced and partly compatible with the data:

1. **Quality improvement is real and unmeasured.** Licensing requirements raise minimum service quality in ways the spec cannot directly observe. If consumers pay more for higher-quality licensed services, the consumer-welfare effect is ambiguous even if the price effect is positive. The hypothesis cannot distinguish "rent capture" from "quality premium" with CPI data alone.

2. **Public-safety externalities.** Licensing in healthcare, electrical work, and food handling addresses genuine externalities. A flat "licensing → consumer loss" frame ignores externality-correction value. Friedman's own treatment acknowledged this and was sharper about cosmetology and interior-design licensing than about medicine.

3. **Measurement of licensing intensity is poor pre-2015.** CPS occupational-licensing supplements only became standard in 2015. Earlier figures rely on Kleiner-Krueger surveys with substantial measurement error. The panel-FE coefficient identified off pre-2015 variation may be partly noise.

4. **Endogeneity.** States with higher service-cost trajectories may license more occupations defensively (licensing as a response to cost pressure, not a cause of it). The within-state FE identification mitigates this only if the policy timing is plausibly exogenous.

5. **Distributional offset.** Even if licensing raises consumer prices on average, it raises wages of licensed workers, who tend to be middle-income. The aggregate consumer-loss frame ignores the distributional transfer dimension.

6. **Reciprocity reform null.** Recent reciprocity-reform episodes (Arizona 2019, others) have not produced large measurable price drops in licensed sectors yet. If licensing intensity were doing serious work, the deregulation events should reverse it visibly.

## What the framework still captures

The dual-outcome gate (price up AND employment down) and the 2015-2022 INFORMATIVE check let the heterodox reading win cleanly if the headline result is artefactual. The spec is honest about its limits — it tests the Friedman price-channel claim, not the full consumer-welfare proposition. A v2 with reciprocity-reform RDD would be much stronger; this is a candidate-status first cut.
