# Steelman — Czech 2017-2019 minimum-wage rises

The strongest disemployment-prediction reading: even in tight labour markets, a 21% cumulative nominal minimum-wage rise should generate measurable disemployment among marginal-skill workers (Roma minority, low-education young workers, peripheral-region low-productivity firms). Czech National Bank research division work documents that the wage compression at the bottom of the distribution did rise meaningfully, and that wage-floor effects extend up the distribution by approximately 1.5x the legal floor (spillover effect) — meaning the labour-cost rise was larger than the headline 21%. The synthetic-control donor pool comparison may understate disemployment if Slovakia and Hungary also raised their floors in coordinated fashion (which they did), absorbing the treatment effect. The bottom-decile-paired outcome is the discipline: if the real-wage gap is below +12%, evasion or non-compliance has substituted for legal employment, and the apparent employment-neutrality is a measurement artefact. A v2 should use Czech administrative wage data (ISPV) by region to identify regional heterogeneity, with peripheral low-wage regions (Ústecký, Karlovarský) as the test for binding.
