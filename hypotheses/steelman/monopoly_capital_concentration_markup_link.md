# Steelman — Monopoly capital: concentration & markup link

**The strongest argument against the Marxian monopoly-capital reading:**

The rising-concentration / rising-markup correlation in the post-1980 US is real but admits at least three non-power readings. The monopoly-capital framing has to do affirmative work to rule them out, and a clean industry-FE test conditioning on productivity dispersion typically deflates the structural-power coefficient substantially.

Specific objections:

1. **Superstar firms (Autor-Dorn-Katz-Patterson-Van Reenen).** Industries with the largest concentration increases are also the industries with the largest within-industry productivity dispersion. The most productive firms gain market share for productivity reasons, and the Lerner-index "markup" they earn is a return to intangible capital and process innovation, not a power rent. Aggregate markups rise as the high-productivity-firm share of activity rises; this is a welfare-improving reallocation, not monopoly extraction.

2. **De Loecker-Eeckhout's own measurement caveats.** The 60% rise in markups DLE report is sensitive to (a) whether SG&A is treated as fixed cost or variable input — DLE's choice to treat it as fixed mechanically pushes markups up over the period; (b) the cost-share weights used in production-function estimation; (c) the global-vs-domestic operations of multinationals. Traina, Karabarbounis-Kondonis, and Bond-Hashemi-Kaplan-Zoch all reproduce the dataset and find materially smaller markup increases under defensible alternative choices.

3. **Fixed-cost / digital-scale industries.** Software, pharma, and platform businesses have very high gross margins because their marginal cost is near zero, not because they have power. A markup measure that compares price to marginal cost will mechanically rise as the economy shifts toward fixed-cost industries — this is a sectoral-composition effect, not a monopoly-capital effect.

4. **Concentration vs market power are different concepts.** Local-market concentration in the US (e.g., labour markets, hospital markets) has actually been falling over the same period that national concentration has been rising; consumers and workers face the relevant local concentration, not the national. A national-concentration-rising story is consistent with intensified national competition for local markets — the Walmart effect.

5. **Markup rises do not require concentration; concentration rises do not require markup increase.** Cross-industry, the correlation between concentration changes and markup changes is positive but modest in industry-FE specifications (Berry-Gaynor-Scott Morton review; Syverson). The headline aggregate-time-series correlation is doing most of the work, and aggregate-time-series is consistent with many causal stories.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Lead with the industry-FE coefficient, NOT the aggregate-time-series correlation; the latter has minimal degrees of freedom.
- Report results both before and after the productivity-dispersion control; the difference is the "monopoly-capital residual".
- Address SG&A and cost-share sensitivity if firm-level markups are used.
- State explicitly that even a "supported" finding does not by itself imply a welfare loss; markup rise + productivity dispersion is welfare-ambiguous.
- Treat post-2015 sub-sample as a meaningful sub-test: if aggregate markup share is flat or falling there, the dynamic story is weaker than the static story suggests.
