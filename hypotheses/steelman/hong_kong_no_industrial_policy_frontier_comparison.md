# Steelman: Hong Kong Frontier Convergence Without Industrial Policy

## Strongest version of the claim
Hong Kong achieved income convergence to the frontier through laissez-faire institutions, free trade, and strong rule of law rather than through state-led industrial targeting. The strongest version predicts that, after controlling for initial income and human capital, Hong Kong's TFP and GDP-per-capita growth are statistically indistinguishable from or superior to Korea's, Taiwan's, and Singapore's.

## Key evidence the claim would need
- Panel estimates showing that low industrial-policy intensity combined with high economic freedom predicts TFP growth as well as or better than high industrial-policy intensity.
- Hong Kong's convergence trajectory does not significantly underperform comparator economies after 1960.
- Robustness to excluding financial-sector booms and real-estate appreciation.

## Best counterarguments
- Hong Kong's entrepôt geography and British legal inheritance are massive confounders that cannot be fully controlled.
- Hong Kong's manufacturing base hollowed out early; its prosperity depends heavily on finance and services, which may not generalise.
- The comparison is unfair to Korea and Taiwan, which started from much lower initial income and had to build industrial capability from scratch.

## Boundary conditions
- The claim applies to city-states or small open economies with strong institutions.
- It does not imply that industrial policy is useless for large, lower-income countries lacking comparable geographic or institutional advantages.
