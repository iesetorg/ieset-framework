# Steelman — Milei reforms reduce Argentine inflation

**The strongest arguments against coding the 2024-2025 inflation decline as vindication of the Milei programme:**

1. **Severe recession costs 2024H1 were real and substantial.** The sub-5% monthly inflation was achieved alongside a GDP contraction of roughly 3-4% year-on-year peak-trough and an unemployment uptick. Austrian / monetarist framing emphasises that the real-adjustment costs of stabilisation are finite and front-loaded; post-Keynesian framing emphasises that they are distributionally regressive and can be avoided with heterodox stabilisation. A clean falsification test must report the output-side cost alongside the inflation-side gain; treating the inflation decline as an unqualified success without the distributional accounting is methodologically incomplete.

2. **Poverty rates spiked to peak ~52% (UCA Observatorio) in 2024H1.** This is a specific distributional consequence of the devaluation-first stabilisation pattern. A proper result card must report the poverty trajectory, the Gini trajectory, and the real-wage trajectory over the same window. Ignoring these would parallel the same critique applied to Thatcherism: inflation discipline that imposes the cost disproportionately on the bottom deciles is not distributionally neutral.

3. **External tailwinds were favourable.** Soybean / agricultural commodity prices were stable-to-rising in 2024; Brazilian real depreciation made Argentina's tradable sector competitive at the new peso rate; global disinflation provided a supportive backdrop. A stabilisation programme run in a favourable external environment is not proof that the same programme would succeed in a hostile one. Attribution to policy content without controlling for external conditions overstates the policy share of variance.

4. **The episode is comparable in magnitude to sanctions-era or war-era shocks.** The 54% December 2023 devaluation plus the ~30% real spending cut year-one constitute a real-economy shock of a scale that historical comparisons (Russia 1998, Ukraine post-2014, etc.) suggest should produce deep-and-persistent hysteresis. The fact that GDP recovered in 2024H2+ is notable but does not settle whether the long-run productive-capacity damage was material.

5. **The 18-month threshold may not survive FX-liberalisation turbulence expected 2025.** Lifting the cepo (FX controls) is expected to produce a one-off price-level adjustment. If the adjustment pushes monthly inflation back above 3% transiently, the strict falsification rule triggers even though the stabilisation programme's substance may be succeeding. The pre-registration is binding on the threshold; post-hoc re-specification would undermine the exercise.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report monthly inflation, GDP growth, unemployment, poverty headcount, and Gini across the entire 2023-2025 window. The inflation result is one series among several.
- Explicitly decompose the disinflation into (a) fiscal-regime-change channel, (b) peso-devaluation overshoot correction, (c) external-commodity and peer-inflation co-movement.
- Compare to pre-registered LatAm stabilisation benchmarks; do not benchmark only against Argentina's own prior runs (which share a policy-content trait that the hypothesis is trying to isolate).
- Acknowledge the distributional costs explicitly. The hypothesis is a narrow inflation-trajectory claim; framing it as "success of the Milei programme" without the distributional reporting would mis-represent the policy package's full impact.
- If FX-liberalisation produces a transient monthly inflation spike, pre-register NOW (in v1 notes) that a v1.1 re-specification will separately account for one-off price-level adjustments vs persistent inflation. V1 does not permit this ex-post adjustment.
