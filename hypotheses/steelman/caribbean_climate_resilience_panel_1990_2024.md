# Steelman — Caribbean climate-resilience panel 1990-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis treats storm-intensity exposure as a primary driver of Caribbean growth lag. The opposing view is that institutional, debt-overhang, and tourism-cycle factors dominate, and that storm exposure is a smaller residual.

Specific objections:

1. **Tourism dependence and debt overhang dominate.** Caribbean economies face structural tourism-cycle and debt-servicing constraints that explain most of the LATAM gap; storm-intensity is a secondary modifier.

2. **CCRIF payouts are too small to be macroeconomically material.** CCRIF parametric payouts are typically <5% of damage; the policy effect on macro outcomes is structurally bounded.

3. **Storm-intensity proxy is methodologically fragile.** EM-DAT damage data are inconsistent across countries; IBTrACS storm tracks measure exposure imperfectly. The constructed storm-intensity index may not capture economically relevant exposure.

4. **Selection into CCRIF is endogenous.** Countries with better institutions joined CCRIF earlier; the "CCRIF effect" partly reflects institutional quality.

5. **Growth-gap residual may be measurement.** Small-island GDP series have substantial measurement error; the apparent growth gap may be partly statistical artefact.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report storm-coefficient with explicit data-quality flag.
(b) Decompose growth gap into tourism-cycle, debt-overhang, and storm components.
(c) Show CCRIF selection bias.
(d) Acknowledge measurement-error caveat for small economies.
