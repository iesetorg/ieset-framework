# Steelman: Protected Infant Industries Fail to Mature

## Strongest version of the claim
Infant-industry protection is theoretically justified when temporary tariffs or subsidies allow domestic firms to overcome initial scale disadvantages and learning costs. However, when protection persists beyond 15 years, it becomes a permanent subsidy to inefficiency. Protected incumbents lose incentive to innovate, fail to achieve export competitiveness, and lobby for indefinite extension. The result is lower long-run productivity growth and weaker export performance than in sectors with time-limited protection (3-10 years) or no protection at all. The 15-year threshold captures the transition from learning phase to entrenched rent-seeking.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Sector-level panel showing negative correlation between protection duration and subsequent export competitiveness.
2. Firm-level data showing R&D and export intensity declining with protection age.
3. Natural experiments of protection removal (e.g., India's gradual tariff liberalisation 1991-2007) showing productivity gains in previously protected sectors.
4. Comparison of sectors with statutory sunset clauses versus open-ended protection.

## Best counterarguments
- Some industries (semiconductors, aerospace, pharmaceuticals) require 20+ years to reach global competitiveness; 15 years is arbitrary.
- The East Asian miracle combined long protection with export discipline; protection duration alone is not the right metric without conditioning on performance requirements.
- Protected sectors may generate positive externalities (skilled labour pools, supplier networks) that are not captured in sectoral productivity or export metrics.
- Endogeneity: governments protect sectors that are inherently uncompetitive due to geography or factor endowments, not because protection caused the failure.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest for traditional manufacturing and agriculture in middle-income economies. Weak for frontier technology sectors and sectors with strong performance-monitoring institutions.
