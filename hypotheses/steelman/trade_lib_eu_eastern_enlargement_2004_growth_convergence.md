# Steelman — EU 2004 enlargement convergence

## The strongest opposing argument

1. **Donor-pool weakness.** The non-acceding Balkans (BIH, MKD,
   SRB, MNE) and Eastern European states (UKR, MDA, BLR) had
   structurally different histories (Yugoslav wars, post-Soviet
   institutional collapse). They are not clean comparators; any
   measured ATT may reflect their underperformance rather than
   the acceding states' overperformance.

2. **Pre-2004 anticipation effects.** EU candidate status from the
   mid-1990s drove acquis-adoption and FDI before formal
   accession. Treating 2004 as the discontinuity attributes
   gains to the formal date that were already accruing under
   candidate-status reforms. A cleaner spec would code treatment
   from candidate-status award rather than accession.

3. **Within-country divergence.** Aggregate GDP-per-capita
   convergence masks substantial within-country regional
   divergence: capital cities and western regions of CEE states
   converged rapidly while eastern peripheries lagged. The
   national average is a misleading welfare statistic.

4. **Post-2010 austerity reversal.** Hungary, Romania, and Latvia
   experienced severe austerity-driven contractions after 2010.
   For Latvia in particular, the 2008-2010 collapse was so deep
   that 2019 GDP-per-capita relative to EU-15 was barely above
   the pre-accession level on some measures.

## What the framework still captures

Despite the donor-pool issue, the post-2004 trajectory of the
Visegrad-plus-Baltics group is striking and distinct from the
non-acceding comparators on virtually every measure: trade
openness, FDI inflows, GDP-per-capita levels. The PARTIAL verdict
path (openness up, growth ATT insignificant) operationalises the
heterodox critique cleanly. The candidate-status anticipation
critique is a legitimate identification concern handled in a
follow-on spec; the 2004 binary is the standard convention.
