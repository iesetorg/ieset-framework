# Steelman — NZ Rogernomics 1984 productivity effect

## Best form of the claim

Lange-Douglas Rogernomics (1984-1993) was the most thoroughgoing
liberalisation of an OECD economy in the post-WWII era — tariffs
abolished, SOEs corporatised then privatised, financial markets
deregulated, the kiwi floated, agricultural subsidies cut to near
zero. The classical-liberal prediction is that this supply-side
reorganisation should show up first in measurable total factor
productivity (the Solow residual): once misallocation falls and firms
face world prices, the same capital and labour produce more output.
The sharpest, dispositive version of the claim is therefore that NZ's
TFP path 1995-2005 should sit measurably above a synthetic
counterfactual built from advanced-economy donors that did not
undertake comparable shock therapy in the same window.

## Weakest defensible form

A weaker variant — "Rogernomics did not lower NZ's productivity
relative to peers" — would survive even a small or zero TFP gap,
but it is no longer an "acceleration" claim. The framework's primary
test deliberately probes the strong form. An agent who wants to
argue the weak form should open a separate hypothesis, because the
falsification logic is different (one-sided non-inferiority instead
of a positive effect).

## Why the strong form might be wrong

NZ's measured TFP acceleration in the late 1990s is contested:
the NZ Productivity Commission, the OECD, and McKinsey reports have
repeatedly found that NZ remains below the OECD productivity frontier
two decades after reform. Plausible mechanisms for a non-result:

1. The reforms also dismantled industry training, R&D and
   apprenticeship infrastructure, depressing the K- and H-channels
   that feed into TFP measurement.
2. NZ's small domestic market caps scale economies regardless of
   openness; the "tariff-removal → catch-up" channel is bounded by
   geography.
3. The synthetic donor pool itself includes Ireland (Celtic Tiger)
   and Finland (Nokia / EU accession), both of which had their own
   reform booms in the 1990s. That raises the counterfactual
   benchmark and biases the gap downward — the test is therefore
   unusually demanding.
4. Several Rogernomics-era reforms (RBA Act 1989, Fiscal
   Responsibility Act 1994) are about institutional discipline, not
   productivity per se. A refutation here is consistent with reforms
   delivering inflation discipline and fiscal sustainability without
   the headline TFP gain — those institutional channels are tested
   separately by `nz_rogernomics_institutional_complements`.
