# Steelman — State size reduces household income growth

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The claim that larger general-government-consumption shares are causally associated with slower household income growth is a cross-country regression artefact dressed up as a structural finding. The sample spans economies whose state-size variation reflects very different kinds of state — Scandinavian high-capacity social-insurance states, East Asian coordination states, Southern European clientelist states, Latin American extractive-rentier states. Pooling these under a single "state size" treatment treats them as if they were drawn from the same data-generating process, and they are not. The panel coefficient is a weighted average of utterly different causal regimes; the scalar "1pp bigger state → 0.05-0.20pp slower income growth" is a population-level statistic with no structural interpretation for any individual country considering any specific policy change.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Taiwan 1960-1990 — high state coordination plus high growth.** Taiwan under the KMT had state-owned enterprises across banking, steel, petrochemicals, telecoms, and airlines; government directed credit, industrial targeting through the Council for Economic Planning and Development, and explicit picking of sectoral winners. General-government consumption was modest but the state's coordinating role was enormous. Household income grew at one of the fastest sustained rates in 20th-century history. A coefficient on "government consumption / GDP" does not capture Taiwan's state-size in any meaningful sense; if it did, Taiwan would look like a small-state case, which would misclassify the most informative counter-example the hypothesis faces. The scalar IV is underspecified for the relationship it purports to measure.

2. **Norway — oil fund plus top-three living standards.** Norway has one of the highest general-government-consumption shares in the sample (typically 24-28% of GDP) and simultaneously one of the highest real household disposable incomes per capita. The Norwegian SWF is state-owned and the Norwegian labour-market model involves extensive coordinated wage-setting. Any specification that produces a negative coefficient on state size is simultaneously producing a large Norwegian residual; the hypothesis's "explanation" of Norway will have to be "Norway is an outlier for resource-rent reasons," which is exactly the kind of ex-post rescue clause the pre-registration is supposed to rule out. If the coefficient is driven by cases like Venezuela and loses magnitude once Norway is correctly handled, the headline claim needs material qualification.

3. **Post-war France dirigisme — Trente Glorieuses strong growth.** France 1945-1975 ran a planned economy with indicative-planning apparatus (Commissariat général du Plan), large nationalised sectors (Renault, EDF, SNCF, banks), and explicit state direction of credit. Real household income growth over the Trente Glorieuses was among the fastest in the OECD historical record. This is outside the 2000-2023 panel window, so the formal regression is not exposed to it; but the existence of the case shows that "state size reduces household income growth" is not a structural law — at minimum it is time-and-regime-specific, and the hypothesis should say so rather than presenting itself as a general finding.

4. **Singapore — high state ownership at the top of the income distribution.** Temasek Holdings and GIC control a portfolio of state-linked companies that account for a substantial share of Singaporean market capitalisation; the state owns the land; public housing accommodates roughly 80% of the population. Singapore has among the highest real household incomes in the sample. A "state size" measure that relies on general-government consumption misses Singapore's state entirely, because the Singaporean state's footprint is via ownership and real-estate holdings, not via current consumption spending. This is a specification-validity problem, not a minor confounder: the primary IV does not measure what the hypothesis claims to test.

5. **South Korea 1961-1997 — industrial policy catch-up.** Park Chung-hee's developmental state, the HCI (Heavy and Chemical Industrialisation) drive, state-directed credit through the Korea Development Bank, and the chaebol-state coordination complex produced the fastest sustained catch-up growth in modern history. Korea in this period had a non-trivial general-government footprint and an enormous state directive-coordinative footprint. The income growth was flat-out spectacular. As with Taiwan, the primary IV misclassifies the case; as with Post-war France, the timing is outside the panel window but the structural lesson is not.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report the synthetic-control results for the four pre-registered pivots separately from the panel coefficient, with each pivot's fit, placebo distribution, and post-treatment trajectory displayed in full. A Chávez pivot that confirms and a Milei pivot that produces a null should be shown as a split, not averaged into a headline claim.

(b) Include a "state-type" moderator: an interaction between the state-size IV and an indicator for East-Asian-coordination-state / Nordic-SWF-state / clientelist-state. If the coefficient is materially different across state-types, the uniform-coefficient headline is misleading and the disaggregated view is the real finding.

(c) Report a "Norway residual" diagnostic: fit the panel model excluding NOR, then refit including NOR and report how much the country-specific residual contributes to the conditional income-growth deviation. If Norway is a multi-sigma residual, the model is misfitting a key case and the result card should say so.

(d) Flag explicitly that the 2000-2023 window excludes the Trente Glorieuses, Taiwan-1960s-miracle, and Korea-developmental-state periods. The finding, whatever it is, is a 21st-century OECD-panel finding and not a claim about "state size and growth" as a trans-historical relationship.

(e) Present the triangulation agreement count (how many of cross-section / panel-FE / synthetic-control pivots produce the pre-registered sign) before presenting any headline magnitude, so that the reader sees the robustness before seeing the point estimate.

The strongest version of the steelman points at an asymmetry: the hypothesis's market-liberal reading of the scalar coefficient is vulnerable to five named counter-cases the author explicitly acknowledges. Pre-registration commits to an honest update rule. If the panel is driven by Venezuelan collapse and Argentine pre-Milei stagnation, with OECD core countries showing null or positive coefficients, the substantive finding is "state failure reduces household income growth," not "state size reduces household income growth" — and the framework's prior should move accordingly.
