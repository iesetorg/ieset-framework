# Steelman — Freiburg Schuldenbremse growth-neutrality (DEU 2009-2024)

## The strongest opposing argument

A strong-Keynesian / post-Keynesian / MMT critic will reject this hypothesis on three grounds:

1. **Open-economy escape valve.** Germany's debt brake bound from 2010 onward, but Germany ran a current-account surplus throughout the 2010-2019 window. External demand (especially from China and the rest of the Eurozone) substituted for the suppressed domestic fiscal impulse. A closed-economy thought experiment with an identical fiscal rule would have produced a much larger output cost; reading the German experience as proof that fiscal rules are growth-neutral generalises a unique condition.

2. **Monetary offset.** ECB unconventional monetary policy (LTRO, OMT, QE, negative rates) ran for most of the binding-rule window. The interaction of constitutional fiscal restriction with maximally accommodative monetary policy is not the regime the strong-Keynesian critique targets — that critique targets fiscal restriction in a depressed economy with constrained monetary policy. The German 2010-2019 case isn't a clean test.

3. **Hidden quality decline.** Headline GDP and investment-share series miss the composition of investment. German public infrastructure (Autobahn, railways), Bundeswehr capability, and digital networks visibly degraded across the binding-rule window. The investment-share is roughly preserved because private residential investment compensates, but the public-good stock erodes. The "no penalty" reading on output ignores a real welfare cost.

4. **Periphery-side damage.** The Eurozone periphery (GRC, ESP, ITA, PRT) bore the demand-leakage cost of Germany's surplus. Reading the German growth path in isolation misses that the within-Eurozone aggregate cost was non-trivial; the spec's single-country frame makes this externality invisible.

## What the framework still captures

The spec is explicitly framed as a within-country counter-prediction test, not as a verdict on optimal fiscal policy. The strong-Keynesian claim that constitutional fiscal restriction produces sustained recessionary drag at the national-accounts level is a falsifiable prediction; if the German series show no such drag, that's an informative single-data-point rejection of the strong form. The companion donor-pool spec (`debt_brake_fiscal_discipline_without_output_cost`) carries the cross-country burden. Together, the two specs let the heterodox reader win on either the cross-country comparison or the channel-decomposition (monetary offset, external demand) without forcing a synthetic verdict.
