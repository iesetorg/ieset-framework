# Steelman — Milei chainsaw / state-capacity decomposition 2024-2025

**The strongest argument against the causal claim:**

The hypothesis is that Argentina's 2024-2025 fiscal correction is a genuine spending-cut-driven
structural correction with bounded output cost and durable expenditure-share decline. The
opposing view is that the headline correction is dominated by transitory inflation-tax erosion
of nominal pension and wage liabilities, that the spending-cut share is overstated, and that
the output cost is being absorbed unevenly by the lower-half of the income distribution and
by deferred capital expenditure that will need to be reversed.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage:

1. **Inflation-tax channel is dominant in 2024.** Pensions, public-sector wages, and provincial
   transfers are largely nominally indexed with delays. When monthly inflation is 10-25%, a
   six-month indexation lag erodes 30-50% of real value automatically. The IMF Article IV
   and several private estimates (Equilibra, Eco Go) attribute 50-70% of the 2024 fiscal
   improvement to this channel. A correction that depends on inflation to clear nominal
   liabilities is reversible the moment inflation falls — exactly the disinflation the
   companion hypothesis claims is happening.

2. **Capital-expenditure deferral is not a structural cut.** The 2024 capex collapse (~75% real
   decline in federal-government infrastructure spending) is achieving the headline number but
   is not the same as eliminating recurring spending. Bridges, schools, and hospitals still
   need maintenance; the bill is being deferred not erased. A genuine structural correction
   requires recurring-spending discipline, not capex postponement.

3. **Provincial-government dynamics are unobserved.** The federal government is ~55% of total
   government expenditure; the provinces (45%) have their own fiscal accounts and have not
   uniformly followed the Milei programme. If provincial spending and provincial debt are
   absorbing some of the federal correction, the consolidated public-sector picture is less
   dramatic. The hypothesis's general-government-expenditure-share metric should pick this up
   but the data is lagged 12-18 months.

4. **Output cost may be permanent for specific cohorts.** Even if aggregate real GDP recovers
   to 2023Q3 levels by 2025Q4, that aggregate-level recovery hides distributional effects:
   informal-sector employment, public-sector workers, and pensioners may experience persistent
   real-income loss. The "output recovery" headline metric is not the same as a "no permanent
   damage" verdict on the population.

5. **Comparison to Greek/Irish austerity is not perfectly clean.** Argentina 2024 is a
   high-inflation, capital-control, dollar-balance-sheet stabilisation; eurozone-periphery
   austerity 2010-2015 was a low-inflation, internal-devaluation, fixed-exchange-rate
   adjustment. The output-cost-per-spending-cut ratio may differ for structural reasons
   (inflation degree of freedom; FX adjustment availability) that have nothing to do with
   the policy quality. A "supported" verdict on bounded output cost may reflect Argentina's
   peso-flexibility advantage rather than the Milei programme's quality.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Decompose the fiscal correction into (real spending cuts) + (real revenue increase) +
    (inflation-tax erosion) + (asset-sale one-offs), with the real-spending-cut share as the
    headline structural-correction measure.

(b) Distinguish capex deferral from recurring-spending restraint. A correction concentrated
    in deferred capex is a different policy from one concentrated in recurring-spending
    discipline.

(c) Report federal vs consolidated-public-sector fiscal balance, with explicit provincial
    pass-through accounting.

(d) Report the output-cost trajectory not just at aggregate level but with distributional
    breakdown (informal-sector employment, real-pension trajectory, real-public-wage
    trajectory) — a hidden cost concentrated on pensioners is a different policy outcome
    from a broadly-shared adjustment.

(e) Compare the sacrifice ratio to Greek/Irish austerity but flag the FX-flexibility
    structural difference. A favourable Argentine sacrifice ratio is partly attributable to
    the peso-floating channel, not just the policy package.
