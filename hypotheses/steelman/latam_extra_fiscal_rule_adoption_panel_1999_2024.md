# Steelman — Latin America fiscal-rule adoption panel 1999-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis claims fiscal rules deliver lower debt and dispersion without growth penalty. The opposing view is that fiscal rules are descriptive markers of pre-existing institutional commitment, not independent causal levers, and that adoption-timing reflects political readiness rather than producing it.

Specific objections:

1. **Selection into adoption is endogenous.** Countries that adopt fiscal rules are those whose elites are already committed to discipline. The post-adoption coefficient identifies elite-commitment, not the rule itself.

2. **Brazil's LRF was repeatedly weakened.** Brazil's fiscal-responsibility-law framework was breached and amended multiple times (2014, 2019, 2023); treating "adoption" as a sustained treatment overstates compliance.

3. **Colombia's fiscal rule has undergone repeated revisions.** The 2011 rule has been suspended (COVID 2020-2022) and renegotiated; the post-adoption period mixes rule-on and rule-off observations.

4. **Mexico's Pemex-and-budget framework is partial.** Mexican federal-government fiscal balance is rule-bound but Pemex liabilities are treated separately, producing apparent compliance with hidden public-sector deficits.

5. **Growth-cost tests are statistically under-powered.** With 5-7 treated countries and idiosyncratic adoption windows, detecting a small growth penalty is hard; absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report selection-on-observables bound.
(b) Show country-by-country compliance trajectory in commentary.
(c) Note Mexico Pemex-treatment caveat.
(d) Acknowledge low-power on growth-cost test.
