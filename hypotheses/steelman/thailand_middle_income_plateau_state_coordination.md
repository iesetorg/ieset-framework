# Steelman — Thailand Middle-Income Plateau and State Coordination

## Strongest version of the claim

Thailand's middle-income plateau—visible in slowed TFP growth and stagnant convergence after the late 1990s and especially post-2005—is rooted in weak product-market competition, fragmented state coordination capacity, and regulatory capture by domestic oligopolies. Despite a successful export-led development phase (1980s–1990s), Thailand failed to build the institutional depth (competition policy, infrastructure planning, education quality) needed for frontier innovation. The result is an economy with competitive export enclaves but a sheltered domestic sector where incumbents block entry, misallocate capital, and suppress economy-wide productivity gains.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing Thailand's post-2005 TFP growth below Vietnam's and South Korea's at comparable income levels, with competition and institutional-quality coefficients positive and significant.
2. **Firm-level data** showing high concentration and political connections in non-traded sectors (construction, retail, banking) and lower productivity growth than in export sectors.
3. **Infrastructure and education gaps** relative to East Asian peers, linking state-coordination failures to productivity bottlenecks.
4. **Political-economy analysis** connecting recurring coups (2006, 2014) to policy discontinuity and infrastructure project delays.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Export sectors remain competitive**. Thailand's automotive, electronics, and agriculture exports continue to grow; the plateau is an aggregate statistical artefact of sectoral composition.
2. **The 1997 crisis legacy**. Balance-sheet damage and financial-sector weakness explain the 2000s slowdown better than competition or institutions.
3. **Demographic transition**. Thailand's rapidly ageing population is the primary drag on growth, not policy failure.
4. **Vietnam is a special case**. Vietnam's faster recent growth reflects a lower initial base and Chinese supply-chain relocation, not superior Thai policy failure.
5. **Tourism volatility**. External demand shocks (SARS, tsunamis, COVID) repeatedly disrupted Thai growth, masking underlying productivity trends.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **domestic non-traded sectors** with observable oligopoly and regulatory capture.
- Less defensible for **export-oriented manufacturing**, where Thai firms remain regionally competitive.
- The claim focuses on aggregate TFP; sectoral disaggregation is essential for fair evaluation.
