# Steelman: Thailand Middle-Income Plateau

## Strongest version of the claim
Thailand's growth plateau reflects weak product-market competition, fragmented state coordination, and regulatory capture by oligopolistic interests rather than a failure of export orientation per se. The strongest version predicts that competition and institutional-quality variables carry positive coefficients on TFP growth, and that Thailand underperforms comparators on these margins.

## Key evidence the claim would need
- Panel estimates showing positive coefficients on product-market competition and institutional quality.
- Thailand's post-2005 TFP growth significantly below Vietnam's and South Korea's at comparable income levels.
- Evidence that infrastructure fragmentation and political instability have independent negative effects on productivity.

## Best counterarguments
- Thailand's export sector remains competitive; the plateau is confined to non-tradable sectors, which is normal.
- Political instability (coups, protests) is the primary cause, not competition policy or state coordination.
- Thailand started from a lower human-capital base than Korea or Taiwan, making convergence harder.

## Boundary conditions
- The claim applies to middle-income countries with competitive export sectors but weak domestic institutions.
- It does not deny the success of Thailand's early export-led growth.

## Relation to existing hypotheses
- Complements `malaysia_developmentalist_plateau_1990_2024` as another Southeast Asian middle-income-trap case.
- Aligns with `competition_policy_enforcement_innovation` by emphasising domestic competition.
