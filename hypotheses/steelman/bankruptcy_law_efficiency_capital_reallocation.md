# Steelman — Bankruptcy Law Efficiency and Capital Reallocation

## Strongest version of the claim

After a financial shock, economies with efficient bankruptcy and insolvency frameworks recover faster and more productively than those that rely on discretionary bailouts, forbearance, and evergreening. Efficient bankruptcy reallocates distressed assets from failing firms to more productive uses, preserves viable businesses through structured reorganisation, and imposes losses on creditors in a predictable, rules-based manner. Discretionary rescue, by contrast, politicises allocation, protects zombie firms, and delays structural adjustment. The claim is that countries in the top tercile of bankruptcy efficiency show significantly stronger TFP growth in the five years following a financial shock than bottom-tercile countries, and that this reallocation advantage outweighs any short-term stabilisation benefit from bailouts.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive coefficient:** Bankruptcy efficiency is positively and significantly associated with post-shock TFP growth in a panel FE restricted to post-shock windows.
2. **Tercile magnitude:** Top-tercile countries show at least 0.75–1.5 percentage points higher annual TFP growth in the five years post-shock than bottom-tercile countries.
3. **Bailout interaction:** The interaction between bankruptcy efficiency and bailout intensity is negative or insignificant, confirming that bailouts do not complement efficient bankruptcy.
4. **Reform episodes:** Event-study evidence around bankruptcy reforms (UK 2002 Enterprise Act, Germany 1999 Insolvency Code, India 2016 IBC) shows productivity improvements relative to pre-reform trends.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Systemic-risk argument:** The Lehman Brothers collapse demonstrated that allowing large, interconnected firms to fail can trigger contagion and credit freezes; discretionary bailouts may be necessary to prevent systemic meltdown even if they protect some zombies.
2. **Endogenous bailout assignment:** Countries with weaker institutions receive larger bailouts because their financial systems are more fragile; the negative correlation between bailouts and recovery may reflect pre-existing weakness rather than bailout inefficacy.
3. **Going-concern value:** Liquidation-oriented bankruptcy may destroy organisational capital, supplier relationships, and trained workforces that are costly to rebuild; reorganisation-friendly regimes (Chapter 11) may outperform fast liquidation.
4. **Political economy of reform:** Bankruptcy efficiency may improve in response to crises rather than cause better recovery; the direction of causation is difficult to establish.
5. **Measurement limitations:** Doing Business insolvency data is available only from 2003; earlier backfills rely on academic series with limited coverage.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to **post-shock windows only** (country-years with a defined financial shock in the prior two years) over 1990–2020. It is most relevant for **middle- and high-income countries** with functioning legal systems. It is weaker for **low-income countries** where formal bankruptcy is rarely used and for **currency/sovereign crises** where the shock originates outside the banking system.
