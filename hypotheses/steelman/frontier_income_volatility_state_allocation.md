# Steelman — Frontier income volatility and state-directed allocation

## Strongest version of the claim

The most compelling formulation is that state-directed allocation systematically misprices risk: political criteria replace market signals, directed credit flows to favoured sectors regardless of expected returns, and state-enterprise dominance suppresses exit. These mechanisms generate over-investment in boom periods and abrupt corrections when fiscal constraints or political priorities shift. The result is larger amplitude boom-bust cycles in countries that rely heavily on state allocation, even when the early catch-up phase shows strong headline growth.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Within-country state-allocation episodes.** Brazil's BNDES-directed credit surge 2007-2014 followed by the 2015-2016 recession; Malaysia's GLC expansion and subsequent currency-crisis vulnerability; China's post-2008 stimulus and subsequent property-sector correction. If these episodes show systematically larger boom-bust amplitude than market-financed investment cycles in matched peers, that supports the claim.

2. **Sectoral misallocation metrics.** Hsieh-Klenow type misallocation indices should be higher in high-state-allocation periods, and the misallocation should predict subsequent output losses.

3. **Cross-country panel with middle-income threshold interaction.** The effect should intensify after middle-income status because low-institutional-capacity states struggle to manage complex allocation decisions at higher development levels (the "middle-income trap" mechanism).

## Best counterarguments

1. **Resource-dependence confound.** Many high-state-allocation economies are also resource-dependent (Russia, Venezuela, Nigeria, Angola). Commodity price cycles drive boom-bust volatility independently of allocation mode. The resource-rents control mitigates but may not fully absorb this.

2. **Successful counter-examples.** Korea 1960s-1980s and Taiwan 1970s-1990s combined high state direction with relatively stable growth. Singapore's GLC model also shows low volatility. The hypothesis must explain why state allocation is volatile in some cases but not others.

3. **Financial-development interaction.** State-directed credit is often a substitute for underdeveloped private financial markets. The observed volatility may reflect financial underdevelopment rather than state allocation per se.

4. **Crisis-definition endogeneity.** Laeven-Valencia crisis dummies may over-count crises in countries with weaker statistical systems or IMF program involvement, biasing the crisis-frequency comparison.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to middle-income and emerging economies where institutional capacity is constrained, not to high-capacity developmental states (Korea, Taiwan, Singapore in their catch-up phases). The post-middle-income window is where the effect should be strongest; at very low income, even inefficient state allocation may raise investment above subsistence levels.
