# Steelman — Competition policy enforcement and innovation

## Strongest version of the claim

Stronger antitrust and competition-policy enforcement predicts higher patent quality and productivity growth over 20-year windows. The mechanism is that monopoly power suppresses innovation incentives (replacement effect dominates Arrow effect when incumbents are secure), while enforcement preserves contestability that forces incumbents to innovate or exit. The claim is bounded to advanced and middle-income economies with functioning competition authorities and patent systems.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive enforcement coefficient:** Panel-FE shows competition-enforcement intensity positively associated with forward citations per patent and TFP growth at p<0.05.
2. **Tercile magnitude:** Top-tercile enforcement countries show at least 0.2 pp higher annual TFP growth and 10-15% more citations per patent than bottom-tercile countries.
3. **Non-US robustness:** Result holds when the United States is excluded, confirming it is not driven by US-specific enforcement patterns.
4. **Quality measure robustness:** Result is stable across WIPO, USPTO, and EPO citation measures.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Inverted-U (Aghion et al.):** The competition-innovation relationship may be inverted-U shaped, with both very low and very high enforcement reducing innovation. If the sample is concentrated on the left side of the U, the positive coefficient may misgeneralise.
2. **Scale economies in R&D:** Mergers can create efficiencies that fund larger R&D budgets. Blocking mergers on antitrust grounds may reduce innovation if the R&D synergies outweigh the competitive harms.
3. **Patent quality noise:** Forward citations reflect legal strategy and examiner behaviour, not just technological importance. A country with aggressive patent lawyers may show high citations without high innovation.
4. **Endogeneity:** Countries with vibrant innovation ecosystems create political demand for stronger competition policy (innovation → enforcement, not enforcement → innovation).

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for economies with established innovation systems where incumbent market power is a binding constraint (US, EU, Japan, Korea). It is weaker for: (i) catch-up economies where technology transfer via FDI or imitation dominates indigenous innovation; (ii) sectors with high fixed costs and natural monopoly characteristics (utilities, telecoms infrastructure); (iii) small open economies where foreign competition disciplines domestic incumbents regardless of domestic enforcement.
