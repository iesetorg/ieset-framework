# Steelman — Ireland market opening and FDI frontier 1987–2024

## 1. Strongest version of the claim

Ireland’s low corporate tax rate and English-language advantage created a self-reinforcing cluster of foreign direct investment in pharmaceuticals and information technology that no classical industrial planner could have replicated. The Industrial Development Authority’s marketing was effective precisely because it operated within a low-tax, open-trade regime, not because it directed capital top-down.

## 2. Key evidence the claim would need

- A positive and significant synthetic-control or synth-DiD gap for Ireland post-1987 relative to EU periphery and small open economy peers.
- Panel evidence that FDI inflows and corporate tax competitiveness (statutory rate or effective rate) positively predict the growth differential.
- Evidence that state-aid intensity (proxied by government consumption or industrial subsidies) does not positively predict the gap.
- Robustness using modified GNI (GNI*) to strip out BEPS distortions.

## 3. Best counterarguments

1. **GDP is not GNP.** Ireland’s headline GDP is heavily distorted by multinational profit-shifting (BEPS). Modified GNI (GNI*) shows more modest convergence, undermining the strong market-optimism claim.
2. **EU structural and cohesion funds.** Between 1989 and 1999, Ireland received large EU transfers that financed infrastructure, education, and training. These were state-directed resource flows that preceded and enabled the FDI boom.
3. **Education expansion was state-led.** Free secondary schooling and expanded university access in the 1960s–1980s were classic human-capital industrial policy. The workforce that attracted FDI was partly a product of state planning.
4. **The 2008 banking crisis.** Ireland’s light-touch financial regulation — a component of the liberalisation model — produced a property bubble and catastrophic banking collapse. This casts doubt on the “stability” limb of the market-opening narrative.
5. **Tax competition is not a growth model.** Ireland’s strategy is partly beggar-thy-neighbour: it captures tax base at the expense of EU peers. Generalising the Irish experience to other countries is logically impossible because not all can undercut each other.

## 4. Boundary conditions

The claim applies most strongly to small, English-speaking EU members with pre-existing ties to US multinational corporations. It is not replicable for large economies, non-EU states, or countries without a deep integration into US corporate supply chains.

## 5. Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis complements `liberal_capital_account_openness_growth_premium_panel` and other trade-openness hypotheses. It contrasts with `asia_taiwan_tsmc_industrial_policy_1985_2024` and other classic industrial-planning success stories by arguing that Ireland’s success was driven by openness and tax competition rather than by targeted sectoral strategy.
