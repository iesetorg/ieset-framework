# Steelman — Public R&D Complements Private Innovation at the Frontier

## Strongest version of the claim

Public R&D spending is not a substitute for private innovation but a complement that raises its marginal product at the technology frontier. Government and university labs produce basic research, general-purpose technologies, and human capital that private firms cannot fully appropriate, generating spillovers that increase private patenting, venture-capital activity, and TFP growth. The complementarity is strongest in sectors with long horizons and high uncertainty (defence, health, clean energy, AI) where private ROI is too low to justify the investment. The claim challenges the view that cutting public R&D frees resources for more efficient private allocation.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing positive coefficient of public R&D on private innovation outcomes, especially in high-income countries where private R&D is already substantial.
2. **Micro studies** of spillover effects: NIH grants → biotech patents, DARPA → IT venture capital, ARPA-E → clean-energy startups.
3. **Natural experiments**: exogenous changes in public R&D budgets (political shocks, budget sequestration) and subsequent private innovation responses.
4. **Sectoral heterogeneity**: the complementarity should be strongest in general-purpose technology sectors and weakest in mature manufacturing.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Crowding out**. Public R&D may raise the wage of scientists and engineers, reducing private R&D employment and output; the net effect may be zero or negative.
2. **Political allocation distortion**. Public R&D is directed toward politically favored sectors (defense, agriculture) rather than socially optimal ones, producing wasteful spillovers.
3. **International spillovers**. Small open economies may free-ride on US or EU public R&D, making domestic public R&D redundant.
4. **Measurement bias**. Public R&D is measured by expenditure, not output; high spending may reflect inefficiency rather than productive input.
5. **Frontier vs catch-up**. In catch-up economies, public R&D may substitute for weak private capacity rather than complement it; the claim is bounded to the frontier.

## Boundary conditions

- Bounded to **high-income frontier economies** where private R&D is already substantial.
- Most relevant for **general-purpose technologies** with long commercialisation lags.
- Less relevant for **incremental process innovation** where private returns are fully appropriable.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with endogenous-growth and innovation-system literatures. Tensions with claims that public R&D is wasteful or that tax cuts for private R&D are sufficient.
