# Steelman — Hayek calculation problem: China SOE capital efficiency

## The strongest opposing argument

The "SOEs are calculation failures" framing applies a 1920-vintage Austrian thought experiment to a modern Chinese institutional configuration that has substantially solved the original calculation problem through internal market mechanisms, performance-management systems, and price reform. The province-level capital-intensity correlation Austrians point to is real but reflects industrial-mix selection (heavy industry concentrated in SOE-heavy regions) far more than calculation failure.

**Key developmental-state / China-watcher objections:**

1. **Industrial composition confound.** SOE-heavy provinces (Heilongjiang, Liaoning, Shanxi) are concentrated in heavy industry (oil, steel, coal, petrochemicals) where capital-output ratios are mechanically high regardless of ownership. Coastal private-firm-heavy provinces (Guangdong, Zhejiang) specialise in light manufacturing where ICOR is mechanically lower. A panel with province FE absorbs the time-invariant component but cannot fully absorb the within-province sectoral evolution.

2. **SOE coordination externalities are real.** The Chinese growth miracle of 1992-2019 was partly delivered by SOE-led infrastructure investment (high-speed rail, electrification, ports, telecoms backbone) that produced enormous private-sector productivity gains downstream. Counting only the SOE's own measured TFP misses these positive spillovers. Lin Justin Yifu's "new structural economics" argues SOEs in upstream sectors are precisely where calculation works *best* because price signals are clearer at scale.

3. **Internal markets within SOEs.** Modern Chinese SOEs use internal transfer pricing, profit-centre management, and performance-linked compensation that mimic many features of price-driven calculation. The pure Misesian critique assumed bureaucratic command without internal market mechanisms; that is not how 2020s Chinese SOEs actually allocate capital.

4. **Selection on observables vs causation.** Provinces with politically-favoured SOEs receive preferential credit allocation, infrastructure subsidies, and investment-promotion benefits. The capital-intensity finding partly reflects political-economy effects (SOEs have access to cheaper capital than private firms, so they use more of it) rather than calculation failure. The right counterfactual — the same SOE without policy preferences — is unavailable.

## What the framework still captures

The cross-sectional pattern — SOE-heavy provinces have lower productivity growth and higher capital intensity than private-firm-heavy provinces — is robust across multiple data sources (China Statistical Yearbook, Hsieh-Klenow misallocation estimates, World Bank productivity reconstructions). The finding is consistent with the Hayekian reading and also consistent with several non-Hayekian readings (industrial-mix selection, soft-budget-constraint dynamics, political-favouritism credit). The pre-registered test resolves the empirical pattern question; the interpretive question (calculation problem vs soft budget constraint vs sectoral mix) is left open.
