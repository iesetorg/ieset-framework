# Steelman: Estonia Market Reform Post-Soviet Growth 1991–2007

The most powerful steelman is EU accession as a confound. The credible prospect
of EU membership — available only to Baltic and Central European states, not CIS
peers — created a rational expectations premium: foreign investors front-loaded
capital knowing Estonia would join the single market, adopt EU legal standards,
and gain access to structural funds. This accession anchor is not a replicable
policy lever; it was geopolitically determined. The comparison of Estonia versus
Belarus therefore conflates market reform with EU membership, which are not
separable.

Estonia's pre-Soviet institutional legacy also differs materially from CIS peers.
Estonia had a functioning market economy and property rights system until 1940
(only 50 years of Soviet rule versus 70+ for Russia/Ukraine), stronger Lutheran
cultural norms around contract and thrift, and closer cultural and geographic
ties to Finland providing both FDI and institutional model transfer. The
lighter Soviet industrial inheritance meant smaller restructuring costs.

On the flat tax specifically, Keen and Simone (2004) demonstrate that Estonian
revenue was maintained through base-broadening and enforcement improvements rather
than behavioural supply-side effects — challenging the Laffer-curve mechanism
while leaving the simplification benefit intact. The Baltic growth slowdown
post-2007 and the severity of the 2008-2009 contraction (Estonia GDP fell ~15%)
suggest the pre-crisis growth partly reflected credit-fuelled overheating rather
than sustainable reform dividends.
