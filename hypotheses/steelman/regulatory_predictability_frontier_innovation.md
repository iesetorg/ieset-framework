# Steelman — Regulatory predictability vs public R&D subsidies for frontier innovation

## Strongest version of the claim

Among high-income frontier economies, regulatory predictability — stable tax codes, low regulatory-turnover rates, and consistent enforcement — predicts innovation outcomes (patent quality, R&D productivity, TFP growth) better than direct public R&D subsidies. The mechanism is option-value reduction: predictable regulation lowers the risk that long-horizon R&D investments will be stranded by sudden rule changes, raising the expected return to private innovation. Public R&D subsidies, while potentially helpful, may crowd out private R&D, politicise research agendas, and suffer from bureaucratic allocation inefficiency.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Horse-race dominance:** Panel-FE regression shows regulatory-predictability index with larger absolute t-statistic than public R&D subsidies on TFP growth and patent citations.
2. **Significance:** Predictability coefficient positive and significant at p<0.05 on both outcomes.
3. **Tercile magnitude:** Top-tercile predictability countries show at least 0.25 pp higher annual TFP growth and 10% more citations per patent than bottom-tercile countries.
4. **Ex-US robustness:** Result holds when the United States is excluded.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Mazzucato critique:** Public R&D subsidies have funded the foundational technologies behind the iPhone, pharmaceuticals, renewable energy, and the internet. The marginal social return to public R&D may vastly exceed its measured correlation with growth because spillovers are not captured in firm-level TFP.
2. **Predictability as luxury good:** Rich, stable countries have both predictable regulation and high innovation. The correlation may reflect general institutional quality and state capacity rather than a specific predictability channel.
3. **R&D subsidy design matters:** Well-designed subsidies (competitive grants, tax credits, prizes) may outperform poorly designed ones (directed loans, sectoral earmarks). Aggregating all public R&D into one variable obscures this heterogeneity.
4. **Innovation without predictability:** Some high-innovation sectors (biotech, AI) operate in highly uncertain regulatory environments but innovate rapidly because expected returns are so large. Predictability may matter more for incremental than radical innovation.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for incremental innovation in established sectors (manufacturing, chemicals, automotive) where long-horizon capital commitment is required. It weakens for: (i) mission-oriented innovation (defence, space, climate) where public R&D coordinates multi-firm research; (ii) sectors with rapid technological obsolescence where regulatory stability is irrelevant because technology changes faster than rules; (iii) small open economies where regulatory predictability is provided by EU or WTO harmonisation.
