# Steelman — Free-trade partner growth premium

## The strongest opposing argument

The Ricardian / Smithian free-trade-causes-growth claim has been the central battleground of empirical trade economics for 30 years. A serious heterodox reading will press:

1. **Rodriguez-Rodrik 2001 critique.** The headline cross-country trade-and-growth correlations identified by Sachs-Warner, Frankel-Romer, and others fail under careful identification — the openness measures are partly endogenous to growth, and the geographic instruments suffer from exclusion-restriction violations.

2. **NAFTA disappointment.** Mexico's per-capita growth post-1994 was modest by emerging-market standards; the productivity-acceleration that FTA proponents predicted did not materialise at the aggregate level. If the canonical large-FTA case fails to deliver, the panel-FE coefficient is doing work elsewhere.

3. **Africa's RTA failures.** ECOWAS, COMESA, SADC, and the AfCFTA have produced limited measurable trade-creation or growth effects. Many of these RTAs are politically real but economically thin (low intra-regional trade share to begin with). Pooling them with EU enlargement smears very different treatments.

4. **EU enlargement is sui generis.** The accession-driven growth effect for EU candidates 2004-2013 is large but reflects the bundle of single-market access + structural-funds transfers + acquis-communautaire institutional upgrade. Reading that as a "free-trade effect" overstates the trade-policy contribution.

5. **Pre-trend cherry-picking.** FTA accession dates are endogenous: countries enter FTAs when their trade-supply elasticities are already rising. The pre-accession trend control mitigates this but doesn't eliminate it; the staggered-treatment ATT may still be biased upward.

6. **Heterodox alternative: imports-as-deindustrialisation.** Trade openness can produce growth at the aggregate level while destroying domestic industrial capability that shows up later (Mexican corn farmers post-NAFTA, US Rust Belt post-China-shock 2001). The 5-year-post growth window misses the lagged structural costs.

## What the framework still captures

The Callaway-Sant'Anna staggered-DiD design is the appropriate estimator for this setting and addresses the Goodman-Bacon TWFE bias. The dual-outcome gate (trade openness AND growth) makes the test harder to satisfy by chance. The ex-EU INFORMATIVE check lets the heterodox reader win cleanly if the headline effect is EU-specific. The hypothesis is a genuine test of the strong Ricardian claim, with the heterodox reading well-positioned to be vindicated where it has the data on its side.
