# Steelman — Modern wealth taxes can yield substantial revenue without catastrophic flight

**The strongest arguments for wealth taxation and against the causal chain:**

1. **Denmark's wealth-tax elasticity was small.** Jakobsen-Jakobsen-Kleven-Zucman (2020) study the 1988 Danish wealth-tax reform and estimate taxable-wealth elasticity of 0.77 at the top, with modest migration responses. Under modern enforcement (CRS, FATCA, DAC6, automatic information exchange), evading by emigrating is much harder than it was in the French ISF era.

2. **The French ISF "exodus" narrative is quantitatively inflated.** Conseil des Prélèvements Obligatoires reviews of ISF emigration estimate roughly 500-600 fiscally-motivated emigrations per year at peak — a rounding error against a HNW population of hundreds of thousands. The narrative effect greatly exceeds the measured effect; survey-based claims of exodus dwarf register-based counts.

3. **Revenue yield is higher under modern regimes than legacy ISF.** Norway's 2022 wealth tax yielded NOK ~28B; Spain's IGF 2022 + harmonising Catalan/Andalusian collection collected more than forecast in year 1; Colombia's 2022 tax met collection targets. The "yield gap" claim is empirically fragile for modern regimes with improved valuation rules and coordinated CRS data.

4. **Pre-2018 France abolition was a political not economic response.** The Macron 2017 decision to replace ISF with IFI (real-estate only) was driven by political-coalition considerations, not by demonstrated failure. Post-IFI revenue on real-estate wealth has been stable; claims the system had collapsed are overstated.

5. **Wealth inequality is a genuine social cost the outcome set excludes.** A hypothesis that tests only revenue yield and emigration flow omits the reason wealth taxes are advocated: reducing the stock of dynastic wealth, curbing political capture by the ultra-wealthy, and funding public goods with ability-to-pay proportionality. Even a below-forecast yield can be welfare-positive if it achieves distributional objectives at low efficiency cost.

6. **Portfolio reshuffling into exempt classes often produces socially desirable allocation.** Business-asset exemptions reward keeping capital in productive enterprises; this is a design feature, not a failure. Reshuffling into pension wrappers pushes wealth into long-horizon claim structures; this is arguably socially desirable. Measuring reshuffling as pure avoidance misses the productive-behaviour effects some exemption structures target.

7. **Enforcement improvements change the calculation.** The CRS went live in 2017; DAC6 since 2020; US FATCA since 2014; country-by-country reporting for MNCs since 2016. Historical estimates of wealth-tax evasion (Zucman 2013 on offshore wealth) are becoming obsolete as the enforcement stack matures. A hypothesis relying on pre-CRS elasticity estimates could produce systematically pessimistic predictions.

8. **Norway's 2022 "emigration spike" is confounded by the timing of realised-capital-gains reform in the same year.** Attributing emigration to the wealth-tax component specifically rather than to the broader tax-package politics is an identification reach; the Norwegian government's own analysis separates the two.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report the first-order revenue yield honestly and prominently; the tax does work in year 1.
- Distinguish register-based emigration counts from survey-based or media-based estimates; report only register-based.
- Control for concurrent tax reforms (Norway 2022 CG reform, French 2017 full reform, Spain 2022 IGF harmonisation).
- Report the Jakobsen-Jakobsen-Kleven-Zucman Danish elasticity as a benchmark against any large estimated elasticity.
- Flag pre-CRS vs post-CRS enforcement regime prominently; do not pool estimates across regimes.
- Treat distributional benefits as out-of-frame for this narrow-outcome test and state explicitly that the test does not adjudicate the welfare case.
- Report yield gap relative to both (a) static government forecast and (b) dynamic-scored forecast; the gap shrinks under dynamic scoring.
