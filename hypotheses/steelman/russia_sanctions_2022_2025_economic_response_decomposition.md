# Steelman — Russia sanctions 2022-2025 economic response decomposition

## The strongest opposing argument

Both readings — "sanctions worked less than expected" and "sanctions are working with lag" — have substantial evidence; the framing as a clean "milder-than-predicted" win for sanctions skeptics overstates a still-evolving picture and understates measurement-distortion effects from a war economy.

**Sanctions-are-binding-with-lag case:**

1. **War-economy GDP reading is misleading.** Russia's 2023 GDP growth was driven by ~10% military-industrial output expansion — tanks, missiles, ammunition counted at production-cost in national accounts. Strip the military-Keynesian fiscal stimulus and underlying civilian-economy growth was negative. The headline GDP "milder than predicted" is partly a defence-spending illusion that won't be sustainable post-war and is consuming long-term capital deepening.

2. **Buffer drawdown is the leading indicator.** NWF liquid assets, FX reserves (excluding frozen ~$300B), and oil-revenue cushion are all declining sharply. The 2024-2025 fiscal arithmetic is deteriorating; CBR raised rates to 21% in 2024-Q4 to defend the ruble, then 16% in 2025. The "stabilisation" is increasingly artificial — capital controls + extreme rate defence — not a genuine equilibrium. A 2027 endpoint would likely show much sharper damage.

3. **Tech-import collapse is the binding constraint.** Even granting GDP/FX held up, productivity growth requires capital-good imports. Aviation grounding (parts shortages), oil-services degradation (Schlumberger/Halliburton exit), and semiconductor smuggling at multiple-of-list-price are eroding long-run capacity. The hypothesis acknowledges this in metric 4 but understates how dispositive it could be for 2027-2030 growth.

4. **The G7 price cap worked partially.** Urals discount to Brent peaked at $25-35/bbl 2022-2023, costing Russia ~$50B in 2023 export revenue. Sanctions skeptics often skip this. The cap evolved with shadow-fleet evasion, but the early-period revenue impact was real.

**Sanctions-skeptic case (supports the hypothesis as written):**
- Russia retained access to global oil markets via shadow fleet + India/China rerouting; the oil-revenue floor that funds the war held.
- Imports from Turkey, UAE, Kazakhstan, China increased dramatically — parallel imports work.
- The IMF March-2022 forecast (-8.5% 2022) was off by ~6pp; that gap is not measurement noise, it's a substantive miss.

## What the framework still captures

The hypothesis tests an empirical question — was the realised damage materially smaller than the March-2022 consensus forecast? — that has a clear data answer regardless of the long-run trajectory. The pre-registered support band (within +/-4pp of forecast) admits both the "much milder" and "tracked forecast" cases as informative outcomes. The multi-metric design correctly decomposes by channel, capturing the "binding-on-tech, blunt-on-macro" pattern that is probably the most accurate summary. Refutation would require either much-worse-than-forecast (2027 trajectory could deliver this) or no-rerouting (clearly false on 2024 data).
