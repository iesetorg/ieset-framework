# Steelman — Industrial Policy in High-Governance States

## Strongest version of the claim

Industrial policy is not universally ineffective; it succeeds when implemented by high-capacity states with export discipline, performance criteria, and sunset clauses. The East Asian developmental states (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) used sectoral targeting, conditional credit, and technology push to accelerate structural transformation and productivity growth beyond what laissez-faire would have produced. The success is conditional on governance: in low-capacity states, industrial policy degenerates into rent-seeking, cronyism, and protectionism. The claim challenges blanket free-market opposition to sectoral targeting by identifying the boundary conditions for success.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing positive interaction between industrial-policy intensity and governance quality for manufacturing TFP and export sophistication.
2. **Case studies** of successful targeting (Korea HCI, Taiwan Hsinchu, Singapore EDB) with counterfactual benchmarking against non-targeted sectors or countries.
3. **Microdata** on firm productivity showing that subsidised/targeted firms in high-governance countries outperform unsubsidised peers, while the reverse holds in low-governance countries.
4. **Programme-evaluation designs** exploiting eligibility thresholds or regional targeting variation.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Selection bias**. High-governance countries may have succeeded despite industrial policy, not because of it; the counterfactual (laissez-faire Korea) is unobservable.
2. **Export discipline is trade policy, not industrial policy**. The success may reflect competitive exposure through export orientation rather than targeting per se.
3. **Post-hoc rationalisation**. Successful cases are cherry-picked; failed industrial policies (Malaysia's Proton, Brazil's informatics reservation, India's license raj) are equally numerous.
4. **General equilibrium effects**. Sectoral targeting may raise productivity in targeted sectors while dragging down the rest of the economy through resource misallocation.
5. **Governance is endogenous**. Successful industrial policy may build governance capacity, making the interaction reverse-causal.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **manufacturing and technology sectors** with clear performance metrics and export markets.
- Less defensible for **services, agriculture, and speculative frontier technologies** where performance criteria are ambiguous.
- Applies to **high-governance middle-income catch-up economies**, not to low-governance or already-frontier economies.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with developmental-state and growth-literature claims. Tensions with blanket free-market opposition to industrial policy and with hypotheses that attribute East Asian success solely to trade openness or factor accumulation.
