# Steelman: West vs East Germany Economic Divergence 1950–1989

The strongest case against attributing the West/East divergence primarily to economic
system rests on initial conditions and external advantages. West Germany received
Marshall Plan transfers (~$1.4bn) and benefited from the Ruhr and Rhine industrial
corridor remaining in the Western zone — the FRG inherited the dominant pre-war
manufacturing heartland. The Soziale Marktwirtschaft was not pure laissez-faire:
it featured codetermination, a generous welfare state, strong union representation,
and Ordoliberal competition policy, meaning the "market economy" comparison is
overstated.

West Germany also gained a one-time labour windfall from Eastern migration before
the Wall (1961), receiving skilled workers without paying their education costs.
Early NATO and EEC integration provided preferential trade access and security
guarantees unavailable to GDR. The Soviet Union extracted substantial reparations
from the Eastern zone rather than providing reconstruction support.

The honest causal test requires a synthetic control holding constant these initial
advantages. Some economists estimate that correcting for Marshall Plan, migration
gains, and EEC trade access reduces the system-attributable divergence substantially.
The GDR's relative performance among Eastern Bloc economies — where it was
consistently the wealthiest — is also consistent with initial industrial advantage
rather than system effects alone.
