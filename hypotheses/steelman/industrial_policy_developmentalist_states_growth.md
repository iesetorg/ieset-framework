# Steelman — Industrial policy and developmentalist state growth

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The East Asian growth miracle is real; its attribution to industrial policy is selection on success. Many countries in the 1960s-1980s attempted selective credit allocation, export promotion, and state-directed investment — and most failed badly. India pre-1991 was a developmentalist-planned economy that under-performed. Latin American import-substitution industrialisation was industrial policy and produced the debt crisis of the 1980s. Africa's parastatals were developmentalist and produced stagnation. The successful East Asian cases may represent survivorship among a universe of similar policy attempts, with the real cause being prior institutional conditions (state capacity, functioning bureaucracy, low corruption) that the policy merely exploited rather than produced.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Survivorship bias in case selection.** Amsden, Wade, and Chang wrote after the East Asian success was already visible. The countries they studied were selected because they succeeded; the developmentalist literature does not engage seriously with the failed attempts. A clean test would list all countries that attempted systematic industrial policy and examine the distribution of outcomes. Such an exercise (Harrison-Rodríguez-Clare 2010, Lee-Lee 2018) typically finds developmentalist policy is associated with mean outcomes close to controls, with high variance — consistent with state-capacity-moderated efficacy, not with industrial policy as an independent treatment.

2. **Authoritarian-era confound.** Korea's developmentalist period is Park Chung-hee 1961-1979 and the later military regime; Taiwan's is Kuomintang single-party rule; Singapore's is PAP-dominant governance; China's is single-party Communist. The developmentalist / democratic split maps closely onto case selection, and the control group includes a mix of authoritarian and democratic regimes. Separating the "developmentalist policy" effect from the "cohesive authoritarian executive can force painful adjustment" effect is very difficult. The falsification rule addresses this by restricting to Polity2 > 0 pre-treatment, but this restriction removes 3 of 4 treatment units.

3. **Export discipline is the hard part; the spec does not measure it.** Amsden's argument rests on the conditionality attached to subsidised credit — subsidies were contingent on export performance, producing hard budget constraints that disciplined firm performance. Latin American ISI lacked this conditionality and produced rent-seeking. The hypothesis does not include a direct measure of export-discipline conditionality; it treats industrial policy as a binary indicator. Two countries with the same indicator value can be implementing very different policies; the indicator is a crude proxy that understates the heterogeneity of what's being compared.

4. **Reverse causation via state capacity.** East Asian states in the 1960s had unusual bureaucratic capacity (Japanese colonial bureaucracies, exam-based administrative traditions in Korea and Taiwan, British-trained civil services in Singapore and Hong Kong). These pre-existing conditions determined whether industrial policy could be administered competently, and the same conditions independently predict growth through other channels (contract enforcement, property rights, tax administration). The developmentalist policy result is partly measuring the state-capacity underlying condition, not the policy per se.

5. **China 1978 is a liberalisation story misread as an industrial-policy story.** China's post-1978 growth coincides with decollectivisation, household-responsibility-system agriculture, Special Economic Zones, foreign-capital invitation, and WTO accession 2001. These are predominantly liberalisation moves, not industrial-policy moves in the Amsden sense. Including China as a developmentalist case conflates two different growth stories; Sachs-Woo 1997 and later work argues China's success was primarily institutional-liberalisation-driven with industrial-policy components secondary. The hypothesis will be substantially driven by China's trajectory and may be systematically mis-coding it.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report the synth-DiD ATT by individual case (not just averaged) so readers can see whether the headline is driven by China (which is a contested coding), by Korea (cleanest case for industrial policy), or by all four. If China alone drives the result, the hypothesis is a different claim.

(b) Include the pre-registered Polity2 > 0 robustness and note that it largely removes the treatment sample — the finding may not survive the authoritarian-confounder test.

(c) Present results with and without a state-capacity proxy (Hendrix 2010 state capacity index; alternatively, pre-1960 bureaucratic-tradition dummy coded from economic-history sources). If the industrial-policy effect collapses when state capacity is controlled, the hypothesis is really about state capacity.

(d) List the failed developmentalist cases explicitly (Argentina's state intervention under Perón and later, Brazil's ISI period, India pre-1991, Ghana under Nkrumah) and report their outcomes alongside the successes. If the pre-registered "3 of 4 positive" threshold is met only because failed cases were excluded from the treatment set, the finding is fragile.

The strongest version of the steelman argues the hypothesis has the causal chain backward: state capacity and cohesive institutions produce both the ability to implement industrial policy and the subsequent growth, with industrial policy as a marker rather than a mechanism. A v2 that uses state-capacity as the treatment and industrial-policy as a mediator would be more defensible and would probably produce different conclusions.
