The "Great Gatsby Curve" relationship between income inequality and immobility is the robust empirical pattern, and the framework's attempt to reinterpret it as institutional-channel-mediated is ex-post rationalisation.

## The Great Gatsby Curve is robust

Corak (2013) documented a roughly 0.8 correlation between a country's disposable-income Gini and its intergenerational earnings elasticity. The relationship survives in updated OECD data, in Piketty-world-inequality data, and in rank-rank-slope specifications (Jäntti-Jenkins). This is the most robust cross-country fact in the mobility literature. The framework's claim that institutional channels explain more than Gini is in tension with the strongest empirical regularity in the field.

## Education-spending equality is endogenous to redistribution

In countries with centralised public-education funding (Nordic, France), education spending is equal by design because it is national, not local. In federal systems with local property-tax school funding (US, some parts of Australia/Canada), education spending varies with local wealth. The "education-spending-inequality" channel is almost deterministic from the fiscal structure: redistributive tax systems produce equal education spending, non-redistributive ones do not. Treating education-spending-inequality as an independent channel from the redistribution level is a conceptual error — they measure the same thing.

## Chetty's US findings may not generalise

Chetty-Hendren-Kline-Saez (2014) and subsequent opportunity-atlas work identify place-based mobility within the US, using within-country variation that does not exist at the cross-country level. The US has the combination of low mobility overall *and* large within-country variation in mobility across commuting zones. Most OECD countries have higher aggregate mobility *and* lower within-country variation, so the Chetty decomposition has less to work with. The cross-country generalisation assumes the US within-country mechanism operates across countries, which is a strong and not-yet-tested assumption.

## Housing affordability may be a symptom, not a cause

High housing prices in high-opportunity cities are a *consequence* of those cities being high-opportunity: that is why people want to live there. Using housing affordability as a causal channel for mobility conflates supply-side restrictions (which reduce mobility) with demand-side agglomeration (which raises it). The hypothesis would need to separate supply-restricted high-price cities (London, San Francisco, Sydney) from genuinely expensive high-productivity cities with elastic supply (Houston, Munich) to test the supply-restriction mechanism cleanly. BIS price-to-income ratios do not distinguish these.

## Measurement of mobility is heterogeneous

OECD's cross-country mobility estimates (Corak 2013, OECD 2018) are built from heterogeneous national sources: administrative-tax-data for Nordics, PSID for US, cohort-study follow-up for UK, survey links for France/Germany. Known methodological differences bias measured mobility downward for countries with shorter earnings-observation windows and upward for administrative-data countries. The US-vs-Nordic contrast may be partly measurement artefact; roughly a quarter of the raw gap is attributable to methodological heterogeneity per Jäntti et al. (2006). The framework's institutional-channel finding could be spurious if measurement differences correlate with fiscal structure.

## The n = 20 power problem

With n = 20 and three correlated institutional channels plus three correlated controls, the effective degrees of freedom are small. Partial R-squared decomposition is unstable; leave-one-out robustness is helpful but does not rescue statistical power. The framework's decision to run this as a cross-section rather than extending to within-country cohort variation is data-availability-driven rather than ideal; a Bayesian prior on the channels would make the limited-information character of the result explicit.

## What would update the framework's prior

If partial R-squared on institutional channels is 0.40-0.50 and Gini-alone partial R-squared is 0.55+, the framework should acknowledge that the Great Gatsby mechanism dominates the institutional-channel story, even with supportive institutional-channel coefficients. Symmetrically, if leave-one-out robustness fails (e.g. dropping the US flips the sign of the housing-affordability channel), the cross-country generalisation is not robust and the hypothesis should be demoted to US-and-UK-specific.

## Policy implications

Great-Gatsby-dominant finding prescribes tax-transfer redistribution to compress disposable-income Gini. Institutional-channel-dominant finding prescribes education-equality, residential-integration, and housing-supply policy. The two prescriptions are not mutually exclusive; the framework should present the institutional-channel result as additive to redistribution, not as a substitute for it.
