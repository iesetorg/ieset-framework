# Steelman — Fed 2022-2024 rate cycle disinflation decomposition

## The strongest opposing argument

Both extremes have advocates with serious arguments — the "mostly Fed" orthodox monetarist case and the "mostly supply" Bernanke-Blanchard case — and the SUPPORTED middle band ([25%, 50%] Fed share) is itself a contestable Solomonic split.

**Mostly-Fed (orthodox/monetarist) case:**
- Inflation expectations are what matter for sustained disinflation, and the Fed's rate cycle is what re-anchored them. Michigan 5-10y expectations peaked at 3.0% in 2022-06 and returned to 2.8-3.0% — without aggressive Fed action, expectations could have de-anchored Turkey-style. The disinflation was *Fed-credibility-driven*, not energy-price-driven; energy is mean-reverting noise around the trend, the trend was the Fed's regime declaration.
- Goods deflation 2023-2024 reflects Fed-induced demand cooling, not supply normalisation alone. PCE goods went outright negative; that's a demand response.
- The 525bp hike was the largest in 40 years; attributing only 1/3 of disinflation to it is implausibly low given monetary-policy effects on aggregate demand have a 12-18 month lag and 2023 timing fits perfectly.

**Mostly-supply (Bernanke-Blanchard) case:**
- The 2022 inflation peak was 80%+ supply-shock-driven (energy + supply chains + PEA reopening), per the Bernanke-Blanchard structural decomposition. Symmetrically, the 2023-2024 disinflation should be 80%+ supply-driven by the same logic. GSCPI returned to neutral by 2023-Q3 — that alone should have removed ~3pp from headline.
- Labour-market unemployment did *not* rise above 4.2% during the disinflation — there was no Phillips-curve-style demand-driven cost; the soft-landing happened because the supply shocks reversed, not because demand was crushed.
- Excess fiscal stimulus ($5T 2020-2021) faded mechanically as transfers ended; that fiscal-fade explains demand softening without monetary credit.

**Why the [25%, 50%] band itself is contestable:**

1. The Fed-share decomposition depends on counterfactual choice. Wu-Xia shadow-rate IV, Romer-Romer narrative IV, and high-frequency-identification each give different shares (10-60%).
2. Goods/services/shelter sub-component dynamics each have different lag structures, and a single-aggregate decomposition averages over heterogeneous channels.
3. Shelter CPI's lagged response (BLS rent measurement uses stale leases) means most of "Fed-credit" 2024 disinflation is actually 2022-2023 housing-market cooling showing up with delay — was that Fed or fiscal-fade-driven housing softening?

## What the framework still captures

The hypothesis structure is honest about admitting the disinflation is multi-causal and pre-registers a band that excludes both extreme readings. The cleanest finding would be either tail — clear refutation of the moderate consensus would be a major prior update on monetary-policy effectiveness in supply-driven inflation episodes. The middle-band SUPPORTED case acknowledges that the truth probably is in that band even if individual analysts can't agree on the precise share.
