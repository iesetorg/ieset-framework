# Steelman: electricity_market_unbundling_price_reliability_panel

## Counter-argument

Electricity-market liberalisation often produces price reductions in the short
run through regulatory gaming and under-investment in reserve capacity, followed
by price spikes and reliability crises as competition erodes system adequacy.
The observed price reductions may reflect cost-shifting to consumers through
volatile spot markets rather than genuine efficiency gains. Moreover,
vertical unbundling can create coordination failures between generation and
grid investment that reduce reliability.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Under-investment in reserves**: Competitive markets may under-provide
   generation reserve capacity because individual firms do not internalise
   system-reliability externalities. This can lead to supply interruptions
   (e.g. Texas 2021, California 2020).

2. **Retail-price volatility**: Liberalised markets often shift price risk to
   consumers through variable-rate contracts. Average prices may fall while
   price volatility and consumer welfare losses rise.

3. **Network under-investment**: Unbundling separates transmission investment
   from generation returns, potentially creating under-investment in grid
   infrastructure that reduces reliability.

4. **Fossil-fuel confound**: Many liberalisation episodes coincided with
   falling natural-gas prices (1990s-2000s) and rising renewable-energy
   subsidies, which independently reduced generation costs.

## What would flip the verdict

- Evidence that liberalised markets experienced more frequent supply
  interruptions (higher SAIDI/SAIFI) than integrated utilities after
  controlling for weather and generation mix.
- A positive coefficient on liberalisation for industrial prices after
  controlling for fuel prices and renewable subsidies.
- Case-study evidence that price reductions were achieved by regulatory
  gaming (e.g. California 2000-2001) rather than efficiency gains.
