# Steelman: Argentina Peronism Recurring Fiscal-Inflation Cycle 1945-2023

The strongest counter-argument is that attributing Argentina's inflation episodes to fiscal-institutional failure conflates cause and mechanism while understating external shocks. Argentina is a commodity exporter with a structurally volatile terms-of-trade: soy, beef, and grain prices drive fiscal revenue directly (via export levies) and indirectly (via growth and tax base). Each of the major inflation episodes identified in the hypothesis coincides with adverse commodity price movements — 1952 drought, 1975 oil shock pass-through, 1989 Brady debt crisis, 2001 EM contagion from Asia and Russia — that would have stressed any government's finances regardless of institutional configuration.

The Granger causality framing is particularly vulnerable: if commodity shocks simultaneously worsen fiscal positions and trigger inflation through import costs and exchange-rate depreciation, the fiscal variable is not a cause of inflation but a co-effect of the same external shock. A properly specified VAR would require commodity price innovations as exogenous instruments, and the identified fiscal → inflation coefficient would likely attenuate substantially.

On the institutional-specificity claim: Brazil experienced its own hyperinflationary episodes (1989-1994, peak 2,700%) with fiscal structures broadly similar to Argentina's during those years. Chile's 1973-1975 hyperinflation followed political rupture, not uniquely Peronist institutions. The cross-country comparison fails to establish that Argentina's institutional configuration is distinctively pathological rather than representative of a broader Latin American fiscal fragility that market-oriented institutional reforms (Brazil's Lei de Responsabilidade Fiscal 2000, Chile's structural-balance rule 2001) subsequently addressed.
