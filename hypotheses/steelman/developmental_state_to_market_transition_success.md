# Steelman — Developmental State to Market Transition

## Strongest version of the claim

The most successful long-run performers among catch-up economies followed a sequenced strategy: state-directed tools (targeted credit, sectoral subsidies, infrastructure coordination, export promotion) accelerated industrialisation and technology absorption during the middle-income phase; then, before reaching the productivity frontier, these countries transitioned to market competition, fiscal discipline, and rule-bound institutions that sustain innovation at the frontier. Countries that maintained state direction into the high-income phase (e.g., Japan's post-1985 attempts to target sectors, China's current model) experienced productivity stalls, misallocation, and financial fragility. The claim synthesises the developmentalist and market-liberal perspectives into a lifecycle theory of development strategy.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing that TFP growth and convergence gaps improve with developmental-state intensity at middle income but flatten or reverse at high income, and that a transition indicator positively predicts performance in the high-income phase.
2. **Case studies**: Korea and Taiwan (transitioned successfully) versus Japan (delayed transition, post-1990 stagnation) versus China (still state-directed, slowing convergence).
3. **Sectoral microdata** showing resource misallocation rising in state-directed high-income sectors (zombie firms, directed lending to declining industries).
4. **Institutional trajectory data**: timing of transition to competition policy, fiscal rules, and independent regulation relative to income level.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Confounding by other factors**. Successful transitioners also had favourable demographics, geopolitical alignments, and colonial legacies that explain their performance.
2. **Japan's stall had multiple causes**. Demographics, deflation, and post-bubble balance-sheet repair explain Japan's lost decades better than industrial-policy persistence.
3. **China's model is still delivering**. Despite slowing growth, China's state-directed model has produced the largest poverty reduction in history; declaring it a failure is premature.
4. **Transition timing is endogenous**. Countries transition when growth slows, so the transition may be a response to failure rather than a cause of success.
5. **Small sample**. There are very few successful transitioners; the pattern may be an artefact of sampling rather than a generalisable sequence.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **manufacturing-led catch-up economies** in East Asia and Eastern Europe.
- Less defensible for **resource-rich economies** or **service-led development** (India, Ireland).
- The timing of transition is critical; premature transition may sacrifice scale economies, while delayed transition may entrench vested interests.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Synthesises developmental-state, institutional-transition, and growth-literature claims. Tensions with both blanket anti-state and blanket anti-market positions, offering a conditional, sequenced framework.
