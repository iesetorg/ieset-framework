# Steelman — Fed funds below Taylor rule and 2008 housing bust as malinvestment

## The strongest opposing argument

Attributing the 2008 housing bust to Greenspan-era loose money is a politically-convenient story that under-weights three structural causes which together account for most of the bust dynamics, leaving little explanatory work for the monetary stance.

**Key New-Keynesian/savings-glut/financial-regulation objections:**

1. **Global savings glut (Bernanke 2005).** The depressed long-term yields of 2003-2007 reflected a global excess of savings — Asian reserve accumulation post-1997, oil-exporter surpluses, German current-account surpluses — flowing into US long-duration assets. The 10-year Treasury yield decoupled from the Fed funds rate ("Greenspan's conundrum") precisely because the marginal demand for long-dated US debt was being set abroad, not by domestic monetary stance. Removing the Fed deviation would have left long rates roughly where they were.

2. **Originate-to-distribute incentives + ratings failure.** The MBS bust was driven by structurally-flawed securitisation — agency conflicts at originators, rating-agency miscalibration of correlation, off-balance-sheet SIV vehicles enabled by Basel arbitrage. None of these depend on Fed funds being below Taylor; an alternative counterfactual where the Fed had run policy at the Taylor rate but the regulatory architecture stayed unchanged would still have produced a substantial MBS bust.

3. **Taylor rule is not a neutral benchmark.** The choice of 1.5 pi-gap weight, 0.5 y-gap weight, 2% r*, and 2% pi* is one specification among many. Using a real-time output gap (vs CBO ex-post), or accounting for the 2003 deflation-risk concerns at the Fed, narrows the deviation considerably. Orphanides-Williams have shown that "deviation from Taylor" estimates are highly sensitive to real-time vs ex-post data.

4. **Multi-metric pattern is over-determined.** Of the 5 metrics, four (residential investment share, mortgage debt, Case-Shiller rise, post-bust drawdown) are mechanically correlated within a single bubble-bust episode. Counting them as independent inflates the apparent "canonical pattern" — they are really one observation about the size of the bubble, not five.

## What the framework still captures

The size of the 2002-2007 deviation from Taylor is large, persistent, and visible regardless of which Taylor-rule variant is used. The size of the housing bubble — by every available metric — is one of the largest in US history. The framework's multi-metric checklist deliberately tests pattern, not causality: a "supported" verdict means the canonical Austrian retelling is consistent with the data, not that ABCT has been uniquely vindicated. The honest finding is that any complete account of 2008 must explain why the Fed ran easy through the bubble; whether that easy stance was *causal* or *permissive* relative to the savings-glut and regulatory channels is contested.
