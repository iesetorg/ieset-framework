# Steelman — FDI openness predicts domestic supplier productivity growth with competition, but not with protected national champions

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that foreign direct investment raises domestic supplier productivity only when domestic markets are competitive enough for local firms to absorb technology, respond to quality demands from foreign affiliates, and avoid being crowded out by privileged multinational entrants. When domestic incumbents are protected (national champions, entry barriers, preferential procurement), FDI may flow to enclave sectors with few local linkages, or may capture rents without spillovers. The claim is an interaction effect, not a main effect: FDI policy alone is insufficient.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive interaction coefficient**: A robustly positive and significant β3 on (FDI openness × competition policy) in a panel FE regression, with the main effect of FDI openness insignificant.
2. **Subsample confirmation**: Countries in the top tercile of competition policy should show a positive FDI-TFP relationship; countries in the bottom tercile should show a null or negative relationship.
3. **Channel evidence**: Backward linkages (domestic inputs into foreign-affiliate output) and technology-transfer proxies (non-resident patents, licensing) should mediate the interaction effect.
4. **IV robustness**: Bilateral investment treaties as an instrument for FDI openness should produce the same interaction sign, reducing endogeneity concern.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Measurement endogeneity**: Competition policy indices (OECD PMR) are highly correlated with general institutional quality and rule of law. The interaction may pick up "good institutions × FDI" rather than "competition × FDI" specifically.
2. **Enclave FDI**: Even in competitive markets, FDI in extractive sectors or export-processing zones may have few supplier linkages. The sectoral composition of FDI matters more than the aggregate restrictiveness index.
3. **Crowding out in competitive markets**: In some competitive markets, efficient foreign affiliates may drive domestic firms out of business, reducing measured average domestic TFP (survivorship bias). The hypothesis assumes net positive spillovers, but selection effects could reverse the sign.
4. **China as a counter-example**: China maintained high FDI openness (special economic zones) alongside extensive state protection of national champions (SOEs). Supplier productivity grew rapidly in some sectors but stagnated in SOE-dominated sectors. The interaction story fits China well, but the cross-country panel may not capture the within-China heterogeneity.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies most clearly to manufacturing FDI in middle-income economies with existing supplier networks. It does not apply to: (a) resource-extraction FDI with few local linkages; (b) service-sector FDI where supplier relationships are harder to define; (c) very low-income economies where domestic firms lack absorptive capacity regardless of competition policy.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis bridges trade policy (FDI openness) and competition policy. It is complementary to `wto_accession_productivity_spillover_panel` and `trade_lib_china_wto_2001_manufacturing_export_surge` by testing a specific mechanism (supplier linkages) rather than aggregate productivity. A null result (FDI openness works regardless of competition) would support the "FDI always helps" view; a negative interaction would support the "FDI without reform is enclave growth" view.
