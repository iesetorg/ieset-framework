# Steelman — Life Satisfaction, Income, and Market Institutions

## Strongest version of the claim

In high-income countries, the Easterlin paradox is overstated because it conflates levels with growth: while life-satisfaction levels show diminishing returns to income, sustained income growth, employment opportunity, and institutional trust continue to predict well-being gains at the margin. State-planning intensity and public-sector employment expansion do not add explanatory power beyond these market-mediated channels. The implication is that prosperity, not planning, delivers well-being at the frontier.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Panel regressions** on high-income countries showing positive, significant coefficients for income growth, employment rate, and institutional trust on life-satisfaction changes, with state-planning proxies adding no significant explanatory power.
2. **Microdata** (Gallup World Poll, European Social Survey, World Values Survey) confirming the pattern at the individual level.
3. **Event studies** of institutional reforms (e.g., post-communist transitions, anti-corruption drives) showing well-being responses.
4. **Robustness** to alternative well-being measures (happiness, Cantril ladder, experienced well-being/day reconstruction).

## Best counterarguments

1. **Social expenditure matters more than income growth**. Nordic countries combine high life satisfaction with high taxes and transfers; the market-institutions framing may capture the social-democratic channel rather than laissez-faire.
2. **Relative income, not absolute income**. Easterlin's evidence that relative comparisons dominate absolute gains is robust; institutional trust may proxy for equality rather than markets.
3. **State planning is poorly measured**. Government consumption share is a weak proxy for planning intensity; active labour-market policies, industrial strategy, and public R&D may improve well-being through security and opportunity channels not captured by the crude proxy.
4. **Adaptation and set-point theory**. Long-run well-being may be genetically and culturally determined, making policy effects transient regardless of institutional structure.
5. **High-income sample selection**. The claim is bounded to high-income countries; this excludes the largest well-being gains from development, which often occur under mixed or state-directed models.

## Boundary conditions

- Bounded to **high-income countries** where basic needs are largely satisfied.
- Institutional trust may proxy for **social cohesion** rather than market institutions per se.
- Short-run business-cycle fluctuations in employment may dominate long-run institutional effects.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with institutional-quality and growth-literature claims. Tensions with welfare-state well-being arguments and with Easterlin-paradox scepticism about income effects.
