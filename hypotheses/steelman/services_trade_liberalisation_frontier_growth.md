# Steelman — Services trade liberalisation predicts frontier productivity better than goods-sector industrial policy after 1990

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that high-income economies after 1990 underwent a structural shift toward services-dominated output, and that productivity growth in this sector was primarily constrained by regulatory barriers to cross-border services provision (professional licensing, nationality requirements, data-localisation rules, restricted FDI in services) rather than by insufficient goods-sector industrial policy. Liberalising these barriers allowed knowledge-intensive services to scale, compete, and innovate, producing TFP gains that goods-sector subsidies and tariffs could not replicate in mature economies.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive services-liberalisation coefficient**: A robustly positive and significant coefficient on services-trade restrictiveness reduction in a TFP growth regression for OECD countries, with the goods-sector industrial-policy coefficient insignificant or negative.
2. **Sectoral TFP evidence**: Services-sector TFP should respond more strongly to services liberalisation than manufacturing TFP responds to goods industrial policy.
3. **Case-study support**: The EU Single Market in services (post-2006 Services Directive) should show TFP gains in member states that implemented it most fully. Australia's competition reforms in the 1990s (National Competition Policy) should show services productivity gains.
4. **Ex-US robustness**: The result should not be driven solely by US deregulation episodes (e.g., Telecommunications Act 1996, airline deregulation).

## Best counterarguments

1. **Thin data**: The OECD STRI begins in 2014; earlier data must be backcast from PMR and regulatory surveys, introducing measurement error that may bias results. Goods-sector industrial policy (tariffs, subsidies) is better measured over the full 1990-2020 window, creating asymmetric measurement precision.
2. **Bundled reforms**: Services liberalisation in high-income countries was typically bundled with privatisation, competition policy, and digital infrastructure investment. Disentangling the services-trade component from the bundle is empirically difficult.
3. **Semiconductor and pharma counter-examples**: The CHIPS Act, EU Chips Act, and Operation Warp Speed (COVID-19 vaccines) are goods-sector industrial policies that produced measurable TFP and innovation gains in frontier economies. The 1990-2020 window excludes the 2022+ chip-policy resurgence, but the existence of these cases weakens the blanket claim.
4. **Baumol's cost disease**: Services sectors (healthcare, education, government) may have intrinsically slower TFP growth due to non-tradability and labour intensity. Liberalising trade in these sectors may raise volumes without raising measured TFP.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to high-income OECD economies where services dominate value added. It does not apply to: (a) middle-income economies where manufacturing still offers catch-up potential; (b) economies where services are predominantly non-tradable (public administration, retail); (c) periods before widespread digital infrastructure made cross-border services delivery feasible (pre-1990s).

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a frontier-economy counterpart to `export_complexity_market_access_vs_subsidy` (middle-income complexity) and `trade_lib_eu_2007_2013_enlargement_balkans` (institutional integration). It engages the industrial-policy debate by testing whether frontier economies should prioritise services liberalisation over goods-sector targeting. A null result would support the "industrial policy remains relevant even in rich countries" view.
