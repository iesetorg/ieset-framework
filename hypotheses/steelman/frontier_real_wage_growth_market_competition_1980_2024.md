# Steelman — Frontier real wage growth and product-market competition 1980-2024

## Strongest version of the claim

The most compelling case is that vigorous product-market competition forces firms to invest in productivity-enhancing technologies and pass a portion of efficiency gains to workers through higher real wages. In countries with lower barriers to entry, incumbent firms face a disciplining threat that raises the elasticity of labour demand and reduces monopsonistic wage-setting. Over long panels, this competition effect should dominate any short-run rent-sharing destruction, producing a positive association between competition indices and real wage growth at the productivity frontier.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Within-country PMR reform episodes.** The OECD product-market regulation indicators improved substantially in several countries (UK 1980s, Netherlands 1990s, Germany 2000s). If real wage growth accelerated in the decade following these reforms relative to matched non-reforming peers, that would be strong evidence.

2. **Sectoral heterogeneity.** The effect should be larger in non-tradable services where international trade provides less competitive discipline and domestic regulatory barriers matter more (retail, professional services, transport).

3. **Wage decomposition.** The competition effect should show up in both median and mean wages, but with a larger productivity-wage pass-through at the median if competition reduces firm-level wage dispersion (Berlingieri et al. 2017).

## Best counterarguments

1. **Monopsony-power destruction.** More competition can reduce worker rent-sharing and union bargaining power if firms were previously sharing oligopoly rents with employees (Blanchard-Giavazzi 2003, Dobbelaere 2004). In coordinated-market economies with strong sectoral bargaining (Germany, Nordics), PMR liberalisation may not translate into wage gains if bargaining institutions capture the surplus differently.

2. **Trade vs domestic competition confound.** Small open economies (Netherlands, Belgium, Ireland) have high wage growth driven by trade integration, not domestic PMR. The panel-FE should absorb this, but cross-sectional variation in trade exposure remains a threat.

3. **Measurement mismatch.** OECD PMR measures statutory barriers; actual competitive intensity may depend on enforcement, digital disruption, and EU single-market dynamics that the index misses.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to high-income/frontier economies where productivity growth is the binding constraint on wages, not to developing economies where surplus labour or institutional wage floors may dominate. The 1980-2024 window captures the "great divergence" in wage growth (US stagnation vs Nordics/Germany); earlier or later windows may yield different results.
