# Steelman — CHIPS Act 2022 semiconductor capacity 2024-2027

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "CHIPS produced a measurable but undershooting capacity step-change" framing:**

1. **The construction-spending headline is misleadingly large.** US private manufacturing construction spending tripled 2022-2024 (FRED TLMFGCONS), and a large share is plausibly semiconductor-fab construction. But this measures dollars poured, not capacity delivered; semiconductor fabs have 24-36 month construction-to-ramp lags, and a fab that breaks ground in 2024 produces wafers in 2027-2028. Confusing the two measures over-credits CHIPS in the 2022-2024 window.

2. **The advanced-node share is the wrong granularity.** US share of global wafer capacity has held roughly stable at 10-12% for two decades; CHIPS targets the leading-edge logic segment (≤7nm) specifically, where the US share is closer to 0% (Intel's leading-edge capacity is a partial exception). Even if Intel Ohio + TSMC Arizona deliver fully, the US ≤7nm share rises from ~0% to ~10% — a meaningful national-security shift but a narrow industrial-capability outcome. The headline "US semiconductor share" framing conflates these.

3. **Asian competitor response was strong, not weak.** TSMC's 2024 capex was ~$32bn, near record; Samsung announced ~$200bn 20-year Korea-cluster commitment; SMIC continued aggressive trailing-edge expansion despite US export controls. The "US gains share" framing assumes Asian competitors held still or fell back; they did not. The CHIPS effect on absolute US capacity may be real, but the relative-share effect is dampened by a counter-response equilibrium.

4. **Workforce and permitting constraints bound the achievable speed.** TSMC Arizona Phase 1 was delayed from 2024 to 2025+ for skilled-labour shortages; Intel Ohio was pushed out 2-3 years citing market conditions and labour. The US semiconductor workforce gap is estimated at 60-80k engineers + technicians by 2030 (SIA). Subsidies cannot conjure trained operators on the timeline policy advocates assumed; realised capacity by 2027 is plausibly 40-60% of headline.

5. **Subsidy-per-job and subsidy-per-dollar of capacity is unfavourable.** $52bn / ~50k direct manufacturing jobs ≈ $1M per job; $52bn / ~30 fab modules ≈ $1.7bn per module while typical greenfield fab cost is $15-25bn (subsidy covers 5-10%). The marginal effect of CHIPS on the construction decision is positive but small as a share of total project economics; a meaningful share of construction would have happened without CHIPS in some form (especially TSMC AZ, which pre-dated the bill).

6. **National-security justification is out-of-frame for this hypothesis.** The CHIPS Act's primary stated rationale is supply-chain resilience and Taiwan-contingency hedging, not industrial-policy ROI. A pure economic-effectiveness measurement (capacity per dollar, jobs per dollar) systematically understates the policy by treating its primary objective as out-of-scope. Steelman: even if the industrial-effectiveness ratios are unfavourable, the implicit insurance value of pre-positioning leading-edge capacity outside Taiwan may dominate. This hypothesis explicitly does not claim to measure that.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report announced vs realised capacity separately. By end-2027 realised capacity is plausibly 50-70% of 2022-2024 announcements.
- Decompose advanced-node (≤7nm) from trailing-edge separately. The headline industrial-capability claim lives in advanced-node; trailing-edge tells a different story.
- Report Asian competitor capex alongside US capex. The "narrowing the gap" framing requires the gap actually narrows, not just that US capacity grows.
- Disentangle subsidy-induced from export-control-induced reshoring. The October 2022 BIS rules were a separate but bundled instrument.
- Report subsidy-per-job and subsidy-per-MW-of-fab-capacity. These are the policy-effectiveness ratios; absolute capex is not a sufficient measure.
- Flag the national-security insurance value as out-of-frame. The hypothesis does not claim to measure full policy merit.
