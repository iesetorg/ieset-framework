# Steelman — France 35-hour law employment effect

The strongest social-democratic reading: the Aubry laws were paired with substantial cotisation reductions and restructured working time in a way that did create observable net employment, with French Trésor evaluations and Estevão-Sá 2008 estimating somewhere between 350,000 and 500,000 net jobs over the 1998-2002 implementation window. The market-liberal reading that the law was net employment-neutral assumes a textbook labour-demand schedule that the cotisation offsets disrupt, and ignores that the law forced a renegotiation of working-time flexibility (annualisation) that arguably raised firm productivity. A donor-pool gap of less than +1pp is therefore not a refutation of the work-sharing logic — it is consistent with the work-sharing-plus-cotisation bundle being net positive for employment but smaller than the rhetorical +700k claim. The dispositive falsification rule respects this: a +0.5 to +1.0pp gap is the partial state, and the strong-positive +1pp threshold is high enough that hitting it is a clean win for the heterodox reading. A v2 should disaggregate by firm size (the law applied first to >20 employees) for an event-study identification on the size threshold.
