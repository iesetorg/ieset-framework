# Steelman — Bukele Phase 2 / authoritarian-growth-premium 2024-2026

**The strongest argument against the causal claim:**

The hypothesis is that Bukele's second term, with cumulative rule-of-law deterioration but
saturated security-channel benefits, maintains the FDI/GDP/tourism trajectory established in
2019-2024. The opposing view is that the institutional-quality channel operates with a
multi-year lag, that the 2024-2026 window is precisely when the lagged effects materialise,
and that the early-phase FDI growth was driven by remittances and post-COVID base effects
rather than a genuine policy-content channel.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage:

1. **Rule-of-law effects on FDI lag 3-5 years.** The Daude-Stein (2007) and Kaufmann-Kraay
   regressions identify rule-of-law effects on FDI with multi-year lags, not contemporaneous
   correlations. The 2019-2024 SLV observation may simply be too early; the institutional-
   regression effects start binding in 2024-2026 as foreign investors re-evaluate the durable
   rule-of-law trajectory. A "supported" verdict on continued FDI growth in 2024-2025 may
   not survive a 2026-2028 follow-up.

2. **Constitutional re-election is a regime-type signal, not just a policy signal.** Bukele's
   2024 constitutional re-election (overruling the constitutional ban on consecutive terms)
   is qualitatively different from the 2019-2024 régimen-de-excepción. It signals durable
   regime-type change, not a temporary security exception. International institutional
   investors who tolerated the 2019-2024 phase as a one-off security policy may re-evaluate
   when the constitutional-order signal resolves to "Hungary/Turkey-style soft authoritarianism".

3. **FDI mix matters: greenfield vs reinvested earnings vs M&A.** SLV FDI inflows include
   significant reinvested earnings from existing multinationals (textiles, BPO) — these are
   relatively sticky and don't reflect new-investor decisions. Greenfield FDI from new
   entrants is the more sensitive metric to institutional-quality deterioration. The headline
   FDI-inflow-share-of-GDP number may be sustained by reinvested-earnings inertia even when
   greenfield inflows decline. The hypothesis should ideally separate these channels.

4. **Bitcoin-brand tourism is post-novelty.** The 2021-2024 Bitcoin-conference + CECOT-tour
   tourism premium was a novelty effect. By 2024-2026, the novelty has faded; sustainable
   tourism demand depends on standard product features (security + price + accessibility) where
   SLV is a moderate, not exceptional, performer. Tourism-arrivals plateauing in 2025-2026
   would be evidence of novelty decay, not necessarily institutional-quality bite.

5. **External-shock contamination.** The 2024-2026 window is co-incident with US Federal
   Reserve cycle dynamics, US recession risk, and possible Trump-administration trade-policy
   disruption. SLV outcomes are highly sensitive to the US business cycle (remittances 24%
   of GDP). If the US enters a slowdown in 2025-2026, the SLV FDI and tourism numbers will
   decline for cyclical reasons that have nothing to do with rule-of-law dynamics. The
   synthetic-counterfactual must control aggressively for US-cycle pass-through.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Disaggregate FDI inflows into greenfield, reinvested earnings, and M&A. A
    rule-of-law-binding effect should appear first in greenfield.

(b) Report cumulative rule-of-law-decline metric alongside FDI trajectory so readers can see
    the "deteriorating institutions, sustained capital inflow" paradox directly.

(c) Include US-business-cycle controls (US GDP growth, US policy rate, US recession indicator)
    explicitly so the SLV-specific institutional-quality test is not contaminated by US-cycle
    pass-through.

(d) Flag that even a "supported" 2024-2026 verdict does not invalidate the institutional-quality
    literature — the lag may simply be longer than 2-5 years. A 2028-2030 follow-up is the
    proper test of durability.

(e) Accept that a "mixed" verdict (FDI sustained, greenfield declining, tourism plateauing)
    is the most likely outcome and that the policy takeaway is "the institutional-quality
    channel is real but slow; security improvements provide a multi-year buffer."
