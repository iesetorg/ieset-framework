# Steelman: In-Work Benefits Low-Income Employment Panel

The best critique is that in-work benefits can subsidise low wages and allow employers to capture part of the transfer through lower pay. They may increase measured employment while leaving workers in unstable, low-hour jobs, and administrative complexity can reduce take-up among the very households the policy is meant to help.

Non-work-conditioned support can also improve employment indirectly by stabilising housing, health, childcare, and job search. If unconditional or universal supports produce similar employment with lower stigma and better poverty outcomes, the claimed advantage of in-work benefits disappears. The hypothesis should be rejected if employment gains are small, poverty rises, or effects are concentrated in low-quality jobs with no earnings progression.
