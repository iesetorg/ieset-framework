# Steelman — Canada real household disposable income post-2015

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "Canadian household purchasing power stagnated under Trudeau" framing:**

1. **Transfer-expansion reshuffles income toward households the headline series doesn't fully capture.** The Canada Child Benefit (CCB, 2016) is a non-taxable, income-tested child transfer that raised disposable income at the bottom and middle of the distribution by several thousand dollars per child-year for many families. The CPP enhancement (phased from 2019) transfers forward to higher replacement rates in retirement. The $10-a-day child-care agreements materially reduce out-of-pocket costs for families with young children — this shows up as household consumption gain but does NOT necessarily show up in headline "real household disposable income per capita" because the reduction is on the expenditure side. A metric focused purely on disposable income ignores this welfare-relevant expenditure reduction; a purchasing-power-focused metric might show a smaller gap than the framework's primary outcome.

2. **The housing-cost channel is real but it's largely a provincial and municipal policy failure, not a federal policy failure.** Canadian housing unaffordability 2015-2023 is driven by land-use controls, zoning, municipal approvals processes, and interprovincial-mobility friction — virtually all provincial and municipal levers. Attributing housing-cost-driven real-wage erosion to federal Liberal policy is a stretch: the federal policy effect operates mainly via immigration-demand channels (increasing housing demand) and via the National Housing Strategy (small supply-side contribution since 2017). The "real wage net of housing" finding in the framework's hypothesis is important, but it should be attributed primarily to supply-restriction policies at provincial-municipal level that are not Trudeau-era Liberal content. The framework should not let its cross-country design conflate "federal Trudeau policy caused this" with "Canadian system delivered this outcome under Trudeau's watch".

3. **The donor pool's trajectories aren't obviously better once housing is honestly netted out across all comparators.** Australia, UK, New Zealand, Norway have also seen substantial house-price-to-income increases 2015-2023. If the framework nets out housing for Canada, it must also net out housing for the donor pool. The honest finding may be "most advanced economies had similar housing-absorbed-real-income problems; Canada is not an outlier". A spec that asymmetrically nets out housing for Canada but not for donors would falsely advantage the framework's preferred conclusion.

4. **The 2020-2022 pandemic transfer bulge is a substantial one-time effect on Canadian household disposable income.** CERB and related COVID transfers added ~CAD 80B+ in direct household payments in 2020-2021. Canadian household disposable income spiked during this window precisely because of these transfers. If the comparison period straddles the pandemic, the Canadian series is boosted by transfers the donor pool may or may not have matched (US CARES Act also large; AUS JobKeeper; NZL wage subsidy). Attributing the post-2022 reversion to "stagnation" without acknowledging the 2020-2021 spike was itself a transfer-driven deviation is cherry-picking the end point.

5. **Real-wage weakness is partly a labour-supply-expansion story, not a compensation-compression story.** Fast immigration and high labour-force participation growth imply that the marginal worker added to the Canadian labour pool 2015-2023 has, on average, lower productivity than the pre-existing pool (newer arrivals with credentials-recognition gaps, learning the local labour market, initial low-skill roles). Average wages falling while the labour force expands rapidly is partly composition shift, not per-worker compensation compression. The framework's per-capita outcome doesn't separate these; an hours-adjusted per-worker-compensation outcome would partially correct this.

6. **The two-spec rule (OECD household series + WDI GNI proxy) risks false negatives if the OECD series itself is revised downward for Canada due to methodological issues.** The OECD Household Dashboard has had multiple methodological revisions 2015-2023 in the treatment of imputed rent, owner-occupier services, and second-home treatment. Canadian real-estate-heavy household accounts are particularly sensitive to imputed-rent treatment. A finding that relies on the OECD series without explicitly honouring the methodological discontinuity notes would be unreliable.

7. **Pension-enhancement economic effects reach beyond the sample window.** The CPP enhancement phased in 2019 begins to raise replacement rates only from the early 2020s for new retirees; its effects on current household purchasing power over 2015-2023 are small, but its effects on intergenerational saving behaviour and lifecycle planning are meaningful and mostly forward-looking. A cross-sectional 2015-2023 window will understate the full welfare effect of the CPP change.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report both the raw disposable-income spec and the housing-netted spec, and report them symmetrically (net out housing for the donor pool too).
- Report the transfer-share decomposition: how much of observed Canadian household purchasing power came from market income vs transfers? A finding of "market income stagnation absorbed by transfer expansion" is a meaningful finding in either direction.
- Report the pandemic-period separately from the pre-pandemic and post-pandemic periods; a single 2015-2023 coefficient hides the CERB spike and the 2022-2023 reversion.
- Explicitly attribute the housing-cost channel to provincial/municipal policy architecture rather than to Trudeau-era federal Liberal content; the data cannot separate these and the framework should say so.
- State that the "v1.1 fetcher requirement" means the v1 run uses GNI proxy, not the canonical household-disposable series; findings are tentative pending the OECD and StatCan cross-walk.
- Acknowledge that this hypothesis is tightly linked to the housing-cost-driven-real-wage-divergence literature (Glaeser, Hsieh-Moretti) and the finding should be reported in that methodological conversation rather than as a Canada-specific political verdict.
