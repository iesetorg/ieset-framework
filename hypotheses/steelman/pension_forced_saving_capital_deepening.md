# Steelman — Forced-saving pension systems raise capital deepening in catch-up periods but do not guarantee frontier productivity growth

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that mandatory pension contributions channel a predictable flow of savings into capital markets, reducing reliance on foreign capital and raising domestic capital per worker. In capital-scarce developing economies, this deepening directly raises labour productivity and GDP per worker. However, frontier TFP growth depends on innovation, reallocation, and institutional quality rather than on the sheer volume of capital. Pension systems do not directly improve education, competition policy, or technological adoption, so their TFP effects are limited. The claim is a scope boundary, not a negative result: forced saving helps on the intensive margin (capital per worker) but not on the extensive margin (TFP).

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive capital-deepening coefficient**: A robustly positive coefficient on pension-fund assets in a capital-per-worker growth regression, significant and of plausible magnitude.
2. **Null or weak TFP coefficient**: The same pension-assets variable should be insignificant or materially smaller in a TFP growth regression.
3. **IV robustness**: Pension-reform indicators (Chile 1981, Australia 1992, etc.) as instruments should produce the same capital-deepening sign, reducing endogeneity concern.
4. **Catch-up gradient**: The capital-deepening effect should be larger in low-income and middle-income subsamples than in high-income subsamples.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Ricardian equivalence**: Mandatory pension contributions may simply displace voluntary private savings one-for-one, leaving national savings and capital deepening unchanged. The observed correlation between pension assets and capital may reflect pre-existing high-savings cultures (East Asia) rather than a pension-system effect.
2. **Directed investment and political capture**: Pension funds in many countries are required to hold government bonds or finance politically favoured projects. The capital stock may rise, but its productivity may be low (white-elephant infrastructure, SOE bailouts).
3. **Chile post-2019 falsification**: Chile's AFP system (1981-) produced high pension assets and capital deepening, but the 2019 protests revealed that pension payouts were inadequate, and the system is being reformed toward greater state involvement. The capital-deepening gains did not translate into political sustainability or broad welfare.
4. **TFP may respond with long lags**: Deepened capital markets may eventually support venture capital, IPO markets, and innovation financing. A 10-year window may miss these effects, and the TFP null may be a timing issue rather than a structural boundary.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to economies with underdeveloped capital markets and low initial capital per worker. It does not apply to: (a) high-income economies where capital is already abundant and the marginal product of capital is low; (b) economies with weak legal protection of pension assets where funds are dissipated through corruption or political lending; (c) rapidly ageing societies where pension fund decumulation may offset accumulation.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a specific test of the `australia_super_guarantee_saving_effect` claim, generalised across countries. It complements `welfare_state_market_flexibility_complement` by testing a different welfare-architecture dimension (pension design rather than transfer size). A null capital-deepening result would support the Ricardian-equivalence view; a positive TFP result would challenge the boundary and suggest that pension systems also support innovation financing.
