# Steelman: Japan 2004 Pension Reform Sustainability

The strongest counter is that the macroeconomic-slide indexation was not actually triggered for over a decade because Japan was in CPI deflation 2004-2014, blocking the slide rule from cutting nominal pensions, with the result that the headline sustainability mechanism existed on paper but not in practice. The fiscal-balance improvement attributed to the reform is mostly explained by demographic-cycle dynamics and BOJ-financed government debt rolling, not by the pension architecture per se. On the adequacy side, elderly poverty in JPN remained near 20% — the highest in G7 — for the entire post-reform window, but this is a level that was already locked in by family-structure changes (decline of three-generation households) and the gendered work-history of the pre-reform female cohort, neither of which the 2004 reform addressed. The 2012 universal-coverage low-income supplement and 2020 part-time-worker enrolment expansion were piecemeal corrections rather than vindications of the original 2004 design. A defensible reading: 2004 reform introduced useful structural mechanisms but their effective contribution to sustainability and adequacy in the 2004-2018 window was modest; the apparent tradeoff is partly mechanical given deflation.
