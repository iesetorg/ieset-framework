# Steelman — Housing cost driven real wage divergence

The strongest opposing argument: the framework's supply-elasticity story is a one-channel explanation for a multi-channel phenomenon, and treating housing-supply-restriction as the identifying variable commits to a policy prescription that doesn't survive close inspection.

## Supply-elasticity measurement is contested

Saiz (2010) derives US metro supply elasticity from topographic constraints (share of land within 50km unavailable due to water, wetlands, steep slopes) interacted with a regulatory index. Subsequent literature (Glaeser-Ward 2009, Gyourko-Molloy 2015, Hsieh-Moretti 2019) has used different elasticity measures producing materially different rankings. Hilber-Vermeulen (2016) UK supply elasticities rely on land-use regulation indices that have been updated and partly retracted by ONS subsequent local-authority data. The v1 spec's use of canonical academic elasticity measures is defensible but not robust to alternative elasticity constructions. A v2 robustness re-run with Gyourko-Molloy elasticities is needed before any strong claim.

## The "amenity-adjusted wage" literature cuts the other way

Roback (1982) and Rosen (1979) establish that high-cost high-opportunity cities also have high amenities (cultural, labour-market depth, consumption variety) for which workers pay via housing costs. Under this reading, high housing costs in restricted metros are compensating differentials for amenities, not pure transfers from worker to landowner. Netting out housing costs without netting in amenities overstates the welfare cost of supply restriction. Albouy (2016) finds amenity-adjusted net-of-housing wages in US metros are roughly flat across metros, implying the framework's "supply-restricted metros are welfare-worse" reading ignores compensating amenities. A cleaner test would incorporate amenity measures.

## Moretti's agglomeration counter-reading

Moretti (2012, 2013) argues that high-productivity metros are high-productivity precisely because of agglomeration externalities enabled by concentration — which, in equilibrium, produces high land rents as land becomes the binding factor. Under this reading, high housing costs are a *consequence* of productivity, not an independent drag on welfare. Moretti-Hsieh (2019) estimate that relaxing housing supply in three US super-star metros would raise US GDP by 3.7-9.5%, which is a productivity/agglomeration argument, not a distributional argument. The framework's distributional framing may be picking up what is really a productivity story.

## The headline-wage series are not as wrong as the framework implies

The US BLS publishes regional CPI with housing weighted appropriately for each metro, and ONS publishes CPIH which includes owner-occupier housing costs. If the v1 "headline real wage" benchmarks use national CPI rather than metro CPI, the constructed "divergence" is partly a measurement choice rather than a substantive finding. The threshold that correlation between headline and net-of-housing wages be ≤ 0.9 depends sensitively on which deflator is used for "headline".

## Migration adjustment is missing

If workers respond to supply-restricted metros by migrating to elastic ones, the observed real-wage-net-of-housing in a supply-restricted metro reflects only the wages of workers who chose to stay. Selection bias: the stayers may be high-income anchored workers (homeowners, high-wage job-locked) or low-income trapped workers (housing-assistance dependent, family-tied). Either way, the metro-level real wage is not the welfare-relevant statistic. The welfare-relevant statistic involves tracking workers across metros. The v1 spec does not do this.

## Tenancy-composition confound

Metros with restricted supply also tend to have higher rental shares (supply restriction raises prices, pushing marginal buyers into rental). Rental payments and imputed-owner-occupier costs are different in welfare incidence: rental is a pure transfer to the landlord, imputed owner-occupier cost is partly notional. The framework's real-wage-net-of-housing calculation may overstate the welfare loss in restricted metros with high rental shares and understate it in elastic metros with high ownership shares. Tenure composition should be controlled or decomposed.

## The three-country sample is selected

UK, US, Australia are the three canonical "housing crisis" Anglo-liberal economies. Including Germany, Netherlands, France — where supply restriction is arguably as severe but the distributional outcomes are different — would test whether the framework's mechanism is Anglo-specific or general. The v1 country selection prejudges the analysis.

## What would update the framework's prior

If the supply-elasticity coefficient is significant with p < 0.05 but quantitatively small (< 0.3pp/annum tercile gap), the framework should acknowledge the mechanism exists but is not the first-order driver of cross-metro welfare divergence; other channels (productivity, amenities, composition) must carry more weight. Symmetrically, if the coefficient fails the sign test (restricted metros showing more favourable net-of-housing trajectories), the framework should engage the Moretti/Albouy readings directly.

## Policy implications

The housing-supply-restriction framing prescribes planning liberalisation. The amenity/agglomeration reading prescribes coordination with transport and public services to capture agglomeration benefits more broadly. The framework should resist the one-shot policy prescription even if the hypothesis is supported; supply liberalisation in high-opportunity metros is likely net-positive but not a standalone solution.
