# Steelman — Export complexity rises more after market access reforms than after subsidy-only industrial policy

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that export complexity is primarily a function of exposure to global demand for sophisticated products, competitive pressure to upgrade, and access to imported inputs and technology — all of which are enhanced by market-access and competition reforms. Subsidy-only industrial policy, by contrast, often protects incumbents, distorts input prices, and creates rent-seeking incentives that do not translate into genuine capability accumulation. The claim is not that subsidies never raise complexity, but that their average effect is weaker and more contingent on complementary institutional conditions.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Panel coefficient ranking**: A clear positive and significant coefficient on market-access reform in an ECI growth regression, with the subsidy coefficient insignificant or of smaller magnitude, robust to country and year fixed effects.
2. **Case-study evidence**: Chile's bilateral FTA cascade and unilateral opening should show ECI gains relative to Argentina's subsidy-heavy ISI periods. Eastern European post-EU-accession economies should outperform comparable non-accession economies in complexity terms.
3. **Channel decomposition**: High-tech export share and product-count expansion should mediate the market-access effect more strongly than the subsidy effect.
4. **China robustness**: The result should survive excluding China, confirming that the association is not driven by China's unique combination of subsidies and WTO accession.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Korea and Taiwan**: Both achieved massive complexity upgrades through state-directed credit, sectoral targeting, and protected home markets. If the cross-country panel codes these as "high subsidy," the subsidy coefficient is likely attenuated by measurement error but the case-study evidence is strong.
2. **Measurement asymmetry**: Market access (tariffs, FTA depth, Doing Business scores) is well measured cross-country. Subsidy intensity (hidden tax breaks, directed credit, regulatory favours) is poorly measured. The regression may falsely reject subsidies due to attenuation bias.
3. **China as falsification**: China's ECI surge after 2001 was driven by a combination of WTO accession (market access) and massive SOE subsidies, export VAT rebates, and directed credit. Disentangling the two in a cross-country panel is nearly impossible; excluding China is necessary but may remove the most informative case.
4. **Complexity as endogenous**: ECI reflects global demand patterns and existing capability stocks, not just policy. Countries that open may already have the human capital and institutions necessary for complexity; the policy effect may be selection rather than treatment.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is most applicable to middle-income economies with existing manufacturing bases and basic technological absorptive capacity. It is least applicable to: (a) low-income economies where complexity is constrained by human capital rather than policy; (b) small island economies where complexity is structurally limited by scale; (c) resource-rich economies where Dutch disease creates a complexity ceiling regardless of policy.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a direct test of the `infant_industry_protection_capability_effect` claim, reframed from "does protection build capability?" to "which policy channel builds complexity faster?" It complements `trade_lib_china_wto_2001_manufacturing_export_surge` by generalising beyond China. A split result (market access wins on average, subsidies win in high-capacity East Asian states) would support a contingent-capacity view of industrial policy.
