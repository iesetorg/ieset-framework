# Steelman — resource-extractor nationalisation reduces output

The strongest opposing argument: the framework's "autonomy matters, not ownership" formulation is sophisticated, but the evidence for it is softer than it appears, and three structural issues complicate the causal identification.

## 1. The Aramco problem

Saudi Aramco is the framework's preferred pro-nationalisation counterexample (high output, preserved technocratic autonomy). But Aramco's "autonomy" was never tested against political interference in the way PDVSA's was, because Saudi domestic political economy stabilises extractor output through patronage flows that **make political interference unnecessary** — the extraction output is already aligned with the regime's survival function. That's not autonomy in the sense the framework claims it is; it's coincidence of regime interest with technical-efficiency interest.

If the discriminating variable were really "autonomy", then the prediction would be: when a regime's political interest diverges from technical efficiency, even nominally autonomous state extractors degrade. Saudi Arabia hasn't tested this because the divergence hasn't occurred at material scale. The counterexample doesn't do the work the framework asks of it.

## 2. Venezuela's output collapse predates the 2003 purge

PDVSA output peaked in 1998 at ~3.5 mbpd and was already on a shallow decline through 2000-2002. The 2003 strike and Chávez's response — which is the framework's canonical "nationalisation with autonomy loss" event — accelerated an existing decline rather than initiating it. Separating the nationalisation effect from the existing decline, the reserves-policy decisions of the 1990s (Apertura), and the collapse of global maintenance investment during the 2014-2016 oil crash is much harder than a simple synthetic-control specification admits.

Hausmann-Rodríguez (2013) and Monaldi (2014) both estimate the nationalisation-specific causal share at 40-60% of the post-2003 decline, not 100%. The framework should prefer the smaller, better-identified estimate.

## 3. Mexico's Pemex is not a clean treatment

The 1938 Cárdenas nationalisation is the framework's long-duration case, but Pemex's long subsequent output decline intertwines with (a) geology (reservoir depletion at Cantarell, geology-driven), (b) Mexico's broader institutional trajectory, and (c) the restriction on private partnership enforced until Peña Nieto's 2013 reform. Attributing Pemex's trajectory to nationalisation-as-such vs geology vs partnership-restriction requires identification the framework should be explicit about.

## 4. Nordic and Gulf NOC "success" cases are sample-selected

The framework includes Norway Statoil + Saudi Aramco as success cases but excludes: Malaysia Petronas (mixed; autonomy + output growth), Brazil Petrobras (high output but massive corruption), Russia Rosneft (high output but serves regime interests that aren't output-maximising), Angola Sonangol (catastrophic governance). Including the full sample of NOCs with technocratic-autonomy pretensions produces a much noisier picture than the tight Norway-Aramco vs Venezuela-Argentina comparison.

## What would update the framework's prior

1. **A well-identified synthetic-control estimate for Venezuela post-2003 showing nationalisation-specific effect < 30%** of total decline — would warrant narrowing the claim.
2. **Any Gulf NOC other than Aramco showing sustained output outperformance with preserved autonomy** — would strengthen the claim.
3. **Norway Statoil/Equinor output trajectory diverging negatively from its synthetic control in years of weak political alignment** — would falsify the autonomy-not-ownership formulation.

## Reading the result honestly

The 0.85 prior is defensible because the framework's formulation (autonomy matters) is nuanced. But the specific empirical claim about output should be honest about attribution: Venezuela's decline has multiple causes, nationalisation is one of them but not the entirety, and the framework should report attribution shares rather than treatment ATT magnitudes.

If the synthetic-control + placebo specification survives scrutiny, the autonomy-not-ownership claim is correctly calibrated. If Norway shows measurable negative divergence during political-interference windows, the framework should update toward "ownership + autonomy both matter" rather than "autonomy is the discriminator".
