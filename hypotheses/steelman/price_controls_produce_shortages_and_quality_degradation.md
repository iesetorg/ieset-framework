# Steelman — price controls produce shortages

The strongest opposing argument: the framework's claim is empirically robust on static allocation grounds but fails to engage three cases where price controls plausibly addressed problems markets weren't solving.

## 1. War-time rationing is not decorative

The framework excludes emergency wartime controls <36 months as a category, but that exclusion is question-begging. US, UK, and Canadian WWII rationing + price controls produced measurably better distributional outcomes than pure market allocation would have under hyper-constrained supply — including for nutrition, which is measurable. Rockoff (1984, *Drastic Measures*) — which the framework cites — is more nuanced than the "always fails" framing implies. Rockoff concludes that wartime controls worked to distribute scarcity, and that the problems appeared when controls persisted past the war.

The 36-month exclusion is reasonable, but it should not be advertised as "no counterexamples" when the framework has pre-excluded the class that might have been.

## 2. Specific non-emergency cases that warrant engagement

- **US wage-price controls, 1971–1972** (before expectations fully adjusted) coincided with temporary inflation deceleration and a GOP electoral benefit. The framework's case rests on 1973–1974 once the freeze was lifted. But the temporary period was not a failure on its own terms — Nixon got re-elected, inflation paused, and the later acceleration came from monetary factors (Burns's accommodation) that would have produced accelerating inflation with or without the price controls.
- **Post-2010 UK energy price caps (Ofgem)** — caps on retail energy margins have produced measurable consumer savings relative to counterfactual competitive pricing in an oligopolistic supply market. The framework should engage with the specific case of price caps in persistently non-competitive markets as distinct from price caps in competitive markets.
- **Pharmaceutical reference pricing in Germany, Australia, and Japan** — negotiated ceiling prices below manufacturer posted prices have reduced drug spending without producing shortages, because manufacturers face demand elasticity at the national-payer level rather than at the individual consumer level. The framework's "binding ceiling → shortage" model applies to competitive markets, not to monopsony-bargaining contexts.

## 3. The model-mechanism caveat

Price ceilings produce shortages when **supply is elastic and demand is inelastic at the ceiling**. When either condition fails (e.g. heavy sunk-cost monopolies with inelastic supply, or genuinely inelastic supply from natural-resource rents), the deadweight welfare cost of a ceiling is much smaller. The framework's 0.95 prior is calibrated on the competitive-market case and should not transfer to monopoly-rent settings without explicit adjustment.

## What would update the framework's prior

1. **Any documented pharmaceutical reference-pricing regime that produced both net-negative welfare and shortages at scale** — would vindicate the universal claim.
2. **Any documented monopoly-rent price cap (e.g. natural gas pipeline rates, terminal-port fees) that produced shortages** — would extend the claim to monopoly contexts the steelman carves out.

## Reading the result honestly

If synthetic-DiD effects are positive-and-significant across episodes AND zero documented counterexamples at policy-relevant scale survive scrutiny, the framework's strong claim holds. If pharmaceutical reference-pricing or monopoly-rent caps survive as counterexamples, the claim should narrow to "price ceilings in competitive markets with elastic supply produce shortages and misallocation", which is still correct and actionable but less sweeping than "no scenario it worked".

The 0.95 prior is defensible on the strong-version claim for the competitive-market case. For the universal claim the steelman pushes back on, 0.85 is more honest.
