# Steelman — Bundesbank rule-bound mandate and inflation track record (1948-1998)

## The strongest opposing argument

A serious heterodox or revisionist reading of the Bundesbank legend rejects the simple "rule-bound mandate → low inflation" causal arrow on multiple fronts:

1. **Social preference, not mandate design.** Hyperinflation 1923 and 1948-49 currency reform left a near-universal German political preference for price stability. The Bundesbankgesetz 1957 codified that consensus rather than imposing it. A counterfactual Germany with the same statutory mandate but different political-economic priors would not have produced the same outcome. The mandate is downstream of the preference, not vice versa.

2. **External surplus and DM undervaluation.** Germany's persistent current-account surplus and the Bretton-Woods-era undervaluation of the DM imported low inflation through cheap imports and competitive export prices. Comparator central banks (Fed, BoE, Banque de France) operated under different external balances; comparing them on inflation-rank is partly comparing external positions, not mandate designs.

3. **The monetary-targeting record is mixed.** Hetzel and Goodhart have argued the Bundesbank missed its own M3 targets in roughly half of years 1975-1998. The "rule-bound" frame elides the substantial discretion the Bundesbank exercised in target-setting and target-revision. The institution was less mechanical and more discretionary than the Freiburg-school reading suggests; its outcomes are partly the product of skilled discretion, not just rule-bound restraint.

4. **Volcker-Reagan disinflation comparison.** The Fed under Volcker delivered a disinflation comparable to the Bundesbank's track record, under a discretionary mandate. That single comparator suggests mandate design is not the binding factor.

5. **Survivorship and selection.** A test that ranks Germany against OECD-only comparators excludes the central banks that lost the inflation game most badly (LATAM, post-Soviet) — making the Bundesbank's rank look impressive against a narrow peer set.

## What the framework still captures

The hypothesis is deliberately narrow: it tests the descriptive claim that the Bundesbank delivered low mean and low-volatility inflation against an OECD peer panel 1948-1998. Even granting that the causal mechanism is contested, the rank-test is robust — if the Bundesbank ranks below median on either dimension, the ordoliberal track-record claim collapses on its own terms. The spec does not claim the mandate caused the outcome; it claims the mandate-bound institution achieved the outcome it was designed for, which is the minimal Freiburg-school assertion.
