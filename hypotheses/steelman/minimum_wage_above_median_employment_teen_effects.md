# Steelman — High-bite minimum wages do not break the causal chain

**The strongest arguments for high-bite minimum wages and against the 2nd/3rd-order chain claim:**

1. **Cengiz-Dube-Lindner-Zipperer (2019) find essentially zero employment effects even for sizeable state hikes.** The canonical "bunching estimator" on the US state panel 1979-2016 finds wage gains at the bottom without offsetting job losses in the relevant occupations. Seattle's hike, the most aggressive US case, shows mixed evidence — the UW team found hours reductions but Berkeley's Reich team found no employment effect using a more defensible control group. If even high-bite US events produce ambiguous second-order evidence, the causal chain is not robust.

2. **Monopsony is the correct default in low-wage labour markets.** The textbook competitive model predicts disemployment from price floors; the monopsony model predicts employment can RISE. Recent evidence on concentration in low-wage labour markets (Azar-Marinescu-Steinbaum) argues many US local labour markets are concentrated enough for a modest minimum wage to move output toward the competitive benchmark. In such markets the "first-order: wages up; second-order: employment down" framing is wrong at the first step.

3. **Measured bite ratios are misleading proxies for "how binding" the minimum is.** The Kaitz index treats the wage distribution as exogenous, but when a minimum wage binds, employers reorganise: compression of the lower wage distribution raises the measured "median" that the minimum is compared to. Bite ratios endogenously become smaller the more employer responses redistribute wages up through compression.

4. **The UK National Living Wage is the strongest counter-example to the causal chain.** The UK Low Pay Commission schedule took the minimum from ~46% of the median in 2015 to ~60% by 2020-21, bite ratios clearly above the "danger zone" in US economics literature. Low Pay Commission evaluations and Dube's UK review find small-to-no employment effects, stable NEET rates, and no informalisation. A first-order success with null second-order effects is exactly what the hypothesis claims would falsify it — and that is the UK's own evaluation evidence.

5. **German Mindestlohn 2015 introduction: productivity channel, not employment channel.** The Mindestlohn at €8.50 was an unambiguously high-bite policy for East Germany. The IAB evaluations found small employment adjustments, some hours reductions, but substantial within-firm productivity gains and formal-sector churn rather than informalisation. The counterfactual is that German firms RESPOND to wage floors with training investment and process reorganisation — the productivity ladder is reinforced, not broken.

6. **NEET and informalisation have powerful confounders.** NEET rates respond to business cycle shocks, migration composition, education-system changes, and housing-cost pressure. Attributing a 0.5-1.0pp NEET divergence to minimum-wage bite is a heroic identification claim; the third-order test may detect noise rather than a real mechanism.

7. **Welfare calculus: wage gains are large, employment losses (if any) are small.** Even under the pessimistic elasticity estimates, a $1/hour wage increase multiplied by all retained workers dwarfs the earnings lost by marginally displaced workers in dollar terms. The first-order gain is real and the second-order cost (if non-zero) is typically smaller than the first-order gain. Hypothesis framing that emphasises the second/third-order chain without weighting the first-order gain is incomplete welfare accounting.

8. **Teens are the wrong outcome group for a welfare test.** Teen employment is substantially education-coupled; a teen dropping out of retail employment to stay in school an extra year is not a welfare loss but a gain. Falsification tests centred on teen E/P divergence can confuse a beneficial substitution toward education for a harmful disemployment effect.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Always report the FIRST-ORDER wage gain alongside any SECOND-ORDER disemployment estimate; present the ratio of welfare gained to welfare lost.
- Distinguish hours reductions from job losses; a shift from 35 to 30 hours at a higher wage is not straightforwardly worse.
- Report the UK NLW case prominently because it is the cleanest high-bite case and the strongest steelman data point.
- Use age-cohort-adjusted education controls before attributing teen-E/P divergence to disemployment.
- Flag monopsony diagnostics (local labour-market concentration) as moderators; results in high-concentration regions should be interpreted with the monopsony model in mind.
- Be conservative about the third-order chain — informalisation is hard to measure, NEET is confounded, and persistence claims require long post-treatment windows.
