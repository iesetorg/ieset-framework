The strongest counterargument is policy endogeneity. Governments often raise
tariffs during recessions, balance-of-payments stress, or import surges, so a
negative tariff-consumption coefficient may partly reflect bad macro conditions
that caused the tariff rather than the tariff causing weaker consumption.

Consumption growth is also a broad welfare proxy. Trade barriers can protect
some domestic jobs or stabilize strategic sectors even when aggregate consumer
variety falls. Product-line counts can move because of reporting changes,
customs thresholds, or HS revisions.

A fair interpretation should separate consumer-welfare evidence from a full
political-economy judgment. Stronger evidence would come from reform episodes
with pre-trends, product-level price data, and distributional incidence.
