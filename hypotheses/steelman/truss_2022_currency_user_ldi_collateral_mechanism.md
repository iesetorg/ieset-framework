# Steelman — Truss 2022 LDI / currency-user-collateral mechanism

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The LDI mechanism is well-documented and the BoE intervention did reverse yields, but reading the episode through a currency-user lens stretches the MMT framework: UK pension funds are domestic institutions, GBP is the host currency, and the "FX collateral" exposure was an internal financial-architecture choice, not the foreign-currency-debt situation MMT typically uses to define currency-user status. Calling this a currency-user episode generalises the framework into a domain where the standard fiscal-credibility reading is also strong.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **The trigger was a fiscal-credibility event regardless of mechanism.** The mini-budget announcement on 23 September 2022 was a £45bn unfunded tax cut. The market response — gilt yields up 80bp on 23-26 September *before* the LDI mechanism activated — is a fiscal-credibility response, not a collateral-call cascade. The LDI mechanism amplified the move but did not initiate it. Coding the entire episode as "institutional rupture" downweights the fiscal-credibility channel that fired first.

2. **Pension fund FX exposure is small in absolute terms.** UK DB pension fund FX-denominated derivative exposure was material relative to UK gilt market liquidity but small relative to the pension funds themselves; calling them "marginal currency-users" at the systemic level is a reach. The mechanism is best characterised as leverage-and-collateral fire-sale rather than currency-user dynamics.

3. **GBP recovery to within 3% by end-October is partly endogenous to fiscal reversal.** Truss resigned on 20 October. Hunt's reversal of the mini-budget on 17 October pre-dates the GBP recovery. The fiscal-credibility-restoration reading explains the GBP path equally well; the LDI-and-BoE reading explains the gilt yield reversal but not the broader currency stabilisation.

4. **The institutional reform that followed targets LDI specifically, not fiscal architecture.** This is the strongest evidence for the hypothesis. The TPR/BoE post-mortem identified LDI collateral standards as the mechanism, and the policy response was financial-stability regulation. But the political response was Truss resigning over fiscal credibility, not Treasury reforming pension regulation. Both readings are partially supported by the policy responses.

5. **Generalisation to non-UK cases is unclear.** If the LDI mechanism is the operative channel, the hypothesis predicts that countries WITHOUT LDI architecture (most of Europe ex-UK, Japan, USA) would not experience similar gilt-style spikes from comparable fiscal news. That prediction is not part of the test, and the framework's utility outside UK pension architecture is not demonstrated.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The result card must:

(a) Distinguish the trigger (mini-budget fiscal shock) from the amplification mechanism (LDI collateral fire-sale). Both readings agree on the latter; they differ on whether the former was a fiscal-credibility event.

(b) Report the 30y gilt yield in two sub-windows: 23-27 September (pre-intervention spike) and 28 September - 14 October (post-intervention reversal). The reversal is dispositive for the BoE-as-issuer claim; the pre-intervention spike is contested.

(c) Report GBP and gilt yields against a fiscal-credibility-counterfactual proxy (US 10y / DXY) to isolate UK-specific from global moves.

(d) State that the hypothesis supports the LDI / institutional-rupture mechanism for the AMPLIFICATION but does not adjudicate the fiscal-credibility status of the TRIGGER.

(e) Note that the policy response (LDI collateral reform) supports the institutional-rupture reading on regulatory action, while the political response (Truss resignation, Hunt fiscal reversal) supports the fiscal-credibility reading. Both are real.
