# Steelman — High public-debt overhang predicts lower private investment and slower prosperity gains over 30-year windows

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that sustained high public debt creates expectations of future taxation, financial repression, or inflation that reduce the expected after-tax return on private capital formation. Additionally, high debt raises sovereign risk premia, crowding out private borrowers from domestic and international credit markets. These mechanisms operate with long lags: the full investment and growth costs of debt overhang may take decades to materialise, as governments initially smooth consumption through borrowing but eventually face binding fiscal constraints.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Synthetic-control gaps**: Clear negative gaps for private investment and GDP growth in debt-overhang episodes versus synthetic counterfactuals, with good pre-trend fit and persistent post-treatment divergence over 30 years.
2. **Dynamic local projections**: A negative and significant impulse response of private investment to debt-overhang initiation, with the effect growing over the first 10-15 years and then plateauing.
3. **Channel evidence**: Long-term interest rates and sovereign credit ratings should deteriorate during overhang episodes, and these financial variables should mediate the investment effect.
4. **Ex-Japan robustness**: The result should survive excluding Japan, confirming that it is not solely a demographic-deflation story.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Post-WWII falsification**: The US and UK emerged from WWII with debt well above 100% of GDP and subsequently experienced strong private investment and growth. The hypothesis must explain why these episodes do not refute the claim — likely because wartime debt was held domestically, accompanied by financial repression, and followed by demobilisation and catch-up demand.
2. **Japan as ambiguity**: Japan has had debt above 90% of GDP since the late 1990s and private investment has been weak. But Japan also has rapid ageing, deflationary expectations, and a corporate savings glut. Disentangling debt overhang from these confounders is difficult.
3. **Reverse causality**: Countries accumulate debt because growth is weak (automatic stabilisers, counter-cyclical policy). The debt-overhang indicator may be a symptom of low growth rather than a cause. Synthetic control and local projections help but do not eliminate this.
4. **Low interest rates**: In the post-2008 era, several high-debt countries (Japan, Germany, US) have faced near-zero or negative real interest rates, weakening the crowding-out mechanism. If debt overhang no longer raises risk premia in a world of abundant global savings, the mechanism may be era-specific.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for economies where debt is held externally, denominated in foreign currency, or where sovereign risk premia are sensitive to debt levels. It is weakest for: (a) reserve-currency countries that borrow in their own currency and face elastic demand for their debt; (b) economies where debt is held by captive domestic investors (pension funds, banks under financial repression); (c) wartime or post-war reconstructions where debt finances productive destruction and rebuilding.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a long-run counterpart to `fiscal_consolidation_credibility_growth` and a complement to `fiscal_dominance_japan_debt_non_crisis`. It tests whether high debt has real costs even in the absence of acute crisis. A null result would support the "debt is just a number in a spreadsheet" view (modern monetary theory, some Keynesian positions); a strong negative result would support the "fiscal space matters" view.
