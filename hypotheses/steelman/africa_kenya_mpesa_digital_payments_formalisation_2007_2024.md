# Steelman — Kenya M-Pesa financial inclusion 2007-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Mobile money is transactional, not intermediated.** M-Pesa's success is in payments and transfers; it is NOT a credit-creation system. Counting the credit-to-GDP rise as M-Pesa's effect overlooks that bank credit growth and M-Shwari (M-Pesa savings/loans, 2012+) are downstream features. The framework should not conflate "people have e-wallets" with "the financial system intermediates more capital".

2. **Kenya's GDP per capita growth 2007-2024 was good but not exceptional in SSA context.** Rwanda, Ethiopia (pre-civil-war), and Tanzania all grew faster from lower bases. Attributing GDP growth to M-Pesa overcredits a feature that, while distinctive, is not the dominant growth driver. The mobile-money channel may have raised the GDP level by 1-2% (Suri-Jack 2016 estimate) — meaningful but not transformative.

3. **The "formalisation" claim is overstated.** Most M-Pesa transactions remain in the cash-substitution range — paying matatu fares, sending remittances within Kenya, buying airtime. Tax revenue collection, formal employment share, and formal-firm registration did not jump. The informal sector remains 70%+ of employment. A "formalisation" thesis needs to show movement on these formality metrics, not just account-ownership.

4. **First-mover does not equal causation.** Kenya's mobile penetration in 2007 was already ahead of much of SSA; Safaricom's market share (>80% of mobile) was a precondition for M-Pesa scale. The hypothesis treats M-Pesa as exogenous treatment but it is endogenous to Kenya's prior telecom-market structure. A synthetic-control design cannot fully address this.

5. **Donor-pool members eventually adopted similar systems.** Tanzania, Uganda, Rwanda, Ghana all built mobile-money rails. By 2024, the Kenya-vs-SSA-peers gap on account ownership had compressed materially. The treatment-effect curve plateaued. The headline 30-ppt-by-2021 gap is the peak; the long-run gap is smaller.

6. **The Ethiopia counterfactual matters.** Ethiopia closed mobile money to non-banks until 2021. Its post-2021 rapid mobile-money rollout (Telebirr) is a natural experiment within the donor pool that could be exploited; the spec only flags it. A more rigorous design would use Ethiopia's 2021 opening as a second event.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report account-ownership and credit/GDP separately; do not combine into a "financial deepening" composite.
- Report cumulative GDP-pc growth as a secondary, not primary outcome.
- Disclose the donor-pool contamination from Tanzania's 2008 M-Pesa rollout.
- Acknowledge the formalisation-claim is harder to test than the headline framing implies.
