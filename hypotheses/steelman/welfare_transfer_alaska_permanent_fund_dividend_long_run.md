# Steelman: Alaska PFD Long-Run Effect

The strongest counter is that AK's poverty trajectory relative to resource-rich peers reflects in-migration selection effects more than the PFD's redistributive mechanism. The PFD is salient enough to attract working-age in-migrants who would otherwise locate in WY/ND/TX, raising AK's pre-transfer income distribution and creating a positive bias in poverty-rate comparisons that is not attributable to the dividend itself. Jones-Marinescu (2018) found AK PFD did not measurably reduce employment, but their identification used only short-horizon variation in dividend amounts; the long-run synth-DiD design is more vulnerable to selection. AK's poverty dynamics are also dominated by Native-Alaskan-village patterns that are non-comparable to the donor-pool states' demographic composition. Resource-cycle exposure differs across donor states (oil-and-gas in WY/TX vs LA Gulf vs ND Bakken phase-2) so the synthetic counterfactual is unstable across post-1982 oil-cycle phases. A defensible reading: PFD has plausibly reduced AK poverty modestly through the direct transfer, but the 3pp+ long-run gap relative to synthetic-control includes selection and resource-cycle confounds that the donor pool cannot fully absorb.
