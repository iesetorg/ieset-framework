# Steelman — Trade openness predicts long-run income convergence more strongly than industrial-policy intensity

## Strongest version of the claim

The most defensible form of this hypothesis is that trade openness operates as a mechanism for technology diffusion and competitive discipline that accelerates income convergence, whereas industrial policy's effect on convergence is contingent on state capacity and is poorly identified in broad cross-country panels. In its strongest version, the claim is not that industrial policy never works, but that its average cross-country effect on convergence is weaker and more heterogeneous than openness's effect, and that the scalar cross-country evidence favours openness as the more robust convergence engine.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **β-convergence interaction**: A positive and significant coefficient on (initial GDP × openness) in a panel growth regression, robust to country and year fixed effects, with the corresponding industrial-policy interaction insignificant or negative.
2. **σ-convergence timing**: Cross-sectional dispersion of income fell more during periods of trade liberalisation (e.g., post-1980s China, India, Eastern Europe) than during periods of industrial-policy intensification.
3. **Case-study triangulation**: Specific episodes — China's WTO accession, India's 1991 liberalisation, Eastern European post-communist opening — should show faster convergence than comparable economies that pursued ISI or heavy industrial targeting.
4. **Channel evidence**: Import penetration and export diversification should mediate the openness-convergence link.

## Best counterarguments

1. **East Asian developmental states**: Taiwan (1960-1990) and South Korea (1961-1997) combined high industrial policy with rapid convergence. If the cross-country panel codes these as "low openness / high industrial policy," the interaction is misspecified. A disaggregated specification (East Asia dummy × industrial policy) may flip the result.
2. **Openness as institutional proxy**: Trade openness may proxy for good institutions, geography, or colonial history rather than cause convergence. The interaction may be endogenous: only countries that have already converged somewhat can afford to open.
3. **China's dual track**: China's convergence post-1980 combined gradual trade opening with extensive industrial policy (SOE sector, directed credit). Disentangling the two is empirically difficult and the composite effect may be misattributed.
4. **Measurement error**: Industrial policy intensity is measured coarsely (Fraser EFW subcomponents are regulatory, not industrial-policy, measures). Attenuation bias from measurement error could falsely make industrial policy look weaker.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is most plausible for middle-income catch-up economies with sufficient state capacity to enforce contracts and property rights. It is least plausible for low-income fragile states (where openness may produce enclave growth without convergence) and for frontier economies (where convergence is definitionally impossible). The 1960-2019 window excludes the immediate post-WWII reconstruction period, during which industrial policy was more effective in Western Europe.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis complements `liberal_free_trade_partner_growth_panel_1990_2020` by shifting the focus from growth levels to convergence dynamics. It is in tension with `infant_industry_protection_capability_effect`, which tests the developmentalist claim directly. A split result (openness wins on average, industrial policy wins in high-capacity cases) is the most likely synthesis.
