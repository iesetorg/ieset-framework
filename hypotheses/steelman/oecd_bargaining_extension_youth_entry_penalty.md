# Steelman: OECD Bargaining Extension Youth Entry Penalty

The best defence of bargaining extension is that it prevents low-road competition, stabilises expectations, and encourages firms to invest in training rather than poaching or churning young workers. Sectoral bargaining may also solve coordination problems by creating portable wage ladders and apprenticeship norms, so a raw extension gap could miss the institutional package that makes the system work.

The measurement problem is serious: bargaining coverage, union density, and legal extension are not the same thing, and the gap between coverage and density may proxy high-trust corporatism rather than rigidity. Youth employment is also shaped by education enrolment, housing costs, immigration, and macro shocks. The hypothesis should be overturned if extension systems with strong training and ALMP institutions match or outperform decentralised systems on youth entry and firm creation.
