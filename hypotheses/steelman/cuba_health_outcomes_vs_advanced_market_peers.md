# Steelman — Cuba health outcomes vs advanced-market peers, 1960-2000

The strongest argument against this harsher companion spec is that it may answer
an ideologically satisfying question more than a fair empirical one. If the real
claim under debate is "Cuba achieved unusually strong health outcomes for a poor,
embargoed country," then jumping straight to Japan, Spain, or South Korea risks
substituting a rich-country benchmark for the original hypothesis. A failure here
would weaken universal-superiority rhetoric, but it would not erase the possibility
that Cuba still outperformed what its income level would normally predict.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **The subgroup is intentionally mean, so threshold design matters a lot.** In a
   7-country pool, one-rank changes are large. A spec that uses rank cutoffs must
   explain why "top half" is the right bar and why the comparison is not just a
   hidden way of guaranteeing failure.

2. **Rich comparators bundle technology, capital depth, and state capacity.** Japan,
   Spain, Israel, Portugal, Greece, and South Korea are not just "market economies";
   they are higher-income societies with richer hospital systems, better equipment,
   and more fiscal capacity. If Cuba trails them, that may reflect wealth and access
   to technology rather than any special failure of socialist public health.

3. **If Cuba still beats its income rank, that is meaningful even if it loses on raw
   rank.** The fairest reading is not just "did Cuba beat Japan?" but "did Cuba's
   health outcomes outrun its material-income position?" A result card that focuses
   only on bottom-half rank while ignoring income context would overstate the case
   against the Cuban model.

4. **The Soviet-subsidy confound becomes even more important here.** If Cuba built
   durable human capital, hospital stock, and preventive-care infrastructure during
   the subsidy era, its later outcomes may still partly reflect external support.
   The subgroup comparison alone cannot distinguish durable institutional quality from
   historical subsidy-financed capital accumulation.

5. **Official-data concerns remain live.** Cuba's IMR and life-expectancy series still
   inherit the usual official-reporting objections. In a small rich-country subgroup,
   modest measurement shifts can move Cuba by a whole rank.

How this steelman should shape the result card:

- Show Cuba's exact 2000 rank on life expectancy, infant mortality, and income.
- State clearly that this is a stress test of the *universal* claim, not the only
  relevant Cuba-health comparison.
- Report whether Cuba at least beats its income rank, even if it does not clear the
  top-half thresholds.
- Keep the LATAM and broad non-LatAm companion specs linked so readers can see the
  gradient: friendly regional pool, broad mixed market pool, and advanced-market-only.
