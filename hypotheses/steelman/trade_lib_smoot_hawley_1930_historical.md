# Steelman — Smoot-Hawley 1930

## The strongest opposing argument

1. **Income collapse, not tariffs, drove the trade collapse.**
   Eichengreen-Irwin (2010) decomposition attributes most of the
   1929-1933 trade fall to the income collapse (driven by gold-
   standard monetary contraction); Smoot-Hawley's marginal
   contribution to the trade fall is estimated at roughly
   one-third of the total or less.

2. **Retaliatory cascade attribution is historiographically
   contested.** Some retaliatory tariff measures predated
   Smoot-Hawley (the 1929 commodity-tariff acts in several
   European countries); the cascade story partly reverses cause
   and effect.

3. **Gold standard mechanics dominate.** The 1931 sterling
   devaluation and subsequent gold-standard exits explain
   much of the bilateral-trade composition shift over 1932-1933.

## What the framework still captures

The descriptive magnitude test is the right scope and is robust to
all three critiques: it records the magnitude of the collapse
without attributing causation. The disclosure explicitly cites the
Eichengreen-Irwin caution. This spec is included for long-history
framework calibration, not to relitigate the Smoot-Hawley
attribution debate.
