# Steelman: Hong Kong Minimal-State Growth Miracle 1960–1997

The strongest case against the laissez-faire attribution is geographic and
demographic. Hong Kong occupies a deep-water natural harbour at the mouth of the
Pearl River Delta — the richest river-delta manufacturing region on earth — a
geographic advantage that would generate trading income under almost any policy
regime. The 1960s–1990s growth coincides with the global manufacturing export
boom from which any open, low-cost East Asian location could benefit; Thailand,
Taiwan, and South Korea all achieved comparable growth with substantially more
state intervention.

Singapore is the decisive comparator: it achieved similar convergence outcomes
with Housing Development Board public housing (80%+ of population), the Central
Provident Fund mandating 20%+ savings, government-linked corporations across
strategic industries, and the Economic Development Board targeting industrial
sectors. If geography and openness explain both, the minimal-state claim is
redundant.

Hong Kong also benefited from a specific unrepeatable shock: massive migration
of entrepreneurial capital and skilled labour fleeing the mainland, first in
1949–1950 and again during Cultural Revolution disruption. This exogenous
human capital endowment significantly overstates what the policy regime alone
could achieve. Post-1997 SAR governance with expanded social spending and
housing intervention maintained strong performance through 2019, further
complicating the attribution of pre-handover growth to minimal government.
