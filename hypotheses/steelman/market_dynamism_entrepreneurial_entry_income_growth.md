# Steelman - market_dynamism_entrepreneurial_entry_income_growth

Claim tested: Higher formal new-business entry density predicts faster subsequent real income growth.

The strongest skeptical reading is that formal entry density is partly a measurement artifact: rich countries register more firms because informality is lower, legal systems are easier to navigate, and services are more formalized. New firms can also be low-productivity churn rather than Schumpeterian renewal. A Marxist account could argue that entry reflects labor-market insecurity or subcontracting rather than broad welfare gains, so the forward-growth test needs to beat income and rule-of-law controls before carrying much evidentiary weight.
