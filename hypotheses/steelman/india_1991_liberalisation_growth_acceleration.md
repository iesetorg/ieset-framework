# Steelman — India 1991 liberalisation growth acceleration

## The strongest opposing argument

The 1991 acceleration is real, but attributing it primarily to the Manmohan Singh liberalisation package overstates the role of the formal 1991 reforms and understates other channels:

1. **Growth was already accelerating from the mid-1980s.** DeLong (2001), Rodrik and Subramanian (2004), and Kohli (2006) have documented that Indian growth picked up around 1980-1981 under Indira Gandhi's pro-business pivot, well before the 1991 crisis. The 1991 reforms extended and formalised a trajectory that was already in motion. Using 1991 as the structural-break date assigns causal credit to the formal reform package when a more careful analysis would split credit with the earlier informal pro-business shift.

2. **Demographic dividend.** India's working-age population share rose steadily through the post-reform period; a material fraction of per-capita growth is demographic-transition mechanics, not policy.

3. **Global growth environment.** The post-1991 window includes the dot-com boom, the Basel-I-era credit expansion, China's WTO accession opening export markets, and broadly favorable global commodity and capital flows. India's integration into that environment produced growth that any reasonably-open economy would have captured; attributing it all to the 1991 package overstates the domestic policy contribution.

4. **Services-sector story is post-2000.** The most-cited Indian growth success (IT services, BPO) is a post-2000 phenomenon with distinct drivers (Y2K demand, dotcom capital, English-language advantage, US outsourcing wave). Folding it into a "1991 reform effect" blurs causality.

## What the framework still captures

Even granting these points, the pre/post annualised-growth gap (≥ 2 pp/yr threshold) is well within the range that a serious reading of Indian economic history supports. The 1991 package was genuinely consequential — industrial delicensing, trade opening, FDI liberalisation — and the structural-break test captures the magnitude without requiring a monocausal attribution.

The honest reading: 1991 is the canonical marker for an Indian liberalisation trajectory that began earlier and deepened later. The framework's simple pre/post test is a valid first-order signal; finer-grained attribution (1980s pro-business shift vs 1991 formal package vs post-2000 services takeoff) is a follow-on question.
