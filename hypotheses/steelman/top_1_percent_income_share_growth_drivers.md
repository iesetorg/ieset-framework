# Steelman — Top 1% income share growth drivers

The strongest opposing argument: the four-channel decomposition asks the wrong question. Top-1% share growth is a political outcome driven by top marginal tax rate reductions and the corresponding behavioural responses — not a marginal-product story overlaid with a smaller rent-extraction correction.

## The Saez-Slemrod-Giertz / Piketty-Saez elasticity reading

Top-1% reported income is elastic to the top marginal tax rate with a cross-country cross-time elasticity of roughly 0.3-0.5 depending on specification. The dominant cross-country pattern — US and UK top-1% shares rose steeply 1980-2020 while France and Germany saw much smaller rises — tracks closely with the steepness of top marginal rate cuts over the same period. Piketty-Saez-Stantcheva (2014) show that the cross-country correlation between top marginal rate reductions and top-1% share growth is roughly 0.5 and survives controls for skill premiums and capital deepening. Under this reading, the framework's "skill services + capital appreciation" channels are proxying for the tax-response mechanism, and the decomposition's claim that (a)+(b) dominate is an artefact of including the tax rate only as a control.

## The decomposition conflates level and change

Cross-country *levels* of top-1% share are strongly associated with skill-services share and capital-intensity. But *changes* over 1980-2020 are associated with political variables — tax policy, union density collapse, minimum-wage real erosion, CEO compensation governance reform — that the framework's decomposition treats as residual. The between-country identification (country FE absorbed) uses only within-country variation, which may be dominated by tax-policy cycles. Running the same decomposition on changes rather than levels could flip the finding.

## The capital-appreciation channel is partly endogenous

Equity price appreciation over 1980-2020 is partly a general-equilibrium response to the same policy shifts driving top-1% wage compensation: lower corporate taxes, weaker labour bargaining, corporate stock buybacks enabled by 1982 SEC Rule 10b-18. Using equity prices as an independent channel for top-1% share growth assumes capital appreciation is exogenous to distributional policy, which is not obviously correct. The channel may be double-counting the rent-extraction story under a "capital-appreciation" label.

## The Nordic anomaly

Sweden, Norway, Denmark all show meaningful top-1% share growth 1980-2020 despite preserving high labour share, compressed wage structures, and strong unions. The framework's decomposition predicts this should not happen — Nordic skill-services shares grew modestly, Nordic equity appreciation was similar to continental Europe, and Nordic concentration is low. If the decomposition cannot account for the Nordic top-1% growth, the framework's cross-country story is missing a mechanism. Candidate: Nordic capital taxation reforms in the 1990s (dual-income-tax systems) created explicit tax-arbitrage between labour and capital income for top earners, generating reported top-share growth without corresponding pre-reform real income growth. This is a tax-response phenomenon, not a marginal-product phenomenon.

## Executive compensation is not specialist-wage

The framework bundles executive compensation into "high-skill services value-added" but the literature on executive pay (Bebchuk-Fried, Edmans-Gabaix) documents governance-failure and stock-option-mechanics drivers that are explicitly not marginal-product. A cleaner test separates exec-comp-driven top-share growth from specialist-wage-driven top-share growth; the SOX-reformed post-2002 US executive compensation data permits this, but the v1 spec does not use it.

## What would update the framework's prior

If the decomposition returns skill-plus-capital share at 0.40-0.50 (just over the threshold) and the top marginal tax rate control absorbs more than 20% of between-country variation, the "supported" finding is weak and the framework should acknowledge the tax-response channel as first-order. Symmetrically, if the decomposition returns skill-plus-capital share above 0.70 with negligible role for finance share or concentration, the framework should probe whether the capital-appreciation channel is picking up tax-policy-driven asset revaluation rather than productive capital deepening.

## Policy implications

A finding dominated by skill-services + capital channels prescribes education investment and capital-income-taxation reforms; a finding dominated by tax-response + rent-extraction prescribes top marginal rate reform, competition policy, and executive compensation governance. The framework should resist preferring the interpretation that aligns with its priors on tax policy.
