# Steelman — Resource rent capture outperforms laissez-faire

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The Norway-Botswana-good / Angola-Venezuela-bad pattern that anchors the hypothesis is not a rent-capture story; it is an institutional-quality story. Countries that are capable of designing, staffing, and running a sovereign wealth fund or a royalty-collection regime that actually captures the rent are countries that started with high state capacity, low corruption, and a political settlement capable of binding future governments to a saving rule. Countries that lack those features cannot implement rent-capture policy even when they nominally declare one — the rent gets captured by connected insiders, not by the sovereign. Looking across outcomes and claiming that the "rent-capture policy variable" explains welfare is therefore partly a tautology: rent-capture-in-name-only countries register as captors in the data while failing on outcomes, but they are failing for the same reason they were unable to capture the rent in the first place.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Dutch disease counter-argument on Norway.** The conventional explanation for Norway's post-1970 trajectory is not the SWF per se but the SWF's function as a Dutch-disease-neutralisation mechanism: by sterilising oil-export earnings abroad, the fund prevents NOK appreciation from destroying Norway's non-oil traded sector. On this reading, the Norwegian SWF is an exchange-rate-management instrument that happens to be structured as a savings vehicle; countries that face less extreme Dutch-disease exposure (Australia's floating AUD with a diversified export base) may obtain the same non-oil-sector protection without needing the SWF. A reading that credits "rent capture" for Norwegian outcomes misattributes: the instrument's effective mechanism is sterilisation, not accumulation.

2. **Small-homogeneous-population moderator.** Norway (5.5m), Botswana (2.4m), Kuwait (4.4m), Qatar (2.9m) are tiny, politically cohesive states where distributional politics over rent are simpler. Australia (26m with federal-state rent-sharing tensions), Canada (40m with similar issues), Nigeria (220m with severe north-south distributional stakes), and Russia (144m with Moscow-region concentration) face collective-action problems that small homogeneous states do not. The "rent-capture policy works" finding may be a "small homogeneous polity" finding; the policy is not transferable to large heterogeneous polities, where the rent-capture design that works at 5m breaks at 200m.

3. **Oil-price regime (1970s vs 2014+) affects extraction incentives.** Rent-capture regimes designed in the 1970s high-price era (Alaska Permanent Fund 1976, Norway's fund architecture 1990) operated against a commodity-price regime of rising or stable oil prices where aggressive rent-capture did not materially shift extraction decisions. Post-2014 shale-driven price collapse inverted the economics: aggressive royalty regimes in marginal-cost basins now shut in production. Australia's comparatively lighter LNG royalty regime may reflect a rational response to a higher-cost-basin-in-lower-price-regime setting, not a laissez-faire ideology failure. Cross-regime comparisons without price-era controls treat all rent-capture designs as equivalent; they are not.

4. **Botswana as a small-country generalisation problem.** Botswana's Debswana partnership with De Beers is genuinely innovative — 50-50 equity split, long-run royalty discipline, SACU customs revenue layered in — and Botswana is the textbook "African resource success" case. But Botswana has 2.4m people and sits in a regional customs union with South Africa, an adjacent middle-income anchor. Generalising from Botswana to the African resource sector at large is an N=1 leap; the next-nearest case (Namibia, 2.5m, diamonds, similar institutional quality) has not produced comparable growth. Two cases in Southern Africa does not discriminate "rent-capture policy" from "Southern African customs-union member with high state capacity."

5. **Alaska Permanent Fund counter-case.** Alaska pays each resident a dividend from the Permanent Fund's earnings (~$1,000-$3,000 per year). Alaska nominally has one of the world's most aggressive rent-capture regimes. Alaska's per-capita GDP is high, but Alaska's non-oil economic development has been notably weak: population growth is slow, non-extractive private-sector employment has stagnated, and Alaska has not generated the kind of diversified post-oil economy that the rent-capture-is-developmental-policy framing would predict. Alaska's within-US comparison (vs Texas, North Dakota) does not obviously favour the aggressive-rent-capture regime on dynamism grounds; it favours it on distributional grounds but the hypothesis's claim is broader than distribution.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Run the primary specification with and without the WGI institutional-quality controls, and report how much of the rent-capture coefficient the institutional controls absorb. If the controls absorb most of the coefficient, the headline should read "rent-capture policy is correlated with good outcomes because it is implemented by high-capacity states" rather than "rent-capture policy causes good outcomes."

(b) Run the anchor-case comparisons as paired case-studies separately from the panel regression. Norway-vs-Australia, Botswana-vs-Angola, Chile-CODELCO-regime-change, and Alaska-Permanent-Fund-regime-change each have their own identification structure; pooling them into a panel coefficient averages away the identifying variation. Each anchor comparison should be reported with its own time series of welfare outcomes, so readers can see the trajectory rather than only the summary.

(c) Test the small-homogeneous-population moderator by splitting the sample at 10m population and re-estimating. If the coefficient is large in the small-population split and null in the large-population split, the "rent-capture works" finding is not a general one and the result card must say so. Policy transfer to large heterogeneous polities is a separate question.

(d) Report the pre-2014 / post-2014 split to address the oil-price-regime moderator. A rent-capture regime that produces positive welfare outcomes in a $100 oil world may produce neutral or negative outcomes in a $60 oil world by shutting in high-cost production.

(e) Disclose that the evidence-type is associational, not causal, and that the hypothesis makes a conditional claim: *conditional on institutional environment being capable of implementing the policy*, rent capture produces better outcomes than laissez-faire. The stronger unconditional claim ("any country would benefit from Norwegian-style rent capture") is not what the data can support, and the result card should say so explicitly.

The strongest version of the steelman argues that the hypothesis is testing whether two variables (rent-capture-policy and institutional-quality) that are highly collinear can be separated in a panel where the variation in one is almost entirely the variation in the other. If the panel coefficient on rent-capture is 0.6 when institutional controls are excluded and 0.1 when they are included, the honest finding is that the two variables are proxies for each other and the policy-transfer implication of the hypothesis is weak. A v2 design with policy-reform within-country identification (Chile copper 1976 reform, Alaska Permanent Fund 1976 inception) is the real route to a causal reading; the cross-country panel is context, not evidence.
