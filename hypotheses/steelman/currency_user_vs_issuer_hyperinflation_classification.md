# Steelman — Currency-user vs issuer classification of hyperinflations

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The classification framework is endogenous: a sovereign issuer that pursues sustained monetary finance against weak fiscal capacity will, in equilibrium, lose foreign-currency access and accumulate FX-denominated stress as creditors flee local currency. Reading every hyperinflation as "currency-user" then becomes a tautology — the state is a currency-user *because* the prior monetary regime had failed, not the cause of the inflation.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Endogeneity of FX-debt accumulation.** Argentina and Turkey did not begin their inflation cycles with high FX debt; FX debt accumulated as a *response* to local-currency unattractiveness driven by prior monetary expansion. Coding the resulting episode as "currency-user-driven" mistakes consequence for cause. A clean test would code FX-debt levels at t-5 years (before the inflation acceleration began) and ask whether they predict the eventual hyperinflation.

2. **The supply-collapse coding is generous.** The 15% real-GDP-per-capita-decline threshold is met by Zimbabwe 2000-2008 and Venezuela 2014-2017, but the supply collapse there was itself partly a consequence of monetary regime breakdown (FX scarcity, import collapse, factor reallocation). Reading supply-collapse as exogenous to monetary regime is ahistorical for these cases. The cleanest supply-collapse cases are wartime (Weimar Ruhr, Hungary 1945-46) where the exogeneity is uncontested.

3. **The "neither" cell is empty by construction, not by physics.** The PK/MMT thesis is that a sovereign currency-issuer with no FX exposure CANNOT hyperinflate from monetary expansion alone. But the historical record contains no such purified case because every state that pursued sustained monetary finance eventually accumulated FX-denominated obligations or experienced supply problems. The empty cell may reflect that the experiment has never been cleanly run, not that the underlying mechanism is absent.

4. **Argentina is the hardest case.** Argentina 1989, 2018-2019, and 2023 all had material FX-debt and populist-fiscal-dominance simultaneously. Coding it as "currency-user" rather than "fiscal-dominance" is a choice. The Argentine literature is divided on which is the operative mechanism; mainstream readings (Cavallo, Sturzenegger) emphasise fiscal dominance, while heterodox readings (Damill, Frenkel) emphasise the FX channel. Both can claim Argentina.

5. **The classification does not say what would have happened otherwise.** Even granting that every episode falls into currency-user or supply-collapse buckets, the test cannot say whether removing those features would have prevented hyperinflation. The descriptive coverage claim is robust; the inferential generalisation (sovereign issuers are immune absent FX/supply collapse) requires a counterfactual the data cannot supply.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The result card must:

(a) Report two coding versions: t-of-breach FX exposure (the spec's primary) AND t-5-years FX exposure (to address endogeneity).

(b) Flag Argentina episodes as "both currency-user and fiscal-dominance"; do not force a single bucket.

(c) State explicitly that the empty-"neither"-cell finding is descriptive, not causal — it documents that no sovereign issuer with clean fiat regime AND no supply collapse has hyperinflated, but cannot rule out that one could.

(d) Note compatibility with hyperinflation_requires_fiscal_dominance — the two hypotheses are co-deterministic on the necessary-conditions reading; they differ on which is the operative mechanism.

(e) Treat Zimbabwe and Venezuela as joint cases; their supply-collapse coding is partly endogenous to monetary regime.
