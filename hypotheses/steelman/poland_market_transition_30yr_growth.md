# Steelman — Poland market transition 30-year growth

## 1. Strongest version of the claim

Poland’s shock therapy created a critical mass of market credibility that attracted foreign direct investment, locked in reforms, and prevented the partial-equilibrium traps that ensnared slower reformers. The combination of early macro stabilisation, rapid privatisation, and later EU regulatory adoption produced a self-reinforcing growth dynamic that persisted for three decades.

## 2. Key evidence the claim would need

- A positive and significant cumulative log-GDP-pc gap between Poland and its synthetic counterfactual over 1990–2024.
- Panel evidence that competition-policy indices and privatisation progress correlate with the growth differential.
- Robustness to excluding EU structural funds (post-2004) or controlling for fund absorption rates.
- Employment and productivity data showing that the gains were broad-based, not concentrated in a few sectors.

## 3. Best counterarguments

1. **Large domestic market.** Poland’s population and agricultural base provided a demand buffer that small CEE states (Estonia, Latvia, Slovenia) lacked. Size, not reform speed, may drive resilience.
2. **EU structural funds.** Post-2004 EU transfers to Poland were among the largest per capita in the bloc. These funds financed infrastructure, R&D, and regional development that directly boosted measured growth.
3. **The comparator is a strawman.** The Czech Republic and Slovenia also reformed deeply and grew robustly. If the “weaker competition” group is limited to Bulgaria and Romania, the comparison may be unfair because those countries started from lower initial income and weaker institutions.
4. **Demographic windfall.** Mass emigration to the UK and Ireland after 2004 reduced labour supply and raised measured wages and productivity, creating a statistical illusion of reform-driven efficiency.
5. **Shock therapy also caused severe transitional unemployment.** The short-run costs (1990–1993) were large; the long-run claim requires that these costs were fully amortised, which is contestable.

## 4. Boundary conditions

The claim is most defensible for large CEE economies with a credible EU accession path. It is less generalisable to non-EU post-communist states (Serbia, North Macedonia, Moldova) or to economies without a large domestic market.

## 5. Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis complements `estonia_market_reform_30yr_income_convergence` and `czech_slovak_market_transition_comparison` as part of the transition-economy cluster. It is in tension with claims that EU accession alone drives convergence, because Poland’s growth differential predates 2004.
