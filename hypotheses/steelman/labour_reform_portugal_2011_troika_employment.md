# Steelman — Portugal troika labour reforms

The strongest objection: Portugal's 2014-2017 recovery was dominated by ECB QE, EUR depreciation, tourism boom, and emigration of around 500,000 working-age citizens — the unemployment-rate fall is partly a denominator effect from labour-force shrinkage, not labour-demand expansion. Blanchard 2007 and IMF reflective evaluations of the Portuguese program acknowledge that internal devaluation through wage compression, not flexibilisation per se, was the binding mechanism — meaning the headline unemployment fall is observationally consistent with a wage-suppression story that pays out as a permanent labour-share decline. The labour-share-paired outcome is the discipline: if Portugal's labour share falls by more than 2pp relative to the donor pool over the 2011-2017 window and stays low, the reforms shifted distribution durably from labour to capital and the employment improvement is the residual side-effect of a wage-suppression-driven adjustment. The PS-led 2015-2019 partial reversal of some MoU labour measures provides a within-Portugal robustness window: if the unemployment improvement persists despite partial reversal, the structural-reform interpretation is supported; if it tracks the partial reversal, the cyclical-tailwind interpretation wins.
