# Steelman — NZ ECA 1991 employment effect

The strongest heterodox reading: New Zealand's 1991-1996 unemployment improvement coincided with a global recovery, an export-commodity tailwind, and the lagged effect of the 1984-1990 Rogernomics liberalisation that arguably did the structural-reform work earlier. Dalziel-Lattimore and the New Zealand Treasury's own retrospective evaluations note that real-wage growth was negative for the bottom three deciles 1991-1999, and that participation rates for Māori and Pacific workers did not recover to pre-1990 levels. A spec that scores ECA on aggregate unemployment risks counting a wage-compression-induced employment increase as a welfare gain when bottom-decile real-wage trajectory is the relevant channel. The productivity-paired outcome is the discipline: if the unemployment improvement is matched by zero or negative productivity growth, the reform's welfare claim is much weaker — labour was just cheaper. Conway 2018 RBNZ analyses show NZ productivity growth was below OECD median throughout the 1990s-2000s, suggesting the ECA bought employment with productivity foregone. A v2 should disaggregate by ethnicity and decile.
