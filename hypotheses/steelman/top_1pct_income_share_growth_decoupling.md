# Steelman — Top 1% income share growth decoupling

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

A defensible market-liberal reading is that the Anglo top-share rise is partly a tax-and-organisational-form measurement artefact, partly a genuine policy-rewarded outcome of an environment more permissive of high-productivity entrepreneurial activity, and that "decoupling" is not the right framing because per-capita growth in the Anglo cluster also accelerated relative to the coordinated cluster post-1980.

Specific objections:

1. **The 1986 TRA S-corporation reclassification artefact (US).** A material fraction of the post-1986 US top-1 rise reflects business income that was previously inside C-corporations now flowing to individual returns; this is an organisational-form change, not a real distribution change. Auten-Splinter and Piketty-Saez-Zucman disagree about the magnitude — Auten-Splinter find roughly half the post-1986 rise dissolves under careful adjustment; PSZ find a smaller correction. The hypothesis as written takes the WID/OWID series at face value; sensitivity to alternative top-share constructions is essential.

2. **Anglo cluster grew faster per capita.** Real GDP per capita 1980-2020 in USA/UK/AUS/CAN was roughly 80-90% cumulative, vs 60-70% in DEU/FRA/SWE/DNK on PWT chained. If the top-1 share rose more in the cluster that also grew more, the "decoupling" framing — that growth and top-share dynamics are independent — is contradicted by the Anglo-cluster sub-sample. The hypothesis claims comparable growth; that empirical premise is contestable.

3. **Bad controls.** Including top marginal income tax rate and union density as controls and asking whether the cluster coefficient survives is a logical error if those policy parameters are themselves part of the "cluster" treatment. The cluster IS lower top rates and weaker unions. Asking whether the cluster effect survives conditioning on its own constituent policies is asking whether there is RESIDUAL cluster effect after accounting for policy content, which is a useful exercise but should not be framed as the binding falsification test.

4. **Coordinated cluster top shares ARE rising.** Germany top-1 share rose from ~10% in 1980 to ~13% by 2020. France from 8% to 10%. Sweden from 5% to 9% (a near-doubling). Calling the coordinated cluster trajectory "flat" overstates the divergence. The right framing is "Anglo cluster rose more steeply", not "coordinated cluster did not rise".

5. **Wage capture by superstar globalised industries.** A non-institutional reading is that the Anglo cluster's higher exposure to global financial-sector and tech-sector returns (English-speaking, common-law, deep capital markets) means a few thousand individuals in finance/tech/CEO compensation capture proportionally more of national income. The cluster-as-institutional-package framing misses that this is a sectoral-composition effect that would persist even if labour-market institutions converged.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report Auten-Splinter-adjusted US top-share alongside WID/OWID.
- Report cumulative per-capita growth alongside top-share rise; do not claim "comparable growth" without showing the data.
- Treat the policy-conditioning specification as informative, not as the binding falsifier; both the unconditional cluster coefficient and the conditional coefficient should be reported with equal weight.
- State explicitly that the coordinated cluster top-share trajectory is also rising, just less steeply, so the framing is "diverging trajectories" not "decoupled vs coupled".
- A sectoral-composition robustness column (controlling for finance + tech sector share of value added) is essential to discriminate between the institutional-bundle and the sectoral-globalisation readings.
