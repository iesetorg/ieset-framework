# Steelman — Asian convergence vs Western stagnation 2000–2023

**The strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Asian convergence is mostly base-effect, not policy-effect.** A country starting at $500 GDP/cap growing at 10% yields faster convergence than a country starting at $40,000 growing at 2% — but the higher growth rate reflects abundant low-hanging fruit (factor reallocation from agriculture to industry, technology transfer from the frontier, population urbanisation), not policy-regime superiority. If Western economies had started from the same base, they would have grown the same way. Attributing Asian growth to specific policy content (market-reform) over-credits policy at the expense of convergence dynamics. The v1 spec controls for 2000-level GDP to address this, but the control is imperfect when the gap is as large as 30x.

2. **The "Western socialism → stagnation" narrative is empirically weak when the data is examined.** Nordic countries have the LARGEST welfare states in the West and grew 2000–2023 at rates comparable to USA. Italy (small welfare state by Nordic standards, high public debt) stagnated. Germany (medium welfare, fiscal discipline) grew moderately. France (medium-large welfare) grew slowly. The relationship between "welfare state size" and "growth rate" across Western economies 2000–2023 is essentially zero. Specific policy content matters more than generic welfare-state categorisation, and the framework should refuse to endorse the simple-category framing.

3. **Asian convergence includes enormous distributional costs often omitted from aggregate growth.** Chinese labour conditions in peak manufacturing era, Bangladeshi garment worker safety (Rana Plaza), Indian farmer indebtedness and protests, Vietnamese environmental collapse, Cambodian land-grab dynamics. Aggregate GDP growth understates these welfare costs. A complete accounting requires disaggregating by labour outcomes, health indicators, and environmental quality, not just per-capita income.

4. **Middle-income trap risk means Asian convergence may stall.** Malaysia, Thailand, Brazil, and Mexico all experienced rapid catch-up phases followed by plateaus at middle-income levels (~$15,000-25,000 GDP/cap PPP). China may be entering a similar phase (demographic headwind, property-sector overhang, productivity slowdown). India faces execution risk on its reform momentum. The "Asia will keep converging at 2000-2023 rates" assumption is itself testable and contested in the literature. The framework should not extrapolate.

5. **Wealth-tier expansion in Asia reflects the SAME growth pattern as income convergence, not additional wealth transfer.** Chinese billionaire counts grew because Chinese GDP grew; these are newly-minted billionaires from underlying economic growth, not wealth transferred from the West. The "Asia gained, West lost" zero-sum framing misreads the mechanism. Global wealth expanded; Asian share grew because Asian GDP share grew. Western wealth also grew, just at slower rates.

6. **Currency + measurement issues matter.** PPP conversion factors are revised every ICP round (2005, 2011, 2017, 2023), sometimes substantially; cumulative growth comparisons can be sensitive to which round is used. Market exchange rate comparisons produce different stories. Informal sector size differs across countries — Asian informal-heavy economies may be systematically undermeasured in early years, overstating later growth. The framework should report sensitivity to these measurement choices.

7. **US has been the exception, not the rule, in Western growth.** If you remove USA from the Western comparator group, the US-vs-West gap is large and the rest of the West (EU-27 + UK + Japan) looks more uniformly slow. "Western stagnation" is mostly "non-US Western stagnation" driven by specific structural features: demographic decline (Italy, Japan, Germany), euro-zone internal imbalances, planning-restriction housing supply (UK), industrial-sector losses from Chinese competition (Germany auto). US is an outlier up, not other West a outlier down.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report Asian cumulative log-growth, Western cumulative log-growth, and the full COUNTRY-LEVEL ranking (so the Western heterogeneity is visible).
- Report conditional-convergence coefficient controlling for 2000 level explicitly.
- Separate USA from "other Western" in the comparator group for a sensitivity spec.
- Include a "what this does NOT show" section explicitly disclaiming the "socialism vs capitalism" binary — the within-Western heterogeneity refutes that framing directly.
- When wealth-tier secondary analysis runs in v1.1, frame billionaire/millionaire count growth as downstream of GDP growth, not as a separate wealth-transfer mechanism.
