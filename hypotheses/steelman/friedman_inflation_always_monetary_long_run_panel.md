# Steelman — Long-run money growth and inflation panel

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is that the long-run cross-country money-inflation correlation is mechanical, not causal. Broad money M2 is dominated by deposits, which are a residual of bank lending and household payment behaviour; nominal demand drives nominal money holdings, not the other way around. A regression of decade-averaged inflation on decade-averaged M2 growth therefore measures the same nominal aggregate twice, and a slope near unity is what an accounting identity predicts under stable velocity, not what a causal QTM would imply. Post-Keynesian and MMT readers treat the regularity as confirmation that money is endogenous, not that it causes inflation. The post-2008 episode — large M2 growth without proportional CPI inflation across the US, EU, UK and Japan — is the empirical kicker: a 100% expansion of base-money plus a 30%+ expansion of M2 produced near-target inflation for over a decade, which the Friedman 1963 prediction does not survive without auxiliary assumptions about velocity collapse. Even on advanced-economy data the cross-section variation post-1990 collapsed (everyone converged to ~2% inflation) so the strong slope is identified almost entirely off 1970s-1980s LATAM and 1990s post-Soviet outliers. A cleaner reading is that the long-run correlation reflects fiscal dominance and exchange-rate regime far more than monetary policy: countries with weak fiscal institutions monetise deficits, get high M2 growth and high inflation, and that single mechanism generates the entire cross-section pattern. The hypothesis would be more robust if it explicitly conditioned on fiscal-dominance status and ran the panel only on countries with autonomous monetary policy and credible fiscal frames; the unconditional slope is a confound between monetary and fiscal regimes.
