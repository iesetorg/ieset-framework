# Steelman — Cuban health outcomes vs LATAM peers, 1960-2000

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The headline claim — that Cuban LE/IMR outperformance over LATAM middle-income peers 1960-2000 demonstrates "socialist health-system superiority" — bundles three separable effects and attributes the joint outcome to whichever is most ideologically convenient. The fair-reader objection is that 1960-2000 Cuba was simultaneously (i) a heavily Soviet-subsidised economy until 1991 (USSR transferred 5-10% of Cuban GDP via favourable sugar/oil terms), (ii) a country with an unusually-flat pre-revolution baseline on health metrics — Cuba was already among the healthier LATAM countries in 1959 — and (iii) a country with a primary-care-emphasising health system. Attribution among (i), (ii), (iii) is the contested parameter. The hypothesis as written treats the joint outcome as evidence for (iii) when (i) and (ii) are at least equally plausible drivers.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Cuba started ahead.** In 1960, Cuban life expectancy was 63.3 years vs a LATAM peer-pool mean of 57.3 years — a +6.0-year head start. By 2000 the gap had narrowed to +3.1 years. This is the OPPOSITE direction from a "Cuba pulled away" story. On LE the data are consistent with "Cuba had a head start; peers narrowed it as health technology and primary-care models diffused." A spec that scores Cuba "supported" on a static 2000 endpoint test misses that LE convergence in this period was very wide and Cuba was a beneficiary of being ahead, not pulling away.

2. **Costa Rica matches or beats Cuba on multiple metrics without the political system.** By 2000 Costa Rica had higher LE (77.3y vs Cuba's 75.9y) and IMR comparable to Cuba's (11.2 vs 6.8 — Cuba lower, but Costa Rica is the second-best in the pool). Chile's LE was 77.1y, also higher. If "primary-care emphasis" is the operative mechanism, Costa Rica's primary-care expansion 1970s-1990s and Chile's post-1990 health reforms produced comparable outcomes without state ownership of the broader economy. The Cuban result is not unique within the pool, which weakens the "socialist exceptionalism" framing.

3. **Soviet subsidies are a confound the data cannot rule out.** Through 1991 Cuba received transfers worth an estimated 5-10% of GDP via concessional sugar/oil terms. Any health system financed by external transfers will outperform a peer on the same income but without transfers. The clean test is the post-1991 sub-period: did the Cuban health-outcome gap hold up after subsidies stopped? In the IMR data, the ratio Cuba/peer-mean was 0.32 in 1991 and remained at 0.32 in 2000 — held flat, neither widened nor narrowed. The LE gap shrank slightly (3.5y → 3.1y). This is consistent with "primary care produced a real but bounded effect, plus some inertia from accumulated capital" — not "socialist system superiority."

4. **Data quality is contested.** Cuban official statistics are produced by a state with strong incentives to report favourable health outcomes. The WDI series back-fills Cuban data from those official sources. The two known concerns: (a) classification practices for early-neonatal deaths (Cuba's IMR may benefit from reclassifying late fetal losses or very-early-neonatal deaths in ways that lower the recorded IMR by 1-3 per 1k); (b) the late-pregnancy abortion rate is high, which removes high-risk pregnancies from the IMR denominator. WHO independent estimates only become available from ~2000, so no contemporaneous external check covers the 1960-2000 window. A 1-3/1k uncertainty band on Cuban IMR would not eliminate the gap (the 2000 ratio is 0.32, well below the 0.50 threshold) but it would shrink it.

5. **The "despite sanctions" framing is selective.** The US embargo applied to bilateral US-Cuba trade but Cuba could (and did) trade with the rest of the world, including health-related imports from Spain, Canada, Latin America, and post-1991 the EU. Treating "sanctions" as a uniform constraint on Cuban health-system performance overstates the embargo's reach during much of this window. Similarly, several peer countries faced their own external shocks (Argentina's 1990s capital-account openings, Venezuela's late-1990s oil collapse, Peru's 1980s hyperinflation) — the embargo is real but is not categorically more health-suppressing than what peers faced.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report the 1960 baseline gap alongside the 2000 endpoint gap so readers can see Cuba started ahead and the LE gap NARROWED. Do not present the supported-verdict as "Cuba pulled away" if only the IMR ratio improved.

(b) Show Costa Rica and Chile's 2000 endpoints alongside Cuba's. If Cuba ranks #3 on LE behind Costa Rica and Chile, the "socialist exceptionalism" framing must be qualified — Costa Rica and Chile achieved comparable LE without a Cuban-style political system.

(c) Report the 1991-2000 sub-period (subsidies ended) so readers can judge how much of the gap depends on Soviet subsidy versus health-system architecture. If the gap held flat or shrank in this sub-period, attribute carefully.

(d) Flag the WDI / Cuban official-data lineage as a known measurement caveat with a 1-3/1k IMR uncertainty band. The WHO independent cross-check is unavailable for most of the window — say so, do not paper over it.

(e) The verdict text should distinguish between "real and important social-outcome gains" (which the data support) and "socialist health-system superiority" (which the data are silent on, because Costa Rica and Chile match Cuba's outcomes without that political system).
