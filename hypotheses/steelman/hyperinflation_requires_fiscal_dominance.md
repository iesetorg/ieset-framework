# Steelman — Hyperinflation requires fiscal dominance

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The "fiscal dominance is necessary for hyperinflation" framing is difficult to falsify because the coding of "fiscal dominance" is flexible enough to fit every episode after the fact. This is less an empirical claim than a definitional one, and the hypothesis risks circularity.

Specific objections the framework must engage with:

1. **Circularity of the coding rule.** "Central bank direct government financing + impaired market access" describes the mechanics of most hyperinflations almost tautologically. If the coding is done post-hoc with knowledge of the episode, every hyperinflation will be coded as fiscal-dominant. The hypothesis becomes unfalsifiable by construction. A genuine test requires pre-episode coding or clear mechanical rules that could in principle identify a non-fiscal-dominant hyperinflation.

2. **Counter-examples are thin but not zero.** Some currency crises with near-hyperinflationary dynamics (Turkey 2021-2023, Argentina intermittently, some transition economies in the early 1990s) have contested fiscal-dominance codings. Turkey's episode involved significant political pressure on the central bank without classical fiscal-dominance mechanics. The hypothesis needs to address edge cases honestly rather than expanding "fiscal dominance" to include any political pressure.

3. **Monetary regime matters independently.** Countries with well-functioning central banks and strong institutions have withstood significant fiscal pressure without hyperinflation. Japan's debt-to-GDP ratio exceeds 200% without inflation collapse. The fiscal-dominance indicator must account for institutional absorption capacity, not just fiscal ratios.

4. **The Sargent 1982 "ends of four big inflations" framing collapses political and fiscal causes.** The four cases (Austria, Hungary, Germany, Poland) all ended inflation via fiscal consolidation AND currency reform AND credible institutional change. Attributing the outcome to fiscal dominance alone understates the institutional machinery.

5. **Necessary ≠ sufficient, and the framework must distinguish.** Even accepting the historical record, fiscal dominance is a necessary but not sufficient condition. Many fiscal-dominant episodes produced high but not hyperinflation. The hypothesis should avoid sliding into the stronger (and false) claim that fiscal dominance always produces hyperinflation.

**How this steelman should be handled in the result card:**

The hypothesis is a *necessity* claim, not a sufficiency claim. The result card must:

(a) Publish the fiscal-dominance coding rule transparently in `hypotheses/monetary/hyperinflation_fiscal_dominance_coding.md` and invite adversarial re-coding of specific episodes as a contribution path;

(b) Report contested episodes (Turkey recent, any marginal cases) explicitly with the coding reasoning;

(c) Avoid the stronger sufficiency claim and frame the result as "fiscal dominance appears to be a necessary precondition historically; the claim survives because no clear counter-example has been identified and the coding rule is made available for adversarial challenge."
