# Steelman — Housing Supply Flexibility and Real Wage Growth

## Strongest version of the claim

In metropolitan areas and countries with elastic housing supply, real disposable wages grow faster because housing-cost inflation is contained, preserving the purchasing power of nominal wages. In supply-constrained jurisdictions, rising rents and house prices erode wage gains even when productivity and headline GDP grow at similar rates. The mechanism is not about aggregate growth but about the distribution of land rents: flexible supply prevents incumbent landowners from capturing productivity gains through scarcity rents.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Metro-area panel** combining Saiz-style housing-supply elasticity, median rent growth, and median wage growth, showing that wage purchasing power (wage deflated by rent/CPI) is higher in elastic-supply cities.
2. **Cross-country evidence** linking OECD PMR construction-barrier scores to real-wage trends after controlling for GDP growth.
3. **Natural experiments**: upzoning episodes (Auckland, Tokyo), housing-boom vs housing-bust comparisons, or interstate migration flows within federal systems (US, Germany, Australia).
4. **Robustness checks**: the result should hold when controlling for endogenous sorting, agglomeration economies, and industrial composition.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Endogenous sorting**. High-productivity workers and firms sort into elastic-supply cities, so the wage correlation reflects selection rather than supply causation.
2. **Elastic supply may mean undesirable locations**. Cheap housing may signal weak amenities, poor public services, or exposure to environmental risk, not a welfare gain.
3. **Agglomeration rents may dominate**. Restrictive zoning in superstar cities may generate productivity spillovers that outweigh housing-cost effects at the national level.
4. **Infrastructure constraints**. Rapid housing expansion without transit or utilities may lower quality of life, offsetting wage gains.
5. **Data limitations outside the US**. Saiz elasticities are US-only; cross-country proxies (PMR, Demographia) are noisy and may mismeasure true supply responsiveness.

## Boundary conditions

- Best tested in **federal or decentralised systems** where metro-area zoning varies within a common national institutional framework.
- Applies to **median and below-median earners** most strongly; top earners are less housing-cost-constrained.
- Less relevant in countries with extensive social housing or rent control that decouple market rents from wages.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to urban-economics agglomeration literature, housing-policy affordability claims, and the broader market-regulation distribution debate. Tensions with claims that restrictive planning preserves environmental and neighbourhood amenities worth the wage cost.
