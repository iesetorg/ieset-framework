# Steelman — School-voucher RCT meta-analysis

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is that the post-2015 high-quality voucher RCTs have produced systematically smaller and often negative effects than the earlier Friedmanite literature claims. The Louisiana Scholarship Program (Abdulkadiroglu-Pathak-Walters 2018) found large negative test-score effects in years 1 and 2 of voucher exposure, the Indiana Choice Scholarship Program (Waddington-Berends 2018) showed null-to-negative reading effects, and the DC Opportunity Scholarship Program follow-up (Dynarski et al. 2018) showed negative maths effects in year 2. The pre-2010 positive findings (Milwaukee, NYC, Charlotte) were drawn from heavily-supplemented programmes operating in narrow geographic contexts; when voucher programmes scale to state-wide eligibility and use programmes with weaker private-school participation regulations, the marginal private school the voucher reaches is worse than the public alternative, and the average effect flips. The Chilean post-1980 case is the textbook scaling test: 40 years of universal voucher financing, and the macro evidence (Hsieh-Urquiola, Bellei-Vanni) shows sorting and stratification without a clear mean test-score gain over comparable systems. This is consistent with a Tiebout-cum-cream-skimming reading: vouchers redistribute between students rather than expand the test-score frontier. From a Hirschman exit-voice perspective the voucher mechanism degrades public-school political coalitions and reduces investment in the public sector for the students who do not exit, so a partial-equilibrium meta-analysis that ignores the spillover on non-voucher students misses the policy-relevant total effect. The hypothesis is also vulnerable to a publication-bias problem that the proposed PET-PEESE correction may not fully address: the most influential pro-voucher findings (Hoxby's charter results, the early Milwaukee studies) come from authors with disclosed advocacy positions, and the meta-corpus is unbalanced. The pre-registered moderator analysis on counterfactual public-school quality is partial protection, but it does not protect against the most common voucher-failure pattern (programme reaches a private school that is no better than the public alternative). The cleanest reading of the post-2015 evidence is that voucher effects are heterogeneous and depend on the regulation of the supply side of private schools, which is not how Friedman framed the proposition. A win on this hypothesis at the +0.05 SD threshold with publication-bias correction would be informative; a win that is sensitive to publication-bias correction should be discounted.
