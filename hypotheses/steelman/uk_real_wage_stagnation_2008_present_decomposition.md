# Steelman — UK real wage stagnation 2008-present decomposition

The strongest opposing argument: the framework's diagnostic (housing + productivity + sectoral + migration + measurement explain the wage gap) is incomplete in ways that matter.

## The strongest rent-extraction reading

Post-2008 UK corporate profit share of GDP rose while wage share fell and did not recover even as employment reached record highs by 2019. This is the pattern that motivated Karabarbounis-Neiman and later the UK productivity puzzle literature: labour share decline in excess of what capital-intensity, globalisation, and measurement explain points to either concentration-mediated markup expansion or bargaining-power collapse.

On this reading, "housing costs", "productivity", and "migration" are symptoms of the same underlying shift, not independent explanatory channels:

- **Housing costs are a concentration story**, not a supply-constraint story. The UK planning system is restrictive, but so are the Netherlands', Germany's, and France's — yet real-wage stagnation is UK-specific. The distinguishing feature is the hyper-concentrated housebuilding oligopoly (top 10 firms build 40%+ of new UK supply, vs. < 15% in continental Europe) enabled by land-banking practices that planning reform alone cannot address.

- **Productivity collapse has a markup-competition explanation**. UK services productivity diverged from continental Europe after 2012 as competition policy weakened. The CMA's post-Brexit retreat from merger enforcement is a reasonable contributing cause that the framework's "productivity" channel obscures.

- **Labour supply expansion stopped being wage-compressing when net migration fell 60% post-Brexit (2016-2019)**, yet real wages did not recover. That falsifies the migration-wage-compression channel the framework leans on.

## Measurement

CPIH was adopted precisely to address the CPI housing-exclusion critique. By the CPIH deflator (the official UK real-wage series since 2017), median real wages fell ~3% between 2008 and 2023. The framework's measurement caveat cuts both ways — it equally supports the rent-extraction reading.

## What would update the framework's prior

If post-promotion econometrics show the listed channels jointly explain < 60% of the UK-to-counterfactual trend gap, the honest conclusion is that market mechanisms did not allocate post-2008 UK gains broadly, and the framework should update its institutional-failure diagnostic to include a rent-extraction / concentration overlay — specifically for UK housebuilding and post-Brexit competition enforcement.

This is a falsifiable prediction, not a framing move. The decomposition residual is the binding test.

## Reading the result honestly

If the residual is < 40%, the framework's market-respecting diagnostic is vindicated: UK stagnation is institutional-failure-specific, fixable by housing supply liberalisation + competition policy revival + productivity-enhancing regulatory reform. If ≥ 40%, the rent-extraction reading earns standing and the policy prescription shifts toward distributional measures (forced-saving architectures, profit-share participation, possibly concentrated-sector regulation).
