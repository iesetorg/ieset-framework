# Steelman — Startup density vs industrial policy at the frontier

## Strongest version of the claim

Among high-income economies, startup density (high-growth firm formation per capita) is a more reliable predictor of frontier prosperity — real GDP per capita growth and labour-productivity growth — than manufacturing-specific industrial-policy spending. The mechanism is that startups are disproportionately responsible for radical innovation, job creation, and productivity spillovers, whereas industrial policy often protects incumbents, distorts competition, and locks in obsolete technologies. The claim is a horse-race: startup density should outperform industrial policy in head-to-head regressions.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Horse-race dominance:** In a panel-FE regression with both startup density and industrial-policy spending, the startup-density coefficient has a larger absolute t-statistic and remains significant when industrial policy is included.
2. **Quartile magnitude:** Top-quartile startup-density countries show at least 0.3 pp higher annual productivity growth than bottom-quartile countries.
3. **Ex-US robustness:** Result holds when the United States is excluded, indicating it is not driven by Silicon Valley alone.
4. **Channel corroboration:** Startup density is positively associated with venture-capital depth, patent quality, and high-tech employment share.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Endogeneity:** Prosperous, high-human-capital countries generate both startup density and productivity growth. The correlation may reflect underlying endowments rather than a causal startup channel.
2. **Industrial policy success stories:** Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore combined significant industrial policy with rapid catch-up and frontier innovation. Israel has high startup density *and* substantial public R&D and military-industrial spillovers.
3. **Sectoral mismatch:** Industrial policy targets manufacturing, while startup density is concentrated in software and services. Comparing their growth effects is comparing apples to oranges if manufacturing productivity is measured differently.
4. **Time horizons:** Industrial policy may have longer gestation lags than startup formation. A 2000-2020 panel may miss multi-decade industrial-policy returns (e.g. Airbus, semiconductor fabs).

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for service-oriented, high-income economies with developed financial markets (US, UK, Nordics, Israel). It is weaker for: (i) economies with dominant manufacturing sectors where scale and capital intensity matter more than startup agility (Germany, Japan); (ii) catch-up economies where technology transfer via industrial policy may be the binding constraint; (iii) periods of financial crisis when VC markets freeze and startup density collapses temporarily without affecting long-run productivity.
