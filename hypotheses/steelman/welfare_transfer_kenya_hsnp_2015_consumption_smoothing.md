# Steelman: Kenya HSNP Shock-Responsive Consumption Smoothing

The strongest counter is that consumption-smoothing measured at the household level may overstate the programme effect because of intra-household redistribution and informal-insurance crowd-out. Jensen-Wantchekon (2004) and subsequent literature show that introducing formal transfers can crowd out informal risk-sharing networks among extended kin, partially offsetting the headline transfer effect. Drought severity in northern Kenya is also spatially correlated within ASAL counties, so the eligibility-cliff comparator design is contaminated by spillover effects through livestock-market prices and pastoralist mobility. The 2017 and 2022 shock-responsive triggers were activated late relative to the underlying drought severity (NDMA monitoring lag of 2-3 months), eroding the timing benefit that motivates the design. Carter-Janzen (2018) found that the consumption-smoothing benefit of HSNP is stronger for sedentary recipients than mobile pastoralists, raising questions about whether the headline ATT generalises. A defensible reading: HSNP's design is innovative and the household-survey effects are real but the magnitude is closer to 8-10pp consumption smoothing rather than 15pp once spillover and informal-insurance offsets are accounted for.
