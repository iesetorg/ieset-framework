# Steelman — Eurozone 1999 trade effect

## The strongest opposing argument

1. **Rose (2000) estimates were anomalous.** The original 3-5x
   effect was driven by small-country fixed-currency unions
   (CFA franc zone, ECCU) and did not generalise to the
   eurozone. Subsequent work (Baldwin 2006; Berger-Nitsch 2008)
   estimated 5-15% effects at most, and some estimates are
   indistinguishable from zero once gravity-model fixed effects
   are properly specified. The trade-creation effect of
   currency unions is much smaller than the original literature
   suggested.

2. **Trade was already converging within the EU.** The single
   market (1993) and prior ERM exchange-rate stability had been
   driving intra-EU trade integration for years before 1999. The
   1999 currency conversion may have added little incremental
   integration. Pre-trends are a serious identification concern.

3. **GBR/DNK/SWE are imperfect controls.** All three opted out
   politically rather than failed to qualify; they are
   institutionally similar but selected on unobservables (UK
   financial-sector status, Danish and Swedish political
   eurosceptic preferences) that may correlate with trade
   trajectories.

## What the framework still captures

A conservative magnitude band ([5, 25] pp) operationalises the
post-Rose consensus. The PARTIAL verdict path (positive but
exceeds 30 pp) flags that something other than the currency
union is being captured. The pre-GFC end-date is the standard
convention for isolating the trade-creation effect from the
sovereign-debt-crisis dynamics that followed.
