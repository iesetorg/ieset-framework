# Steelman — Bukele fiscal trajectory tax cuts IMF 2019-2024

**The strongest argument against the causal claim:**

The hypothesis is that Bukele-era reforms (bond buyback + 2023 tax reform + IMF-signalled consolidation) caused a genuine and peer-outperforming fiscal improvement in El Salvador, with spending compression and debt-service compression as the dominant channels. The opposing view is that the measured fiscal improvement is largely cyclical and externally driven, not reform-driven, and that the reform content is less coherent than the coalition narrative claims.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage:

1. **Remittance growth is the dominant cyclical driver.** SLV remittance inflows grew from $5.9bn (2019) to $8.2bn (2023) — an $2.3bn increase equal to roughly 7% of GDP over the period. This is a direct VAT / consumption-tax base expansion that is not caused by Salvadoran policy but by US labour-market tightness post-COVID and the Salvadoran-diaspora population's employment recovery. The revenue-channel portion of the primary-balance improvement is substantially attributable to this external factor, not to tax-reform design. A decomposition that does not separate remittance-driven VAT revenue from structural-base revenue will overstate the reform effect.

2. **The 2023 tax reform is more redistribution-inside-the-base than net rate cuts.** Careful reading of the 2023 package shows the headline rate reductions for corporations and middle-bracket personal income tax were offset by base-broadening via digital tax administration, e-invoicing (facturación electrónica), and improved enforcement. Net revenue effect is ambiguous. A claim that "Laffer-style cut produced a revenue increase" requires attribution to the rate cut specifically, not to the enforcement improvement — which is a separate policy mechanism and one that does not require the lower-rate frame.

3. **The bond buyback was financed by new debt, not by primary surplus.** The Sep 2022 and Apr 2023 liability-management operations retired ~$650m of external debt at discount, but the financing came from CABEI multilateral loans and domestic T-bill issuance. The net effect on total public debt was modest; the main benefit was reducing the near-term maturity wall to avoid forced default, and compressing spreads. This is a refinancing operation, not a fiscal consolidation. Classifying the buyback as a "fiscal-tightening" reform overstates what it did.

4. **The IMF EFF conditionality reveals the IMF's own skepticism.** The Dec 2024 $1.4bn EFF required: Bitcoin-legal-tender removal, public-sector BTC purchase constraints, primary-balance targets, debt-ceiling rules, and governance reforms (including audits of Bitcoin Office holdings). If the pre-EFF fiscal trajectory had been robustly improving, the conditionality would not need to be this granular. The EFF exists precisely because the IMF concluded the self-driven fiscal framework was insufficient to restore debt-sustainability without external anchoring. The "Bukele delivered fiscal consolidation" narrative is therefore partly a forward claim about what the EFF will enable, not an observation about what the pre-2024 trajectory delivered.

5. **Debt-to-GDP remains elevated and sensitive to rate shocks.** SLV public debt-to-GDP peaked ~85% in 2020 and declined to ~75% by end-2024 — still above the 70% Latin American median and among the highest in Central America. Debt-service as a share of revenue remains ~20%. The structural position is fragile: a US-policy-rate-driven spread widening or a commodity-cycle reversal could reopen the debt-dynamics problem quickly. A "fiscal improvement" claim must be conditional on continued external tailwinds, not a permanent structural shift.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Present a decomposition of revenue growth into remittance-driven VAT, digital-tax-administration enforcement, and statutory-rate-driven revenue — with the rate-driven component isolated. If the rate-driven component is small, the "Laffer-style reform" interpretation is not supported.

(b) Report the bond-buyback effect on total public debt separately from its effect on near-term debt service, so the refinancing-versus-consolidation distinction is clear.

(c) Present the primary-balance peer-gap (SLV minus Central American peer average) net of remittance-growth differential, to isolate the reform-driven component.

(d) Report the SLV sovereign-spread (EMBI+) trajectory relative to peers. A compressing spread is consistent with either reform effect or external demand for Central American EM debt; both need to be controlled for.

(e) Accept that the causal attribution of fiscal improvement to reform content is likely partial, and report the result as a conditional "reform content contributed X percentage points of the Y percentage point peer-gap, with the remainder attributable to cyclical factors." A clean null — reform content contributed less than half the peer-gap — would materially update priors against the Bukele fiscal-reform narrative.
