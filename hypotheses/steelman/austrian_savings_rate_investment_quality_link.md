# Steelman — Savings rate vs credit-financed investment, TFP differential

## The strongest opposing argument

The "savings vs credit" distinction is a category error. In a globally-integrated capital market with fungible finance, the source of investment funding has no first-order causal effect on the productivity of the capital stock. The high-savings East Asian / German / Swiss outperformance is real but is overwhelmingly a story about institutional quality, demographic timing, and export-led industrialisation — not about the inter-temporal preference content of who provided the financing.

**Key mainstream / heterodox objections:**

1. **Capital is fungible at the margin.** Modigliani-Miller-style reasoning, plus open-capital-account financial integration, implies that whether a given factory is financed by Germany household savings or by US bank credit makes no difference to the factory's productivity. The "good vs bad investment" categorisation conflates financing with the underlying real allocation decision.

2. **East Asia's success was industrial policy, not savings purity.** Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and Japan combined high savings with aggressive export discipline (Amsden, Wade, Chang), targeted credit allocation (POSCO, Samsung), and explicit currency-management regimes. Attributing their TFP performance to "genuine savings" cherry-picks one feature of a much richer policy bundle. Germany's savings rate also coexists with codetermination, vocational training (dual system), and Mittelstand-oriented industrial structure — none of which are Hayekian.

3. **The Anglo "credit-financed" comparison is selection bias.** The US, UK, and Australia — the credit-driven economies — also have the world's deepest equity markets, the most globally-integrated firms, and substantial inward FDI. They generate enormous TFP growth in tradable services and tech that PWT measures imperfectly. Comparing TFP growth rates across these very different production structures conflates measurement issues with real differences.

4. **Current-account-deficit-financed investment can be hugely productive.** The textbook counter-example is post-1990 emerging Asia financed substantially by Japanese and European capital flows, or US 19th-century railroad expansion financed by British capital — both episodes of foreign-credit-financed investment delivering enormous long-run TFP gains. The Austrian framing predicts these should have been malinvestment booms followed by liquidation; in fact they were among the most successful capital-formation episodes in history.

## What the framework still captures

The cross-country correlation between persistently-high savings and persistently-high TFP growth is real and large. Whether that correlation reflects an Austrian "investment-quality" mechanism, an institutional-bundle effect, a demographic-window effect, or a culturally-mediated time-preference effect is genuinely under-identified. The framework's pre-registered ratio test is a deliberately weak version of the Austrian claim — strong enough to be falsifiable if savings and credit really are fungible, but not strong enough to vindicate the full Misesian inter-temporal-coordination thesis.
