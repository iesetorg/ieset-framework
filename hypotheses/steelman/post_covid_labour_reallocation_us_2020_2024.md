# Steelman — US post-COVID labour reallocation 2020-2024

## The strongest opposing argument

The "structural reallocation + WFH entrenchment" reading over-claims a permanent regime change from what may largely be a cyclical reopening sequence dressed up in sectoral clothing.

**Key opposing objections:**

1. **Cyclical demand, not structural reallocation.** Leisure/hospitality wage over-recovery is the predictable result of a binding labour-supply constraint during a fiscally-juiced reopening: enhanced UI, stimulus checks, and household excess savings tilted reservation wages up sharply for low-pay services where labour supply is most elastic. As that fiscal impulse faded 2023-2024, low-pay-services wage growth decelerated faster than the headline — consistent with a transitory Phillips-curve episode, not a durable reallocation.

2. **WFH may revert further.** SWAA and ATUS measurements of remote-work share peaked in 2021-2022 and have drifted down each subsequent year as RTO mandates accumulated (Amazon, JPMorgan, federal-government 2025). Calling 25%+ "structural" assumes the trajectory has stabilised, but the gradient through 2024 was still negative. A cleaner test would require a 2026-2028 endpoint.

3. **The information-sector "recovery" is a composition artefact.** Information-sector employment includes both software-publishing (which boomed) and traditional media/telecom (which shrank). Aggregate-level recovery masks divergent sub-sector trajectories; the WFH-entrenchment story holds for tech but not for the broader information aggregate.

4. **Brick-mortar retail decline pre-dates COVID.** General-merchandise and department-store employment was already in secular decline 2010-2019 from e-commerce displacement. Attributing 2024 shortfalls to COVID overstates the pandemic's marginal contribution; counterfactual extrapolation of the pre-2019 trend would explain most of the gap.

## What the framework still captures

The asymmetric-shock-then-reallocation pattern is genuine and historically unusual: a sharp services collapse with goods over-spending in 2020-2021, followed by services over-recovery and wage compression at the bottom. Even if WFH partially reverts and the wage-overrecovery is cyclical, the pandemic accelerated trends that would have taken a decade — the 2024 sectoral mix is materially different from the 2019 trend extrapolation. The hypothesis is best read as "COVID compressed a decade of reallocation into four years," with the multi-metric design naturally exposing how durable each leg of that reallocation proves to be.
