# Steelman: labour_market_flexibility_employment_growth_panel

## Counter-argument

Labour-market flexibility can reduce job security and worker bargaining power,
leading to lower wages, higher in-work poverty, and reduced consumption demand
that offsets any employment gains. Coordinated labour-market institutions
(Germany, Nordic countries) achieve high employment through sectoral bargaining
and active labour-market policies rather than through deregulation. The observed
positive correlation between flexibility and employment may be driven by the
USA and UK, which have unique institutional features beyond EPL strictness.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Demand-side offset**: Flexible labour markets reduce worker bargaining
   power and wage shares, depressing aggregate demand and potentially reducing
   net employment.

2. **Nordic/German model**: Denmark (flexicurity), Sweden, and Germany combine
   moderate-to-high EPL with high employment through ALMP and sectoral
   bargaining. This suggests that flexibility is neither necessary nor
   sufficient for high employment.

3. **Job-quality trade-off**: Flexible markets may raise employment quantity
   at the expense of job quality (part-time, temporary, low-wage work).
   Employment-to-population ratios do not capture this quality dimension.

4. ** Anglo-Saxon outlier bias**: The USA and UK are extreme outliers on
   flexibility and also have high employment. Excluding them often eliminates
   the cross-country correlation.

## What would flip the verdict

- A null or negative coefficient after excluding USA and UK.
- Evidence that flexibility gains are concentrated in part-time or temporary
  employment with no rise in full-time equivalent employment.
- Panel-FE estimates with country-specific time trends showing that within-
  country flexibility reforms do not raise employment.
