# Steelman — rent control reduces housing supply and quality

**The strongest argument for rent control:**

The framework's coding reflects mainstream economic consensus but several serious researchers have argued that modern rent regulation differs meaningfully from the textbook price-ceiling case, and that the aggregate costs may be smaller than the consensus claims while the distributional benefits may be larger.

Specific objections:

1. **"Modern" rent regulation with vacancy decontrol + means-testing + annual-adjustment + new-construction exemptions is not the textbook price ceiling.** Cases like post-2019 New York State rent stabilisation or Berlin 2020 Mietendeckel had specific design features (new-construction exemptions, phase-in, sunsets) that mitigate supply effects. Pooling all rent-control regimes obscures design-heterogeneity effects; some designs do less damage than others.

2. **Landlord market power matters.** In concentrated rental markets, market rent may be above the competitive-market rent. In these cases, a well-calibrated rent cap can reduce deadweight loss from monopoly pricing without reducing supply, similar to capping monopoly prices. The Diamond-McQuade-Qian finding of supply reduction occurred in San Francisco's highly competitive rental market; in less competitive markets, the supply-reducing effect may be smaller or absent.

3. **Distributional effects include genuine benefits.** Long-tenure tenants' consumer surplus is a real welfare gain; the framework's typical GDP-per-capita-outcome coding misses this. Whether the supply-side costs exceed these gains depends on valuation of stability versus mobility for specific populations. Research on displacement and involuntary mobility (Desmond 2016) suggests rent-controlled tenants value stability at levels higher than housing-mobility economics typically assumes.

4. **The Stockholm queue system is a design failure, not an inherent feature of rent control.** Swedish rent regulation is tied to a specific negotiated-rent bargaining regime that produces the queues. Attributing the Stockholm outcomes to "rent control" conflates the rent-regulation component with the separate allocation mechanism (queue-based rather than price-based).

5. **The 93% consensus figure is about PRICE CEILINGS below market, not about the class of policies labeled "rent control".** The IGM survey question was specific; generalising to "rent regulation" loses precision. Academic debate continues on design specifics even when the textbook case is settled.

**How this should shape the result card in v1.1 (when run):**

- Disaggregate by design regime. Berlin 2020-2021 (strict ceiling, short window, struck down) is distinct from San Francisco 1994 (vacancy decontrol) is distinct from Stockholm (continuous negotiated).
- Report supply AND quality AND distributional outcomes together.
- Acknowledge that specific design features can mitigate costs; the framework's directional coding should not imply uniform magnitude across regimes.
- The validation case for the framework is strong only if it correctly identifies the supply effect; distributional interpretation remains contested in the literature.
