# Steelman: New Zealand 1991 Benefit Cuts Child Poverty Effect

The strongest counter is that NZ's 1991 child-poverty rise is partly absorbed by the parallel labour-market shock — the 1990-1992 recession produced unemployment near 11% — so attributing the step-change in child poverty to the benefit-cut decision rather than the cyclical position of the economy requires shutting down a co-moving shock. The synthetic-control donor pool (AUS, IRL, GBR, CAN) had heterogeneous cycles in the same period, and pre-treatment fit on a small panel can produce spurious gaps. NZ's child-poverty time-series also has a measurement break in 1992 when the household-economic-survey was redesigned, raising questions about the comparability of pre/post observations. The 1990s benefit cuts were partly reversed under the 1999-2008 Labour government (Working for Families 2004), and intermediate-term (10-year) gaps may understate or overstate the structural effect depending on whether reversals are coded as control or treatment. A defensible reading: 1991 cuts contributed materially to child-poverty rise but the +6pp identified-as-policy effect is at the upper end of what the cycle-disentangled identification supports.
