# Steelman: Malaysia Developmentalist Plateau

## Strongest version of the claim
Malaysia's post-1990 growth slowdown reflects the drag from national-champion policies (Proton, GLC preference) and ethnic-allocation constraints that reduced competition and distorted resource allocation after early catch-up gains. The strongest version predicts that these policy variables carry negative and significant coefficients on TFP growth in a panel of middle-income comparators.

## Key evidence the claim would need
- Negative and significant coefficients on national-champion protection and ethnic-allocation indices in a panel regression.
- Malaysia's post-1990 TFP growth significantly below Korea's and Taiwan's after controlling for initial income and commodity dependence.
- Sector-level evidence that protected sectors (automotive, government-linked services) underperformed tradable sectors.

## Best counterarguments
- Malaysia's plateau is primarily a resource-dependence and middle-income-trap phenomenon, not a policy failure.
- Ethnic-allocation policies reduced social conflict and may have raised stability, with offsetting benefits.
- Proton created backward-linkage industries and skilled employment that are not captured in TFP measures.

## Boundary conditions
- The claim applies to middle-income countries that maintained protective policies after reaching upper-middle-income status.
- It does not deny the potential benefits of early industrial policy in low-income settings.

## Relation to existing hypotheses
- Complements `national_champions_long_run_productivity_drag` and `state_owned_enterprise_share_growth_plateau`.
- Tensions with `industrial_policy_high_governance_success` if Malaysia is treated as a moderate-governance case where industrial policy failed.
