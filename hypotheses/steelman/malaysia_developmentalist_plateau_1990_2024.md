# Steelman — Malaysia Developmentalist Plateau

## Strongest version of the claim

Malaysia's growth plateau after 1990 is causally linked to protected national-champion industrial policy (Proton, GLC preference, sectoral reservation) and ethnic-allocation constraints (Bumiputera equity requirements, preferential procurement) that reduced competition, distorted incentives, and entrenched rent-seeking. After successful catch-up in the 1970s and 1980s—driven by electronics FDI, commodity exports, and infrastructure investment—Malaysia's TFP growth and income convergence stalled because domestic policy protected inefficient incumbents and constrained market entry, while comparator economies (South Korea, Taiwan, Chile) liberalised more aggressively and sustained convergence.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Structural-break panel** showing Malaysia's post-1990 TFP growth significantly below Korea's and Taiwan's after controlling for initial income and commodity dependence.
2. **Firm-level data** showing lower productivity and higher survival of inefficient firms in GLC-linked and Bumiputera-priority sectors compared to export-oriented FDI sectors.
3. **Sectoral comparison** between protected sectors (automotive, steel, utilities) with stagnant productivity and open sectors (electronics, palm oil, tourism) with continued growth.
4. **Institutional indices** showing Malaysia's product-market regulation and competition policy enforcement lagging behind post-1990 reformers.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Commodity dependence, not policy**. Malaysia's growth plateau mirrors the resource-curse pattern; commodity price cycles and Dutch-disease effects explain more than industrial policy.
2. **Bumiputera policy reduced inequality**. Ethnic-preference policies were politically necessary for stability; without them, Malaysia might have faced conflict and worse growth.
3. **Small domestic market**. Malaysia's size limits scale economies, making national champions a second-best response to import competition from larger economies.
4. **Electronics FDI sustained growth**. The plateau narrative ignores continued FDI inflows and export growth; Malaysia's issue is TFP, not aggregate output collapse.
5. **Governance, not targeting**. Malaysia's GLC problems stem from political capture and corruption (1MDB), not industrial policy per se.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **manufacturing and services sectors with explicit Bumiputera or GLC preference**.
- Less defensible for **commodity and FDI-export sectors**, where policy distortions are weaker.
- The claim isolates the post-1990 period; it does not deny Malaysia's early catch-up success.
