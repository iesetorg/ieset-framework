# Steelman: Eastern Europe outmigration × wages

## Strongest case for the claim

Post-2004 EU enlargement opened mass labour mobility for new member states. Poland alone saw
~2 million emigrants 2004-2010. Standard labour-supply theory predicts upward wage pressure
at origin. Polish, Romanian, and Baltic real wages have indeed grown rapidly since
mid-2000s. The hypothesis is a panel-FE test of this in cumulative-stock terms, controlling
for productivity.

## Strongest case against

Productivity growth in CEE has been rapid (catch-up convergence to EU-15) and could fully
account for wage growth without any migration channel. Migration is also endogenous to
local economic conditions — emigrants leave when origin wages are low, biasing OLS toward
zero or negative.

## Key uncertainties

- Productivity-control absorption.
- Compositional effects (high-skill vs low-skill emigrants).

## What would change my mind

Productivity control fully absorbing the migrant-share coefficient would refute the
labour-supply-tightening channel and leave only the convergence story.
