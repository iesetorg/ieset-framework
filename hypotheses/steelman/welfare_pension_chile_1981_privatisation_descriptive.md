# Steelman: Chile 1981 Pension Privatisation

The strongest counter is that Chile's macro-savings rise is over-attributed to the AFP system. Corbo-Schmidt-Hebbel (2003) decomposed the 1981-2000 savings rise and found AFP contributed only 30-40% of the increase, with corporate-savings reform and macro-stabilisation explaining the remainder. The replacement-rate comparison is also unfair to AFP: the 1981 cohort transitioning into the system had insufficient contribution years to accrue full benefits, and once the 2030+ steady-state cohort begins drawing pensions, replacement rates are projected to rise materially. Coverage-gap-driven low replacement rates reflect informal-employment patterns in Chile's labour market, not the AFP design — a PAYG counterfactual would face the same coverage problem. The 2008 Pilar Solidario was a low-cost addition that brought elderly poverty down from 22% to roughly 5% by 2020, demonstrating that the AFP architecture is upgradeable rather than structurally broken. A defensible reading: AFP is neither the unmitigated success nor the documented failure of competing narratives — its macro-savings effect is real but smaller than the headline, and its adequacy gaps reflect labour-market structure as much as pension design.
