# Steelman — Contract enforcement and FDI productivity spillovers

## Strongest version of the claim

Stronger contract enforcement determines whether foreign direct investment (FDI) produces productivity spillovers to domestic firms or remains an enclave with no local diffusion. The mechanism is supplier relationships: multinationals source from and train local suppliers only when contracts are enforceable, preventing hold-up and ensuring quality compliance. Without enforcement, multinationals import inputs or use wholly-owned affiliates, cutting domestic firms out of learning opportunities. The claim is conditional: FDI is associated with domestic-firm TFP growth only above a contract-enforcement threshold.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive interaction:** Panel-FE shows FDI inflows × contract-enforcement interaction positively associated with domestic-firm TFP growth at p<0.05.
2. **Threshold effect:** FDI coefficient is not significantly positive on domestic TFP in below-median contract-enforcement countries.
3. **Legal-origin robustness:** Result holds when using legal-origin dummies (common law vs civil law) as an alternative contract-enforcement proxy.
4. **Sectoral heterogeneity:** Interaction is stronger in manufacturing (where supplier networks matter) than in extractive sectors (where enclave effects dominate regardless of enforcement).

## Best counterarguments

1. **Technology gap too large:** In very poor countries, domestic firms may lack absorptive capacity regardless of contract enforcement. The binding constraint is skills and technology, not legal institutions.
2. **Informal enforcement:** In many developing countries, relational contracting, ethnic networks, and reputation mechanisms substitute for formal contract enforcement. FDI spillovers may occur through these informal channels.
3. **FDI type matters:** Greenfield FDI in manufacturing produces spillovers; M&A FDI in services or extractives does not. The interaction may be driven by FDI composition rather than enforcement per se.
4. **Reverse causation:** Countries with stronger domestic supplier networks attract more manufacturing FDI and also develop better contract enforcement. The causal arrow may run from supplier capacity to enforcement, not the reverse.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for manufacturing FDI in middle-income countries with some existing industrial base (Mexico, Thailand, Malaysia, Poland). It weakens for: (i) extractive-sector FDI where enclave effects are structural; (ii) very poor countries with minimal manufacturing base; (iii) countries in regional value chains where enforcement is provided by lead-firm governance rather than domestic courts.
