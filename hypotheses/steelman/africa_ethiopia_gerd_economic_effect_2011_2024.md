# Steelman — Ethiopia GERD economic effect 2011-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **GERD is mostly a supply-side capacity investment that has yet to translate into delivered consumption.** Commissioning was scheduled for 2017 and slipped repeatedly to 2022+ for first units. As of 2024, only 4 of 13 turbines were operational. Counting the construction period as a "treatment" overstates the actual-energy-delivered story; the relevant treatment date is closer to 2022 with much shorter post-treatment window.

2. **Transmission bottlenecks dominate the supply story.** Ethiopia's grid lacks the high-voltage backbone to deliver GERD output to Addis Ababa, regional towns, and export interconnections. Until grid investment catches up, generation capacity is partially stranded. The hypothesis tests delivered consumption per capita — which depends as much on grid as on generation.

3. **The Tigray war 2020-2022 destroyed grid infrastructure in northern regions.** Substations and lines were physically damaged; this depresses Ethiopian electrification trajectory mechanically and contaminates the GERD effect with the war effect. The pre-registered specification flags but does not isolate this; the v1 result needs to report Tigray-region-excluded estimates.

4. **Electrification gains pre-date GERD.** The Universal Electric Access Programme launched 2011 alongside GERD groundbreaking; its grid-extension contribution is independent of GERD generation. Ethiopia went from ~17% access (2010) to ~50% (2020) before GERD came online. The post-2022 incremental effect is what the hypothesis must isolate, and that window is too short for a statistical test.

5. **Egypt-Sudan downstream-impact dispute is an externality the framework ignores.** GERD filling created Nile-basin diplomatic tensions affecting Egyptian and Sudanese water security. The Ethiopian welfare gain has counterpart-country welfare losses. A complete framework should at minimum acknowledge the bilateral externality.

6. **Manufacturing-share-of-GDP did NOT rise meaningfully.** Despite GERD's industrial-policy framing as enabling Ethiopian manufacturing, the manufacturing share of GDP in 2024 is approximately the 2011 level. The "industrial-policy-via-energy" narrative has not delivered on its second-order promise.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Distinguish the 2011-2022 (construction) and 2022-2024 (commissioning) windows.
- Report grid-coverage and transmission-investment as separate channels.
- Run the Tigray-region-excluded specification prominently.
- Disclose the downstream Egypt/Sudan externality even if not directly tested.
- Frame GERD as "infrastructure with delayed payoff" rather than "completed transformation".
