# Steelman — Market Reform Without State Capacity Fails

## Strongest version of the claim

Market-oriented reforms are not self-executing. Trade liberalisation, privatisation, and price decontrol require a minimum state capacity—tax collection, contract enforcement, regulatory oversight, and public-goods provision—to deliver sustained prosperity. In low-capacity environments, market reforms produce crony capitalism, asset stripping, tax evasion, and informality rather than competitive markets. The claim is a boundary condition on laissez-faire: state capacity and market reform are complements, not substitutes.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing that the interaction between market-reform intensity and state-capacity indices is positive and significant for GDP growth, TFP, and investment.
2. **Case comparisons**: high-capacity reformers (Estonia, Poland, Chile) versus low-capacity reformers (Russia 1990s, Albania, Bolivia) with divergent outcomes.
3. **Microdata** on firm entry and investment showing that reform raises formal-sector activity only where contract enforcement and tax administration function.
4. **Natural experiments**: within-country variation in state capacity (federal systems, decentralisation episodes) interacted with reform timing.

## Best counterarguments

1. **State capacity is endogenous**. Market reforms may build state capacity through revenue growth and formalisation; the interaction may reflect this reverse causation.
2. **Gradual reform builds capacity simultaneously**. Countries that liberalised gradually (China, Vietnam) built state capacity while reforming, contradicting the sequencing claim.
3. **Measurement of state capacity is contested**. WGI government effectiveness and tax ratios capture different dimensions; a composite index may obscure which capacity matters.
4. **Reform may succeed despite low capacity**. Some low-capacity countries (e.g., Georgia post-2003) showed rapid growth after radical liberalisation by leveraging simplicity (flat tax, easy business entry) rather than bureaucratic depth.
5. **Omitted institutional variables**. Democracy, colonial history, and geography may drive both state capacity and reform success, making the interaction spurious.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **large, complex economies** where market coordination requires extensive institutional scaffolding.
- Less defensible for **small open economies** where trade integration substitutes for domestic state capacity.
- Applies to **broad liberalisation packages**, not targeted reforms (e.g., simplifying business registration) that may work at low capacity.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with institutional-quality and state-capacity literatures. Tensions with naive laissez-faire claims and with hypotheses that treat institutions as purely endogenous to economic openness.
