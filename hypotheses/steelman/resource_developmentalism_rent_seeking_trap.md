# Steelman: Resource Developmentalism Rent-Seeking Trap

## Strongest version of the claim
Resource-rich states that channel commodity rents into state-led industrialisation and diversification strategies ("resource developmentalism") initially achieve visible public-investment gains—infrastructure, heavy industry, urbanisation—but subsequently fall into a rent-seeking trap. State control of resource rents creates concentrated patronage networks, distorts exchange rates, crowds out private non-resource tradables, and reduces long-run diversification. Over 30-year windows, these states underperform market-open resource peers (e.g., Botswana, Chile post-1985) that allowed competitive private-sector development and sovereign-wealth saving rather than state-directed spending.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Cross-country panel comparing export diversification and TFP in resource developmentalist versus market-open resource economies.
2. Fiscal data showing higher non-resource tax effort and lower rent dependence in market-open peers.
3. Firm-level evidence of political connections and rent extraction in state-led resource economies.
4. Natural experiments of resource-boom windfalls showing differential outcomes based on state-versus-market allocation.

## Best counterarguments
- Norway's state-led resource management (Statoil, Government Pension Fund Global) demonstrates that resource developmentalism can succeed with strong institutions and democratic accountability.
- Market-open resource peers may simply be lucky (geology, colonial institutions, US proximity) rather than policy-superior.
- 30-year windows miss very-long-run structural transformation; state-led industrialisation may require 40-50 years to mature.
- Export diversification is not always welfare-enhancing; comparative advantage in resources may be optimal.
- The comparison conflates resource developmentalism with macro mismanagement (overvaluation, fiscal profligacy); well-managed developmentalist states may perform differently.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest in resource-dependent middle-income states with weak institutions. Weak in high-institutional-quality states (Norway, Canada, Australia) where resource rents are managed transparently.
