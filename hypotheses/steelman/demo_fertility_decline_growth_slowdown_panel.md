# Steelman: Fertility decline → growth slowdown (panel post-1980)

## Strongest case for the claim

The Bloom-Williamson demographic-dividend literature documents that East-Asian growth miracles
loaded on a one-time bulge in working-age share following fertility transition. Symmetrically,
when fertility falls below replacement and the bulge shifts into the dependent-old bracket,
per-capita growth should decay. Japan post-1995 and Italy post-2000 are presumptive case
studies; Korea post-2015 is the live test. The 25-year lag isolates the cohort-arrival
mechanism from short-run business-cycle effects.

## Strongest case against

(1) Sub-replacement fertility correlates with pre-existing income levels, so apparent post-
fertility-transition growth slowdown is just convergence dynamics. (2) Capital deepening and
TFP can offset working-age contraction (see Acemoglu-Restrepo on automation in ageing
economies). (3) Migration substitutes for domestic fertility for many advanced economies
(USA, AUS, CAN), severing the TFR-growth link entirely. (4) The 25-year lag invites
cherry-picking; the relationship should hold at multiple lag lengths or it is fragile.

## Key uncertainties

- Mediation share via working-age share vs. innovation/productivity channels.
- How much migration absorption substitutes for domestic fertility.
- Whether AI/automation breaks the historical demographic-growth link going forward.

## What would change my mind

A clean test on countries with closed migration (Japan, Korea, Italy) showing TFR-lagged
coefficient close to zero, OR a finding that migration-inclusive working-age share fully
absorbs the TFR coefficient, would refute the underlying demographic-dividend mechanism
operating through fertility specifically.
