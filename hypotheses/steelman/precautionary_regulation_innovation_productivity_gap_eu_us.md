# Steelman — The EU-US innovation gap is mostly non-regulatory

**The strongest arguments for EU precautionary regulation and against the productivity-gap chain:**

1. **Capital-market depth is the dominant EU-US gap driver.** The EU has no unified capital market, pension-fund allocation constraints, 27 insolvency regimes, and bank-dominated SME financing. These structural factors explain most of the VC-per-capita gap, the unicorn-count gap, and the scale-up gap. Attributing the gap to GDPR or REACH risks overreading what is mostly a capital-market story predating the regulations by decades.

2. **EU R&D intensity is not actually lower than the US in many sub-sectors.** EU-15 R&D intensity is ~2.1% of GDP vs ~3.5% in the US; but the German R&D intensity is ~3.1%, Sweden ~3.4%, Finland ~2.9%, comparable to or exceeding most US states outside California and Massachusetts. The "EU lags US" framing pools heterogeneity that would show some EU sub-regions matching the US frontier.

3. **US tech-firm concentration is an antitrust-era artefact.** The Chicago-school antitrust consensus (1980-2015) permitted FAANG-scale network-effect concentration. US "scale advantage" in digital is not a story about the US having lighter recent regulation; it is a story about the US getting there first under a permissive antitrust regime and locking in network effects. A hypothesis comparing post-2018 EU-US regulation on digital-sector scale runs against a pre-existing concentration gap.

4. **EMA-FDA lag is small and shrinking for novel drugs.** The EMA has adopted parallel scientific advice, PRIME (priority medicines), accelerated assessment, and rolling review; median time-to-first-approval gap between FDA and EMA for novel cancer drugs is ~3-6 months in 2020-2024, down from 12-18 months historically. The launch-lag second-order mechanism is weaker than the narrative suggests.

5. **REACH has delivered genuine environmental and health benefits.** Restriction and authorisation of substances of very high concern has reduced exposure to persistent, bioaccumulative, and toxic chemicals across the EU market and globally (by spillover via value-chain compliance). A hypothesis that measures only firm-exit and compliance cost without these benefits is one-sided welfare accounting.

6. **GDPR has been widely copied as a global standard.** Brazil's LGPD, California CCPA, India's DPDP, Japan's APPI revisions, and UK GDPR all broadly track the EU model. If the EU regulation were simply anti-innovation, the global copying pattern is puzzling; a more plausible interpretation is that the regulation hits closer to the welfare-optimal frontier than US under-regulation on privacy.

7. **AI Act impact is unobserved and may be moderate.** The AI Act's high-risk obligations bind progressively through 2025-2027; almost all 2024 data precedes the bite. Strong claims about its productivity effect are premature and risk being based on industry lobbying rather than measurement.

8. **Bankruptcy-regime and immigration-policy gaps matter enormously.** H-1B access to global talent, Chapter 11 reorganisation, and acceptance of serial-entrepreneurship failure are US institutional features that make scale-up easier. These are not regulatory in the GDPR/REACH sense but dominate firm-formation outcomes; the hypothesis must control for them, and when it does, the "regulatory residual" is small.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report the EU-US coefficient BEFORE and AFTER the capital-market-depth + bankruptcy + immigration control set. The residual is the real regulatory effect.
- Report EU intra-country heterogeneity prominently; Ireland, Sweden, Estonia, Netherlands performance calibrates the "EU as a monolith" framing.
- Acknowledge privacy + environmental benefits as out-of-frame welfare; frame the result as a narrow productivity/scale measurement.
- Report the EMA launch-lag trend over time; the gap has narrowed and is shrinking.
- Be cautious about AI Act attribution; the data is ex-ante and the effect is speculative.
- Do not headline findings without the citizen-protection first-order delivery alongside the productivity-cost measurement.
