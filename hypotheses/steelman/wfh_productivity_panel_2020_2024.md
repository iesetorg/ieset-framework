# Steelman — WFH productivity panel 2020-2024

## The strongest opposing argument

Both the "WFH boosts productivity" and "WFH hurts productivity" camps have evidence the wash hypothesis ignores.

**Strong-boost case (Bloom-Barrero-Davis):**
- The Stanford WFH Research project's CTrip natural experiment and follow-up SWAA panel show 4-13% productivity gains from hybrid work, mediated by reduced commuting time, lower attrition, and self-selection of workers into roles where they perform best. Aggregate cross-sector measures dilute these within-firm gains because measured sector productivity captures composition shifts (high-productivity workers WFH-shifting up) rather than pure mechanism.
- Revealed preference: firms voluntarily continue WFH where they can; if it were a strong negative, return-to-office would be universal rather than partial.

**Strong-drag case (Mortensen, Mas-Pallais, Emanuel-Harrington):**
- Microdata RCTs in call-centre work show 4-8% individual-level productivity declines for fully-remote workers, with worse mentoring outcomes for junior staff. Aggregate effects underestimate this because senior tenured workers (who self-select into WFH) carry the average upward while the long-run pipeline degrades.
- Information-spillover and innovation losses (patent-citation distance, Brucks-Levav videoconferencing creativity experiments) won't show up in 2020-2024 productivity but will compound through 2030.

**Why the wash claim is structurally fragile:**

1. **Measurement.** Service-sector productivity is real-value-added-per-hour where prices are imputed from input costs — a near-tautology. Any productivity coefficient on WFH is partially mechanical.
2. **Selection.** Sectors with high WFH feasibility (info, finance, professional services) had high pre-COVID productivity growth; the coefficient on WFH-share absorbs that pre-trend.
3. **Endpoint dependence.** 2020-2021 measured productivity surged in the US (compositional — low-pay workers exited); 2022-2024 it reverted. The "wash" finding may flip with a 2026 endpoint.

## What the framework still captures

The wash result, if it holds with the pre-COVID trend control, is a meaningful negative finding: it would refute strong claims in both directions and frame WFH as primarily a worker-welfare gain (commuting time, flexibility) rather than a measured-productivity story. Even an imprecise null on a $20T sectoral panel is a useful prior update against the most-confident takes from either camp.
