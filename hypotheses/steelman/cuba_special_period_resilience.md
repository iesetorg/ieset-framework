# Steelman — Cuba Special Period resilience, 1991-2000

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The hypothesis frames Cuba's 1991-2000 trajectory as evidence that "socialist
planning preserved health and education outputs under severe external shock"
better than market-economy peers. The fair-reader objection is that this
framing conflates four very different things:

1. **The Special Period was the shock, not the test of the policy.** Cuban
   real GDP per capita fell ~35% between 1989 and 1993 by Cuban official
   estimates. Daily caloric intake fell from ~2,900 kcal/day to ~1,800
   kcal/day at trough. An adult male population of ~3.5m lost on average
   5-9kg of body weight 1990-1995. These are catastrophic shock outcomes,
   not resilience outcomes. Any "socialist planning preserved outputs"
   reading must explain why life-expectancy and infant-mortality continued
   to improve through the very years when basic nutrition collapsed — and
   the most parsimonious explanation is measurement: the public-health
   metrics WDI publishes are produced by the state experiencing the
   shock, with strong incentives to report favourable trajectories. WHO
   independent contemporaneous cross-checks are sparse for the window.

2. **Cuba started the period structurally ahead on health.** Cuba's 1991
   life expectancy (73.8 years) was already higher than every Caribbean
   peer in the comparator pool except a near-tie with Jamaica (71.8).
   Its 1991 IMR (10.3/1000) was already the lowest in the pool by a
   factor of 2-10. A test that scores Cuba "supported" because the
   1991-2000 *change* is favourable misses that mature health systems
   show smaller absolute changes — Cuba had less room to improve LE
   from 73.8y than Haiti had from 53.5y. The IMR percentage-reduction
   test partly corrects for this, but a fair reader would still want
   to see whether the post-Soviet pool's trajectory (UKR, MDA, ARM, GEO
   all suffered similar transition shocks) is more comparable than the
   Caribbean pool, and whether Cuba beats post-Soviet specifically.

3. **The post-Soviet comparators are the cleaner natural experiment.**
   UKR, MDA, ARM, GEO all faced the same 1991 demand shock as Cuba
   (loss of Soviet trade) and shifted away from socialist planning, not
   toward it. Their LE *fell* (UKR 70.3 → 67.8, MDA 67.9 → 67.5) while
   Cuba's *rose* (73.8 → 75.9). On a "did the political-economic system
   matter under shock?" reading this looks like Cuba won. But the post-
   Soviet states experienced (i) violent state collapse, (ii) hyper-
   inflation, (iii) civil wars (Georgia/Armenia/Moldova), and (iv)
   loss of basic public-health surveillance — none of which Cuba faced.
   Attributing the LE gap to "socialism vs market reform" rather than
   "intact state capacity vs collapsed state capacity" is a weak
   inference from the data.

4. **The Caribbean comparators were not in shock at all.** JAM, DOM, HTI,
   NIC during 1991-2000 were on their own development trajectories
   driven by tourism, remittances, and external lending — they were not
   experiencing a Cuban-magnitude external shock. Comparing Cuba's
   1991-2000 health trajectory to theirs is comparing a shocked country
   to non-shocked countries, which is the wrong comparison for a
   hypothesis about resilience to shock. The right comparison would be
   each country's deviation from its own pre-shock trend, and the
   Caribbean pool has no shock to deviate from.

5. **The "school enrolment" leg was the actual stress on the system.**
   The hypothesis as written demotes school enrolment to "informative,
   not gating" because of WDI coverage gaps. But the position-claim
   being tested specifically asserts education was preserved. Cuban
   primary-school enrolment in WDI shows a small drop 1991-1995 (101 →
   100) then a recovery to 104 by 2000 — not the kind of collapse the
   shock magnitude would predict, again raising the measurement
   question. The Special Period saw real declines in school-supply
   inputs (textbooks, transport, school-meal programmes documented in
   the secondary literature) that the gross-enrolment-ratio metric is
   not designed to capture. A fair reader would weigh "GER held flat"
   against "qualitative inputs collapsed" and conclude the metric is
   not informative either way.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v2 replication's result card should:

(a) Report Cuba's 1991 baseline alongside the 1991-2000 change so readers
can see Cuba started near the top of the comparator pool on health.

(b) Report the Caribbean pool and the post-Soviet pool separately as well
as combined. Cuba beating the post-Soviet pool is doing different
inferential work from Cuba beating Jamaica.

(c) Flag the measurement caveat: Cuban Special Period public-health
statistics are produced by a state with strong incentives to report
favourable trajectories, with limited contemporaneous external
verification.

(d) The verdict text should distinguish between "Cuba's recorded health
metrics improved through a severe demand shock" (which the WDI data
report) and "socialist planning preserved health outputs better than
market alternatives would have" (which would require a counterfactual
the data cannot supply, and which is contaminated by the post-Soviet
comparators' state-capacity collapse confound).
