# Steelman — Market Openness Without Adjustment Institutions Risks Backlash

## Strongest version of the claim

Trade and capital-account liberalisation generate aggregate efficiency gains but also create concentrated distributional losers—workers in import-competing sectors, regions dependent on declining industries, and low-skill labour in high-wage countries. Without complementary adjustment institutions (active labour-market policy, wage insurance, regional transfers, retraining), these losses fuel political backlash that manifests as tariff increases, capital controls, populist platforms, and policy reversal. The claim is that openness is not self-sustaining; it requires an embedded liberalism bargain to remain politically viable.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing that trade openness raises the probability of policy reversal or populist-platform vote share, but only in countries with low ALMP spending and weak unemployment insurance.
2. **Regional evidence** within countries (US Rust Belt, UK deindustrialised regions, French factory towns) showing that exposure to import competition predicts voting for protectionist and populist parties.
3. **Event studies** of trade shocks (China shock, NAFTA, EU enlargement) and subsequent policy or electoral responses.
4. **Historical pattern**: the post-war embedded liberalism order combined openness with welfare states; its erosion after 1980 coincided with rising protectionist sentiment.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Identity politics, not economics, drives backlash**. Voting for populists is better predicted by cultural conservatism, immigration attitudes, and racial resentment than by economic exposure to trade.
2. **Compensation may not work**. Even generous adjustment programmes (Trade Adjustment Assistance in the US) have limited take-up and political impact; the backlash may be ideological rather than material.
3. **Measurement of adjustment institutions is crude**. ALMP spending captures inputs, not effectiveness; some low-spending countries (e.g., Denmark's flexicurity) achieve high adjustment through design rather than budget.
4. **Reverse causation**. Populist governments may cut adjustment institutions and then blame openness, making the correlation spurious.
5. **Openness may reduce inequality globally**. Even if openness raises within-country inequality, it reduces between-country inequality; the political-economy framing is parochial.

## Boundary conditions

- Most relevant for **democratic middle- and high-income countries** where policy reversal is electorally mediated.
- Less relevant for **autocracies** where openness can be imposed regardless of distributional consequences.
- Applies to **manufacturing trade shocks** more than services or capital-account shocks.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to trade-liberalisation, welfare-state, and political-economy literatures. Tensions with claims that openness is unconditionally welfare-improving and with hypotheses that attribute backlash solely to cultural factors.
