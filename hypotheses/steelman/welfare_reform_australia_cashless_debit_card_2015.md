# Steelman: Australia Cashless Debit Card

The strongest counter is that the CDC operated alongside parallel community-services interventions (alcohol-management plans, family-support programmes) so the no-incremental-effect finding cannot be cleanly attributed to the card mechanism per se — it may be that conditionality plus services produced effects but conditionality alone (counterfactual) would have been worse. Some evaluations (Mavromaras 2017, Final Evaluation 2019) found small reductions in self-reported gambling and alcohol consumption among CDC participants, even if hospitalisation and DV-report aggregate measures did not move. The synthetic-control donor pool of comparable remote regions is also small (4 trial sites versus 6-8 reasonable donors), giving low statistical power to detect modest effects. Indigenous-leadership groups that supported CDC introduction (Andrew Forrest, some Aboriginal community boards in trial regions) argued the cashless mechanism reduced humbugging — pressure on welfare recipients to share cash with extended family — which is hard to capture in administrative-data outcomes. A defensible reading: CDC's null finding on macro outcomes is consistent with a small effect on hidden-harm channels that don't surface in hospitalisation/police-report data; the strong "no effect" framing may be stronger than the evidence warrants.
