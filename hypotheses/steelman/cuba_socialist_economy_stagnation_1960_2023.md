# Steelman: Cuba Socialist Economy Stagnation 1960-2023

The most serious counter-argument targets the GDP measurement problem directly. Cuba has not participated in standard national accounts reporting for most of its post-revolutionary history, and the Maddison and OWID series for Cuba are reconstructions with acknowledged uncertainty intervals. Cuba's 2004-2008 GDP revisions — accepted by CEPAL — nearly doubled estimated output by reclassifying health and education services at higher values, a methodologically defensible choice for a non-market economy where these services are genuinely produced and consumed but underpriced in market terms. Under revised estimates, Cuba's 2023 GDP per capita may be meaningfully above its 1960 level, making the "zero net growth" claim an artefact of measurement convention rather than economic reality.

On the comparator countries: Dominican Republic, Costa Rica, and Panama benefited from qualitatively different structural endowments. The DR and Panama are US-dollar-integrated financial hubs (Panama City's banking sector, DR's tourism infrastructure built with US capital). Costa Rica benefited from a specific Intel manufacturing investment that is non-replicable. None faced a comprehensive trade and financial embargo from their largest trading partner for 60 years. CEPAL's 3-4% GDP embargo cost estimate is a lower bound based on direct trade diversion; indirect costs via technology exclusion, credit market exclusion, and FDI chilling are harder to quantify and may be substantially larger.

Cuba's social outcomes — life expectancy now exceeding the US average, infant mortality below the US average, near-universal secondary education — are not separable from the material economy. These outcomes require real resource allocation that GDP measures miss, and they represent genuine welfare achievements that a PPP GDP comparison obscures.
