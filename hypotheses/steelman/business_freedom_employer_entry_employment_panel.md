# Steelman: Business Freedom Employer Entry Employment Panel

The strongest objection is that business-freedom indices may mostly proxy income, state capacity, and investor-friendly ideology rather than the specific entry costs faced by real entrepreneurs. High registration counts can include shell companies, tax planning, or low-productivity necessity self-employment, while some highly regulated countries support productive small firms through credit, procurement, training, and export institutions.

Employment gains may also come from demand, education, or sector mix rather than regulatory freedom. If business freedom raises churn without raising survival, wages, or employer status, the entrepreneurship mechanism is weak. A clean refutation would show positive business-freedom scores but no increase in employer share, no increase in surviving firms, or gains concentrated in own-account work with lower earnings.
