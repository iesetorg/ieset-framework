# Steelman — Could the Fed have prevented the Great Recession by holding M2 growth at trend?

The strongest argument against this hypothesis is that the 2008 M2 slowdown was not a failure of Fed will but a structural consequence of bank-credit contraction that the Fed could not directly offset within the operating framework available in 2008. Broad money is dominated by deposits, which are created when banks lend; when bank lending contracts (because banks are repairing capital ratios after large mark-to-market losses, not because reserves are scarce) M2 contracts mechanically, regardless of central-bank reserve creation. The Fed expanded the monetary base by ~100% between September 2008 and March 2009 and M2 growth still stalled, because households and firms parked the new base in reserves rather than spending it (the velocity collapse documented in the linked velocity-break hypothesis). A counterfactual in which M2 grows at 6% from 2008Q3 onward is therefore not a counterfactual the Fed could actually have produced with the tools it had — it would have required the Treasury to inject deposits directly to households (helicopter drop), which neither institution had legal authority for. The Bernanke-Gertler-Gilchrist financial-accelerator framework and the Mian-Sufi household-debt-overhang work both predict that even a successful M2 stabilisation would have left the recession deep, because the binding constraint was household balance-sheet repair and broken-intermediation pricing, not aggregate liquidity. The hypothesis is also vulnerable to a methodological objection: the SVAR counterfactual fixes M2 growth at 6% while leaving the financial-stress shock (TED spread) at its realised value, which embeds an inconsistent counterfactual — in any world where the Fed successfully held M2 at 6%, the financial stress would have been different too, and there is no clean way to separate the M2 channel from the credit-stress channel. Even Hetzel's market-monetarist case for "avoidable" Great Recession concedes that the test requires an NGDP-targeting framework with helicopter-drop tools available, which is a much stronger institutional-reform claim than "the Fed should have done more". The cleanest reading of the data is that the Fed in 2008 was operating at a binding constraint imposed by its instrument set, not making a discretionary error of the 1929-1933 sort, and the Friedman-Schwartz analogy therefore stretches further than the evidence supports. Prior_confidence at 0.55 already reflects this; the falsification rule's requirement that the SVAR M2-shock impulse on GDP be statistically positive is the load-bearing element, and the reasonable expectation is that it will not be at the magnitudes the hypothesis requires.
