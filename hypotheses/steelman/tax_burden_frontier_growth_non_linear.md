# Steelman — At high-income levels, very high tax burdens predict weaker long-run productivity growth unless paired with unusually high state capacity and labor flexibility

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that tax burdens affect growth not through a simple linear "taxes reduce incentives" channel, but through a conditional channel: high taxes fund public goods and redistribution that can support growth when the state is competent and labour markets are flexible enough to absorb shocks, but become pure deadweight loss when the state is corrupt or rigid. In high-capacity, flexible-labour-market settings (Nordics, Netherlands), high taxes are offset by efficient public-goods provision, active labour-market policies, and wage flexibility. In low-capacity, rigid settings (Southern Europe, Latin America), the same tax burden produces informality, capital flight, and reduced work effort.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Triple-interaction pattern**: A negative marginal tax effect in the low-capacity / low-flexibility cell, and an attenuated or positive effect in the high-capacity / high-flexibility cell, with statistical significance in at least one cell.
2. **Subsample confirmation**: Split-sample regressions should show the tax-growth gradient negative and significant below median state capacity, and null or positive above median state capacity.
3. **Channel evidence**: R&D expenditure and business entry rates should mediate the pattern — high taxes with high capacity fund public R&D and infrastructure; high taxes with low capacity crowd out private activity.
4. **Ex-Nordic robustness**: The pattern should survive excluding Nordic countries, confirming that it is not a Nordic-specific artefact.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Nordic exception as falsification**: Sweden, Denmark, and Finland have had tax revenues above 45% of GDP for decades while maintaining strong TFP growth. If the hypothesis requires both high capacity AND high flexibility, these cases fit. But Norway's oil fund complicates the interpretation: high tax revenue is partly oil-revenue accounting, not distortionary taxation.
2. **Endogeneity of state capacity**: Countries with stronger growth may build state capacity and tolerate higher taxes. The causal arrow may run from growth to state capacity to tax tolerance, not from taxes to growth conditional on capacity.
3. **Measurement of labour flexibility**: OECD EPL measures job-protection rules, not wage flexibility or active labour-market policies. A country with strict EPL but strong ALMPs (Denmark) may be misclassified as rigid.
4. **Tax composition matters more than level**: Corporate tax rates, marginal income-tax rates, and VAT may have very different growth effects. A scalar "total tax revenue / GDP" misses composition, and the interaction may be picking up correlation between tax composition and institutional quality rather than a true threshold effect.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to high-income OECD economies where tax revenue is above 35-40% of GDP. It does not apply to: (a) low-income economies where the binding constraint is tax capacity (too little revenue, not too much); (b) resource-rich economies where tax revenue includes resource rents; (c) periods of war or reconstruction where high taxes are temporary and broadly accepted.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a non-linear counterpart to `state_size_reduces_household_income_growth` and a complement to `welfare_state_market_flexibility_complement` and `welfare_architecture_market_openness_nordic`. It engages the classical-liberal vs social-democratic debate by testing whether the Nordic model is replicable (requires specific complementary institutions) or generalisable. A null result (taxes never matter, or always matter linearly) would favour either the pure public-goods view or the pure supply-side view.
