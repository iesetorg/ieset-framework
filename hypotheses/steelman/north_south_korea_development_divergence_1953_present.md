# Steelman: North vs South Korea Development Divergence 1953–Present

The developmentalist steelman (Ha-Joon Chang, Alice Amsden) argues that South
Korea's miracle was explicitly state-directed industrial policy, not free markets.
Park Chung-hee's government picked winners through the Heavy and Chemical
Industry drive, channelled directed credit via state-owned banks, maintained
capital controls, used export quotas, and built the chaebols through preferential
access — practices that contradict the free-market narrative. South Korea's
success, on this reading, validates strategic state intervention rather than
market liberalisation.

North Korea's failure is at least partly explained by the severity of external
pressure: US-led military enforcement of embargo, sanctions cumulating post-1990
nuclear programme, and loss of Soviet subsidised inputs after 1991 — a shock
comparable in magnitude to what Cuba experienced. A socialist system facing such
external pressure cannot be cleanly compared to a market economy with US security
guarantees and open capital markets.

The comparison also conflates two variables: central planning versus markets, and
autarky versus openness. A market economy under complete autarky might perform
similarly poorly. The causal test requires isolating the planning mechanism from
the openness mechanism, which the Korea comparison cannot cleanly do. Cuba's
health outcomes under embargo demonstrate some socialist systems can deliver
public goods even under severe external constraints.
