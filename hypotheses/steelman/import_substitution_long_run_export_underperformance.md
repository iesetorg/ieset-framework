# Steelman: Import Substitution Long-Run Export Underperformance

## Strongest version of the claim
Import-substitution industrialisation (ISI) created protected domestic markets that discouraged efficiency, innovation, and scale economies. Over 25-year windows, ISI adopters (Latin America 1940-1980, Africa 1960-1990) consistently underperformed export-oriented economies (East Asia 1960-1990) in GDP per capita, labour productivity, and export competitiveness. The mechanism is straightforward: protection reduces competitive pressure, overvalues exchange rates, creates rent-seeking constituencies, and biases investment toward non-tradables. Even when ISI built physical industrial capacity, the productivity and learning-by-doing gains were inferior to export discipline.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Cross-country panel comparing GDP per capita and productivity growth under ISI versus export-oriented regimes.
2. Sectoral studies showing lower productivity growth in protected ISI sectors versus export sectors within the same country.
3. Exchange-rate and trade-data showing ISI-associated overvaluation and anti-export bias.
4. Natural experiments of ISI reversal (Chile 1975, India 1991) showing post-liberalisation growth acceleration.

## Best counterarguments
- ISI created the industrial base that East Asia later exploited; without ISI, there would have been no factories to export from.
- East Asian export success depended on prior land reform, state capacity, and Cold War geopolitical rents that ISI adopters lacked.
- ISI was often accompanied by macro mismanagement (fiscal deficits, overvaluation) that was the true cause of underperformance, not protection per se.
- Some ISI sectors (Brazilian aerospace, Indian pharmaceuticals) became globally competitive after long gestation periods.
- 25-year windows miss very-long-run structural effects; ISI may have been necessary but insufficient.

## Boundary conditions
Strongest for small and middle-income economies without prior industrial base. Weaker for large continental economies where domestic market size can substitute for exports during early industrialisation.

## Relation to existing hypotheses
