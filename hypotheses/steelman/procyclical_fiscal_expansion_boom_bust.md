# Steelman — Procyclical fiscal expansion and subsequent boom-bust

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The apparent link between boom-era fiscal expansion and deeper subsequent recessions conflates the fiscal-policy choice with the underlying economic fragility that produced both. Countries that run procyclical policy during booms tend to have weaker institutions, more commodity-dependent revenues, and more volatile credit cycles; countries that run counter-cyclical policy have stronger institutions that also generate lower volatility for reasons having nothing to do with the fiscal stance. The fiscal rule is an epiphenomenon of the institutional difference, and treating it as the treatment variable misidentifies the cause.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Revenue illusion drives many "procyclical" classifications.** Greece 1999-2009 and Spain 2003-2008 ran what looked like countercyclical policy in real time — headline primary balances were in surplus. Only ex-post, once real-estate revenues were stripped out, did the structural deficit become visible. The Spanish and Greek fiscal authorities were not "running procyclical policy"; they were misclassifying transient revenue as permanent. A hypothesis that treats the realised procyclical impulse as the treatment partly measures how badly the fiscal authority's output-gap estimate was off, not the fiscal choice itself.

2. **Reverse causation via the credit-boom channel.** Private credit booms during expansions generate tax revenue and apparent fiscal space simultaneously, then reverse and generate the subsequent recession. The fiscal impulse and the subsequent recession are both downstream of the credit cycle; conditioning on the fiscal impulse as the treatment loads the credit-cycle coefficient onto fiscal. Spec controls for trade openness and terms-of-trade but not for the Borio-Drehmann credit-to-GDP gap, which the literature (Schularick-Taylor 2012) identifies as the dominant predictor of recession depth.

3. **Canonical-case selection.** The four positive canonical cases (ARG, GRC, ESP, GBR 2001-2008) are four of the most-discussed cases in the post-2008 literature; the countercyclical counter-cases (CHL, NOR, Nordics) are the most-celebrated successes. Anchoring the falsification rule on these cases partly pre-selects the answer. If the rule demanded symmetric treatment — "any country whose cyclically-adjusted balance worsened by more than 3pp during a positive output gap" — the case list would include Australia, Ireland pre-2008, and the US under the Bush tax cuts, some of which showed very different subsequent downturns.

4. **The 5-year-forward window is arbitrary.** Greece's worst downturn was 2008-2014, a ~7-year window; Spain's was roughly 2008-2013. Using a 5-year-forward window from the boom-era impulse may capture the front half of the collapse but miss the depth. Extending to 7 years picks up endogenous recovery policy responses (austerity, ECB OMT) and distorts the attribution. There is no clean window length.

5. **The Chilean structural balance rule is reverse-causally contaminated.** Chile adopted the structural-balance rule in 2001 after decades of macroeconomic discipline established under different regimes. The rule codified existing practice rather than producing it. Attributing Chile's 2003-2011 counter-cyclical behaviour to the rule rather than to the underlying political equilibrium treats the easy case backward — the UK and Greece failed to adopt such rules precisely because the political economy that would have sustained them did not exist, and the rule would not have survived if adopted.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report the local projection coefficient with and without the credit-to-GDP gap as an additional control. If the fiscal coefficient collapses once the credit gap is added, the hypothesis is partly or wholly measuring the credit cycle, not the fiscal choice.

(b) Present the canonical-case comparison alongside a mechanical "any country with > 3pp procyclical impulse" comparison, so readers can see whether the pre-coded case list is doing heavy lifting.

(c) Acknowledge the revenue-illusion problem explicitly: for Spain and Greece, run the spec once on the realised procyclical impulse and once on the ex-post revision of the cyclically adjusted balance (using OECD / IMF ex-post reconstructions). If the two produce materially different results, the framework must note that the finding depends on the fiscal authority's output-gap estimate rather than on policy choice alone.

(d) Report the distribution of 5-year-forward versus 7-year-forward outcomes and note which tightens or weakens the finding.

The strongest version of the steelman points at a v2 research agenda: condition on a measure of fiscal-authority output-gap-estimate accuracy (e.g. real-time vs ex-post revisions from OECD Economic Outlook databases) and test whether the "procyclical surprise" — revenue the authority treated as permanent that turned out to be transient — is what actually predicts subsequent recession depth. If so, the hypothesis is really about fiscal-authority information, not about fiscal rules.
