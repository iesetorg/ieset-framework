# Steelman — Expropriation risk, investment horizons, and capital intensity

## Strongest version of the claim

Higher expropriation risk shortens investment horizons and reduces capital intensity in tradable sectors across emerging-market and developing economies. The mechanism is asset specificity: long-horizon investments in structures, machinery, and infrastructure are irreversible and cannot be relocated if expropriated. When expropriation risk is high, investors shift toward liquid, short-term assets (inventories, financial instruments) and away from fixed capital. The claim is bounded to tradable sectors where capital-labour substitution is measurable and where foreign and domestic investors face similar risk perceptions.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Negative structures coefficient:** Panel-FE shows expropriation-risk index negatively associated with structures-and-machinery share of investment at p<0.05.
2. **Negative capital-intensity coefficient:** Panel-FE shows expropriation risk negatively associated with capital-labour ratio in manufacturing at p<0.05.
3. **Magnitude:** A one-standard-deviation increase in expropriation risk reduces structures-machinery share by at least 1.5 pp and manufacturing capital-labour ratio by at least 5%.
4. **Sectoral heterogeneity:** Effect is stronger in mining and manufacturing (high asset specificity) than in services (low asset specificity).

## Best counterarguments

1. **Extractive enclaves:** Expropriation risk is highest in resource sectors, yet mining FDI often involves massive fixed-capital commitments because resource rents are so large that expected returns exceed expropriation risk. The sectoral composition may drive the result.
2. **Political-risk insurance:** Multinationals can purchase political-risk insurance (OPIC, MIGA) and structure investments through offshore holding companies that mitigate expropriation losses. Sophisticated investors may not reduce capital intensity.
3. **Joint-venture strategies:** Investors may respond to expropriation risk by partnering with politically connected local firms rather than reducing capital intensity. The adjustment is on ownership structure, not asset composition.
4. **Reverse causation:** Countries with low capital intensity (poor infrastructure, weak financial markets) attract low-quality investment that is more vulnerable to expropriation. Low capital intensity causes high expropriation risk, not the reverse.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for middle-income emerging markets with moderate expropriation risk where investors are at the margin of committing fixed capital. It weakens for: (i) very poor countries where all investment is short-horizon regardless of risk; (ii) very rich countries where expropriation risk is negligible; (iii) strategic sectors (defence, utilities) where governments guarantee returns regardless of general expropriation risk.
