# Steelman

Redistribution can reduce poverty durably even without rapid market-income growth when transfers are well targeted, reliable, and paired with human-capital conditions. Cash transfers may keep children in school, reduce malnutrition, and prevent distress sales of assets, creating future earning capacity.

The strongest counterprediction is that tax and government-consumption channels predict poverty exits as strongly as, or more strongly than, GDP growth and employment. If transfer-heavy poverty reductions persist after growth controls, the market-income dominance claim is wrong.
