# Steelman: Sweden 1990s Welfare Consolidation Outcome

The strongest counter is that Sweden's 1990s consolidation did materially erode universal-transfer scale and the apparently-stable Gini hides composition shifts. Between 1991 and 2007 Swedish disposable-income inequality rose by roughly 30% — Gini went from 0.21 to 0.27 in LIS-comparable series — which is well outside a "within 5% of pre-crisis" band depending on the chosen baseline year. The 1998 NDC pension reform shifted demographic risk onto cohorts via balancing mechanisms; the 2007 alliance-government cut sickness insurance further; the privatised unemployment funds saw declining membership through the 2000s, increasing churn and benefit denial. Labour-share decline 1995-2010 is among the steepest in OECD. Fiscal-sustainability success is partly an accounting artefact of moving liabilities off the central balance sheet (PPM individual pension accounts) rather than a real shrinking of welfare obligations. A defensible reading: Sweden retained more universalism than the most market-oriented critique allows, but "scale preservation" overstates the case once the 1995-2010 trajectory is fully accounted for and the rising Gini is taken at face value.
