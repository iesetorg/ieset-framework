# Steelman — Egypt Sisi-era macro stabilisation 2014-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **The 2016-2019 IMF programme is the actual treatment, not "Sisi era".** Egypt's headline stabilisation gains track the IMF programme period closely; pre-IMF (2014-2016) and post-IMF (2019-2024) Sisi-era windows are macroeconomically weaker. Coding the entire 2014-2024 as Sisi-era treatment overcredits the regime and underclassifies the IMF-anchoring channel.

2. **Mega-project investment may be unproductive.** New Administrative Capital (~$58bn), Suez economic zone, monorail, etc. — these construction-GDP boosts may not generate proportionate returns and have crowded out private investment. The headline GDP-growth outperformance partially reflects state-led construction GDP that doesn't translate to long-run productivity.

3. **Military economic role expansion is an institutional-quality regression.** The armed forces' control of food, construction, retail, energy, and infrastructure sectors crowded out private firms and distorted competition. This institutional shift is not captured in the spec's WGI control or in the standard outcome variables; the framework should at minimum acknowledge.

4. **Gulf financial support is large and the model depends on it.** UAE/Saudi/Kuwait deposit support, FDI, and direct purchases (Ras El Hekma $35bn 2024) are essentially political flows. A "Egypt achieved stabilisation" story that depends on continued Gulf willingness to bail out is fragile. The framework should explicitly identify Egypt as a politically-supported BoP regime, not a market-stabilised one.

5. **Donor pool is small and Tunisia/Jordan are themselves under stress.** Tunisia's post-2021 democratic backsliding and economic crisis make it a contaminated donor; Jordan's IMF programme contemporaneous with Egypt's contaminates pre-treatment. The synthetic-control fit may be unreliable.

6. **Tourism and Suez Canal revenue dominate the BoP story.** Sisi-era growth was sensitive to tourism and Suez fees; both fluctuated due to Sinai security, Russia-Ukraine, and Houthi Red Sea attacks. Disentangling policy-driven outperformance from external-revenue-driven outperformance is difficult.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report IMF-programme period (2016-2019) effect separately from broader Sisi-era windows.
- Report mega-project / construction-GDP share separately from organic non-construction GDP.
- Explicitly identify Gulf bailout dependency in the phase-2 narrative.
- Frame as "stabilisation conditional on external support" rather than "Egyptian-led recovery".
