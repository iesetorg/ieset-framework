# Steelman — Global green-industrial-policy + chip race 2022-2026

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "comparative-effectiveness, mostly redistribution" framing:**

1. **Program-design scoring is author-coded and politically loaded.** Coding the US IRA as "low export-discipline" and Korea K-Chips as "high export-discipline" embeds priors that other observers would contest. The Korean export-discipline framing is itself a partly-myth (Korea's chaebol-led semiconductor build-out was substantially state-coordinated but not export-conditional in the rigorous Amsden/Wade-1990 sense). A different scoring rubric (cost-of-capital subsidy intensity, technology-target specificity, IP-transfer terms) could flip the comparative ranking. The hypothesis result depends heavily on a contestable design score.

2. **Net-vs-redistribution test depends on the counterfactual trend, which is not stable.** Pre-2022 (2018-2021) clean-energy and semiconductor capacity additions trends were already accelerating; extrapolating that trend forward 2022-2026 produces a counterfactual already showing strong growth, against which 2022-2026 actuals look like only modest acceleration. A different counterfactual baseline (e.g., 2010-2019 average) would show 2022-2026 as much larger acceleration. The "redistribution vs creation" verdict is sensitive to baseline choice.

3. **Effectiveness ratios (spend per MW / spend per kWspm) penalise high-cost-base economies.** A US plant costs more per MW than a Chinese plant for reasons partly orthogonal to industrial-policy design (labour, permitting, supply chain). Comparing $/MW across countries conflates "policy efficiency" with "country cost level." The right metric is spend-per-MW relative to country-cost-base or against country-specific counterfactual capacity-without-policy, not raw cross-country comparison.

4. **National-security / supply-chain-resilience objectives are out-of-frame and dominate some programs.** US CHIPS is fundamentally a Taiwan-contingency hedge; EU CRMA is fundamentally a critical-minerals diversification away from China. Measuring these on industrial-effectiveness ratios systematically understates them by treating their primary stated objective as out-of-scope. A pure economic-effectiveness ranking ranks programs in a way that does not match how their architects assessed them; this is a measurement-frame problem, not necessarily a policy-design failure.

5. **The 2022-2026 window is too short for industrial-policy outcomes.** Steel + semiconductor + battery manufacturing capacity decisions have 5-10 year build-and-ramp cycles; 2022-2026 captures announcements + early-stage construction, not operating capacity. Korea's 1970s HCI drive looked unfavourable on 1973-1980 metrics and favourable on 1980-2000 metrics; analogous lag may apply here. The hypothesis's window-end choice biases against programs with longer pay-off horizons.

6. **Industrial policy may produce path-dependence and option-value gains that capacity counts miss.** A US battery-manufacturing ecosystem that does not exist absent IRA may produce 2030-2040 returns (workforce skills, supplier networks, R&D adjacency) that are uncountable in 2022-2026 capex / capacity. The "redistribution vs creation" framing treats industrial-policy outputs as one-period quantities; the dynamic gains are systematically uncounted in a pure-2022-2026 measurement.

7. **China comparison is data-fragile.** Chinese industrial-policy disbursement data is opaque; "national IC fund" + provincial fund + state-bank-directed credit + export-rebate transfers compose a flow that no single source measures cleanly. Including China in the comparative effectiveness ranking with low-quality denominator data biases its ranking either direction depending on how missing data is imputed.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report sensitivity to program-design scoring with at least 3 alternative codings (author rubric, plus two alternative author-defined rubrics).
- Report counterfactual-baseline sensitivity (2010-2019, 2015-2021, 2018-2021 trends).
- Normalise effectiveness ratios by country cost-base before cross-country ranking.
- Acknowledge national-security objectives as out-of-frame; do not interpret an unfavourable economic-effectiveness ratio as full policy verdict.
- Flag the 2022-2026 window as too short for steady-state effectiveness assessment; commit to v2.0 update at 2030.
- Treat China data with explicit caveats; report results with and without China in the comparator pool.
- Discuss option-value / path-dependence as out-of-frame benefits explicitly.
