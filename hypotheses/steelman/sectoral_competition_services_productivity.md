# Steelman — Sectoral Competition in Services and Productivity

## Strongest version of the claim

In high-income economies, the services sector—retail, transport, communications, and professional services—has become the dominant employer and output source. Competition within this sector, measured by low entry barriers, low incumbent protection, and high churn, is a stronger predictor of long-run prosperity than manufacturing-specific industrial policy spending. Services competition reduces markups, accelerates technology diffusion, and reallocates labour to more productive firms. Manufacturing industrial policy, by contrast, may lock in obsolete technologies, protect declining sectors, and divert resources from dynamic services. The claim is that, in a horse-race regression, services-sector competition outperforms manufacturing policy in predicting labour-productivity growth.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Horse-race dominance:** The absolute t-statistic on services-sector competition exceeds that on manufacturing industrial-policy spending in a panel FE regression predicting labour-productivity growth.
2. **Tercile magnitude:** Top-tercile services-competition countries show at least 0.3 percentage points higher annual labour-productivity growth than bottom-tercile countries.
3. **Sub-component robustness:** The result holds when using PMR retail-trade and professional-services sub-components separately, rather than only the aggregate services index.
4. **EU-15 robustness:** The pattern is present in the EU-15 sub-sample, confirming that it is not driven by US deregulation exceptionalism.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Manufacturing spillovers:** Manufacturing generates R&D spillovers, export earnings, and supplier networks that benefit services; a horse-race may underestimate manufacturing policy by ignoring these externalities.
2. **Longer lags:** Manufacturing industrial policy may have longer gestation periods than services deregulation; a contemporaneous regression may be unfair to the manufacturing channel.
3. **Measurement asymmetry:** OECD PMR services data is well-measured and objective; manufacturing industrial-policy spending is patchy outside the EU and may be underestimated.
4. **Coordination economies:** Germany and the Nordics achieve high productivity through regulated coordination (vocational training, long-term contracting) rather than low PMR; the services-competition channel may be specific to liberal-market economies.
5. **Endogeneity:** High-productivity countries may deregulate services because they can afford the transition costs, making the observed correlation reflect growth → deregulation rather than the reverse.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is scoped to **high-income OECD economies** over 1990–2020. It is most relevant for countries where services dominate employment and output. It does not apply to **catch-up economies** where manufacturing-led growth is still the primary convergence mechanism. Systemic banking crises are excluded because credit contractions distort both services competition and industrial policy implementation.
