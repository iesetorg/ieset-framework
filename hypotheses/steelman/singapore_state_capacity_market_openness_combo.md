# Steelman — Singapore State Capacity and Market Openness Combo

## Strongest version of the claim
Singapore sustained prosperity from 1965 to 2024 is better explained by its exceptional trade openness, strong rule of law, and competitive service sectors than by state ownership alone. While state-owned enterprises (via Temasek) are significant, they operate in a framework of hard budget constraints, international competition, and transparent governance that makes them functionally closer to private firms than to traditional SOEs. The city-state success is therefore a market-openness-plus-institutions story rather than a state-capacity-alone story.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. Time-series evidence that trade openness and rule of law have larger and more significant coefficients than state-ownership share in predicting Singapore GDP-per-capita growth.
2. Evidence that Temasek-linked firms face market discipline (competitive hiring, international benchmarking, performance-linked pay) comparable to private multinationals.
3. Counterfactual reasoning: if Singapore had maintained the same institutions but fully privatised Temasek assets, growth would not have been materially lower.

## Best counterarguments
- **City-state uniqueness:** Singapore geographic position as a entrepot and its small size mean that trade openness is almost a structural given, not a policy choice; the counterfactual of a closed Singapore is implausible.
- **Temasek as sovereign wealth:** Temasek returns have been strong and its portfolio companies are globally competitive; state ownership may have been a net positive by providing patient capital and strategic direction.
- **Reverse causation:** High income enabled investment in rule of law and institutions; the causal arrow may run from growth to institutions rather than the reverse.
- **Survivorship:** Singapore is one of very few city-states; selection on success makes it hard to distinguish policy effects from structural advantages.

## Boundary conditions
- Claim is most relevant to small, trade-dependent economies with strong state capacity; large continental economies may face different constraints.
- The post-2000 period saw rising state involvement in biotechnology and green energy; the claim may be era-specific.
- Comparison to Hong Kong (no Temasek equivalent) is a natural robustness test.
