# Steelman: Zimbabwe Hyperinflation and Land Reform Output Collapse 2000–2009

The decolonisation steelman (Ndlovu-Gatsheni, Patrick Bond) argues the Fast Track
Land Reform Programme was a legitimate and overdue correction of colonial land
dispossession — a moral claim that must be separated from the economic outcome.
The economic collapse, on this view, is primarily attributable to Western
retaliation: ZIDERA (Zimbabwe Democracy and Economic Recovery Act, 2001) restricted
IMF and World Bank credit lines, and Western capital withdrawal was coordinated
and politically motivated. South Africa and other African states experienced no
comparable capital flight after democratic land debates, suggesting investor
reaction was disproportionate to the fundamentals.

The Mugabe regime also suffered from specific governance failures — military
intervention in the DRC (1998-2002), systematic looting by ZANU-PF elites, and
destruction of independent institutions — that are separable from the land reform
concept itself. Successful historical land reforms in South Korea (1950), Taiwan
(1953), and Japan (1946) demonstrate that redistribution need not cause output
collapse; the mechanism in Zimbabwe was inadequate compensation and destruction
of commercial scale, not redistribution per se.

On hyperinflation causation, some heterodox economists (Epstein) argue that
sanctions-driven import contraction created supply-side price pressure independent
of money creation, making the purely monetarist account incomplete. The timing
ambiguity between sanctions (2001) and farm invasions (2000) is close enough
that exogenous shock contributions cannot be dismissed without careful identification.
