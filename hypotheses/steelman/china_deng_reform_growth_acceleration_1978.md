# Steelman — China Deng-era growth acceleration 1978

## The strongest opposing argument

The post-1978 Chinese growth acceleration is real, but attributing it primarily to "Deng-era market reforms" understates three other channels:

1. **Demographic dividend.** China's total fertility rate peaked around 1965 at ~6.4 and declined rapidly through the 1970s. The working-age population as a share of total population rose from ~55% in 1978 to ~75% by 2010. Pure demographic transition mechanics account for a material share of per-capita growth acceleration — no policy required.

2. **Pre-1978 investments paying off.** The Maoist-era build-out of primary education, basic public health, and industrial infrastructure (much of it at appalling human cost) left the 1978 economy better-positioned than its per-capita GDP suggested. The "reform" was operating on a pre-built substrate. A counterfactual where 1978 China retained Maoist policies but also had the demographic dividend and the education base might still have grown, just more slowly.

3. **Catch-up convergence mechanics.** China in 1978 was poor — $308 GDP per capita in constant USD, below most of Sub-Saharan Africa. Even modest policy improvements combined with technology transfer from more-developed neighbours (Hong Kong, Taiwan, Japan, Korea) would produce convergence dynamics that look like "acceleration" from within the country but are structurally just catch-up.

4. **Counterfactual trajectory.** India over the same window (1978-2019) accelerated too, though more modestly. The proper counterfactual isn't "Maoist China" but "China as Vietnam / India", both of which grew substantially post-their-own-reforms. The Chinese accelration is therefore partly distinctive (larger) but not uniquely so (same direction as neighbours).

## What the framework still captures

The framework's structural-break test is deliberately simple (pre/post annualised growth rate gap ≥ 3 pp/yr). That gap is so large that the demographic-dividend + catch-up explanation, even generously credited, cannot account for all of it. The 1978 reforms contributed independently — the magnitude of the post-1978 acceleration exceeds what pure demographics + catch-up would predict.

The honest reading is that Deng-era reforms were necessary but not sufficient — they unlocked a growth trajectory that required the demographic and infrastructural substrate to run at the observed scale. Pre-registering the structural-break test captures the magnitude honestly; decomposing into channels requires follow-on work.
