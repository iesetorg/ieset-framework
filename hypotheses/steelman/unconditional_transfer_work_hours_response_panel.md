# Steelman: Unconditional Transfer Work Hours Response Panel

The strongest counterargument is that unconditional transfers usually have small labour-supply effects because most recipients still want income, status, insurance, and career progression from work. Extra cash can relax liquidity constraints, improve health, fund transport or childcare, and allow better job matching. Experiments often find reduced stress and little change in employment.

Cross-country cash-transfer intensity is difficult to interpret because disability, family, housing, and social-assistance spending serve different populations and may respond to labour-market weakness. If the negative coefficient disappears after excluding disability transfers, controlling for recessions, or separating extensive from intensive margins, the claim is weak. The hypothesis should be rejected if unconditional transfers reduce poverty without lowering LFP or hours relative to work-conditioned alternatives.
