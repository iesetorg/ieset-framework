# Steelman: bilateral_investment_treaty_fdi_panel

## Counter-argument

Bilateral investment treaties are often signed *after* FDI has already begun
flowing, making the BIT-FDI correlation reflect anticipation rather than
causation. Moreover, BITs primarily protect investors from developed countries
operating in developing hosts, meaning the FDI increase may not translate into
broad host-country growth. BITs can also constrain host-country regulatory
space (e.g. environmental or health regulation), creating welfare losses that
offset any FDI gains.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Reverse causality**: Countries that begin receiving FDI sign BITs to
   reassure existing investors, not to attract new ones.

2. **Developed-country bias**: Most BITs are between a developed and a
   developing country. The FDI effect may be entirely on flows from the
   developed signatory, not a general increase in host-country investment.

3. **Regulatory chill**: BIT investor-state dispute settlement (ISDS) can
   deter host countries from enacting socially beneficial regulation,
   creating hidden welfare costs not captured in GDP growth.

4. **Heterogeneity**: BITs vary enormously in content (pre-establishment
   national treatment, fair-and-equitable-treatment clauses, ISDS scope).
   A simple count measure ignores this heterogeneity.

## What would flip the verdict

- Instrumental-variable estimates (using linguistic distance or colonial
  ties as instruments for partner choice) showing no significant effect.
- Evidence that FDI increases are concentrated in non-tradable sectors
  (real estate, utilities) with low spillovers to productivity.
- Post-2012 data showing that countries terminating BITs (e.g. South Africa,
  Indonesia) experienced no FDI decline.
