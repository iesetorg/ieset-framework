# Steelman — Product-market regulation and long-run TFP growth

## Strongest version of the claim

Lower product-market regulation (PMR) is a robust predictor of higher long-run TFP growth across OECD panels, after controlling for education, capital deepening, and initial income. The mechanism operates through entry liberalisation (lower barriers raise firm formation), price flexibility (regulated prices distort allocative efficiency), and reduced state control (privatised incumbents face harder budget constraints). The claim is bounded to OECD countries where PMR data is of reasonable quality and where within-country PMR variation over 30+ years provides identification.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Significant negative coefficient:** Panel-FE regression shows PMR composite index negatively associated with 10-year-forward TFP growth at p<0.05.
2. **Economic magnitude:** A one-standard-deviation PMR reduction corresponds to at least 0.15 pp higher annual TFP growth.
3. **Sub-component robustness:** The result holds for the barriers-to-entry sub-component, not just the aggregate index.
4. **EPL separation:** The coefficient remains significant when employment-protection legislation is included, confirming that product-market rather than labour-market regulation is the active channel.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Varieties of Capitalism:** Coordinated-market economies (Germany, Austria, Nordics) often have higher PMR than liberal-market economies but achieve comparable or superior TFP growth through vocational training, long-term finance, and relational contracting. PMR may proxy for coordination mechanisms that are productivity-enhancing.
2. **Endogeneity:** PMR reforms are often adopted during crises or under EU pressure, which coincide with other growth-enhancing changes (trade liberalisation, macro stabilisation). The PMR coefficient may capture bundled reforms.
3. **TFP measurement noise:** TFP residuals are notoriously sensitive to capital-utilisation assumptions and labour-quality adjustments. A PMR-TFP correlation may partly reflect measurement artefact.
4. **Sectoral composition:** Countries with low PMR often have larger service sectors where TFP growth is harder to measure. The correlation may reflect sectoral composition rather than genuine productivity differences.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for catch-up OECD members (Southern Europe, Ireland, CEE) where PMR reductions coincided with EU accession and convergence. It is weaker for frontier economies where the binding constraint may be innovation policy or human capital rather than product-market entry. It does not apply to non-OECD countries where OECD PMR data is unavailable and institutional quality differences swamp PMR variation.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Complements `firm_entry_rate_long_run_productivity` (PMR is a policy lever that raises entry) and `sectoral_competition_services_productivity` (PMR services-sector component).
