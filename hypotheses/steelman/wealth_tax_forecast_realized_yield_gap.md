# Steelman — wealth_tax_forecast_realized_yield_gap

**The strongest case for grading this SUPPORTED** (i.e. that the yield gap
*is* systematic), written before the run.

## 1. The "no-gap" countries are measured against the wrong forecast
France's IFI (2018-2020) and Norway (2022-2023) appear to *beat* forecast —
but those forecasts were deliberately conservative. The PLF-2018 IFI line was
set at €850m precisely because the government did not trust the new
real-estate-only base; "beating" a low-balled number is not evidence of no
behavioural erosion. The honest counterfactual is realised revenue vs the
*static-base* projection (what the old ISF would have yielded on the same
wealth), and against that benchmark France's wealth-tax take collapsed when
the base was narrowed. A ratio-vs-headline-forecast test mechanically
launders a base-shrinking reform into an apparent success.

## 2. Four countries is too thin to call anything "not systematic"
The sample is 4 countries / 8 case-years, all post-2015, with France
over-weighted (4 of 8 rows). A 2/4 split is one well-documented French
forecast away from 3/4. The registered synth_did sibling exists precisely
because the clean test needs the full publisher panel (minefi/dian/aeat) plus
HNW-emigration counts (Henley/ICIJ) that are not yet on disk. A PARTIAL here
should not be read as "the gap is disproven" — only as "not visible in eight
hand-curated rows."

## 3. The gap shows up exactly where the stakes are highest
The two clear gaps (Spain −58%, Colombia −55%) are the two *new* taxes — the
cases where mobility and avoidance had the least time to be priced in and the
most room to surprise. If anything, that pattern supports the mechanism: the
gap is largest at introduction, then the base and the forecast co-adapt. A
test that pools mature instruments (where forecasts have already absorbed the
erosion) with fresh ones will understate a real introduction-shock gap.

## 4. Realised figures are provisional and upward-revised
Colombia's DIAN F-420 aggregates and Spain's first-year ITSGF take are
provisional; first prints of new-tax revenue are typically revised *up* as
late filings and litigation settle, which would shrink the measured gap.
Conversely, the "no-gap" Norwegian figures are SSB *assessed* (utlignet)
totals that can exceed cash collected. End-state cash ratios could move the
country count either way.

## Why the spec author still expects PARTIAL/REFUTED, not SUPPORTED
The Denmark third-party-reporting evidence (Jakobsen-Jakobsen-Kleven-Zucman
2020) and the on-disk pattern both say the same thing: with decent
enforcement, the behavioural response is modest and a *systematic* below-
forecast gap does not materialise — it concentrates in optimistically-forecast
new taxes. The steelman's strongest point (§1, the conservative-forecast
laundering) is a reason to build the static-base counterfactual as the next
data lift, not a reason to score the headline-forecast test SUPPORTED today.
