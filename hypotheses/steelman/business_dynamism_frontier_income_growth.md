# Steelman — Business dynamism and frontier income growth

## Strongest version of the claim

Among high-income frontier economies, sustained business dynamism — high firm-entry rates, elevated job reallocation, and low incumbent-protection — is a necessary condition for maintaining strong real income growth over multi-decade horizons. Countries that protect incumbents, restrict entry, and suppress churn experience slower productivity growth and eventual stagnation. The claim is bounded to frontier economies where catch-up growth is exhausted and growth must come from within-frontier innovation and reallocation.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Panel-FE or quartile results:** A positive, significant association between a composite dynamism index and real income growth in OECD panels, robust to country and year fixed effects.
2. **Quartile magnitude:** Top-quartile dynamism countries show at least 0.3-0.4 pp higher annual growth than bottom-quartile countries.
3. **US robustness:** The result does not disappear when the United States is dropped, confirming it is not a single-country outlier effect.
4. **Mechanism corroboration:** Dynamism is positively associated with productivity growth and negatively associated with markups or rent-sharing measures.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Nordic exception:** Sweden, Norway, and Denmark maintain strong income growth with moderate dynamism, high unionisation, and substantial incumbent protection. Their model suggests that human-capital investment, R&D subsidies, and coordinated wage bargaining can substitute for churn.
2. **Maturation hypothesis:** Declining dynamism reflects a natural shift from manufacturing (high churn) to services (low churn), not a pathology. Attempting to restore manufacturing-era dynamism in a service economy is misguided.
3. **Japan puzzle:** Japan has low entry rates, low churn, and strong incumbent networks, yet maintained respectable productivity growth until the 1990s and still outperforms many high-dynamism economies in manufacturing quality.
4. **Reverse causation:** Declining growth reduces dynamism (fewer entrepreneurs enter shrinking markets), so the observed correlation may reflect growth → dynamism rather than dynamism → growth.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies most strongly to Anglo-Saxon and liberal-market economies where reallocation is the primary adjustment mechanism. It is weaker in coordinated-market economies (Germany, Nordics) where vocational training, long-term employment, and incremental innovation sustain growth without high churn. It does not apply to catch-up economies where technology transfer via FDI or state-led industrial policy can dominate entry-driven growth.
