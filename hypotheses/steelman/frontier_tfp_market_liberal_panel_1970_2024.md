# Steelman

The market-liberal case is that TFP growth near the frontier requires experimentation across many firms, rapid exit for failed projects, competitive pressure on incumbents, and protection from political allocation of capital. Product-market regulation and state ownership can preserve incumbents and slow reallocation even when they appear stable.

The strongest interventionist reply is that many frontier technologies are rooted in public R&D, procurement, and industrial coordination. The relevant question is not state versus market in the abstract, but whether competition and property-rights variables retain predictive power after public R&D, education, and macro stability are controlled.
