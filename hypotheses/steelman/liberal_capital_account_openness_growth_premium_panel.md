# Steelman — Capital-account openness and growth

## The strongest opposing argument

Capital-account openness is the classical-liberal claim with the weakest modern empirical support. The strong heterodox reading has substantial backing:

1. **Rodrik 1998 / Quinn-Toyoda.** Cross-country panels of capital-account openness on growth show fragile-to-zero coefficients in defensible specifications. The Smith-Mill prior is not borne out at the aggregate level once basic identification is applied.

2. **Kose-Prasad-Rogoff-Wei 2009 conditional reading.** The positive effect, where it exists, is conditional on threshold institutional quality, financial-development depth, and macro-policy framework. The unconditional Friedman claim that "open capital accounts raise growth" is not the modern consensus position even among classical-liberal economists.

3. **Crisis vulnerability.** The 1997 Asian crisis, 1998 Russian crisis, 2001 Argentine collapse, and 2008 GFC contagion all involved capital-account-openness amplifying domestic shocks. The benefits-vs-volatility tradeoff is real and the spec's exclusion of major-crisis decades arguably writes out the most important data.

4. **Stiglitz IMF critique.** The 1990s IMF push for capital-account liberalisation in emerging markets is widely seen as having contributed to the Asian crisis. The IMF itself partially walked back the unconditional liberalisation prescription post-2010 (institutional view on capital flows, 2012).

5. **Reserve currency asymmetry.** The benefits of openness flow disproportionately to reserve-currency issuers (USA, UK, Eurozone, JPN) and to economies with deep financial systems. Emerging economies face a different cost-benefit calculus that the cross-country panel-FE design averages over.

6. **Selection bias in the sample.** Countries that opened capital accounts and survived to be in the sample are a non-random selection — those that opened, suffered crises, and re-imposed controls are differently represented in different decades.

7. **EFW measurement issue.** EFW area-4 freedom-to-trade includes both trade and capital openness. Using it as a capital-openness proxy partly identifies off trade-policy variation. Heritage IEF investment-freedom is a more pointed proxy but has its own subjective-grading issues.

## What the framework still captures

The PARTIAL verdict path (positive only in high-RL sub-sample) is the modern consensus reading; the spec is structured to reach exactly that nuanced conclusion if the data support it. The threshold-effect framing aligns the test with the more defensible Friedman-as-refined-by-Kose-Prasad-Rogoff-Wei position rather than the strong unconditional claim. The heterodox reader has a clean win path through the full-sample-insignificant + high-RL-positive PARTIAL outcome, which would update the framework toward the conditional reading without forcing a binary verdict.
