# Steelman — 2022 energy crisis European industrial relocation

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "intra-EU policy-design heterogeneity drove differential industrial relocation" framing:**

1. **The Iberian cap "won" mostly because Iberia was already low-gas-intensity.** Spain and Portugal entered 2022 with electricity mixes heavy in renewables + nuclear (FRA) and limited gas-fired generation marginal pricing exposure (ESP, PRT). Crediting the Iberian gas-price-cap mechanism for preserving energy-intensive manufacturing capacity confuses a structural pre-condition with a policy intervention. A counterfactual ESP without the cap would have outperformed DEU anyway, because ESP's gas exposure is fundamentally different.

2. **Germany's relief was larger in fiscal magnitude than Spain's intervention; size of intervention is not the dimension that matters.** The Doppelwumms package (~€200bn) dwarfs the Iberian cap subsidy in absolute and per-capita terms. Coding the German response as "retail-only" understates the substantive industrial-bridge support (Industriestrompreis discussions, Gas-Strompreisbremse). The hypothesis's intervention-intensity ordinal coding is contestable — different codings could flip the sign.

3. **Capacity re-siting decisions are slow and overdetermined.** A petrochemical plant shutdown in Germany 2024 reflects multi-year cumulative cost pressures (Energiewende electricity prices, ETS, gas, declining German automotive demand for chemicals, Asian competition); attributing the shutdown to "2022 gas shock + DEU intervention design" is monocausal. Dow Stade, BASF Ludwigshafen rationalisations were planned pre-2022 in some cases. The structural-relocation channel reads more cleanly than it actually is.

4. **The US Gulf Coast comparator was already the destination for global petrochemical capacity for a decade.** ACC capacity expansion 2014-2021 (post-shale) added ~$200bn of investment, almost all of which pre-dated the 2022 EU shock. Treating 2022-2025 Gulf Coast capacity additions as "EU relocation" mis-attributes a long-running shale-gas-driven trend. The marginal effect of the EU shock on US capacity decisions is plausibly small relative to the secular shale advantage.

5. **Persistence claim is data-fragile.** By 2024Q4 some EU energy-intensive subsectors (refining, paper, glass) had partially recovered; the "structural relocation" framing requires sustained 2024-2025 underperformance vs counterfactual, which the data may or may not support. A v1 result that codes 2022-2023 contraction as structural risks being overturned by 2025 readings.

6. **Policy "successes" (Iberian cap, EDF renationalisation) imposed real costs that this hypothesis does not measure.** The Iberian cap raised cross-border electricity exports to France (gas-fired Iberian generation increased), shifting emissions and cost rather than reducing them. EDF renationalisation cost the French taxpayer ~€10bn. If "intervention preserved capacity" is the headline, the off-balance-sheet costs of those interventions need to be netted; this hypothesis does not do that accounting.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Report intra-EU country-level heterogeneity prominently, not pooled-EU averages.
- Decompose the Iberian outperformance into structural pre-condition (gas-mix) vs marginal policy effect. The marginal-policy share is plausibly 20-50%, not 100%.
- Test alternative coding of intervention-intensity (binary, fiscal-spend per GDP, regulatory-binding) and report sensitivity.
- Separate cyclical 2022-2023 contraction from structural 2024-2025 reading; the persistence claim binds on 2024-2025.
- Net out the off-balance-sheet costs of price-cap interventions where measurable.
- Acknowledge that the Gulf Coast comparator is overdetermined by shale; the "relocation to US" framing is partial.
