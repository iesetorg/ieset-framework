# Steelman: Sweden 1990s Market Reform Recovery

## Strongest version of the claim
Sweden's 1990s crisis forced a decisive shift from the unsustainable state-expansion model of the 1970s-1980s (high marginal tax rates, indexed pensions, rigid product markets) to a more market-oriented framework. The resulting reforms—expenditure ceilings, pension privatisation, tax reform, and central-bank independence—restored fiscal sustainability and generated stronger GDP and productivity growth during 1995-2024 than would have occurred under continuation of the pre-crisis trajectory. The Nordic welfare state was preserved but made more efficient through market mechanisms.

## Key evidence the claim would need
1. GDP and productivity time series showing Sweden outperforming its pre-1990 trend and matching or exceeding Nordic peers post-1995.
2. Fiscal-sustainability indicators (debt/GDP, deficit/GDP) demonstrating post-reform stabilisation.
3. Labour-market participation rates, especially among older workers, rising after pension reform.
4. Product-market regulation indicators (OECD PMR) showing Swedish deregulation relative to peers.

## Best counterarguments
- Sweden's recovery was driven by global ICT boom (Ericsson, Nokia spillovers to Finland/Sweden) and a competitive currency depreciation, not domestic reform.
- The pre-crisis "state-expansion" model produced higher equality and lower poverty; post-reform growth came with rising inequality and welfare-state retrenchment.
- Norway's oil wealth and Denmark's flexible labour market (flexicurity) provide alternative explanations for Nordic resilience that do not require Swedish-style reform.
- The 1990s reforms were partly reversed or softened after 2000; the claimed causal mechanism may be weaker than postulated.

## Boundary conditions
Applies to high-income social democracies with strong institutions and crisis-induced reform windows. Generalises poorly to low-institutional-capacity states or those without prior universal-welfare infrastructure.

## Relation to existing hypotheses
Links to nordic_outcome_persistence_decomposition and fiscal_rule_presence_dampens_statist_drift. Tensions with hypotheses attributing Nordic success to welfare-state continuity rather than market reform.
