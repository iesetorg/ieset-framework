# Steelman — Constitutional fiscal rules and growth stability

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest formulation is that constitutional or statutory fiscal rules act as commitment devices that solve time-inconsistency problems in fiscal policy. By binding current and future governments to numerical targets (debt brakes, deficit ceilings, expenditure-growth limits), rules reduce the pro-cyclical bias of discretionary policy, lower sovereign-risk premia, and create a stable environment for long-horizon private investment. The claim is not that rules raise growth in booms, but that they reduce volatility and protect growth during busts, producing higher mean growth over complete cycles.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Rule-adoption event studies.** Germany's Schuldenbremse (2009), Switzerland's debt brake (2003), and Sweden's surplus target (1997) should show identifiable reductions in growth volatility and/or sovereign spreads relative to synthetic controls in the decade following adoption.

2. **Cross-country panel with bindingness coding.** The effect should be driven by genuinely binding rules (Germany, Switzerland) rather than nominal rules that are routinely breached (France, Spain pre-2012). A continuous bindingness measure should outperform a binary rule-presence dummy.

3. **Discretionary-surge comparison.** Countries with large discretionary public-investment surges (China post-2008, Brazil 2007-2014) should show higher volatility and lower long-run mean growth than rule-bound peers with similar initial income and resource endowments.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Endogeneity / selection.** Countries adopt fiscal rules because they already have fiscally conservative political cultures (Germany, Switzerland). The rule is an expression of restraint, not its cause. Without an exogenous instrument, the correlation is observationally equivalent to selection.

2. **Pro-cyclical straitjacket critique.** Eurozone periphery experience post-2010 shows fiscal rules can force procyclical austerity, deepening recessions and extending recovery times. If rules reduce volatility by suppressing booms but amplify busts, the mean-growth claim fails.

3. **Circumvention.** Germany's Sondervermögen (special funds) and France's consistent rule-breaching show that rules can be evaded through off-balance-sheet vehicles or political override. A test that treats nominal rule presence as treatment will mismeasure the true effect.

4. **Small treated sample.** Only a handful of countries have had genuinely binding rules for 10+ years. Panel-FE with n_treated ≈ 10-15 has limited power, and synthetic-control comparisons rely on donor pools that may not match treated units well.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to democracies with independent judiciaries or constitutional courts that can enforce rules (Germany's Bundesverfassungsgericht, Switzerland's direct democracy). In parliamentary systems without judicial enforcement (UK), rules are political commitments, not binding constraints. The claim is also weaker for Eurozone members where EU fiscal surveillance may be the true binding constraint, not domestic rules.
