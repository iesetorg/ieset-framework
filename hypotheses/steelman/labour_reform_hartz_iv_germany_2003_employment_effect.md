# Steelman — Hartz IV employment effect

The strongest heterodox reading: Germany's post-2005 employment improvement was not driven by Hartz IV's benefit-tightening but by the contemporaneous euro-area demand boom (China-export tailwind, Eastern-European supply-chain integration, ECB rate environment), and the synthetic-control gap conflates Germany's idiosyncratic export exposure with a counterfactual the donor pool cannot recover. Even where the headline unemployment rate fell, the bottom-quintile real-wage trajectory and minijob proliferation reveal that the reforms shifted measured unemployment into low-pay precarious employment rather than producing genuine labour-market improvement. Carolin Bock's work on Hartz reception and Dustmann et al. 2014 on German wage compression support the view that the reforms re-distributed labour-market risk downward without creating net new productive employment, and that the donor-pool comparison would dissolve if the outcome were restated as "high-quality full-time employment" rather than "labour-force participation". A clean falsification path is therefore: if the long-term-unemployment-exit channel is statistically null OR if a Northern-only donor pool produces a gap of less than 1pp, the headline estimate is mostly capturing trade-cycle and donor-composition artefacts rather than a Hartz-specific treatment.
