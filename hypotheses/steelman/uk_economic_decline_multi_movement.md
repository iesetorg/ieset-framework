# Steelman — UK economic decline multi-movement

**The strongest argument against the framework's UK-decline framing:**

The "UK is in decline" narrative is more folk-economics than documented fact. UK nominal-USD GDP per capita is below the 2007 peak in dollar terms largely because of sterling depreciation, which is a currency-market repricing rather than a real-economy decline. On log PPP per capita (the outcome the hypothesis uses), UK is within the normal range of advanced-economy experience over 1996-2023 — ahead of France, Italy, Japan; behind Germany, USA, Nordic small economies. Coding this trajectory as "decline" privileges the comparison to faster-growing countries rather than to the UK's own long-run trend.

Specific objections:

1. **The US comparison is cherry-picked.** Most developed economies have slowed relative to the US over 2008-2023 — not just the UK. Japan, Germany, France, Italy, Canada all show the same pattern. If this is "decline" it's a general advanced-economy phenomenon driven by US-specific tailwinds (shale, tech concentration, dollar-reserve status, capital-market depth). Attributing UK-specific share to UK policy requires separating the country-fixed effect from the general developed-economy-vs-US divergence.

2. **The donor pool choice determines the finding.** NLD, CHE, NOR are small-population high-institutional-quality economies with unique features (Dutch disease-adjacent gas revenue for NLD, Norwegian SWF, Swiss pharmaceutical/financial-services specialisation). Matching UK against them via synthetic control tends to produce large negative gaps for UK simply because those countries have features UK cannot replicate. A fairer donor pool of large-ish advanced-economy-average countries (FRA, ITA, JPN, KOR) would tell a very different story.

3. **Productivity measurement is contested.** UK ONS methodology changed multiple times over the sample period; services-sector productivity measurement remains difficult; the "productivity puzzle" has not been convincingly decomposed into cyclical vs structural components even in the academic literature. Attributing a specific magnitude of UK decline requires believing measurement precision the ONS itself is cautious about.

4. **2008 is a confounded treatment date.** The GFC was a global shock, and the UK's greater banking-sector share of GDP meant UK-specific absorption was mechanical, not policy-driven. Coding the post-2008 trajectory as "UK policy response produced decline" conflates the shock's differential impact with the policy response's effectiveness.

5. **Post-2016 Brexit effects are genuinely uncertain.** Academic estimates range from 2% to 6% GDP scarring at various horizons. The 2020-2023 period is dominated by COVID + energy crisis + supply-chain disruption, all of which hit non-Brexit countries too. Identifying UK-specific Brexit scarring in a period of multiple global shocks is methodologically hard; confident attribution is an overclaim.

6. **UK may be converging to its sustainable long-run trend.** The 2007 peak was financial-sector-boosted; the post-2008 level is closer to UK's institutional substrate can sustain. "Decline from 2007 peak" and "reversion to trend" are observationally similar in short-window data.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report both the US-as-donor and the France-Italy-Japan-as-donor comparator specifications.
- Decompose the 2008-2023 gap into GFC-absorption + post-GFC-recovery + Brexit-era components rather than reporting a single post-2008 number.
- Acknowledge that UK GDP per capita in 2023 is at or above UK 2007 in PPP real terms; the "decline" is relative to counterfactual, not absolute.
- Treat the finding as directionally informative but precisely-uncertain; a v2 with better identification (synthetic control per-movement, longer historical window via Maddison + JST) is called for before strong attribution.
