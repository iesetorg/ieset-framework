# Steelman — Chile vs Venezuela divergence 1999-2023

## The strongest opposing argument

The Chile-vs-Venezuela divergence over 1999-2023 is not primarily a policy-content story — it is primarily an **oil-price exposure + US-sanctions story**, and attributing it to Chavismo policy content overstates the policy effect and understates the exogenous-shock effect.

**Key points a defender of Chavismo (or a minimisation argument) would make:**

1. **Oil price collapse 2014-2020.** Venezuela's economy is structurally more oil-dependent (~95% of export earnings) than Chile's (copper-dependent but more diversified). The 2014-2016 oil-price collapse was exogenous and asymmetric — Venezuela absorbed a much larger real-income shock than Chile did from the simultaneous copper-price cycle. A meaningful share of the divergence is therefore a commodity-cycle story, not a policy story.

2. **US sanctions 2017-present.** Financial sanctions on PDVSA (2017) and broader secondary sanctions through 2020 imposed measurable additional costs on Venezuelan production, currency convertibility, and debt service — costs Chile did not bear. A clean policy-content counterfactual would need to control for the sanctions regime; subtract that, and the divergence narrows.

3. **Chavismo-era social gains 1999-2013 were real.** Misiones Bolivarianas cut multidimensional poverty meaningfully during the high-oil-price era. A full pre/post accounting that ends at 2023 averages over a heterogeneous trajectory — strong social gains 1999-2013, then collapse 2014 onward — and attributes everything to "Chavismo policy" when the collapse-era is substantially oil+sanctions.

4. **Chile's own institutional premium predates 1999.** Chile's fiscal rule, central-bank independence, and market-oriented structure were built 1990-1998 (Concertación era). Using 1999 as the window origin mechanically assigns Chile's pre-existing institutional quality to the within-window policy difference.

## What the framework still captures

Even granting every point above, the magnitude of the divergence (≥1.2 log-points ~ 230% relative by 2023) is so large that the commodity-cycle + sanctions explanation cannot account for all of it. Venezuela's productive capacity destruction (PDVSA mismanagement, monetary-fiscal fusion, price/FX controls) contributed independently to the gap.

The honest reading is **both-and**: policy content matters, and exogenous shocks matter, and attributing the full 230% gap to either alone is over-claiming. The framework's pre-registered test picks up the magnitude honestly and leaves the decomposition to follow-on hypotheses (`venezuela_chavismo_framework_validation` addresses the policy-content channel directly with a matched donor pool).
