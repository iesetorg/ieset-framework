# Steelman — Financialisation industry-share decoupling

**The strongest argument against the over-accumulation reading:**

The rising finance-sector share and falling manufacturing share are real empirical phenomena, but the "structural over-accumulation" framing is one of several admissible readings, and the most common alternative — that a deepening financial sector is a complement to, not a substitute for, productive investment — has substantial empirical support up to a country-specific threshold.

Specific objections:

1. **Finance-growth complementarity at low-to-mid levels.** The Levine and King-Levine empirical literature shows financial-sector deepening is positively associated with growth and productive investment up to roughly 100% private-credit-to-GDP. Most of the OECD sample post-1970 is below or near that threshold. Above the threshold (Cecchetti-Kharroubi finding) the relationship inverts, but that is a non-linearity the hypothesis does not yet test.

2. **Manufacturing-share fall is Balassa-Samuelson / Baumol.** As advanced economies accumulate physical and human capital, services demand income-elasticity exceeds 1 and manufacturing labour productivity grows faster than services productivity, so the relative price and relative employment of services rise. This is a textbook structural-change pattern (Herrendorf-Rogerson-Valentinyi review). Calling the manufacturing-share fall "over-accumulation" attributes structural inevitability to a specific Marxian causal story.

3. **Under-measured intangibles.** Gross-fixed-capital-formation in standard national accounts mis-measures the post-1990 economy because much investment is intangibles (software, R&D, organisational capital, brand) that pre-2013 BEA / pre-ESA-2010 Eurostat treated as intermediate consumption. A "falling investment share" can be an artefact of the measurement boundary not catching up to the economy. The Corrado-Hulten-Sichel adjustment shows total intangibles+tangibles investment share has been flat-to-rising, not falling.

4. **Finance share includes a lot of non-rent activity.** NACE section K covers retail banking, insurance underwriting, pension administration, and asset management. Most of that is fee-for-service intermediation, not surplus absorption. An over-accumulation reading would predict the rise is concentrated in trading-and-securities activity, not retail intermediation. The aggregate finance-share metric does not separate these.

5. **Non-finance services sector grew much more than finance.** As a share of value added, professional and business services (NACE M+N) grew from ~10% to ~20% across the OECD 1970-2020. Finance grew from ~5% to ~7%. The "rising finance" framing focuses on the smaller of two big services-sector trends; the manufacturing-share fall is matched primarily by the rise in non-finance services, which the over-accumulation reading does not fit at all.

6. **Falling investment share has alternative readings.** Demographics (slowing population growth reduces required net investment), durables longevity (capital depreciates more slowly), and shifts in the stock-of-capital denominator all matter. Attributing falling I/Y purely to over-accumulation requires ruling these out.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report the non-linearity in finance-share / investment-share relationship; if the OECD sub-sample is below the Cecchetti-Kharroubi threshold, the over-accumulation reading is on shakier ground.
- Add an intangibles-adjusted gross-fixed-capital-formation series as robustness; the headline I/Y fall typically attenuates substantially.
- Decompose the manufacturing-share fall into "absorbed by finance" and "absorbed by non-finance services". If the latter dominates, the framing must shift.
- State explicitly that "supported" on this hypothesis is consistent with, but does not by itself confirm, the over-accumulation reading; complementary tests on financial-fragility / debt-driven-demand are needed for the full Marxian causal story.
