# Steelman — Maduro-era Venezuelan collapse decomposition (2013-2023)

**The strongest arguments against coding the 2013-2023 collapse as predominantly policy-driven:**

1. **The 2014-2016 oil-price crash is the largest single proximate exogenous shock.** Brent fell from ~USD 100/barrel to ~USD 35/barrel. Given Venezuela's pre-crash ~95% oil-export share, this alone represents a terms-of-trade shock of a magnitude few economies have absorbed without deep contraction. The Rodriguez (2024) CEPR analysis estimates the oil shock alone accounts for a substantial fraction of the 2014-2016 contraction. A Shapley decomposition that assigns less than 30% variance to the oil shock understates what any competent analysis would recognise.

2. **US sanctions post-2015 are an external policy shock of uncertain but material magnitude.** SDN 2017 on officials, PDVSA designation January 2019, secondary sanctions on tanker operators August 2019, and associated financial-system isolation restricted Venezuela's ability to repatriate oil revenues, refinance debt, import essential goods, and maintain oil-sector capacity. Rodriguez (2024) estimates ~20-30% of post-2019 output loss attributable to sanctions. A decomposition that assigns under 20% to sanctions likely under-represents this channel.

3. **The Chávez-era institutional inheritance (pre-2013) is load-bearing for the post-2013 dynamics.** PDVSA politicisation 2003, FX controls 2003, expropriations 2007-2015 all pre-committed the policy architecture Maduro inherited. Attributing monetary-fiscal-fusion and political-destabilisation channels to "Maduro-era policy" rather than to "inherited Chavismo institutions" is a timing-attribution choice that the decomposition method cannot neutrally resolve.

4. **Hyperinflation 2017-2019 is an endogenous response to the fiscal gap produced by channels (a) and (c), not an independent policy choice.** The regime's monetisation of deficits 2017-2019 was the residual instrument once oil revenues collapsed and market access was denied. Coding this as channel (b) with substantial variance share risks double-counting: the monetary-fiscal fusion is the *effect* of the oil shock + sanctions, not an independent cause.

5. **Mass emigration (~7-8 million) is a supply-side shock that mechanically affects per-capita measurements.** Population-denominator effects, skilled-worker loss, and labour-supply compression all distort per-capita comparisons. A synthetic-control comparison on aggregate GDP faces the issue that the population trajectories diverge radically across treated and donor units.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report the Shapley decomposition variance shares with confidence intervals. If channel (b) dominates, report it; if channel (a) dominates, report that. The symmetric reporting is the core methodological commitment.
- Separate the pre-2017 (oil-shock-dominated) and post-2019 (sanctions-era) sub-periods. Channel shares may differ markedly across sub-periods.
- Include both aggregate real-GDP and real-GDP-per-capita panels to distinguish aggregate-output from per-capita measurement artefacts of emigration.
- Acknowledge the timing-attribution problem explicitly: the hyperinflation episode's variance share can be attributed to Maduro-era monetary-fiscal fusion OR to the Chavismo institutional inheritance that pre-committed the architecture. Both framings are defensible; the v1 spec commits to attributing the post-2013 monetary-fiscal-fusion INTENSITY to the Maduro era while noting the pre-existing architecture.
- Present the Rodriguez (2024) sanctions-share estimate alongside the Shapley decomposition. If the decomposition assigns a smaller variance share to sanctions than Rodriguez's analysis, the discrepancy is a publishable result, not a method-validation failure.
- Acknowledge that VEN post-2015 macroeconomic data quality is partial; the confidence intervals on all decomposition shares are wider than for a clean-data case.
