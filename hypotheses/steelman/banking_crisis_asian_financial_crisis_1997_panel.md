# Steelman — Asian Financial Crisis 1997 panel

## The strongest opposing argument

The Asian Financial Crisis is sometimes characterised by Stiglitz-aligned critics as an IMF-induced crisis: rapid capital-account liberalisation pushed by the Washington consensus produced the foreign-currency-denominated short-term-debt overhang that made the peg-defence collapse so destructive. On this view, "high prior of supported" is begging the question — the multi-metric pattern was caused by the policy mix the framework should be testing, not independently confirming.

Malaysia's deviating response (capital controls in September 1998, no IMF programme) is a natural counterfactual that the panel-level "4 of 5" metric structure undercounts: Malaysia recovered comparably fast despite breaking the policy rules, which is evidence the rules were not what made recovery possible.

## What the framework still captures

The metric-set deliberately mixes external-balance signals (current-account reversal, depreciation) with domestic-bank signals (Laeven-Valencia coding) and policy events (IMF programme). The 4-of-5 country threshold accommodates Malaysia opting out of programmes without forcing a refutation. What the test is doing is confirming the canonical pattern, not adjudicating the IMF-policy debate.
