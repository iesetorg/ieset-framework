# Steelman — US-EU GDP per capita divergence, policy causes

The strongest opposing argument: the ~50% US-EU gap is real, but attributing it primarily to policy channels requires ignoring three structural facts and a measurement problem.

## 1. The welfare-adjusted gap is much smaller

GDP per capita over-weights market production. Once adjusted for the **consumption equivalent** of EU public goods (universal healthcare at ~10% of GDP vs US ~18% per capita, subsidised higher education, longer paid vacation, shorter work week preference), the effective well-being gap is substantially narrower — Jones-Klenow (2016) estimated ~20% rather than ~50%, and that was before US healthcare costs accelerated further.

This is not a deflection. It is a decomposition of what GDP per capita actually measures. A framework that treats the headline gap as a policy-failure gap is making a category error if a large portion of the gap is EU residents buying more of a different composition of consumption.

## 2. The Ireland / Luxembourg problem is worse than the spec admits

Excluding IRL and LUX from baseline is necessary, but the same FDI-routing distortion is present at smaller magnitudes in the Netherlands, Switzerland (not in sample), and to a lesser extent Belgium. Cleaning the measurement for profit-shifting across all 27 EU states — not just the two most extreme cases — compresses the gap further. Blanchard-Toubal (2024) estimates the MNE-attribution correction alone closes 8-12 percentage points of the measured EU-US gap.

## 3. US GDP is increasingly concentrated in sectors the EU explicitly does not want

US outperformance since 2010 is disproportionately from five tech firms. If the hypothesis is "US policy produces better outcomes", the honest question is: "Do EU residents want those specific outcomes?"

The answer, revealed by sustained democratic preference for GDPR, platform regulation, AI Act, and DMA/DSA, is no. A framework that reports "EU is 50% poorer through policy" without acknowledging that the EU policy set is the revealed preference of EU voters across 27 democracies is presenting a political argument as an economic one.

## 4. The US itself has ~20% of the population in states (MS, WV, AR, NM, KY) with GDP per capita comparable to Portugal

The "US-EU" comparison uses national aggregates. Disaggregated by state and region, US within-country dispersion is now larger than EU within-EU dispersion. Framing this as "US > EU" smooths over the US distribution in ways that the corresponding EU comparison does not smooth its own.

## What would update the framework's prior

If the decomposition survives these objections — i.e. (a) after MNE-attribution correction, (b) after welfare-adjustment for public-goods composition, (c) after regional-disaggregation, (d) after excluding the top-5 US tech contributions — policy channels still explain > 50% of the remaining gap, then the framework's claim is robust.

If any one of those corrections closes > 20 percentage points of the gap, the framework should narrow its claim to: "US policy produces greater **market-GDP** outperformance than EU policy, with uncertain welfare implications", not "US policy produces 50% more prosperity".

## Reading the result honestly

This is the most politically consequential hypothesis in the framework's growth track. If it survives the steelman corrections and the decomposition still puts policy channels at > 50% of explained variance, the conclusion — that EU regulatory architecture materially suppresses prosperity — is a defensible empirical claim backed by provenanced data. If it doesn't survive, the framework should say so prominently, not bury it.
