# Steelman — Corruption and the growth effects of state allocation

## Strongest version of the claim

State allocation of resources (government consumption, SOE output, public investment) has negative long-run effects on TFP growth when corruption is high, but neutral or positive effects when corruption is low. The mechanism is allocative efficiency: in high-corruption environments, state allocation channels resources toward politically connected firms and patronage networks, generating capital misallocation and rent-seeking. In low-corruption environments, state allocation can correct market failures, provide public goods, and coordinate industrial upgrading. The claim is an interaction: the growth effect of state allocation is conditional on institutional quality.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Negative interaction:** Panel-FE shows state-allocation intensity × corruption interaction negatively associated with TFP growth at p<0.05.
2. **Sub-sample asymmetry:** Marginal effect of state allocation is negative and significant in high-corruption sub-sample, but not significantly negative (and possibly positive) in low-corruption sub-sample.
3. **Magnitude:** High-corruption countries with high state allocation show at least 0.3 pp lower annual TFP growth than high-corruption countries with low state allocation.
4. **Alternative corruption measures:** Result holds using V-Dem corruption index or Transparency International CPI instead of WGI Control of Corruption.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Corruption as grease:** In highly regulated economies, corruption may speed up bureaucratic processes and enable investment that would otherwise be blocked. The negative interaction may reflect over-regulation rather than state allocation per se.
2. **Measurement endogeneity:** Corruption indices are highly correlated with general state capacity, income, and geography. The interaction may capture "state capacity matters" rather than "corruption conditions state allocation effects."
3. **East Asian exception:** Korea and Taiwan had significant corruption during their high-growth periods while channelling large state allocations to targeted industries. The "low corruption" threshold may be too high, excluding successful developmental states.
4. **SOE productivity success:** Some state-owned enterprises (Singapore's Temasek, Norway's Statoil/Equinor) are highly productive despite operating in sectors with state allocation. Sectoral heterogeneity may be more important than the aggregate interaction.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for cross-country panels with large variation in both corruption and state allocation (Latin America, Africa, post-Soviet states). It weakens for: (i) Nordic and East Asian countries where state capacity is high and corruption is low, making the interaction less relevant; (ii) sectors with natural monopoly characteristics where state allocation is unavoidable; (iii) crisis periods where state allocation stabilises demand regardless of corruption levels.
