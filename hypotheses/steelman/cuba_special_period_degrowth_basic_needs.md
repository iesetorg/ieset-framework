# Steelman — Cuba Special Period as degrowth-with-basic-needs success, 1989-2000

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The headline claim — that Cuba's 1989-1993 ~35% GDP contraction is a clean demonstration that basic-needs provision can be preserved during forced material-throughput reduction — bundles three separable phenomena: (i) a real institutional choice (universal free health and education survived the contraction), (ii) measurement artefacts in Cuban official statistics (the WDI series for Cuba back-fills from MINSAP and the ONE, both produced by a state with strong incentive to report favourable basic-needs outcomes), and (iii) a confound from emigration as a revealed-preference welfare measure (the 1994 balsero crisis and steady-state high emigration rates throughout the 1990s suggest substantial fractions of Cubans did not regard the basic-needs settlement as adequate). The fair-reader objection is that the four headline indicators (GDP, LE, IMR, primary enrolment) are jointly the most-favourable subset of basic-needs measures available; on broader welfare measures (caloric intake, electricity hours per day, transport availability, consumer-goods access, freedom-of-movement-as-revealed-by-emigration) the Special Period was a substantial welfare loss the headline indicators do not capture.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **The basic-needs indicator set is the favourable subset.** The hypothesis selects life expectancy, infant mortality, and primary-school enrolment — three indicators where Cuba was already a regional outlier in 1989, and where the institutional commitments (free universal health, free universal primary education) were maintained as policy priorities precisely to preserve the regime's legitimacy claim. It does not include caloric intake (FAOSTAT shows a ~30% per-capita drop 1989-1993), electricity availability (rolling blackouts of 12+ hours/day in 1993-1994), urban transport (collapse of bus services, shift to bicycles imported from China), or consumer-goods access (introduction of strict rationing across most categories). A complete basic-needs accounting would include these; the hypothesis's narrow indicator set materially favours the supported verdict.

2. **Emigration as revealed preference for the welfare bundle.** The 1994 balsero crisis (~35,000 Cubans attempted the Florida Straits crossing in August alone) and sustained high emigration rates throughout the 1990s are revealed-preference evidence that a large minority of Cubans regarded the Special Period welfare settlement as insufficient. A pure "basic-needs-preserved" account would predict low emigration intent; the observed pattern is the opposite. The hypothesis as written does not account for this.

3. **The Soviet-subsidy baseline is a confound.** Pre-1989 Cuban basic-needs outcomes were achieved with Soviet bloc transfers worth an estimated 5-10% of GDP via concessional sugar (above-world-price) and oil (below-world-price) terms. Some of the post-1989 basic-needs continuity is inertia from the accumulated capital — clinics already built, doctors already trained, primary-school infrastructure already in place — that the contraction did not yet fully erode. A 5-10 year window (1989-2000) is short relative to the depreciation horizon for clinical and educational infrastructure. A 20-year window (1989-2010) might show different basic-needs trajectories as the inherited capital depreciated faster than it could be replaced under autarky.

4. **Cuban official statistics have known reporting incentives.** Cuban infant-mortality statistics in particular are produced by MINSAP (the health ministry) and reported through ONE (the state statistics office). The international literature flags two known concerns: (a) classification practices for early-neonatal vs late-fetal deaths may shift 1-3 deaths per 1,000 from the IMR numerator into a different category; (b) the late-pregnancy abortion rate is high and removes high-risk pregnancies from the IMR denominator. The WDI vintage carries these concerns through unchanged. WHO independent estimates begin only ~2000, so no contemporaneous external check covers the Special Period window.

5. **The "preserved" comparator counterfactual is unspecified.** "Preserved" relative to what? Absolute 1989 baseline (the spec's choice) is one option; relative to a same-income comparator pool (Caribbean / lower-middle-income LATAM / SSA) is another. On the absolute test Cuba does well — life expectancy actually rose 1989-2000. On a comparator test the question is whether other LMI countries also improved their LE 1989-2000 (most did, by 2-5 years driven by global health-technology diffusion), in which case Cuba's continued improvement is partly the secular trend and not a Special Period success. The spec's absolute-baseline test is the easier test; the comparator test is the harder one and is not reported.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v2 result card must:

(a) Report the four headline indicators with their full 1989-2000 trajectories so readers can see Cuba's GDP did contract sharply AND the four favourable indicators did hold up AND the welfare measures the spec excludes (caloric intake, electricity, emigration) are NOT in the test.

(b) Flag explicitly that the "basic-needs preserved" verdict is preserved-on-headline-indicators, not preserved-in-full. Caloric intake, electricity availability, transport, consumer goods, and revealed-preference emigration are not in the test and the welfare loss along those dimensions was substantial.

(c) Note that some of the basic-needs continuity is inertia from pre-1989 Soviet-subsidised investment in the health and education capital stock. A 1989-2000 window does not test the long-run sustainability question.

(d) Note the Cuban-official-statistics reliability caveat for IMR (1-3/1000 uncertainty band) without resolving it; WHO independent series start too late for cross-check.

(e) The verdict text should distinguish between "headline basic-needs indicators were preserved during a sharp contraction" (which the data support if the four indicators are taken at face value) and "Cuba demonstrated that welfare can be decoupled from GDP" (a much stronger claim that requires accounting for the welfare losses the indicator set excludes).
