# Steelman — Uruguay Frente Amplio social investment 2005-2020

**The strongest argument against the case:**

The hypothesis treats Uruguay as a successful centre-left redistribution case. The opposing view is that Uruguay's pre-existing institutional and demographic advantages dominate, and the FA programme effect is small once those are netted.

Specific objections:

1. **Pre-existing institutional advantage.** Uruguay entered 2005 with the strongest rule-of-law, lowest corruption, highest education attainment, and most demographically transitioned population in Latin America. Treating outcomes 2005-2019 as a programme-effect ignores the high baseline.

2. **Commodity supercycle.** Soybean and beef prices rose substantially 2003-2014. Uruguay's small open economy benefits mechanically from this; LATAM peer comparisons must net out the commodity tailwind, which the spec attempts but cannot perfectly isolate.

3. **Fiscal sustainability deteriorated late in the window.** FA-era fiscal deficits widened materially after 2014 as commodity tailwinds faded; gross public debt rose. The "macro orthodoxy" framing is partly retrospective; the LACAC era (2015-2019) ran sustained deficits that constrained the Lacalle Pou successor administration's options.

4. **Growth performance was modest.** Uruguay's cumulative growth 2005-2019 was respectable but not exceptional within LATAM. Chile, Peru, and Panama out-grew Uruguay over the same window.

5. **Reform reversal in 2020+.** The post-2020 Lacalle Pou administration's adjustments suggest the FA-era structural framework was not fully sustainable; some fiscal-consolidation measures were politically costly to reverse.

**How this should shape the result card:**

(a) Report Uruguay-vs-Chile direct comparison given similar baseline institutional quality.
(b) Show fiscal trajectory 2005-2019 explicitly to address sustainability question.
(c) Net commodity-price control out of the cumulative-growth comparison.
(d) Acknowledge the high-baseline confound in commentary.
