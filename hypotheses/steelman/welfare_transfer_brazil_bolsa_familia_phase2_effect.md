# Steelman: Brazil Bolsa Família Phase-2 Diminishing Returns

The strongest counter is that the apparent diminishing returns reflect mechanical floor-effects rather than programme weakness — extreme poverty was already pushed below 5% by 2010, leaving fewer marginal recipients to reach. Phase-2 BFP is therefore evaluated against a smaller available reduction, and per-recipient targeting efficiency may have improved (Cadastro Único quality, urban-coverage gains) without showing up in headline poverty-headcount declines. The 2014-2016 Brazilian recession (cumulative 8% GDP contraction) was an unusually severe macro shock that any cash-transfer programme would have struggled against, and the BFP phase-2 mitigation effect is plausibly large counterfactually even if absolute outcomes worsened. The 2019-2021 Bolsonaro restructuring period is also short and operationally chaotic, biasing the panel-FE estimate of the post-2010 marginal effect downward through implementation noise rather than programme-design failure. A defensible reading: BFP phase-2 maintained outcomes against severe macro headwinds; the diminishing-returns interpretation conflates programme effect with floor effects and macro shocks rather than attributing properly.
