# Steelman — Green transition cost trajectory electricity prices

**The strongest arguments against the framework's "aggressive green transition + bad grid-design = high industrial cost" framing:**

1. **The gas-price spike 2021-2023 swamps the policy signal.** Russia-Ukraine war drove European gas prices 4-10x normal, which mechanically drove electricity prices up in gas-indexed markets independent of renewable-share or phase-out choices. Attributing the cost divergence to green policy rather than geopolitics requires separating the two — which the staggered TWFE design does imperfectly. Without the 2021-2023 gas shock, the DEU-vs-USA industrial price gap would likely be much smaller. The "aggressive green = high cost" claim is partly piggybacking on Putin's war.

2. **Industrial electricity prices had been converging 2005-2014 before the alleged policy impact.** Germany's industrial price was competitive with US industrial price in the 2010-2014 window. The divergence accelerates after 2015 coinciding with grid-fee structure changes, coal phase-out acceleration, and gradually tightening Emissions Trading Scheme. Attributing the specific timing to "green policy" requires explaining why the effect took 4+ years to materialise.

3. **Industrial-sector output decline is partly demographic + secular structural change.** German manufacturing VA relative to GDP has declined since 1990s — long before green policy became binding. The green-transition hypothesis is vulnerable to attributing to policy what is actually long-run deindustrialisation + services-sector shift common to advanced economies.

4. **The "bad grid-design complements" story is policy-actionable — exactly the framework's preferred finding.** If the claim is "green transition + well-designed grid (baseload retention, market-design reform, interconnection investment) = low cost" — Sweden, Norway, France, arguably USA — then the framework's actionable recommendation is "do green transition better, not less green transition." This reframing removes the "green = degrowth" framing the user evoked; the data supports the more nuanced claim but not the slogan.

5. **Climate-welfare benefits are outside the productivity framing.** The hypothesis measures industrial electricity prices + manufacturing VA. It does not measure climate-change damages avoided, health-benefits-from-air-quality-improvement, or the option value of decarbonisation at the margin where runaway warming becomes possible. The framework's narrow outcome imports a value judgement (productivity + manufacturing output are the welfare metrics) that climate advocates contest.

6. **The aggressive-treatment coding is author-defined.** "Stringency + gas indexation + firm-capacity phase-out" coding separates DEU/GBR/BEL/NLD from FRA/USA/SWE/NOR cleanly, but the coding itself embeds a theory about which policy combinations are costly. Alternative codings (just renewable-share, just EUA carbon price, just coal-phase-out date) would produce different samples and possibly different results.

**How this should shape the v1 result card when run:**

- Split 2015-2020 vs 2021-2023. The two windows have very different drivers; separating them clarifies whether the effect is policy vs gas-shock.
- Interact treatment with grid-design complements (interconnection capacity, baseload share, wholesale market structure). The "well-designed grid" countries (SWE, NOR) inside the treated group — if coded as treated — should show milder price effects.
- Acknowledge climate-welfare omission explicitly. This framework tests a narrow economic question; full welfare accounting requires pairing with a climate-damages-avoided hypothesis.
- Don't overclaim magnitude. Even if the treatment effect is positive and significant, the attributable share of the total industrial-price gap between EU-treated and comparators is likely 30-50%, not 100%. The remainder is gas-shock + secular trends + currency.
- Frame the actionable recommendation as grid-design-complements, not anti-transition. The data supports "green transition with well-designed grid" as a positive direction; not "degrowth is inherent to green policy."
