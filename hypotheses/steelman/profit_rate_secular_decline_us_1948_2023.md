# Steelman — Profit rate secular decline (US 1948-2023)

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The "Marxian profit rate" as constructed here is a fragile artefact of measurement choice. Corporate profit margins (CP / GDP), after-tax corporate profit shares, and S&P 500 return-on-equity all show the OPPOSITE pattern — namely a flat-to-rising profit share since the 1980s. The Marxian construction depends on dividing by replacement-cost capital stock, which has been growing faster than corporate income partly because of structural changes (rising R&D and intangibles capitalised under the 2013 BEA revisions, longer service-lives on equipment) that are measurement-driven, not economically meaningful.

Specific objections:

1. **Numerator-denominator coherence.** A defensible profit rate divides operating surplus accruing to a defined capital stock by the value of that capital stock. Net cash flow (FRED CNCF) includes financial income, depreciation allowances, and inventory valuation adjustments that are not "Marxian surplus value". Replacement-cost net stock includes intangibles in some vintages and not others. The series may be telling us about BEA accounting changes more than about capital accumulation dynamics.

2. **The 1948-1973 baseline is anomalous, not normative.** WWII destruction of competitor capital stocks left the US in a uniquely high-rents position 1948-1973 that no theory predicts as steady-state. Treating the post-1973 fall as "secular decline" rather than "regression to a non-anomalous mean" is a question-begging framing. A more defensible test is whether post-1973 is below a long-run pre-WWII baseline; that comparison weakens the secular-decline reading considerably.

3. **Capacity utilisation matters.** The Marxian rate falls when capital stock grows faster than output, but capital stock can grow because firms hold spare capacity for option-value reasons (volatile demand, just-in-case manufacturing). A "falling rate" caused by precautionary capital holding is not the same theoretical phenomenon as a falling rate caused by an exhausted accumulation regime. The hypothesis must condition on utilisation.

4. **Financial-sector profits and intangible capital are missing from the construction.** Post-1980 US corporate sector includes a much larger finance share (separate hypothesis: financialisation_industry_share_decoupling) and a much larger intangibles-driven sector (software, biotech, brand). Both have very different K/Y dynamics from manufacturing. A whole-corporate-sector Marxian rate mixes them and produces a misleading aggregate.

5. **Post-2010 profit margins refute the secular-decline reading.** S&P 500 net margins reached all-time highs in 2018-2021, and corporate profits as share of GDP (FRED CP) reached 12% by 2021 vs 6% in the 1970s. If the "Marxian rate" is falling while accounting profit margins are rising, the most likely explanation is that the Marxian rate is mis-measuring something — capital stock denominators inflated by intangibles, or numerators that exclude rising rents. The hypothesis must reconcile this divergence honestly.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Report the Marxian rate ALONGSIDE the corporate profit margin (CP / GDP) and the after-tax profit share, on the same chart, so readers can see the divergence.
- Run robustness with alternative capital-stock vintages (pre-2013 vs post-2013 BEA revision).
- Condition explicitly on capacity utilisation; report both unconditional and conditional trends.
- State explicitly that the descriptive finding does not by itself validate any forward-looking Marxian prediction (collapse, accumulation crisis); it is a stylised fact about ONE construction of profitability, not a falling-rate-tendency proof.
