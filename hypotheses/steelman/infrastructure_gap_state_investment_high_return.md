# Steelman — State Infrastructure Investment Below Access Thresholds

## Strongest version of the claim

In countries below basic infrastructure access thresholds, state-led infrastructure investment delivers high economic returns because the marginal productivity of connecting unconnected households and firms is large. The returns are measured not only in GDP growth but in agricultural yield improvements, firm productivity gains, and poverty reduction. This validates the core developmentalist argument: at low levels of development, the state must coordinate lumpy, non-excludable investments that private markets will not finance due to externality and coordination problems. The claim does not generalise to high-access countries where marginal projects may be white elephants.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing positive association between public infrastructure investment and growth in below-threshold countries, with weaker or negative effects above threshold.
2. **Regional/micro evidence** from specific projects (rural electrification in India, road building in Ethiopia, port expansion in Vietnam) showing local productivity and income gains.
3. **Cost-benefit studies** of state infrastructure projects with documented returns above social discount rates.
4. **Counterfactual comparisons**: regions that received infrastructure versus matched regions that did not.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Corruption and misallocation**. State infrastructure projects in low-capacity countries often suffer from massive cost overruns, ghost projects, and political targeting that drive returns to zero or negative.
2. **Private provision is feasible**. Concessions, build-operate-transfer contracts, and public-private partnerships can deliver infrastructure without full state ownership or direction.
3. **Maintenance failure**. State-built infrastructure often deteriorates rapidly due to lack of maintenance funding, eroding long-run returns.
4. **Crowding out**. Infrastructure spending may displace private investment through higher interest rates or tax burdens.
5. **Selection bias**. Successful projects are studied and publicised; failed projects are buried, inflating perceived returns.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **basic access gaps** (electricity <80%, paved roads <20 km/100km2, clean water <90%).
- Less defensible for **prestige projects** (high-speed rail, mega-dams, skyscrapers) in low-access settings.
- Governance quality moderates returns: the threshold for positive returns rises as governance falls.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with developmental-state and public-investment literatures. Tensions with claims that private infrastructure provision outperforms state provision at all income levels.
