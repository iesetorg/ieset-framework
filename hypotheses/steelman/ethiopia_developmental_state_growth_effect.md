# Steelman — Ethiopia developmental-state growth effect (1995-2010)

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The "Ethiopia developmental state produced the fastest sustained African growth" framing reads the 1995-2010 window as causally driven by EPRDF industrial policy, infrastructure-led catch-up, and export-credit allocation. The strongest critique runs in three layers: (1) Ethiopia's headline growth in the WDI panel is not actually the fastest in Sub-Saharan Africa over the relevant window — Mozambique and Rwanda both grew faster from comparably low post-conflict bases — so the rank claim fails before any identification work begins; (2) the post-Derg recovery starting from a 1991 trough that was itself depressed by famine, civil war and Soviet-style collectivisation creates mechanical low-base catch-up, not industrial-policy treatment effect; and (3) the period coincided with massive donor aid inflows, very favourable coffee and primary-commodity prices, and a once-in-a-generation demographic dividend, all of which would have produced rapid growth under a wide range of policy regimes.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **The headline rank does not hold in the standard panel.** WDI NY.GDP.PCAP.PP.KD shows Mozambique cumulative log-GDPpc growth 1995-2010 at +0.85 log points and Rwanda at +0.65, both above Ethiopia's +0.60. Both are post-conflict-recovery cases — Mozambique from the 1992 peace accord, Rwanda from the 1994 genocide — that share with Ethiopia a "war-end-plus-rebuild" structural dynamic but had no comparable developmental-state programme. The "fastest sustained African growth" framing is empirically wrong against the obvious peer pool, and the developmentalist reading is left having to explain why two countries with no industrial-policy programme out-grew the supposedly model case.

2. **Post-Derg low base inflates the growth rate.** Ethiopia's per-capita PPP GDP at 1991 was the lowest in Sub-Saharan Africa among non-conflict-active states (log = 6.66), pulled down by 17 years of Derg-era collectivisation, the 1983-85 famine, and the late-1980s civil war. Any reasonable post-conflict reconstruction would have produced large headline growth-rate numbers from that base purely through the recovery of pre-existing productive capacity (livestock herds, smallholder land tenure, urban services). The hypothesis treats high growth from a low base as causal evidence of policy success, but the base itself is policy-dependent in the opposite direction (Derg policy depressed it), so the high growth rate post-1991 is partly the rebound from prior policy failure rather than the lift from new policy.

3. **Donor aid was an enormous shock — Ethiopia was the largest aid recipient in SSA over much of the window.** Net ODA to Ethiopia ran at 8-15% of GNI through the 2000s, peaking above $3bn/year. The developmental-state account treats infrastructure investment as state-led, but the financing was substantially external concessional. A counterfactual where Ethiopia received Mozambique-level aid and ran the same industrial policy would not have built the same infrastructure stock, and the policy-vs-financing channels are not separable in a country-time panel without an aid-flow control. The spec includes no aid variable.

4. **Synthetic-control evidence on this run does not vindicate the developmental-state reading.** Running a Dirichlet-search synthetic counterfactual on pre-treatment 1991-1994 levels of log-GDPpc finds Mozambique gets ~64% of the donor weight (because Mozambique is the only peer that matches Ethiopia's pre-treatment level closely), and the synthetic Ethiopia *outgrows* actual Ethiopia by roughly 7 log-points by 2010. That makes Ethiopia an under-performer relative to its closest pre-treatment match, the opposite of what a "fastest growth" framing would expect. The developmentalist reading must explain why Mozambique — which had no industrial parks programme, no export-credit allocation, and no developmental-state ideology — out-grew Ethiopia from the same starting level.

5. **The window endpoint matters.** Ethiopian growth accelerated after 2010 with the GTP-I infrastructure programme, the Hawassa industrial-park strategy, and the Renaissance Dam construction. Pushing the window to 2019 (still pre-COVID) and excluding the 2018-2019 reform-era turbulence would push Ethiopia higher in the ranking. The 2010 cut-off captures the developmental-state strategy era as the spec defines it, but it also truncates the period when state-led investment accumulated into measurable productivity gains. A v2 should report 1995-2019 results as a robustness check.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Report the ranking honestly. Ethiopia at #3 (behind MOZ and RWA) is the dispositive primary failure on rank; the gap-to-median primary held at +20.6 log-points but only marginally and only because of the bottom of the distribution (BDI -18, MDG -2 pull the median down).

(b) Report the synthetic-control-lite gap (-7 log points) prominently as the informative robustness check. A reader inclined toward the developmentalist reading needs to see that the closest matched counterfactual outgrew Ethiopia, not the reverse.

(c) Avoid claiming the developmental-state strategy "caused" the headline growth. The honest reading is "Ethiopia grew well above the SSA peer median over 1995-2010, but did not lead the pool, and a synthetic counterfactual matched on pre-treatment levels outgrew the actual path." That is consistent with industrial policy contributing some lift, but not with the strong "fastest sustained growth" framing.

(d) Flag aid-flow and post-conflict-rebound channels as the most likely confounders. A v2 with an ODA/GNI control and a post-conflict-trough indicator would be the obvious refinement.

The strongest version of the steelman argues that the rank claim fails empirically, that the gap-to-median claim is a low-base mechanical effect rather than a policy treatment effect, and that the synthetic counterfactual evidence runs against the developmentalist reading. The hypothesis is not refuted in the strict "Ethiopia grew slower than peer median" sense, but the headline "fastest sustained growth driven by developmental state" framing is not supported by the WDI panel either. Partial verdict, with the methodological refinements above as the v2 path.
