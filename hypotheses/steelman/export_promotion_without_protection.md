# Steelman — Export promotion combined with domestic competition outperforms export promotion combined with domestic protection

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that export promotion is most effective when it operates in a competitive domestic environment where firms must earn their export status through productivity rather than through political connections, rent-seeking, or sheltered monopoly positions. In this setting, export promotion (information, financing, trade missions) corrects market failures (information asymmetries, missing foreign networks) without creating new distortions. When combined with domestic protection, export promotion becomes a tool for entrenching incumbents: protected firms export not because they are competitive but because subsidies and privileged access lower their effective cost, and domestic consumers bear the cost of cross-subsidisation.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Interaction ranking**: A robustly positive coefficient on (export promotion × competition) and a null or negative coefficient on (export promotion × protection) in export-growth and TFP regressions.
2. **Case-study triangulation**: Mauritius (export-processing zones with relatively open domestic entry) should outperform Argentina (export promotion under ISI protection). Korea's export-promotion system, which combined credit subsidies with intense domestic competitive pressure (via multiple chaebol rivalry), should show positive effects.
3. **Channel evidence**: Export diversification and manufacturing TFP should mediate the competition interaction more strongly than the protection interaction.
4. **Ex-China/Korea robustness**: The result should survive dropping the two dominant East Asian export-promotion cases, confirming a general pattern rather than a regional story.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Korea's chaebol system**: Korea combined export promotion with significant domestic protection (import quotas, credit rationing in favour of chaebol). Yet export growth and TFP growth were spectacular. The "competition" side of Korea is intra-chaebol rivalry and export-market discipline, not domestic entry freedom. The hypothesis's competition measure (OECD PMR) may misclassify Korea.
2. **Measurement error**: Export promotion intensity is poorly measured cross-country (agency budgets are often opaque, and informal promotion through state-owned banks is unobserved). Protection is also measured via PMR rather than actual tariff and non-tariff barriers. The interaction coefficients may be noise.
3. **China's dual track**: China's export promotion (SEZs, tax rebates, cheap credit) coexisted with extensive domestic protection (SOE privileges, local content requirements). Export growth was extraordinary, but whether it translated into broad TFP growth is disputed. The interaction story fits China poorly.
4. **Small-country exception**: Small economies may need temporary protection to achieve scale before exporting. The hypothesis assumes that protection is always harmful; infant-industry theory disagrees, and the cross-country panel cannot resolve this timing issue.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for middle-income economies with existing manufacturing bases and where export promotion takes the form of information and matching services rather than large subsidies. It is weakest for: (a) low-income economies where the binding constraint is infrastructure rather than market structure; (b) economies where export promotion is primarily large-scale subsidised credit (risk of rent-seeking regardless of domestic competition); (c) very small economies where a single firm may dominate exports for scale reasons unrelated to protection.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis is a direct test of the `infant_industry_protection_capability_effect` claim, reframed as a policy-bundle comparison. It complements `africa_mauritius_export_zone_model_1980_2024` and `trade_lib_china_wto_2001_manufacturing_export_surge` by asking what domestic conditions make export-oriented growth strategies effective. A split result (competition works for information-based promotion, protection works for capital-intensive promotion) would support a contingent-capacity view.
