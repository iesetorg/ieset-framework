# Steelman: Productivity-Compensation Gap

The strongest version of the claim is that after the early-1970s break, productivity growth no longer translated one-for-one into worker compensation growth. A conservative version should still appear in BLS/FRED nonfarm-business output-per-hour and real hourly compensation indexes, not only in more dramatic median-worker cash-wage series.

The strongest counterargument is measurement. Total compensation includes benefits, deflators differ, and average compensation can hide distributional divergence. A conservative FRED/BLS index test can understate median-worker stagnation, but if even this measure shows a large gap, the broad decoupling claim survives a tougher specification.
