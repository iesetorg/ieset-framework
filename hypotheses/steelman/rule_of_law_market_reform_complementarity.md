# Steelman — Rule of Law and Market Reform Complementarity

## Strongest version of the claim

Market reforms—privatisation, trade liberalisation, and price decontrol—do not generate durable growth in an institutional vacuum. They require a minimum threshold of rule-of-law quality to protect property rights, enforce contracts, and prevent rent-seeking capture of newly created markets. Below this threshold (approximately the 40th percentile of global rule-of-law distribution), market reforms show insignificant or even negative associations with growth because reform creates opportunities for predation without providing the institutional infrastructure to channel competition productively. Above the threshold, reforms unlock sustained productivity gains. The claim is therefore an institutional-complementarity hypothesis: markets and rules of law are co-dependent inputs to prosperity.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive interaction:** The market-reform × rule-of-law interaction term is positive and significant in a panel FE regression on GDP per capita growth.
2. **Threshold effect:** The marginal effect of market reform is not significantly positive when rule of law is below -0.5 (WGI scale), and is positive and significant above it.
3. **Post-Soviet robustness:** The result holds when post-Soviet transition countries are excluded, confirming that the pattern is not driven solely by one historical episode.
4. **Reform-episode validation:** Event-study analysis around large reforms (India 1991, China 1978, post-Soviet transitions) shows that pre-reform rule-of-law levels predict post-reform growth trajectories.

## Best counterarguments

1. **China outlier:** China achieved rapid growth from 1978–1992 despite very low formal rule-of-law scores, suggesting that party-state enforcement, local experimentation, and political commitment can substitute for formal legal institutions.
2. **Co-determination:** Market reforms and rule of law may improve simultaneously as part of a modernisation package, making it impossible to identify a causal complementarity from observational data.
3. **Shock therapy critique:** Post-Soviet failures in low-RL countries may reflect the speed and sequencing of reforms rather than institutional preconditions per se; gradual reform might have succeeded even in weak institutional environments.
4. **Reverse threshold:** Some argue that market reforms themselves build rule of law by creating constituencies for property-rights protection, implying that reform should precede rather than follow institutional development.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to **broad cross-country panels** over 1980–2020, spanning high-income, middle-income, and transition economies. It is weaker for **single-country cases** where idiosyncratic political dynamics dominate. The first three years of post-communist transition are excluded because institutional flux makes classification unreliable. The threshold is approximate and may shift with alternative institutional measures (V-Dem, Fraser EFW legal integrity).

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Linked to `property_rights_long_run_income_frontier` and `shock_therapy_institutional_preconditions_conditionality`. Tensions with Rodrik’s policy-first view and with AJR’s institutions-as-fundamental-causes view; this hypothesis occupies a middle ground.
