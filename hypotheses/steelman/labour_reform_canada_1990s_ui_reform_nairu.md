# Steelman — Canada 1990s UI reform NAIRU

The strongest counter-reading: Canada's 1990s unemployment-rate decline is a story of NAFTA-driven export expansion, US-cycle co-movement, and the 1995-1997 Bank of Canada disinflation completing — none of which are caused by UI reform. Setterfield and Lavoie argue the NAIRU concept itself is a moving target driven by aggregate demand, and that any "structural" decline in the Canadian unemployment rate during the 1990s reflects hysteresis-reversal from the deep 1990-1992 recession rather than genuine institutional shift. The Atlantic-Canada region-heterogeneity test is the discipline: if Atlantic provinces (where the frequent-claimant rule bit hardest) do not show outsized gains relative to Ontario and Western Canada, the channel-specific story fails and the gain is reassignable to the macro tailwind. Lin et al. 1998 and HRSDC reviews show Atlantic claimant numbers fell substantially but employment rates did not recover symmetrically — workers exited the labour force rather than transitioning to employment. A v2 should incorporate provincial labour-force participation as a paired outcome.
