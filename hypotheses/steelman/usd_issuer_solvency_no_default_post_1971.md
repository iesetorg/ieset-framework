# Steelman — USD issuer solvency: no default post-1971

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The zero-default count post-1971 is descriptively true but inferentially trivial. The MMT framing wants the absence of default to license the inference that the US faces no fiscal constraint at all — but the absence-of-default observation is consistent with at least three rival explanations that have nothing to do with currency-issuer operational solvency.

Specific objections the hypothesis must engage with:

1. **Survivorship by political choice, not operational mechanics.** The US has not defaulted because Congress has, every time, raised the debt ceiling and the Treasury has, every time, prioritised debt service. Each of those is a political choice. The 2011, 2013, and 2023 brinkmanship episodes show that the operational-solvency claim is conditional on a political consensus that has held but is not guaranteed to hold. A future debt-ceiling impasse that produced even a one-day technical default would falsify the spec — but that falsification would not refute the underlying MMT claim about the operational mechanics, only about the political environment.

2. **The reserve-currency premium does the work.** The US dollar's role as global reserve currency means foreign demand for Treasuries is structurally elevated, suppressing yields and absorbing supply that would otherwise strain auction clearing. The zero-default record may be specific to the reserve-currency issuer, not generic to any sovereign currency-issuer. If the test is run on UK, Canada, Australia, Japan separately, the claim might still survive but with weaker margins, and the strong MMT generalisation would not be supported.

3. **Inflation is the true constraint, and the spec sets it aside.** The hypothesis explicitly excludes inflation from its outcome set. But operational-solvency-without-inflation is the empty claim; the real-world fiscal limit binds via inflation, not via missed coupons. By restricting the outcome to default events, the spec can only confirm a claim that no serious mainstream economist disputes (the US can technically always print to pay) and cannot speak to the contested claim (whether expansion is non-inflationary).

4. **Debt-ceiling brinkmanship is excluded by exclusion rule.** The spec drops the 2011, 2013, 2023 episodes from the default-event count because no coupon was actually missed. But the credit-rating downgrades (S&P 2011, Fitch 2023) and the documented Treasury contingency planning for prioritisation are themselves distress signals. Excluding them as "no actual default" is methodologically defensible but rhetorically weak — readers will note that the zero-event count is partly a coding choice.

5. **2024-end is not 2050.** The hypothesis evaluates a 53-year window. The strongest version of the rival claim is generational, not annual: at some debt-to-GDP level, the political consensus that has produced the zero-default record will break, and the operational-mechanics claim will become testable for the first time. The hypothesis cannot speak to that; it can only document the record up to the close of the sample.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The result card must:

(a) Distinguish the descriptive zero-event finding from the broader MMT operational-solvency inference. Support for the hypothesis is support for the descriptive count, not for the generalised currency-issuer claim.

(b) Report S&P 2011 and Fitch 2023 downgrades as INFORMATIVE near-events; note that downgrade ≠ default but is in the distress-signal family.

(c) Run a parallel descriptive count for UK, JPN, CAN, AUS in their respective fiat eras as a robustness check on the reserve-currency-premium objection.

(d) State explicitly that this hypothesis is silent on inflation. The companion hypothesis monetary_finance_zlb_no_inflation tests the inflation channel separately.
