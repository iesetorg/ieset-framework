# Steelman — China Post-WTO Market Opening vs. Subsidy TFP

## Strongest version of the claim

China's most robust total factor productivity growth occurred during the WTO-accession window (2001–2008), when tariff reductions, regulatory harmonisation, and increased foreign competition forced domestic firms to upgrade efficiency and technology. After 2008, and especially after 2015, the shift toward subsidy-heavy state direction (Made in China 2025, directed credit, local-government industrial funds) reversed the TFP acceleration. The post-2008 regime is associated with rising capital misallocation, excess capacity in targeted sectors, and declining marginal returns to investment, even after controlling for demographic shifts, capital deepening, and the global financial crisis.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Time-series structural break** in China's TFP growth around 2008, with 2001–2008 significantly higher than 2009–2020 after controlling for capital deepening and human capital.
2. **Firm-level microdata** showing declining MRPK dispersion during 2001–2008 and rising misallocation post-2008, especially in subsidised sectors (steel, solar, EVs, semiconductors).
3. **Sectoral comparison** between WTO-era liberalised sectors (consumer goods, electronics assembly) with continued productivity gains and post-2008 targeted sectors with overcapacity and zombie-firm proliferation.
4. ** Provincial variation** exploiting differential exposure to tariff cuts and subsidy intensity across Chinese provinces.

## Best counterarguments

1. **The 2008 crisis was the break, not policy**. Global demand collapse after the GFC explains China's slowdown; attributing it to domestic subsidy policy conflates external and internal causes.
2. **Subsidy sectors are capital-intensive by nature**. Lower apparent TFP in semiconductors and EVs reflects long gestation periods, not misallocation; returns may materialise after 2030.
3. **WTO gains were low-hanging fruit**. Tariff cuts and FDI liberalisation produced one-off efficiency gains that were bound to fade; subsequent policy is necessary for frontier catch-up.
4. **China's TFP data are unreliable**. Official statistics smooth growth; independent estimates vary widely, making structural-break claims fragile.
5. **National security justifies subsidies**. Technology self-reliance reduces geopolitical vulnerability; TFP losses are a cost of strategic resilience, not policy error.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **manufacturing and tradable sectors** with clear pre- and post-2008 policy regimes.
- Less defensible for **infrastructure, defence, and strategic technology**, where social returns differ from private TFP.
- The claim compares regimes within one country, reducing cross-country confounding but raising data-quality concerns.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Aligns with `china_wto_accession_productivity_growth_effect` and `hayek_calculation_problem_china_soe_capital_efficiency` by linking state direction to misallocation. Tensions with `china_extra_made_in_china_2025_outcomes`, which evaluates sectoral output rather than TFP.
