# Steelman — UK Thatcher union law 1980s

The strongest heterodox reading: the UK strike-days collapse 1980-1990 is partly mechanical — the 1981-1983 manufacturing recession destroyed the heavily-unionised steel, coal, and shipbuilding industries that generated most of the strike days, so strike days fell because the unionised industries no longer existed, not because the laws were biting. Disney-Gosling-Machin 1995 and Brown et al. 1997 show union-wage-premium decline predates the 1984 watershed and continued through the post-1992 recovery, suggesting structural change in the economy was at least as important as the legal regime. The donor-pool comparison cannot recover the counterfactual cleanly because no donor experienced the same magnitude of manufacturing-sector decline in the same window. A clean refutation pathway: if the strike-days gap is large but the union-wage-premium gap is statistically null once industry-mix is partialled out, the legal-regime channel washes out and the deindustrialisation channel wins. The spec must report the industry-mix-controlled robustness for that reason.
