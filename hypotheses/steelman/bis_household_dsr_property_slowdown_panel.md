# Steelman — Household DSR stress predicts property slowdown

The strongest alternative explanation is policy reaction. Household DSR stress often appears during tightening cycles, and forward house-price weakness may be driven by higher discount rates, unemployment expectations, or macroprudential rules rather than household payment burden itself. The panel screen cannot fully separate those channels.

Real house-price indexes also embed supply-side and demographic forces. Countries with severe housing shortages can maintain price growth despite household stress, while oversupplied markets can fall without high DSR. A common BIS panel threshold may therefore understate local market structure.

The country-relative 75th percentile threshold is helpful for comparability but can reclassify very different absolute stress environments as equivalent. The added absolute 12 percent floor reduces this problem, but does not eliminate it.

If supported, the narrow claim is that BIS household DSR stress predicts weaker forward real property-price growth. It should not be read as a full causal decomposition of rates, incomes, credit standards, and housing supply.
