# Steelman — Estonia market reform 30-year income convergence

## 1. Strongest version of the claim

Estonia’s reforms were uniquely comprehensive and credibly committed — the currency board acted as a hard monetary anchor that gradual reformers could not replicate. This institutional credibility, combined with rapid privatisation and trade opening, produced a durable growth premium that persisted for three decades, not merely a post-independence bounce.

## 2. Key evidence the claim would need

- A positive and significant cumulative log-GDP-pc gap between Estonia and its synthetic counterfactual over 1991–2024.
- Rising institutional-quality scores (WGI government effectiveness, rule of law) relative to gradual-reform peers.
- Higher trade openness and FDI inflows that correlate with the timing of reform.
- Robustness to donor-pool composition (e.g., excluding Baltic states or excluding Russia).

## 3. Best counterarguments

1. **Geographic accident.** Estonia borders Finland and is a short ferry ride from Sweden. Nordic spillovers, EU accession anticipation, and geographic compactness may explain convergence regardless of policy content.
2. **The Baltic Tigers also grew fast.** Latvia and Lithuania implemented more gradual reforms yet also converged strongly, especially after EU accession. If the donor pool already contains fast growers, the synthetic gap may be small even if Estonia outperformed the post-Soviet average.
3. **Donor-pool noise.** Russia’s 1990s collapse, Ukraine’s repeated crises, and Belarus’s authoritarian stagnation create extreme donor-pool heterogeneity. Permutation inference with such a noisy pool yields wide confidence intervals.
4. **Pre-existing industrial structure.** Estonia had a relatively light Soviet industrial footprint (electronics, services), making transition easier than for heavy-industry regions. The reform “effect” may be a composition effect.
5. **Small-sample inference.** With only one treated unit and a modest donor pool, standard inference is fragile; the result may be driven by a single outlier donor.

## 4. Boundary conditions

The claim is most plausible for small, open, post-Soviet economies with credible EU accession prospects and geographic proximity to rich markets. It is not generalisable to larger post-Soviet states (Russia, Ukraine) or to landlocked, institutionally weak economies.

## 5. Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis complements `post_soviet_transition_institutional_variation` by testing a single-country extreme case rather than a cross-sectional institutional gradient. It sits in tension with claims that EU accession alone drives convergence, because Estonia’s reforms pre-date accession by more than a decade.
