# Steelman — WTO accession predicts productivity spillovers through competition and market access

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that WTO accession binds governments to lower tariff and non-tariff barriers, which increases import competition and raises the quality of available intermediate inputs, both of which drive domestic firm productivity growth. When domestic entry barriers also fall, new domestic firms can enter and compete with imports, amplifying the productivity effect. The claim is causal in intent (accession is a discrete policy shock) but relies on event-study and DiD identification rather than pure exogeneity.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Clean event-study dynamics**: Pre-trend coefficients flat (no anticipatory effects), followed by a sustained positive post-accession TFP trajectory.
2. **Competition and input-quality channels**: Import penetration rises post-accession, and the TFP effect is larger in sectors with higher initial input-tariff levels.
3. **Entry-barrier interaction**: The effect is materially larger in countries that also reduced domestic entry barriers, confirming the complementarity claim.
4. **Cross-estimator agreement**: Callaway-Sant'Anna, synthetic DiD, and local projections should all produce positive post-accession gaps.

## Best counterarguments

1. **China dominates the sample**: China's 2001 WTO accession is the largest and most studied episode. If the average effect is driven entirely by China, the finding is a single-country result, not a general WTO effect. Ex-China robustness is essential.
2. **Shallow commitments**: Many WTO members maintain high applied tariffs bound well below MFN rates, or use non-tariff barriers (SPS, technical regulations) to protect domestic producers. Accession may not change actual trade policy much.
3. **Endogenous accession timing**: Countries accede when they are already reforming. The productivity gain may reflect the pre-accession reform momentum rather than accession itself.
4. **No TFP acceleration in some accessions**: Russia (2012), Saudi Arabia (2005), and several small island economies did not experience clear TFP accelerations post-accession. A null or negative average effect is possible if these cases offset China.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for manufacturing-oriented middle-income economies with sufficient state capacity to implement WTO commitments. It is weakest for: (a) resource-dominated economies where trade policy is irrelevant to the dominant sector; (b) economies that accede with very high binding overhang (applied tariffs already low); (c) economies where accession is symbolic and not accompanied by domestic regulatory reform.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis directly engages `trade_lib_china_wto_2001_manufacturing_export_surge` by generalising beyond China. It is complementary to `liberal_free_trade_partner_growth_panel_1990_2020` (FTA effects) and tests whether multilateral (WTO) liberalisation has distinct productivity effects from preferential liberalisation. A split result (WTO works only with domestic reform) would support the "bundled reform" view common in the growth literature.
