# Steelman — Quality-Adjusted Consumption in Market-Liberal Economies

## Strongest version of the claim

Market-liberal economies generate faster growth in quality-adjusted consumption than state-directed peers over long horizons, because competitive entry and trade liberalisation expand product variety, improve quality, and drive down relative prices faster than central planning or industrial targeting. The quality-adjustment matters: even when headline consumption growth is similar, the market-liberal path delivers more variety, better durability, and faster adoption of new goods, which standard national-accounts consumption deflators understate.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Broda-Romalis or Feenstra-style variety-adjusted price indices** showing larger terms-of-gains from variety in market-liberal regimes.
2. **Household-level microdata** (scanner data, CEX, HFCS) documenting faster quality upgrading and new-product adoption in liberalised markets.
3. **Cross-country panel** with PMR, trade openness, and EFW predicting consumption growth after controlling for initial income, human capital, and demographics.
4. **Event studies** of trade-liberalisation episodes (e.g., post-Uruguay Round, China's WTO accession, India's 1991 reforms) showing consumption-basket improvement.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Quality adjustment is measurement fantasy**. National accountants already quality-adjust; further claims are speculative and vulnerable to cherry-picking.
2. **East Asian developmental states delivered massive consumption gains**. South Korea and Taiwan combined state direction with rapid consumption growth; the market-liberal framing ignores successful heterodox cases.
3. **Public provision may outperform markets in specific domains**. Healthcare, education, and public transport quality may improve faster under state direction, offsetting market advantages in consumer durables.
4. **Inequality matters for median consumption**. Market-liberal economies may show faster mean consumption growth but slower median growth if gains concentrate at the top.
5. **Environmental and social costs are externalised**. Quality-adjusted consumption may rise while environmental quality or leisure falls, producing no true welfare gain.

## Boundary conditions

- Most defensible for **tradeable consumer goods** where variety and price competition are strong.
- Less defensible for **non-tradeable services** (health, education, housing) where market failures are more severe.
- Catch-up economies may show fast consumption growth under state direction because technology adoption is faster than frontier innovation.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to trade-liberalisation welfare gains, competition-policy consumer-benefit claims, and the broader market-institutions prosperity literature. Tensions with industrial-policy success stories and with inequality-focused critiques of market liberalisation.
