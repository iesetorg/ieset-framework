# Steelman — Judicial Independence and Growth Persistence

## Strongest version of the claim

Judicial independence is the institutional bedrock that makes economic reforms credible and durable. When courts can constrain the executive, enforce contracts impartially, and protect property rights against political reversal, investors respond to reforms with long-horizon commitments rather than short-term extraction. Consequently, countries with stronger judicial independence experience lower investment volatility and stronger post-reform GDP growth over the following decade. The claim is not that judicial independence directly raises growth, but that it moderates the growth trajectory after major reform episodes, preventing the stop-go patterns observed in countries where reforms are repeatedly reversed.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive interaction:** The reform-episode × judicial-independence interaction is positive and significant for post-reform GDP growth in a panel FE specification.
2. **Tercile magnitude:** Top-tercile judicial-independence countries show at least 1.0 percentage point lower standard deviation of investment/GDP and at least 0.4 percentage points higher annual growth in the 10 years post-reform than bottom-tercile countries.
3. **Exclusion robustness:** The result survives dropping post-Soviet transitions and China, confirming it is not driven by transition-specific dynamics or authoritarian exceptionalism.
4. **Mechanism evidence:** Judicial independence is associated with lower policy-reversal risk (measured by Fraser EFW stability or political-risk ratings), supporting the credibility channel.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Institutional bundle:** Judicial independence is highly correlated with democracy, press freedom, bureaucratic quality, and general rule of law; isolating the marginal effect of courts is difficult and the coefficient may capture bundled good governance rather than judicial constraints per se.
2. **Authoritarian exceptions:** Singapore and Dubai achieved rapid growth with relatively independent commercial courts despite limited democratic accountability, suggesting that commercial-rule-of-law can be decoupled from full judicial independence.
3. **Reform success may build courts:** Countries whose reforms succeed may invest in judicial independence ex post to lock in gains, making the direction of causation ambiguous.
4. **Reform definition noise:** Major reform episodes are identified from large Fraser EFW changes, which may conflate genuine structural reforms with measurement error or short-term policy fluctuations.
5. **Two-year exclusion is short:** The exclusion of only the first two years post-reform may not fully capture implementation lags, biasing the interaction toward countries with faster-adjusting economies rather than stronger institutions.

## Boundary conditions

The claim applies to **a broad global panel** over 1980–2020. It is most relevant for **countries undergoing identifiable structural reforms** (privatisation, trade opening, price liberalisation). It is less relevant for **stable high-income economies** where reform episodes are rare, and for **conflict-affected states** where courts do not function regardless of formal independence.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Linked to `rule_of_law_market_reform_complementarity` and `property_rights_long_run_income_frontier`. Tensions with hypotheses that attribute reform success to initial income, human capital, or external anchors (EU accession, IMF programmes) rather than domestic judicial capacity.
