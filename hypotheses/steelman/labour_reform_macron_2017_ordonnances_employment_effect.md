# Steelman — Macron ordonnances employment effect

The strongest objection: France 2017-2019 was a euro-area cyclical upswing window in which every euro-area economy grew labour, so a synthetic-control gap of +1pp lies inside the noise band of donor-pool weights and within the range of yellow-vest-induced fiscal stimulus that ran in parallel. The 2018 ISF wealth-tax abolition and CSG-CICE conversion are bundled into the same period and are arguably what moved the labour-demand schedule, not the ordonnances. Critics including Cahuc and the Insee 2020 evaluation have argued the dismissal-cost ceiling was rarely binding because severance had been bargained around the prior Macron-ceiling levels for years, making the ordonnance a paper change rather than a structural one. A clean falsification pathway: if employment gains are concentrated in apprenticeship and CDD (fixed-term) rather than CDI (open-ended) — which is what the early DARES microdata suggest — then the ordonnances reduced the cost of precarious employment without expanding stable employment, and the headline +1pp masks a substitution between contract types that the steelman reader can use to argue the reform was distributionally regressive. Pairing employment with poverty rate at 60% median is the discipline: if poverty rises within the same window, the labour-market gain has come at a transfer-side cost and the headline claim is weakened.
