# Steelman — Informality and Market Entry Barriers

## Strongest version of the claim

Informal employment is not merely a symptom of poverty or low state capacity; it is a rational response to high entry barriers, burdensome regulation, and insecure property rights that make formalisation unattractive or impossible. Even when the state invests heavily in infrastructure and development projects, persistent informality signals that the regulatory environment discourages formal business creation. The policy implication is that regulatory simplification and secure tenure are necessary complements to public investment for formal-sector job creation.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Cross-country panel** showing positive, significant association between entry-barrier indices (PMR, Doing Business cost) and informal-employment shares, after controlling for public investment and infrastructure quality.
2. **Microdata** from enterprise surveys (World Bank Enterprise Surveys, GEM) documenting that regulatory burden and licensing costs are top-cited barriers to formalisation.
3. **Natural experiments**: one-stop shop reforms, regulatory-guillotine programmes, or land-titling drives that reduced informality.
4. **Heterogeneity**: the effect should be stronger for small firms and in sectors with low capital requirements.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Informality is primarily a tax-avoidance strategy**. High taxes and weak enforcement, not entry barriers, drive informality; cutting red tape without cutting taxes will not help.
2. **Public investment raises productivity and draws workers into formality**. Infrastructure and state-led development projects may directly reduce informality by creating formal public-sector and construction jobs.
3. **Social protection encourages informality**. Generous social programmes may reduce the incentive to formalise because informal workers still receive benefits.
4. **Measurement error**. Informal-employment estimates are notoriously noisy and incomparable across countries; the panel correlation may reflect measurement artefacts.
5. **Cultural factors**. Informal work arrangements may be socially embedded and resistant to regulatory change.

## Boundary conditions

- Most relevant for **middle-income countries** where formalisation is economically feasible but administratively costly.
- Less relevant in **low-income agrarian economies** where informality is structural and most non-agricultural activity is by definition informal.
- Tax enforcement intensity may confound the entry-barrier channel.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

Connects to regulatory-burden, institutional-quality, and labour-market hypotheses. Tensions with claims that public investment and social protection are sufficient to reduce informality without regulatory reform.
