# Steelman

The progressive-tax growth-cost claim faces the Nordic and continental European counterexample. High recurring taxes may finance education, child care, health care, infrastructure, and insurance against income shocks. These can increase risk-taking and labour-market matching even when statutory tax burdens are high.

The best redistribution-friendly prediction is that the high-tax threshold disappears after controls, or turns positive in high-capacity states. If high-tax regimes grow as fast as lower-tax peers conditional on initial income and institutions, the threshold mechanism fails.
