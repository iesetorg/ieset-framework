# Steelman — Spain reported sexual-assault rate with definitional change controlled

This is a politically charged topic. The framework's role is to enforce measurement discipline, not to take sides. The steelman below engages the strongest objections from every relevant direction: the legal, the feminist-criminological, the demographic, and the civil-libertarian. A result that would be defensible to all four constituencies is the standard the framework holds itself to.

**Structural point the steelman must not lose sight of.** The Ley Orgánica 10/2022 ("solo sí es sí") reform did two distinct things, and both must be distinguished before any other argument is credible. (a) It merged the prior "abuso sexual" and "agresión sexual" offence categories into a single consent-based "agresión sexual" category, with the legal burden shifted to the presence or absence of freely-given consent rather than the presence or absence of violence/intimidation. (b) It broadened the range of conduct prosecutable as sexual assault (e.g., certain non-consensual acts previously prosecutable only as lesser offences or not prosecutable). Both changes mechanically expand the count of offences that fit the "sexual assault" statistical cell without any change in underlying behaviour. Any pre-vs-post comparison that does not decompose these is scientifically invalid. This is not a contested claim; it is definitional arithmetic.

**Legal-framing steelman (what a Spanish criminal lawyer would argue):**

1. The 2022 reform generated a well-known transitional sentencing anomaly: because the consolidated single category carried a different penalty range than the two prior categories, several hundred already-sentenced offenders had sentences reduced under the principle of retroactive application of the more-lenient law, and a subset were released earlier than they would have been. This attracted intense political and media attention. The statute was partially corrected by Ley Orgánica 4/2023. The reporting climate during late 2022 and through 2023 was therefore not neutral: it included intense coverage of the sentencing anomalies, which plausibly affected both victim reporting behaviour and police/prosecutor classification behaviour. The framework's "residual component" must be read in this context — it is not a clean residual.

2. Police-classification practice under the new statute is not fully settled; different Fiscalías may have resolved borderline cases differently in the first 12-18 months of the new regime. Reported-rate data aggregated at country-year level conceals this heterogeneity. A rigorous analysis requires regional (CCAA) level disaggregation and comparison of classification rates across Fiscalías — which the v1 hypothesis does not do. The v1 finding on aggregate data should be labelled provisional pending the CCAA-level decomposition.

**Feminist-criminological steelman (what specialists in sexual-violence research would argue):**

3. Reporting-rate changes after high-profile legal reforms and after public awareness campaigns are a well-documented phenomenon. Sweden's 2018 consent-law reform was followed by a reported-rate increase that the Swedish National Council for Crime Prevention (BRÅ) attributed in large part to increased reporting, not to increased offending. The German 2017 StGB §177 reform produced a similar pattern. In these cases, the data interpretation the feminist-criminological literature converged on is: the reform BROADENED the legal definition of assault in a way that better reflected victim experience, and the increase in reports is partly a closure of the previously-existing under-reporting gap — which is a public-health improvement, not a deterioration in public safety. The framework's decomposition must be read with this interpretation made available as a primary candidate for the residual component, not treated as one hypothesis among many.

4. Victimisation-survey evidence is the gold standard for distinguishing reporting-rate from offending-rate change. The Encuesta Nacional de Violencia contra la Mujer (ENVS) 2019 wave is pre-reform; its 2023 or later post-reform wave is the single most important piece of evidence for separating the residual component. Until a post-reform wave is available, claims about whether underlying offending changed are unresolvable from aggregate administrative data. The framework must state this limit honestly and not overreach.

5. The "definitional component explains the spike" framing is correct arithmetically but can be deployed in bad faith to dismiss the finding that REPORTED assaults rose substantially. A report-rate increase is itself a policy-relevant outcome even if offending did not rise, because it reflects victim willingness to report, confidence in the legal system, and the extent to which the legal system's category boundaries matched victim experience. Reducing under-reporting is a policy objective in its own right. Presenting "it's just a definitional change" as dismissive overlooks this.

**Demographic steelman (what a migration-and-population demographer would argue):**

6. Spain's foreign-born population share rose from ~14% in 2015 to ~18% in 2023. Any cross-country comparison of reported-offence rates must adjust for compositional shifts, and the framework includes migration-stock as a control. But: (a) the relationship between migration composition and reported offence rates in aggregate administrative data is confounded by differential reporting rates (victims within migrant communities may under-report to police for legal-status, cultural-barrier, or trust-in-institutions reasons), (b) the raw correlation between migration-share changes and reported-offence-rate changes is NOT evidence of a compositional causal effect once these reporting-rate differentials are accounted for, and (c) the framework should be explicit that the migration-stock control is for statistical-adjustment purposes and is not advancing a claim about any specific subgroup. The framework's own language on this point should be tightened in the result card to prevent misinterpretation.

7. Population-age-structure shifts in Spain 2015-2023 are modest; the 15-34 male population share did not change dramatically, so the age-and-sex-composition channel is a small explanatory factor. This is an empirically-constrained observation, not a political one.

**Civil-libertarian steelman (what a due-process-focused critic would argue):**

8. The 2022 reform's broadening of what counts as sexual assault has legitimate civil-liberties concerns (scope of criminal law, standards of proof, retroactivity treatment). These concerns are legal-philosophical and are not within the framework's scope. The framework should not take a position on them. It should also not allow its statistical decomposition to be read as endorsing or criticising the reform. The only claim the framework advances is that the reform created a measurement-regime change that must be controlled for in data interpretation; any further claim is beyond the framework's competence.

**Combinatorial steelman (what integrates the four):**

9. A defensible result card states: "Spain's pre-reform (2015-2021) reported sexual-offence rate trajectory was close to the European average reporting-culture trend once media-attention and reporting-propensity proxies are included. The 7 October 2022 reform produced a substantial level-shift in the reported-offence series; decomposition attributes the majority to definitional reclassification, with a residual component that the framework cannot further decompose from aggregate administrative data alone. Separating the residual into (underlying-offending change) vs (reporting-propensity change) vs (police-classification change) requires victimisation-survey evidence (ENVS post-reform wave), regional Fiscalía-level data, and cross-referencing with conviction-rate series. Until those are available, the framework's finding is narrowly about measurement discipline, not about whether Spanish public safety has deteriorated under Sánchez-era governance."

**What the framework MUST NOT do:**

- Report a raw pre-vs-post reported-rate comparison without decomposition. This would be scientifically illegitimate and the framework knows it.
- Advance any claim about subgroup offending from aggregate administrative data with reporting-rate confounds.
- Use the definitional-change finding to dismiss legitimate public concerns about reporting-rate trends; under-reporting closure is a real phenomenon with its own policy relevance.
- Use the under-reporting-closure framing to dismiss legitimate concerns that some residual component may include real offending-rate change; the framework does not know, and says so.
- Take a position on the merits of Ley Orgánica 10/2022 as a legal or political matter. That debate belongs to lawyers, legislators, and the Spanish electorate.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Headline statistic is NOT the raw change in reported-offence rate 2021 → 2023. That number, read raw, is not a scientifically valid input to any conclusion.
- Headline statistic is the decomposition: how much of the level-shift is definitional, how much is residual, and what the residual's confidence interval is.
- Victimisation-survey post-reform wave is called out as a v2 requirement the framework will not pre-register findings on until data is available.
- Every statement in the result card should be one a Spanish criminal lawyer, a feminist criminologist, a migration demographer, and a civil-libertarian critic could each disagree-with-but-accept-as-scientifically-competent. If any of the four would say "that is scientifically illiterate", the result card is not ready.
