# Steelman — Capital account openness supports prosperity only above institutional-quality thresholds

## Strongest version of the claim

The strongest version is that capital account liberalisation is not a uniform treatment. In high-institutional-quality environments, it facilitates efficient capital allocation, technology transfer via FDI, and risk diversification, raising long-run growth. In low-institutional-quality environments, it enables capital flight, currency mismatches, and boom-bust cycles, with crisis costs that outweigh any allocative benefits. The claim is not that capital controls are optimal for poor institutions, but that the net growth effect of openness is conditional and likely negative below a threshold.

## Key evidence the claim would need

1. **Positive interaction**: A robustly positive coefficient on (capital openness × institutional quality) in a growth panel, with the main effect of openness insignificant or negative.
2. **Subsample split confirmation**: Above-median institutional quality = positive openness-growth link; below-median = null or negative link.
3. **Crisis-channel confirmation**: The below-median subsample should show higher crisis frequency or severity following liberalisation, confirming the mechanism.
4. **Flow-type decomposition**: Long-term FDI inflows should be associated with growth even in weaker institutional settings, while portfolio and debt flows should drive the crisis result.

## Best counterarguments

1. **Reverse causality**: Countries with stronger growth prospects and deeper financial markets liberalise first. The institutional-quality interaction may capture growth potential rather than a true threshold effect.
2. **Coarse index**: The Chinn-Ito KAOPEN index is based on IMF AREAER coarse categories and does not capture enforcement, de facto openness, or the composition of flows. A country with high de jure openness but extensive macroprudential controls may be misclassified.
3. **IMF-program endogeneity**: Many liberalisation episodes were IMF conditions during crises. The post-crisis recovery may be misattributed to liberalisation, or the crisis may be misattributed to pre-liberalisation conditions.
4. **China's capital controls**: China maintained extensive capital controls while growing rapidly, but also accumulated large foreign reserves and experienced capital-flight episodes. The case does not clearly support either side.

## Boundary conditions

The claim is strongest for emerging markets with intermediate institutional development. It is weakest for: (a) frontier economies where capital markets are already deep and openness is largely complete; (b) failed states where any financial policy is irrelevant; (c) resource-rich economies where capital-account policy is secondary to commodity-price dynamics.

## Relation to existing hypotheses

This hypothesis bridges trade/financial policy and institutional quality. It is complementary to `fiscal_dominance_japan_debt_non_crisis` and `gfc_balance_sheet_recession_post_2008_household_dual_mandate` by focusing on the capital-account side of financial stability. A clean threshold result would support the "institutions first, then openness" sequencing view associated with the Washington Consensus critique.
