# Steelman: government_spending_tfp_drag_panel

## Counter-argument

Government spending can raise TFP through public investment in infrastructure,
education, and basic research that private markets under-provide due to
positive externalities. High-spending Nordic countries consistently score
among the highest TFP levels globally, suggesting that spending *composition*
matters more than spending *level*.

## Strongest empirical objections

1. **Composition endogeneity**: WDI government spending aggregates consumption
   (wages, transfers) with investment (infrastructure, R&D). A negative
   coefficient may reflect high consumption shares in low-TFP countries rather
   than a causal effect of spending level.

2. **Reverse causality**: Countries experiencing negative TFP shocks (e.g.
   commodity busts, financial crises) may increase automatic stabiliser spending,
   creating a spurious negative association.

3. **Nordic exception**: Denmark, Sweden, and Norway combine high government
   spending with high TFP. If the negative association is driven by low-income
   high-spending countries, it may not generalise to well-governed high-spending
   regimes.

4. **Threshold effects**: The relationship may be non-linear, with positive
   returns at low spending levels (public goods provision) and negative returns
   only beyond a threshold.

## What would flip the verdict

- A positive and significant coefficient on government spending after
  controlling for investment composition.
- The Nordic subsample showing a *positive* spending-TFP association.
- Instrumental-variable estimates (using IMF programme exposure or natural
  disaster incidence as instruments) showing no significant effect.
