# Steelman — Thatcher-era UK privatisations and productivity

**The strongest argument against this hypothesis:**

The "Thatcher privatisations produced measurable productivity gains" claim is contested on identification grounds and on whether the productivity gains attributed to privatisation in the 1980s are properly attributed to the change of ownership at all. Three families of objection are weighty enough that a country-level DiD around 1984 must engage them.

1. **The pre-privatisation labour-shedding phase under public ownership did most of the lifting.** Massimo Florio's "The Great Divestiture" (MIT Press, 2004) and a long line of LSE/CEP work (Newbery, Pollitt, Parker) shows that the largest productivity acceleration at BT, British Gas, British Steel and the electricity industry occurred BEFORE the formal share-sale dates, during the 1979-1984 public-sector restructuring under Thatcher's "fattening for sale" preparation. The CEGB shed roughly a third of its workforce 1979-1989, with most of the cuts before the 1990 unbundling. If the pre-1984 productivity acceleration is misclassified as the "pre" baseline against which privatisation is measured, the DiD recovers a within-restructuring trend rather than a privatisation effect.

2. **The macro confound: 1980-1981 recession plus mid-1980s recovery.** UK 1980-1981 was the deepest recession since the 1930s, with GDP falling about 4.6% and manufacturing output by ~14%. Productivity growth in the recovery phase 1983-1988 was a Europe-wide phenomenon (FRA, ITA, DEU all show recovery-phase productivity acceleration). Attributing the GBR mean 1984-1990 TFP growth to privatisation rather than to mean-reversion off a deep recession trough confuses "back to trend" with "above trend". A clean test must show GBR pulling away from comparator OECD economies; without that, the "privatisation effect" is plausibly the recovery effect.

3. **Cost-shifting via regulator-set price caps and under-investment.** The post-privatisation regime relied on RPI-X price-cap regulation (Littlechild). Apparent productivity gains in the privatised utilities partly reflect deferred investment in network infrastructure (most famously water — Ofwat capex deficits accumulated through the 1990s and 2000s, contributing to the post-2020 sewage-discharge and bills crisis), and partly reflect labour-shedding that pushed costs onto the dole queue and the social-security budget. Sector-level productivity numbers that ignore the consumer-side and fiscal-side cost shift can read as net gains when the welfare arithmetic is closer to a wash.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

The v1 result card must:

(a) Distinguish the pre/post change inside GBR from the GBR-vs-comparator differential. If the pre/post change is positive but the differential is zero, the productivity acceleration is pan-OECD recovery-driven, not privatisation-driven, and the hypothesis is at most PARTIAL.

(b) Report the 1979-1983 vs 1984-1990 GBR change against pre-1979 baseline (1975-1978) so readers can see whether the inflection point is the 1979 election (consistent with the labour-shedding-under-public-ownership story) or 1984 (consistent with the privatisation story). PWT TFP at constant national prices (rtfpna) supports this.

(c) Note that this country-level test cannot separate the privatised-sector effect from the rest-of-economy effect. A firm-level FAME panel of BT, BG, BAA, water-and-sewerage companies vs comparison utilities would be the proper test; until that data is in the vintage tree, the country-level result is at most suggestive.

(d) Be neutral about the price/welfare side of the claim. PWT TFP measures real output per composite input; it does not capture consumer-price changes in privatised sectors, network under-investment, or the social-security cost of labour-shedding. The claim has a price-reduction component this run cannot test; the result card must say so.

The strongest version of the steelman argues the country-level DiD will recover a positive but small effect that does not separate privatisation from the surrounding Thatcher labour-market reforms (1980 Employment Act, 1982 Employment Act, 1984 Trade Union Act, 1984-85 miners' strike) and from the post-recession recovery. A v2 with sectoral electricity/telecoms/water productivity series and a firm-level event study against comparison utilities is the proper test.
