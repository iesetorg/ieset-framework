# Steelman — Jamaica IMF debt restructuring 2010-2024

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis treats Jamaica as a debt-sustainability success. The opposing view is that the success was achieved at substantial growth and welfare cost, and that the model is brittle to climate or commodity shocks.

Specific objections:

1. **Growth performance was very weak.** Cumulative real-GDP growth 2013-2019 averaged only ~1.0% annualised — well below Caribbean peers and below DOM. The "fiscal success at growth cost" frame is closer to the truth than "fiscal success without growth penalty".

2. **Primary-surplus targets crowded out public investment.** Capital expenditure as a share of GDP fell to historically low levels through the EFF window. Infrastructure depreciation is a deferred fiscal cost.

3. **Migration as adjustment channel.** Jamaican migration to North America accelerated through the window, with remittance inflows offsetting weak domestic earnings. The "internal" sustainability metric understates the external-adjustment dependence.

4. **Climate-event vulnerability.** Hurricane Beryl (2024) and frequent storm events impose contingent fiscal costs not captured in the descriptive trajectory. The 50-percentage-point debt reduction is fragile to a single major event.

5. **Distributional cost.** Public-sector wage compression and social-spending restraint imposed real income costs on civil servants and welfare recipients. The political sustainability is partly a function of bipartisan elite support, not broad consensus.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Report cumulative growth alongside debt-reduction.
(b) Show public-investment trajectory.
(c) Note migration/remittance role.
(d) Acknowledge climate-event contingent-liability risk.
