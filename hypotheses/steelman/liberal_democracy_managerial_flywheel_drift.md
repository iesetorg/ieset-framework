# Steelman — Liberal-democracy managerial-flywheel drift

## The strongest case AGAINST this hypothesis

The hypothesis frames multi-decade drift toward more state activity as a
quasi-mechanical consequence of democratic political-economy incentives. There
are several substantial reasons to doubt that framing.

### 1. The drift index measures *legislated direction*, not *level* — and most liberal democracies started from very different baselines

Greece in 1976 had a markedly larger state share, more rigid labour rules and
heavier sectoral licensing than the median country in the sample. Its
post-2010 negative drift in the index is real, but it is drift from a high
baseline toward a position that's still well above the OECD median. Reading
that as "Greece reversed the creep" is too strong. The same applies in the
other direction: Germany's positive drift is from a constrained baseline
(Schuldenbremse, post-1990 fiscal restoration, ordoliberal price-stability
regime) to a position that is still well below where many high-statist
countries sat in 1976. Until the index is calibrated to absolute levels —
which requires a series like government-spending-share-of-GDP across the full
window, not just legislated moves — the directional pattern can be misread as
a level claim.

### 2. The composite-axis weighting is author-chosen and embeds priors

The author's framework counts ten axes as "pro-state-expansion" and four as
"pro-market." The choice of which axis goes in which bucket — and the choice
of equal weighting across axes — is not a neutral measurement decision. A
defender of regulatory expansion would argue that
`regulatory.environmental_stringency` increases capture *positive externality
correction*, not state expansion per se; that
`regulatory.financial_deregulation` increases (which the schema labels as
"tighter regulation") capture *post-2008 prudential restoration* of rules that
had been wrongly removed; that `regulatory.sectoral_licensing` increases
capture *consumer protection*. Reweighting the index — for example, treating
environmental stringency as neutral or excluding it — would change the sign of
the composite for several countries.

### 3. The "creep" mechanism candidates are partly empirically refuted

The two most-cited mechanisms — median-voter ageing and fiscal-flywheel
politics — have mixed empirical track records. Japan has the most aged
electorate in the sample, but its post-2012 statist-drift slope is actually
modest in the index, not extreme. Italy has aged dramatically and gone in the
*opposite* direction (negative drift) under post-2010 austerity politics. The
fiscal-flywheel argument predicts that progressive-tax expansion + transfer
expansion should co-move, but the corpus shows numerous cases where
high-transfer countries (Sweden, Denmark) actually cut top marginal tax rates
in the 1990s while preserving the transfer system. The mechanism is plausible
but not as universal as a strong-form managerial-flywheel reading would
suggest.

### 4. The political-economy literature itself has split predictions

Tullock, Olson, Buchanan and Niskanen (the public-choice canon the hypothesis
draws on) predict expansion. But other strands — Acemoglu's institutional
endogeneity (institutions self-correct under high state capacity), the fiscal
limits literature (debt-to-GDP ceilings bind politically), and the
"varieties-of-capitalism" tradition (corporatist coordination keeps
state-share roughly stable) — predict different equilibria. The hypothesis
treats public-choice as the default; the literature doesn't.

### 5. Survivorship + selection effects in the corpus

The framework's movement corpus over-represents major reform episodes (which
get well-documented) relative to status-quo-preserving administrations (which
don't). A government that doesn't legislate much shows up as a thin corpus
entry with few `axes_summary` moves; an aggressive reformer shows up as a
dense entry with strong moves. This biases the drift index toward whichever
side is more associated with reform energy in any given era. In the 1980s
that was market-pivot reform (Thatcher, Reagan, Roger Douglas, Mulroney); in
the post-2008 era it has been state-expansionary reform (ARRA, IRA, Bürgergeld,
Energiewende). The same data could equally be read as "drift in whichever
direction is currently being legislated about" rather than as a structural
asymmetry.

### 6. Reversal episodes are not as rare as the hypothesis claims

The hypothesis frames Mulroney Canada, NZ Rogernomics, post-1990 Sweden, post-
2010 Greece + Italy + Spain + Ireland as exceptional. The framework's own
corpus has these countries plus several others with sustained net-negative
drift (Czech Klaus era, Slovakian Dzurinda flat-tax, Estonia post-1992,
Australia Hawke-Keating reforms, UK Thatcher-Major 1979-1997, Spain post-1992
ERM convergence, Portugal post-1986 EEC accession, Sweden post-1992 banking
crisis reforms). Counting them honestly, the corpus shows reversal as a
*regular* feature of liberal-democracy policy history, not a rare exception.

## What would change my mind

If the share of liberal democracies with positive composite drift is
statistically indistinguishable from 50%, or if a multivariate spec controlling
for initial spending-level + fiscal-rule presence collapses the median-positive
finding, then the strong-form managerial-flywheel hypothesis is not what the
data is showing — it's just describing a transient era of state expansion in
some places. A weaker form — "the political-economy incentives generate drift
*conditional on the absence of binding fiscal rules and absence of severe
fiscal crises*" — is consistent with both the corpus pattern and the
literature, but it's not the falsifiable headline.
