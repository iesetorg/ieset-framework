# Steelman — UAE diversification 1990-2024

**Strongest arguments against the framework's framing:**

1. **Geographical hub premium is the dominant non-policy factor.** Dubai's location at the centre of Asia-Europe-Africa shipping/aviation networks generates entrepôt rents that no other GCC country could replicate even with identical policy. The geography-adjusted specification is essential; if the residual policy-effect is small after controlling for geography, the "policy bundle drives outcomes" framing is wrong.

2. **The Dubai 2009 debt-crisis exposed the financialisation fragility.** Dubai's pre-2008 growth was leveraged real-estate and infrastructure expansion. The 2009 standstill on Dubai World debt (~$26bn) required Abu Dhabi bailout and revealed that Dubai's "diversified" economy was heavily dependent on cyclical leverage. Counting 2003-2008 boom GDP without acknowledging the post-2009 deleveraging cycle overstates the policy-success story.

3. **Migrant labour and kafala system underwrite the productivity numbers.** UAE non-oil sector productivity is built on cheap migrant labour (~88% of population is non-citizen). Aggregate per-capita GDP is computed including migrant labourers earning sub-poverty wages; GDP-per-citizen would be very different. Welfare implications differ substantially based on which population is in the denominator.

4. **PIF / Mubadala / ADIA cross-investment confounds independence.** UAE sovereign-wealth funds invest globally but also support domestic projects, recapitalise local firms, and absorb private-sector losses. The boundary between "private-sector growth" and "state-capital allocation" is blurred. Counting all non-oil GDP as private-sector dynamism is misleading.

5. **Other GCC peers are converging.** Saudi Arabia (Vision 2030), Qatar (post-2010 LNG buildout), Oman (Vision 2040), Bahrain (financial sector). The UAE-vs-GCC gap that was wide in 1995 is narrower in 2024. Choosing endpoint matters; peak-divergence years 2005-2015 may overstate the long-run comparative gap.

6. **Common-law jurisdictions (DIFC, ADGM) are real institutional innovation but limited in scope.** They serve foreign-investment corporate disputes; the broader UAE legal system remains civil-law with sharia influences. Counting DIFC/ADGM as full institutional reform overstates depth.

**How this should shape the v1 result card:**

- Report geography-adjusted and unadjusted gaps prominently.
- Report GDP-per-citizen as a memo item alongside GDP-per-capita.
- Distinguish Dubai (services hub) and Abu Dhabi (hydrocarbon SWF) trajectories where data permit.
- Acknowledge the migrant-labour welfare/aggregation problem.
- Frame as "successful entrepôt-hub case study with confounding geographical advantage" rather than universal-replication model.
