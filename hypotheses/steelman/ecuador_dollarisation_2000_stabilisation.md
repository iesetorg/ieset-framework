# Steelman — Ecuador dollarisation 2000 stabilisation

**The strongest argument against:**

The hypothesis treats dollarisation as a successful inflation-stabilisation. The opposing view is that the apparent success is largely a function of the post-crisis base effects and the oil-price tailwind 2003-2014, and that the structural costs of irrevocable monetary surrender will manifest in the long run.

Specific objections:

1. **Post-crisis base effects.** Inflation fell from 91% (2000) almost mechanically: a 91% rate is unsustainable regardless of regime. Most of the inflation collapse 2000-2003 reflects mean reversion plus the elimination of currency-mismatch panic, not dollarisation per se.

2. **Oil-price tailwind dominates 2003-2014 fiscal accounts.** Brent rose from $25/bbl (2002) to $110/bbl (2011). Ecuador's petroleum-export revenues during this period funded fiscal expansion regardless of monetary regime.

3. **Inability to absorb shocks.** The 2014-2016 oil-price collapse imposed a procyclical fiscal contraction Ecuador could not offset with monetary devaluation. Real GDP fell, and the social cost of the adjustment was substantial.

4. **Banking-sector fragility.** Without lender-of-last-resort capacity in domestic currency, Ecuador's banking system is structurally vulnerable to deposit runs in any crisis. The 2020 pandemic and 2024 fiscal stress required substantial multilateral support.

5. **Growth performance modest.** Cumulative log GDP-pc growth 2000-2019 was respectable but not exceptional in LATAM; Ecuador did not converge on Chile or Peru. The "stabilisation" success did not translate to a growth premium.

**Result-card implications:**

(a) Decompose inflation collapse into base-effect vs regime-change components.
(b) Show oil-price-controlled fiscal trajectory.
(c) Note 2014-2016 procyclical adjustment as a regime cost.
(d) Frame finding strictly as "inflation success", not "growth success".
