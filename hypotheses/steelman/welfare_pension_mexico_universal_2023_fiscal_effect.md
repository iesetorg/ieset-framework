# Steelman: Mexico Pension Universal 2023 Fiscal Effect

The strongest counter is that Mexico's pre-2023 social-spending share of GDP was unusually low for a middle-income country (around 7.5% versus 14% LatAm average), so a 1pp rise represents a normalisation toward regional norms rather than structural-fiscal deterioration. Mexico's debt-to-GDP ratio remained below 50% through 2024 — well within IMF sustainability thresholds — and the pension expansion is partly self-financing through reduced demand on residual social-assistance programmes that previously caught uncovered elderly. Sheinbaum's 2024 election platform argued the pension is the keystone of an AMLO-era social compact that depressurised political support for opposition movements, and its political-economy stability function offsets the headline fiscal cost. The IMF baseline projection itself was contested: opposition technocrats like Carstens argued the cost was underestimated; Hacienda argued it was overestimated. A defensible reading: the pension's fiscal cost is real but its sustainability depends on PEMEX restructuring and revenue-modernisation that are independent of the pension design itself.
