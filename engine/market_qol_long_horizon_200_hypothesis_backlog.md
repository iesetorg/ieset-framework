# 200 Long-Horizon Market Institutions and Quality-of-Life Hypotheses

This backlog expands the long-horizon free-market vs intervention thesis into quality-of-life outcomes. The central claim to test is:

> Over broad samples and long windows, freer market institutions, competition, property rights, openness, price signals, and rule-bound governance tend to raise durable quality of life; persistent market intervention, discretionary allocation, protection, price distortion, and incumbent favoritism tend to reduce it or create hidden tradeoffs.

This is not a conclusion. It is a falsifiable agenda. Broad scope reduces anomaly weight, but every test must still preserve heterogeneity: low-income catch-up, high-income frontier, small open economies, resource exporters, post-socialist transitions, and crisis episodes should be split where needed.

## Execution Notes

- Promote these in waves of 20-40, not all at once.
- Each wave should include at least 20 percent adversarial/boundary hypotheses so the framework remains credible.
- Use broad samples for the main thesis, but always stratify by income level, state capacity, resource dependence, and regime type.
- Quality of life should not be reduced to GDP. Use consumption, wages, housing affordability, health, education, life satisfaction, migration, corruption, innovation, and service access.
- Broad scope reduces anomaly weight only if the samples are not cherry-picked and if contrary cases remain visible.
