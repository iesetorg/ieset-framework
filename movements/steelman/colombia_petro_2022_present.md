# Steelman — Petro government (Colombia, 2022-present)

**The strongest argument that the Petro era should not be prematurely coded as an economic underperformer:**

1. **Only ~2 years of observable data.** Tax reform passed December 2022; pension reform passed 2024 then suspended; labour reform partially advanced; health reform shelved. A v1 assessment at the 2024-2025 horizon captures the transition phase, not the steady-state effect. The framework's integrity requires pre-registering the spec now and running the analysis at a later horizon (2027+) where the policies have had time to propagate. Early-term GDP slowdown in 2023 reflects global commodity-cycle and monetary-tightening effects, not Petro policy content per se.

2. **Global macro context 2022-2024 is unusual.** Post-COVID inflation surge peaked in 2022-2023 across LatAm; every peer economy (Chile, Peru, Mexico) faced real-rate tightening-driven demand compression regardless of fiscal stance. Attributing Colombia's growth slowdown to Petro policy without controlling for the regional monetary-tightening cycle overstates the policy share of variance. Synthetic-control / donor-pool methods controlling for the LatAm peer average are the minimum-acceptable specification.

3. **Major reforms were legislatively or judicially blocked.** Pension reform suspended 2025 by the Constitutional Court on procedural grounds; health reform shelved; labour reform partially enacted. The Petro *policy* content is substantially smaller than the Petro *proposed* content. Assessing actual policy impact requires scoping to what was enacted, not what was proposed.

4. **The "economy of life" energy-transition posture has not yet been operationalised.** No new oil-gas exploration contracts have been signed, but existing contracts continued execution and production has held up near term. The reserve-replacement ratio threat crystallises on a ~5-10-year horizon, outside the v1 assessment window. Coding the policy content is correct; attributing measured economic outcomes to it is premature.

5. **Redistributive tax reform yielded ~1.3% of GDP in revenue without the collapse-of-investment scenario critics predicted.** FDI in 2023-2024 remained broadly in line with LatAm peer trends. The tax reform's measurable first-order effect is a revenue boost, with second-order effects pending longer horizons.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Pre-register explicit donor pool (Peru, Chile, Mexico) with comparable-institutional-quality weighting; do not benchmark against OECD average or Brazil alone.
- Report the 2023-2024 growth differential with monetary-cycle controls explicit; separate policy-content effects from regional commodity-cycle effects.
- Only count enacted (not proposed) reforms in the treatment exposure measure.
- Report pending-reform disposition (blocked / modified / enacted) as an institutional-quality outcome, not as policy-content treatment.
- Commit to v1.1 re-evaluation at 2027+ horizon. The pre-registered spec locks the donor pool, estimator, and falsification rule so the later run is meaningful.
- Acknowledge that the causal-inference window for novel left-wing Colombian presidency is long (first instance, no within-country pre-treatment for comparison of this specific treatment).
