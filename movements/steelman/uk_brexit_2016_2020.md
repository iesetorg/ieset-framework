# Steelman — Brexit 2016–2020

1. **Brexit economic effects are under-powered to identify cleanly.** The UK had multiple concurrent shocks — austerity, COVID, energy crisis, high interest rates — which the same 2020-2023 window absorbs. Attributing specific UK underperformance to Brexit rather than to the confluence of other factors requires a credible counterfactual. Synthetic control work (Dhingra et al., Springford at CER) provides estimates but the donor pool selection is contested.
2. **The services-sector access story is more complex than "trade barriers hurt GDP".** UK financial services retained substantial EU access under equivalence decisions and adapted via EU-subsidiary arrangements. Goods-sector frictions were real; the aggregate services-sector hit was smaller than feared in several early projections.
3. **Regulatory autonomy has genuine value that GDP per capita doesn't capture.** Ability to set own policy on genetic engineering, financial-services rules, immigration composition, etc., is a political good the framework's narrowly economic coding excludes. D.1.6 and the framework-level disclosure recognise that aggregate GDP is not the only metric.
