# Steelman — Thatcherism

**The strongest argument against the framework's market-liberal framing of Thatcherism:**

The Thatcher period's headline productivity and growth figures disguise substantial costs that the framework must not underweight: regional deindustrialisation that produced long-term worklessness in former industrial towns, North Sea oil revenues that papered over fiscal stress, and a financial-sector expansion that imported 2008 crisis fragility. A full accounting treats Thatcherism as a mixed case with durable positives (privatisation of genuinely broken state enterprise, financial competitiveness, inflation-discipline restoration) and durable negatives (inequality trajectory, regional capital depletion, financialisation risk).

Specific objections the framework must engage with:

1. **North Sea oil as confounder.** Roughly 300 billion pounds of oil revenue flowed to the Treasury 1979–1990, coinciding with the privatisation programme. Attributing the fiscal turnaround to market reforms over oil windfall is a sequencing error. Norway's contemporaneous use of equivalent resource rents produced materially different outcomes (see `nordic_institutional_design` — the SWF mechanism).

2. **Regional and industrial costs.** South Wales, Yorkshire, the North-East, parts of Scotland experienced worklessness that persisted into the 2010s. The productivity gain at aggregate level coexisted with durable local capital destruction that generic "restructuring" framings understate.

3. **Financial-sector growth as embedded risk.** Big Bang 1986 liberalisation created the conditions under which London's derivatives and structured-products exposure grew, contributing to 2008-era fragility. Crediting the growth without noting the embedded systemic risk is incomplete.

4. **Poll tax failure.** Community Charge (1990) was a genuine policy failure — regressive design, mass non-compliance, and a precipitating factor in Thatcher's removal. The framework's axes summary should not omit it.

5. **Counterfactual question.** What the Heath / Callaghan governments would have achieved given the same external conditions (North Sea discoveries, post-1981 global disinflation, Soviet collapse trade effects) is genuinely uncertain. The "saved the UK" framing runs ahead of the counterfactual evidence.

**How this steelman should shape the result card:**

Linked hypotheses need to decompose the productivity gain into privatisation-attributable, oil-revenue-attributable, macroeconomic-stabilisation-attributable, and residual components. The framework should publish the decomposition even where it weakens the market-liberal headline, per the author's disclosed prior-updating commitment.
