# Steelman — Chavismo / Bolivarian Revolution

1. **Oil-price dependence was the binding constraint, not policy.** Venezuela's economy was oil-dependent before Chávez; the 2014-2016 oil-price crash would have produced severe contraction regardless of political regime. Blaming Chavismo for what OPEC did (and what Saudi Arabia's 2014 pricing strategy did) overstates policy's role. A counterfactual Venezuela under a market-friendly government during a 60%+ oil-price collapse would also have contracted sharply.
2. **US sanctions after 2015 (and especially after 2019) materially worsened the outcome.** Sanctions on PDVSA, financial-sector isolation, and oil-export restrictions produced supply-side damage that is distinct from the preceding policy failures. Coding the entire post-1999 trajectory as "Chavismo caused the collapse" conflates policy-induced dysfunction with externally-imposed economic warfare.
3. **Social outcomes in 2005-2012 — poverty reduction, literacy gains, infant mortality — were real.** The Chavismo era produced distributional improvements during the oil-revenue boom that aggregate GDP per capita misses. Framework-level coding requires being honest about where the movement delivered as well as where it failed, per D.1.6's distinguishing of growth from distributional outcomes.

**How this should shape the result card:**
- Decompose the post-1999 trajectory into pre-2013 (boom years, mixed outcomes), 2013-2014 (Maduro succession + oil peak), 2014-2016 (oil price crash absorbed disproportionately), 2017-2019 (hyperinflation onset), 2019+ (sanctions era). The four sub-periods have distinct characters.
- Compare to an oil-exporter peer set (Colombia, Ecuador, Mexico) to control for commodity cycle exposure; if Venezuela still shows much deeper decline than peers, the institutional-quality and policy-content channels are load-bearing.
- Acknowledge distributional gains 2003-2012 explicitly, even though the aggregate economic outcome is catastrophic.
