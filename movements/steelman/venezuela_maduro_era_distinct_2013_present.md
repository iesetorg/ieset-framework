# Steelman — Maduro-era Venezuela (distinct from Chávez era)

**The strongest argument that the Maduro-era collapse is not cleanly attributable to the regime's policy content:**

1. **The 2014-2016 oil-price crash was an exogenous ~60% collapse in Venezuela's dominant export.** PDVSA's pre-crash price of USD ~100/barrel halved within 18 months. A Colombia-Ecuador-Mexico donor pool only partially controls for this because oil's share of Venezuelan exports (~95%) vastly exceeds the pool average. Any regime — market-friendly or otherwise — would have faced severe fiscal compression. Attribution to Maduro-era policy without a credible oil-exposure-matched counterfactual overstates the domestic policy weight.

2. **US sanctions escalated substantially post-2015 and disproportionately post-2019 (PDVSA SDN designation, secondary sanctions on tanker operators, financial-system isolation).** The Rodriguez (2024) CEPR work argues that sanctions are a meaningful share of the 2019-2023 output loss, distinct from policy-induced dysfunction. A clean assessment separates pre-2015 / 2015-2019 / 2019+ sub-periods.

3. **Hyperinflation 2017-2019 has a specific proximate mechanism — fiscal-monetary fusion triggered by the collapse of oil revenues — that is endogenous to the oil shock, not a free policy choice.** Treating it as a pure Maduro-era policy failure understates the constraint: the regime faced a fiscal gap of such magnitude that monetisation was the only available instrument given market-access loss.

4. **Chavismo-era legacy institutions (PDVSA politicisation 2003, FX controls 2003, expropriation wave 2007-2015) pre-committed the Maduro administration to a degraded policy starting point.** The "distinct from Chávez" coding risks attributing to Maduro the accumulated consequences of decade-prior decisions he inherited rather than designed.

5. **Mass emigration of 7-8 million is itself a supply-side shock that compounds every other measurement.** Population denominator effects on per-capita GDP, labour-force composition shifts, and skilled-worker departure all change the measured outcome mechanically. A regime with better policies but identical external shocks would also have seen material emigration; the incremental Maduro-attributable share is unclear.

**How this should shape the result card:**

- Decompose the 2013-2023 cumulative output loss into the four pre-registered channels (oil shock, monetary-fiscal fusion, sanctions, political destabilisation) rather than a single aggregate treatment effect.
- Report the pre-sanctions Maduro period (2013-2015) and the deep-sanctions period (2019+) separately so the channel-specific attribution is visible.
- Acknowledge that the Chávez-era institutional inheritance (PDVSA, FX controls, expropriations) is load-bearing for the post-2013 trajectory and is coded in the predecessor movement, not re-attributed to Maduro.
- Present distributional indicators alongside aggregate ones: the 2013-2023 poverty and nutrition trajectories are themselves part of the outcome card.
- The hyperinflation episode is separately analysed via hyperinflation_requires_fiscal_dominance rather than re-attributed to general Maduro policy.
