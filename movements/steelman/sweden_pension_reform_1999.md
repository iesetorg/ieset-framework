# Steelman — Sweden pension reform 1999

**The strongest argument against the framework's positive coding:**

The Swedish NDC + funded reform transferred risk to workers and shrank the political commitment to retirement adequacy. Post-reform, the auto-balance mechanism has bitten repeatedly, cutting pensions below the implicit promise of the pre-1994 system. Treating the architectural switch as a success requires comparing outcomes on dimensions the framework's disclosed priors care about, not just fiscal sustainability.

Specific objections:

1. **Reduced retirement adequacy for affected cohorts.** The transition cohort experienced pension levels materially below what the old ATP would have delivered. "Fiscal sustainability" and "recipient welfare" are distinct; coding this as a Nordic success privileges the first.

2. **Premiepension fund choice has been widely criticised.** The individual-account second pillar has faced scandals (Allra 2017, AIP 2020) and median returns net of fees have disappointed. Retail asset-management markets with 700+ funds produce paralysis rather than informed choice for most participants.

3. **Automatic balancing shifts risk without shifting power.** The balance mechanism cuts pensions when demographic assumptions deteriorate. Contributors have no say; the state retains tax authority. "Forced saving with individual accounts" sounds market-oriented but the fund universe is regulated and contribution rate is set by law — this is a state-operated risk-transfer scheme in market-mechanism clothing.

4. **The cross-party consensus was enabled by Swedish institutional quality.** Other countries' attempts at NDC (Poland 1999, Latvia 1996, Italy's partial Dini 1995) produced more contested and partial transitions. The Swedish design works partly because Swedish political culture sustains the commitment; the architecture alone does not generalise.

**How this should shape the v3 analysis:** treat the 1999 reform as a forced-saving architecture shift, not a generic pro-market reform. Distinguish its fiscal-sustainability effects (supported) from its recipient-welfare effects (contested). The Nordic decomposition's "welfare architecture matters" claim is load-bearing on this interpretation.
